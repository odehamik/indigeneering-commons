WÂGUC ANDĒG KAYÄ.

WAAGOSH AANDEG GAYE.

THE FOX AND THE CROW.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ninguding wâgus pạbāmiba‘tōd ugīnisān wâbōzōn.

**Ningoding waagosh babaamibatood ogii-nisaan waaboozoon.**

*Once on a time, while a Fox was running about over the country, he killed a hare.*

Ningoding (once) waagosh (a fox) babaamibatood (while running about) ogii-nisaan (he killed) waaboozoon (a hare).

(2) Mīdạc kī∙ạ∙mwād ābi‘ta, ugīnạgạnān ābi‘ta.

**Mii dash gii-amwaad aabita, ogii-naganaan aabita.**

*Accordingly, when he had eaten one half of it, he left behind (the other) half.*

Mii dash gii-amwaad (and when he had eaten) aabita (half), ogii-naganaan (he left behind) aabita (half).

(3) Minawā kībạbāmibạ‘tō, kāwīn mīnawā ugīnisāsīn wâbōzōn.

**Miinawaa gii-babaamibatoo, gaawiin miinawaa ogii-nisaasiin waaboozoon.**

*Again he went running hither and thither, but he did not kill another hare.*

Miinawaa gii-babaamibatoo (again he ran about), gaawiin miinawaa (but not again) ogii-nisaasiin (did he kill) waaboozoon (a hare).

(4) Mī∙i∙dạc mi‘kwändạnk udạsạndcigun wâbōzōn ābi‘ta kī∙ạ∙sā‘pan.

**Mii idash mikwendang odasanjigon waaboozoon aabita gii-asaapan.**

*Whereupon he remembered his cache where he had placed one-half of a hare.*

Mii idash mikwendang (and then he remembered) odasanjigon (his cache) waaboozoon aabita (the half of the hare) gii-asaapan (he had placed there; *preterite*).

(5) Ninguding kayä wīn āndēg pạpāmisät owâbạmān wâbōzōn ạbinit.

**Ningoding gaye wiin aandeg bapaamised owaabamaan waaboozoon abinid.**

*Once when the Crow too was flying about, he saw where there was a hare.*

Ningoding (once) gaye wiin aandeg (the crow for his part) bapaamised (flying about) owaabamaan (he saw) waaboozoon abinid (a hare lying there).

(6) Kistciminwändạm tcīwīsinit.

**Gichi-minwendam ji-wiisinid.**

*Greatly pleased was he, now that he was going to have some food to eat.*

Gichi-minwendam (greatly pleased was he) ji-wiisinid (that he was going to eat).

(7) Cayīgwạdạc wāmādạngdcigät owâbạmān wâgucạn pīdciba‘tōnit.

**Zhayiigwa dash waa-maadanjiged owaabamaan waagoshan biijibatoonid.**

*And just as he was about to eat, he saw a Fox coming along on a run.*

Zhayiigwa dash (and just then) waa-maadanjiged (as he was about to begin eating) owaabamaan (he saw) waagoshan (a fox) biijibatoonid (running this way).

(8) Mēdạc änwät: “Ha’wi, ha’wi, ha’wi\!”

**Mii dash enwed: “Ha’wi, ha’wi, ha’wi\!”**

*Whereupon he exclaimed: “Háwi, háwi, háwi\!”*

Mii dash enwed (and this is how he cried out): “Ha’wi, ha’wi, ha’wi\!” (*the crow’s cry, kept as spoken*).

(9) Mīdạc kī∙u∙dā‘pināt kīmādcīnāt, mi‘tigunk kīpūni; ā‘pidci pa‘kadäbạn ‘aᵍa’ᵘ āndēg.

**Mii dash gii-odaapinaad gii-maajiinaad, mitigong gii-booni; aapiji bakadeban a’aw aandeg.**

*And so, taking up (the meat), he carried it away; upon a tree he alighted; very hungry at the time was the Crow.*

Mii dash gii-odaapinaad (and so taking it up) gii-maajiinaad (he carried it off), mitigong gii-booni (on a tree he alighted); aapiji bakadeban (very hungry was; *preterite*) a’aw aandeg (that crow).

(10) Wâgucidạc ugīwâbạmān kī‘pūnīnit āndēgwạn.

**Waagosh idash ogii-waabamaan gii-booniinid aandegwan.**

*Now, the Fox saw the Crow alight.*

Waagosh idash (now the fox) ogii-waabamaan (saw him) gii-booniinid (alighting) aandegwan (the crow).

(11) Kī∙i∙jā imāⁿ tîbickō ạgōzinit, mēdac änāt: “Āndēk, kä‘gä‘t unicicinōn pāzi‘kimạn. Ā‘pidci kimino‘kwạnayä.”

**Gii-izhaa imaa dibishkoo agoozinid, mii dash enaad: “Aandeg, geget onizhishinoon baazikaman. Aapiji gimino-gwanaye.”**

*He went over to the place underneath where (the Crow) was perched, and this he said to him: “Crow, truly beautiful is the garment you have on. Very handsomely are you clad.”*

Gii-izhaa imaa (he went over there) dibishkoo agoozinid (directly under where he perched), mii dash enaad (and this he said to him): “Aandeg (Crow), geget onizhishinoon (truly beautiful are) baazikaman (the things you wear). Aapiji gimino-gwanaye (very handsomely are you clad).”

(12) Mīdạc mīnawā pā‘pit; “‘Āᵃ, ‘āᵃ, ‘āᵃ, ‘āᵃ\!” uzāmidạc ki‘tāwạni pā‘pit; mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙i∙jipicigunāt wâbōzōn kīpạngicinūn mi‘ta‘kạmig.

**Mii dash miinawaa baapid; “’Aa, ’aa, ’aa, ’aa\!” onzaam idash gii-daawani baapid; mii iw gaa-izhi-bizhigonaad waaboozoon gii-bangishinoon mitakamig.**

*And so when (the Crow) laughed, “’Āᵃ, ’āᵃ, ’āᵃ, ’āᵃ\!” too wide did he open his mouth as he laughed; whereupon, when he dropped the hare, it fell to the ground.*

Mii dash miinawaa baapid (and then as he laughed); “’Aa, ’aa, ’aa, ’aa\!” (*the crow’s laugh, kept as spoken*) onzaam idash gii-daawani (too wide he opened his mouth) baapid (as he laughed); mii iw gaa-izhi-bizhigonaad (whereupon he let slip) waaboozoon (the hare) gii-bangishinoon (and it fell) mitakamig (to the bare ground).

(13) Wāgucidạc ugīnawạdinān uwâbōzumạn; ugīpā‘pi∙ā∙n āndēgwạn, “Hwạᵘ, hwạᵘ, hwạᵘ, hwạᵘ\!” Mīdạc kī∙ạ∙mwāt.

**Waagosh idash ogii-nawadinaan owaaboozoman; ogii-baapi’aan aandegwan, “Hwaw, hwaw, hwaw, hwaw\!” Mii dash gii-amwaad.**

*So the Fox seized his hare; he laughed at the Crow, “Hwaᵘ, hwaᵘ, hwaᵘ, hwaᵘ\!” Whereupon he ate (the hare).*

Waagosh idash (so the fox) ogii-nawadinaan (seized) owaaboozoman (his hare); ogii-baapi’aan (he laughed at) aandegwan (the crow), “Hwaw, hwaw, hwaw, hwaw\!” (*the fox’s laugh, kept as spoken*) Mii dash gii-amwaad (and then he ate it).

(14) Āndēgidạc kīnickādizi, ānawi pä‘kic pā‘pi, “‘Āᵃ, ‘āᵃ, ‘āᵃ, ‘āᵃ\!” Mī∙i∙dạc nindawa kī∙ạ∙nipạsigwa∙u∙‘t.

**Aandeg idash gii-nishkaadizi, aanawi bekish baapi, “’Aa, ’aa, ’aa, ’aa\!” Mii idash nindawaa gii-ani-bazigwa’od.**

*Now, the Crow was angry, even though at the same time he laughed, “’Āᵃ, ’āᵃ, ’āᵃ, ’āᵃ\!” And so with that he rose and flew away.*

Aandeg idash (now the crow) gii-nishkaadizi (was angry), aanawi bekish baapi (even though at the same time he laughed), “’Aa, ’aa, ’aa, ’aa\!” Mii idash nindawaa (and so with that) gii-ani-bazigwa’od (he rose up and flew away).

(15) Mīsai ä‘kōzit.

**Mii sa i’ ekoozid.**

*That is as far as (the story) goes.*

Mii sa i’ ekoozid (that is as far as it goes; *the traditional closing*).

*¹ It is plain where this tale comes from, but it is simpler (more naïve) than the usual European versions; in fact, if the narrator had in mind any of the morals usually attached to the European versions, he concealed them absolutely. (Editor’s 1919 note, attached to the title — the narrator declined the European moral.)*
