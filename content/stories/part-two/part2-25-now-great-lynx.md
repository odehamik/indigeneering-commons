MĪ∙I∙WE PECIPEJĪ.

**MII IWE BESHIBEZHII.**

NOW GREAT-LYNX.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Mäwija anicinābäk mōⁿjạg ogīwâbạndānāwa mämindagä iᵘ tînunk kīni‘tāpimidciwạnk nibi. Ugīku‘tānāwa anicinābäk. Mī∙i∙dạc iᵘ kā∙u∙ndcitōdạmōwād nāningudinōng kā∙u∙ndci pạgidinạmawāt nîbī‘kāng asämān gayä.

**Mewizha anishinaabeg moonzhag ogii-waabandaanaawaa memindage iw dinong gii-nitaa-bimijiwang nibi. Ogii-gotaanaawaa anishinaabeg. Mii idash iw gaa-onji-doodamowaad naaningodinong gaa-onji-bagidinamawaad nibiikaang asemaan gaye.**

*Long ago people often used to see something in places, especially where the current was swift. The people feared it; and that was the reason of their practice of sometimes throwing offerings to it into the water, even tobacco.*

Mewizha anishinaabeg (long ago the people), moonzhag (often), ogii-waabandaanaawaa (they would see it), memindage (especially), iw dinong (in that kind of place), gii-nitaa-bimijiwang (where the current ran strong), nibi (the water). Ogii-gotaanaawaa anishinaabeg (the people feared it). Mii idash iw gaa-onji-doodamowaad (that is why they did as they did), naaningodinong (sometimes), gaa-onji-bagidinamawaad (why they put offerings down for it), nibiikaang (into the water), asemaan gaye (tobacco too; *asemaa animate*).

(2) Ningudingidạc imāⁿ Pāgwācing¹ kī∙i∙dạming mī∙i∙māⁿ ninguding pimickāwāgubạnän i‘kwäwạg. Mī∙i∙dạc äjiwäbạdining kistcipîmidciwạninik ni’bi, kägā kunạbickāwạg; ā‘pidci sägiziwạg.

**Ningoding idash imaa Baagwaashing gii-idaming mii imaa ningoding bimishkaawaagobanen ikwewag. Mii idash ezhiwebadining gichi-bimijiwaninig nibi, gegaa gonabishkaawag; aapiji zegiziwag.**

*Now, once yonder, at what is called Shallow-Water,1 was where some women were once passing by in a canoe. Accordingly there happened to rise a mighty current of water, nearly were they capsized; exceedingly frightened were they.*

Ningoding idash (and once), imaa Baagwaashing (there at Shallow-Water), gii-idaming (as the place is called), mii imaa ningoding (it was there that once), bimishkaawaagobanen (women who were once paddling by; *preterite participle*), ikwewag (the women). Mii idash ezhiwebadining (then it so happened), gichi-bimijiwaninig nibi (a great current of water rose), gegaa gonabishkaawag (nearly did they capsize); aapiji zegiziwag (greatly were they frightened).

*ni’bi — internal-apostrophe print (again at the den passage, p. 260); ci’gwa-family. kistcipîmidciwạninik — kistci- print, attested outside series III (error-vs-habit question, banked). Jones’s footnote ¹ at Pāgwācing: “The name for Ross Port.” — the marker stands in both texts; the note prints at the foot of English p. 259.*

(3) Mägwā pimickāwāt änigu‘k owâbandānāwa pîjīpîjī∙o∙su pa‘kidcisänig; ga‘kina nīgān tcīmāning kī∙a∙‘pạgizōwạg sägiziwāt.

**Megwaa bimishkaawaad enigok owaabandaanaawaa bizhiibizhii-ozow bakijisenig; gakina niigaan jiimaaning gii-apagizowag zegiziwaad.**

*While they were paddling with all their might, they saw the tail of a Great-Lynx come up out of the water; all flung themselves up into the forward end of the canoe in their fright.*

Megwaa bimishkaawaad enigok (while they paddled with all their might), owaabandaanaawaa (they saw it), bizhiibizhii-ozow (the tail of Great-Lynx), bakijisenig (coming up out of the water); gakina (all of them), niigaan jiimaaning (into the bow of the canoe), gii-apagizowag (flung themselves), zegiziwaad (in their fright).

*The being’s name prints with both initials across this story — Pecipejī (title), pîjīpîjī-/pîcipîjī- beside mîcipîjī-/mîcīpîjī- — each print is carried as set; flagged for review.*

(4) Päᵉjikidạc i‘kwä imā äyāt owâbạndān tcīmān wī∙ạ∙nikuzābīckānit; mē∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙ji∙i∙jāt imāⁿ udā‘kāning udạbwi ogī∙u∙mbibān wīpa‘kitä∙a∙nk ‘iᵍi’ᵘ mîcīpîjī∙o∙su.

**Bezhig idash ikwe imaa eyaad owaabandaan jiimaan wii-ani-gozaabiishkaanid; mii idash gaa-izhi-izhaad imaa odaakaaning odabwi ogii-ombibaan wii-bakite’ang i’iw mishiibizhii-ozow.**

*Now, one of the women that was there saw that the canoe was going to sink; accordingly, when she had gone to the stern, she raised the paddle in order to strike the tail of Great-Lynx.*

Bezhig idash ikwe (and one woman), imaa eyaad (who was there), owaabandaan jiimaan (saw the canoe), wii-ani-gozaabiishkaanid (that it was going to sink); mii idash gaa-izhi-izhaad (so then she went), imaa odaakaaning (there to the stern), odabwi ogii-ombibaan (she raised her paddle), wii-bakite’ang (in order to strike) i’iw mishiibizhii-ozow (that tail of Great-Lynx).

*ogī∙u∙mbibān — as printed; a b-for-n print suspected (ogī∙u∙mbinān “she raised it” expected); cf. the m/n type-confusion family (\#45/\#73/\#74).*

(5) Mēdạc ä‘kidot: “Mägwa kī∙u∙ckinīgiyān mōⁿjạg ningīma‘kadä‘kä. Mīdạc iwa‘pi animi‘kīg kīmījiwāt upạgamāganiwā.”

**Mii dash ekidod: “Megwaa gii-oshkiniigiyaan moonzhag ningii-makadeke. Mii dash iw apii animikiig gii-miizhiwaad obagamaaganiwaa.”**

*And this she said: “While I was young, often did I fast. It was then that the Thunderers gave me their war-club.”*

Mii dash ekidod (and this she said): “Megwaa gii-oshkiniigiyaan (while I was young), moonzhag ningii-makadeke (often did I fast). Mii dash iw apii (it was then that), animikiig (the Thunderers), gii-miizhiwaad (gave me), obagamaaganiwaa (their war-club).”

(6) Mī∙i∙dạc pa‘kitä∙ạ∙nk pîcipîjī∙u∙su, mīdạc kā∙i∙jipō‘kwuganāndạnk ‘iᵍi’ᵘ pîjipijī∙u∙su. Mīdạc kīmō‘kisäg ‘iᵍi’ᵘ tcīmān, mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙i∙jimādcīkwạjiwäwāt; mī∙i∙’ᵘ kīpîmādisiwāt.

**Mii idash bakite’ang bishibizhii-ozow, mii dash gaa-izhi-bookoganaandang i’iw bizhibizhii-ozow. Mii dash gii-mookiseg i’iw jiimaan, mii iw gaa-izhi-maajiikwazhiwewaad; mii iw gii-bimaadiziwaad.**

*Thereupon, when she struck the tail of Great-Lynx, she then broke the tail of Great-Lynx in two. Thereupon up to the surface rose the canoe, after which they then started on their way paddling; and so they were saved.*

Mii idash bakite’ang (then as she struck), bishibizhii-ozow (the tail of Great-Lynx), mii dash gaa-izhi-bookoganaandang (then she broke it in two with the blow), i’iw bizhibizhii-ozow (that tail of Great-Lynx). Mii dash gii-mookiseg i’iw jiimaan (then up to the surface rose the canoe), mii iw gaa-izhi-maajiikwazhiwewaad (whereupon they paddled off on their way); mii iw gii-bimaadiziwaad (and so they lived).

(7) Pāᵉjikidạc kayä i‘kwägubạn ogī∙u∙dā‘pinigōn īni’ᵘ pîcipîjīn. Mī∙i∙dạc ‘aᵍa’ᵘ täbādcimōgubạnän iwidi ändāwāgubạnän ‘aᵍa’ᵘ mîcipîjī mōⁿjạg ogīmī‘kindci∙ā∙n anicinābän.

**Bezhig idash gaye ikwegoban ogii-odaapinigoon iniw bishibizhiin. Mii idash a’aw debaajimogobanen iwidi endaawaagobanen a’aw mishibizhii moonzhag ogii-miikinji’aan anishinaaben.**

*Now, one of the women was seized by Great-Lynx. Therefore she it was who had told at home that Great-Lynx was continually harassing the people.*

Bezhig idash gaye ikwegoban (and one woman — now gone; *preterite*), ogii-odaapinigoon (had been seized by him), iniw bishibizhiin (by that Great-Lynx). Mii idash a’aw (so it was she), debaajimogobanen (who had once told of it; *preterite participle*), iwidi endaawaagobanen (yonder where they once dwelt), a’aw mishibizhii (that Great-Lynx), moonzhag ogii-miikinji’aan (was constantly harassing), anishinaaben (the people).

(8) ‘Aᵍa’widạc a‘kiwäⁿzimîcipîjī ānawi mōjạⁿg ogīkạnōnān ogwisạn: “Kägu ‘iᵍi’ᵘ tōtawā‘kän anicinābäk tcîmī‘kindci∙ạ∙twā.” Kāwīn ugīpîzindawāsīn ōsạn.

**A’aw idash akiwenzii-mishibizhii aanawi moonzhag ogii-ganoonaan ogwisan: “Gego i’iw doodawaaken anishinaabeg ji-miikinji’adwaa.” Gaawiin ogii-bizindawaasiin oosan.**

*And though the master of the Great-Lynxes would always speak to his son, saying, “Do not plague the people,” yet he would never listen to his father.*

A’aw idash akiwenzii-mishibizhii (and that old-man Great-Lynx; *the master*), aanawi moonzhag (though always), ogii-ganoonaan ogwisan (he spoke to his son): “Gego i’iw doodawaaken (do not so do to them) anishinaabeg (the people) ji-miikinji’adwaa (harassing them).” Gaawiin ogii-bizindawaasiin (he did not listen to) oosan (his father).

(9) Ninguding i∙i∙māⁿ Pā∙u∙‘ting māmạwi tāgwâbạn anicinābäk. Ninguding päᵉjik wīgiwâming āswā‘kwicinugubạn ạbinōdcī ta‘kupisut ti‘kināgạning; mīdạc ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kā∙u∙ndciwanicink awabinodcī.

**Ningoding i’imaa Baawiting maamawi daawagoban anishinaabeg. Ningoding bezhig wiigiwaaming aaswaakwishinogoban abinoojii dakobizod dikinaaganing; mii dash a’aw gaa-onji-wanishing aw abinoojii.**

*Once, yonder at the Sault, together in a body were the people living. Once against a certain wigwam was leaned a child bound to a cradle-board; and then the child was missed from that place.*

Ningoding i’imaa Baawiting (once there at the Sault), maamawi daawagoban (together they once dwelt; *preterite*), anishinaabeg (the people). Ningoding bezhig wiigiwaaming (once against a certain wigwam), aaswaakwishinogoban (there once leaned; *preterite*), abinoojii dakobizod (a child bound), dikinaaganing (to a cradle-board); mii dash a’aw gaa-onji-wanishing (and from there went missing), aw abinoojii (that child).

(10) Ugīwâbandānāwa ani∙ä∙nadawângising ti‘kināgan mi‘tāwạngānk. Mīwidạc nōndawâwāt mạdwämạwinit īni’ᵘ abinōdcīyạn anāmaya∙ī∙ pi‘kwadināng.

**Ogii-waabandaanaawaa ani-enadaawangising dikinaagan mitaawangaang. Mii idash noondawaawaad madwe-mawinid iniw abinoojiiyan anaamaya’ii bikwadinaang.**

*They saw the sign of the cradle-board where it had been dragged along in the sand. Thereupon they heard the voice of the child crying beneath a rugged hill.*

Ogii-waabandaanaawaa (they saw it), ani-enadaawangising (the trail it left dragging through the sand), dikinaagan (the cradle-board), mitaawangaang (on the sand). Mii idash noondawaawaad (thereupon they heard), madwe-mawinid (crying somewhere within), iniw abinoojiiyan (that child), anaamaya’ii bikwadinaang (beneath the rugged hill).

(11) Ānawi kīpagidāsowạg ạnicinābäk tcîpạgidînāt īni’ᵘ ạbinōdcīyạn ‘aᵍa’ᵘ mîcīpîjī, kabäya∙ī∙ ānawi ugīkagāⁿzumāwān, kāwīndec ugīpạgidināsīn.

**Aanawi gii-bagidaasowag anishinaabeg ji-bagidinaad iniw abinoojiiyan a’aw mishiibizhii, gabeya’ii aanawi ogii-gagaanzomaawaan, gaawiin dash ogii-bagidinaasiin.**

*Even though the people made offerings in the hope that Great-Lynx might set the child free, even though for a long while they besought him with prayers, yet he would not let it go.*

Aanawi gii-bagidaasowag (though they made offerings), anishinaabeg (the people), ji-bagidinaad (that he might release), iniw abinoojiiyan (the child), a’aw mishiibizhii (that Great-Lynx), gabeya’ii (for a long while), aanawi ogii-gagaanzomaawaan (though they kept urging him with speech), gaawiin dash ogii-bagidinaasiin (yet he would not release him).

(12) Käga‘pī∙i∙dạc anicinābäg kī∙i∙‘kidōwạg nindawātc tcinîsāwāt īni’ᵘ mîcīpîjīn. Mī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙jimādci‘tāwāt kīmūni‘käwāt kwaya‘k imāⁿ abinōdcī ändạni‘tāgusit.

**Gegapii idash anishinaabeg gii-ikidowag nindawaaj ji-nisaawaad iniw mishiibizhiin. Mii idash gaa-izhi-maajiitaawaad gii-mooniikewaad gwayak imaa abinoojii endanitaagozid.**

*So at length the people said that therefore they might as well slay Great-Lynx. Accordingly they began digging straight for the place from whence the sound of the child could be heard.*

Gegapii idash (then at last), anishinaabeg gii-ikidowag (the people said), nindawaaj (they might as well), ji-nisaawaad (kill him), iniw mishiibizhiin (that Great-Lynx). Mii idash gaa-izhi-maajiitaawaad (so then they set to work), gii-mooniikewaad (digging), gwayak imaa (straight to the place), abinoojii endanitaagozid (where the child could be heard).

(13) Wī‘kādec ugīpạgwanä∙a∙nāwa ‘iᵍi’ᵘ mîcīpîjīwāc. Owâbạndānāwa ni’bi pāpīdāgạmickānig.

**Wiikaa dash ogii-bagwane’aanaawaa i’iw mishiibizhiiwaazh. Owaabandaanaawaa nibi baabiidaagamishkaanig.**

*And after a while they had a hole dug to the den of Great-Lynx. They saw water coming in and out (like the tide).*

Wiikaa dash (and at long last; *wiikaa here in its at-long-last sense*), ogii-bagwane’aanaawaa (they broke a hole through to it), i’iw mishiibizhiiwaazh (the den of Great-Lynx). Owaabandaanaawaa nibi (they saw the water), baabiidaagamishkaanig (coming in and going out; *like the tide*).

(14) Mīdạc imāⁿ käᵉgä‘t wäwäni ānukạnōnāwāt īni’ᵘ mîcīpîjīn, kāwīndạc opạgidînāsīn abinōdcīyạn. Käyābi unōndawāwān mạdwämawinit. Mīdạc ä‘kidōwāt: “Nindawā mōnawātā tcînisạnk.”

**Mii dash imaa geget weweni aano-ganoonaawaad iniw mishiibizhiin, gaawiin dash obagidinaasiin abinoojiiyan. Geyaabi onoondawaawaan madwe-mawinid. Mii dash ekidowaad: “Nindawaa moonawaadaa ji-nisang.”**

*It was true that even then they spoke kindly to Great-Lynx, yet he would not let the child go. Still yet they could hear the voice (of the child) crying. Accordingly they said: “Therefore let us dig to where he is, that we may kill him.”*

Mii dash imaa geget (and then there truly), weweni (kindly), aano-ganoonaawaad (in vain did they speak to him; *aano- in-vain*), iniw mishiibizhiin (that Great-Lynx), gaawiin dash obagidinaasiin (but he would not release), abinoojiiyan (the child). Geyaabi onoondawaawaan (still they could hear him), madwe-mawinid (crying within). Mii dash ekidowaad (so they said): “Nindawaa (therefore), moonawaadaa (let us dig for him), ji-nisang (that we may kill him).”

(15) Käᵉgä‘t ugīnōswāni‘kānāwān. Ninguding pīdāpōtä ti‘kināgạn, abinōdcī gayä ta‘kupisut. A‘pī∙i∙dạc näwadinạmuwāt ti‘kināgạn owâbamāwān abinōdcīyạn pō‘kindipäcink; kīnîsāgwän aᵘ mîcīpîjī.

**Geget ogii-nooswaanikaanaawaan. Ningoding biidaaboode dikinaagan, abinoojii gaye dakobizod. Apii idash newadinamowaad dikinaagan owaabamaawaan abinoojiiyan bookondibeshing; gii-nisaagwen aw mishiibizhii.**

*Truly they dug after him, following him up. By and by out came the cradle-board floating on the water, together with the child that was bound to it. And when they caught hold of the cradle-board, they observed that the child had a hole crushed into its head; Great-Lynx must have slain it.*

Geget ogii-nooswaanikaanaawaan (truly they dug along after him). Ningoding biidaaboode (by and by came floating out on the water), dikinaagan (the cradle-board), abinoojii gaye dakobizod (and the child bound to it). Apii idash newadinamowaad (and when they took hold of), dikinaagan (the cradle-board), owaabamaawaan abinoojiiyan (they saw the child), bookondibeshing (its head broken in); gii-nisaagwen (he must have slain it; *dubitative*), aw mishiibizhii (that Great-Lynx).

(16) Mīdạc kā∙i∙jinōswāni‘kānāwāt; päᵉjikidạc anicinābä mäckawändāguzit kī∙i∙‘kido wīn wīnîsāt mîcipîjīn. A‘pī ädimāni‘kānāwāt, pigwä‘ki‘tā ‘aᵍa’ᵘ mîcīpîjī. Mīdạc kīpa‘ki‘täᵒwāt ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kā∙i∙‘kidot wīnisāt. Käᵉgä‘t ugīnisān.

**Mii dash gaa-izhi-nooswaanikaanaawaad; bezhig idash anishinaabe meshkawendaagozid gii-ikido wiin wii-nisaad mishibizhiin. Apii edimaanikaanaawaad, bi-gwekitaa a’aw mishiibizhii. Mii dash gii-bakite’waad a’aw gaa-ikidod wii-nisaad. Geget ogii-nisaan.**

*Thereupon they followed him up, digging after him; and one man that was famed for his strength said that he would kill Great-Lynx. When drawing upon him, as they dug after him, round towards them turned Great-Lynx. Thereupon him struck he who said that he would kill (Great-Lynx). Sure enough, he slew him.*

Mii dash gaa-izhi-nooswaanikaanaawaad (then they went on digging after him); bezhig idash anishinaabe (and one man), meshkawendaagozid (held to be strong), gii-ikido (said), wiin wii-nisaad (that he himself would kill), mishibizhiin (Great-Lynx). Apii edimaanikaanaawaad (when their digging drew upon him), bi-gwekitaa (round this way turned), a’aw mishiibizhii (Great-Lynx). Mii dash gii-bakite’waad (then him struck), a’aw gaa-ikidod (the one who had said), wii-nisaad (he would kill him). Geget ogii-nisaan (sure enough, he slew him).

*kīpa‘ki‘täᵒwāt — superscript ᵒ as printed (first ᵒ print recorded in this corpus); read as the devoiced o of the bakite’w- stem.*

(17) A‘pī∙i∙dạc wā‘kubināwāt ugīwâbamāwān kīckānowänit. Mī∙a∙’ᵘ Pāgwācing kāpa‘kidä wāwindibạnän; i‘kwäwạn ạbwi kāpa‘tä∙u∙gut.

**Apii idash waakobinaawaad ogii-waabamaawaan giishkaanowenid. Mii aw Baagwaashing gaa-bakide’waawindibanen; ikwewan abwi gaa-bakite’ogod.**

*And when they pulled him out, they saw that his tail was cut off. That was the one that had been struck at Shallow-Water; by a woman with an oar had he been struck.*

Apii idash waakobinaawaad (and when they pulled him out), ogii-waabamaawaan (they saw him), giishkaanowenid (with his tail cut off). Mii aw (that was the one), Baagwaashing (at Shallow-Water), gaa-bakide’waawindibanen (who had once been struck; *preterite*); ikwewan (by a woman), abwi (with a paddle), gaa-bakite’ogod (had he been struck).

*kāpa‘tä∙u∙gut — as printed; a dropped ‘ki suspected (kāpa‘ki‘tä∙u∙gut expected; cf. \#53’s dropped g, \#74’s mi‘inā‘k). kāpa‘kidä wāwindibạnän — printed with a space mid-word; converted as one word; the d-print (pa‘kidä beside pa‘ki‘tä, l. 15) carried.*

(18) Mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙i∙jiwäba‘k. Käyābi nānūmaya kī∙i∙nāgwạt ī∙i∙’māⁿ kīmūni‘käwâgubạnän anicinābäk; Ketcimō‘kumāna‘kīng¹ ina‘ka‘käyā imāⁿ Pāwi‘ting. Mīsai.

**Mii iw gaa-izhiwebak. Geyaabi naanoomaya gii-inaagwad i’imaa gii-mooniikewaagobanen anishinaabeg; Gechi-mookomaanakiing inakakeyaa imaa Baawiting. Mii sa.**

*That was what happened. Only not long ago was seen the place where the people had once dug the hole; (it is) over toward the Big-Knife country,1 over by the Sault. That is all.*

Mii iw gaa-izhiwebak (that is what happened). Geyaabi naanoomaya (still just recently), gii-inaagwad (it could be seen), i’imaa gii-mooniikewaagobanen (there where they had once dug; *preterite participle*), anishinaabeg (the people); Gechi-mookomaanakiing (toward the Big-Knife country; *the United States*), inakakeyaa (in that direction), imaa Baawiting (there by the Sault). Mii sa (that is all).

*Jones’s footnote ¹ at Ketcimō‘kumāna‘kīng: “The United States.” — prints at the foot of English p. 261.*
