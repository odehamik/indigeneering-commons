MI‘KINĀ‘K WÄNTUPẠNI.

**MIKINAAK WENDOBANI.**

SNAPPING-TURTLE ON THE WARPATH.

*(Told by Wāsāgunäckạnk — Wasagunackang. Series III header on p. 338: “SERIES III. Nos. 44–61. (Told by Wāsāgunäckạnk.)”)*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ni’ngudingsa mi‘kinā‘k wīnạntupạni, mīdac äjinạndwäwämāt wâwītcīwāt. “Awänän käwītcīwạgä tcīnạntāpạnīyān?” Mīdạc īni’ᵘ mōzōn nä‘kwä‘tâgut: “Nīne’, nīne’\!” Mōzōn kīpītāsamusäwạn opapīⁿsi‘kawāgạn kāgigizikạnk; nabạnä∙a∙ya∙i∙ kī∙u∙jāwackwāni kimiskwānidạc kayä; miskukitcipisonạn ogīkitcipisonạn; mīdạc īni’ᵘ täwä∙i∙gạnạn ugī∙ā∙cōnigwa∙a∙. Kāpīci∙u∙jikābāwi‘tāgut, mīdạc äjikanōnāt: “Agataskumā kagwätcīn\!”

**Ningoding isa mikinaak wii-nandobani, mii dash ezhi-nandwewemaad waa-wiijiiwaad. “Awenen ge-wiijiiwaged ji-nandobaniyaan?” Mii dash iniw moozoon nakwetaagod: “Niine’, niine’\!” Moozoon gii-biidaasamosewan obabiinzikawaagan gaa-gigishkang; nabane-aya’ii gii-ozhaawashkwaani gii-miskwaani dash gaye; misko-gijipizonan ogii-gijipizonan; mii dash iniw dewe’iganan ogii-aazhoonigwa’aa. Gaa-bi-jigaabawi’taagod, mii dash ezhi-ganoonaad: “Agadashkomaa gagwejiin\!”**

*Once on a time Snapping-Turtle desired to go to war, whereupon he went about calling for them in whose company he would go. “With whom shall I go when I go to war?”¹ And then by Moose he was answered: “Me, me\!” (He saw) the Moose coming along on a walk. A coat he wore; on one side it was green and red; with a red belt was he girdled; and then a hand-drum he had hanging under (one) arm, suspended from the (other) shoulder. And when (Moose) came up beside him and stood, (Snapping-Turtle) then spoke to him, saying: “Pray, (let me see) you make trial (of what you could do)\!”²*

Ningoding isa (once on a time) mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) wii-nandobani (desired to go to war), mii dash ezhi-nandwewemaad (whereupon he went calling for) waa-wiijiiwaad (those who would go with him). “Awenen ge-wiijiiwaged (with whom shall I go) ji-nandobaniyaan (when I go to war)?” Mii dash iniw moozoon (and then by the Moose) nakwetaagod (he was answered): “Niine’, niine’\! (me, me\!)” Moozoon gii-biidaasamosewan (the Moose came walking toward him) obabiinzikawaagan gaa-gigishkang (wearing his coat); nabane-aya’ii (on the one side) gii-ozhaawashkwaani (it was green-blue) gii-miskwaani dash gaye (and it was red too); misko-gijipizonan (a red belt) ogii-gijipizonan (he wore girdled); mii dash iniw dewe’iganan (and a hand-drum) ogii-aazhoonigwa’aa (he carried slung from the shoulder). Gaa-bi-jigaabawi’taagod (when he came and stood beside him), mii dash ezhi-ganoonaad (he then spoke to him): “Agadashkomaa gagwejiin\! (pray, make trial\!)”

*Jones’s footnotes: ¹ The request is chanted. ² In battle. — W.J. Print notes: Ni’ngudingsa with apostrophe inside the word; mīdac beside mīdạc on the same page (both carried); ugī∙ā∙cōnigwa∙a∙ as printed (expected final -ān; flagged); Agataskumā — the chanted request-particle, converted by character rules and flagged (cf. Agạtaga §5).*

(2) Mīdạc ạᵘ mōⁿs äjipapāmipa‘tōd, upapāpagi‘tä∙o∙wān mi‘tigōn. Mīdạc äjikạnōnāt aᵘ mi‘kinā‘k: “Kāwäsā\! Kitāpō‘kutcīngwanänāgū; mī kāwīn kīwīwītcīwiⁿsinōn.”

**Mii dash aw moonz ezhi-babaamibatood, obabaa-bagite’owaan mitigoon. Mii dash ezhi-ganoonaad aw mikinaak: “Gaawesaa\! Gidaa-bookojiingwanenaagoo; mii gaawiin giwii-wiijiiwisinoon.”**

*Whereupon Moose ran hither and thither, he struck first one tree, then another, about the place. And then to him spoke Snapping-Turtle, saying: “Impossible\! You might have the (lower part of the) femur (of your hind leg) broken by a weapon; so therefore I do not care to go with you.”*

Mii dash aw moonz (whereupon the Moose) ezhi-babaamibatood (ran hither and thither), obabaa-bagite’owaan mitigoon (he went about striking the trees). Mii dash ezhi-ganoonaad aw mikinaak (and then Snapping-Turtle spoke to him): “Gaawesaa\! (impossible\!) Gidaa-bookojiingwanenaagoo (you might get your femur broken — \*flagged\*); mii gaawiin giwii-wiijiiwisinoon (so I will not go with you).”

*Kitāpō‘kutcīngwanänāgū — booko- ‘broken’ + -jiingwan- ‘femur/knee’ suspected; the final -änāgū (passive ‘by a weapon’, per Jones) unparsed — converted by character rules, flagged. §7 prints the same verb with final -nāgō (both carried).*

(3) Mī intawā kī∙ạ∙nimādcāt aᵘ mōⁿs.

**Mii indawaa gii-ani-maajaad aw moonz.**

*Accordingly then departed Moose upon his way.*

Mii indawaa (accordingly then) gii-ani-maajaad (off on his way went) aw moonz (the Moose).

(4) Mīsaⁱ mīnawā äjipībāgit: “Awänän käwītcīwạgä tcinạntāpạniyān?”

**Mii sa miinawaa ezhi-biibaagid: “Awenen ge-wiijiiwaged ji-nandobaniyaan?”**

*And so again he called with a loud voice: “With whom shall I go when I go to war?”*

Mii sa miinawaa (and so again) ezhi-biibaagid (he called with a loud voice): “Awenen ge-wiijiiwaged (with whom shall I go) ji-nandobaniyaan (when I go to war)?”

*Mīsaⁱ — superscript ⁱ printed on Mīsa, carried in the Jones line and flagged (cf. the liaison-print family). The repeated war-call prints tcinạntāpạniyān here, tcīnạntāpạnīyān at §1 — ī/i spread within the chant, both carried.*

(5) “Nīna’, nīna’\!” Pimạdwä∙i∙‘kitowạn aᵘ ma‘kwạn kīpītāsạmusäwạn. Cigwa imāⁿ pitạgwicinōn, “Agạtaga, kạgwätcīn\!”

**“Niina’, niina’\!” Bimi-madwe-ikidowan aw makwan gii-biidaasamosewan. Zhigwa imaa bi-dagwishinoon, “Agadaga, gagwejiin\!”**

*“Me, me\!” came the voice of Bear when approaching hitherward on the walk. When at the place Bear was come, “Pray, (let me see) you make trial (of what you could do)\!”*

“Niina’, niina’\! (me, me\!)” Bimi-madwe-ikidowan (came the voice along, saying it,) aw makwan (of the Bear) gii-biidaasamosewan (as he came walking hitherward). Zhigwa imaa bi-dagwishinoon (when there he was come), “Agadaga, gagwejiin\! (pray, make trial\!)”

*Nīna’ here where §1 prints Nīne’ — the answering cry itself varies between the two singers (both carried). Agạtaga beside §1’s Agataskumā — the request-particle in a second shape; both flagged together.*

(6) Mīdạc papāpāsagupināt mi‘tigōn, kayätạc ugīta‘kwamān mi‘tigōn. “Mīsagunā kä∙a∙ī∙jiyāmbān mawinạciwäyạnk,” utigōn.

**Mii dash babaa-baazagobinaad mitigoon, gaye dash ogii-dakwamaan mitigoon. “Mii sa gonaa ge-a-izhiyaambaan mawinazhiweyang,” odigoon.**

*Thereupon round about he went clawing one tree after another, and he also bit the trees. “Now, this is just what I would do when we rush to the attack,” (he was told).*

Mii dash babaa-baazagobinaad mitigoon (thereupon he went about clawing the trees), gaye dash ogii-dakwamaan mitigoon (and he also bit the trees). “Mii sa gonaa (now this is just) ge-a-izhiyaambaan (what I would do — \*preterite conjunct\*) mawinazhiweyang (when we rush to the attack),” odigoon (he was told by him).

(7) “Kāwäsā\! Kītāpō‘kwutcīngwanänāgō.”

**“Gaawesaa\! Gidaa-bookojiingwanenaagoo.”**

*“Impossible\! You might have the lower part of your femur broken by a missile.”*

“Gaawesaa\! (impossible\!) Gidaa-bookojiingwanenaagoo (you might get your femur broken by a missile — \*flagged\*).”

*The same rejection-verb as §2, printed -kwutcīngwanänāgō there -kutcīngwanänāgū — one verb, two prints, both carried; the flag travels with the pair.*

(8) Mī∙i∙mā kī∙ạ∙nimādcānit. Mīnawā ägipīpāgit: “Awänän käwītciwagä?” “Nīne’, nīne’\!” Kägä‘t pā‘tạnīnwäwitạmōᵍ mädwäna‘kumigutcin. Mīdạc ānuwinābit aᵘ mi‘kinā‘k; mi‘ta‘kạmig owâbạmā iᵍi’ᵘ miskwâtäsiwaᵍ; kägä‘t osām mīnawā. Cigwa dạc imā kītạgwicinuᵍ, “Agataga kagwätcīg\!” ogī∙i∙nän.

**Mii imaa gii-ani-maajaanid. Miinawaa egi-biibaagid: “Awenen ge-wiijiiwaged?” “Niine’, niine’\!” Geget baataniinwewidamoog madwenakamigodjin. Mii dash aano-winaabid aw mikinaak; mitakamig owaabamaa’ i’iw miskwaadesiwa’; geget osaam miinawaa. Zhigwa dash imaa gii-dagwishinog, “Agadaga gagwejiig\!” ogii-inaa’.**

*Whereupon (from) thence went (Bear) on his way. Again he called with a loud voice: “With whom shall I go when I go to war?” “Me, me\!” In truth, many were the voices he heard in reply to what he had said. And then to see (who they were) Snapping-Turtle looked; on the ground he beheld the Painted-Turtles; in truth, (they were) ever so many. And when over there they were come, “Pray, (let me see) you make trial (of what you could do)\!” he said to them.*

Mii imaa gii-ani-maajaanid (whereupon thence went he on his way). Miinawaa egi-biibaagid (again he called aloud): “Awenen ge-wiijiiwaged? (with whom shall I go?)” “Niine’, niine’\! (me, me\!)” Geget baataniinwewidamoog (truly many were the voices sounding) madwenakamigodjin (the ground was heard rumbling with them — \*flagged\*). Mii dash aano-winaabid (then, trying to look,) aw mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle); mitakamig (on the bare ground) owaabamaa’ (he beheld them) i’iw miskwaadesiwa’ (those Painted-Turtles); geget osaam miinawaa (truly ever so many). Zhigwa dash imaa gii-dagwishinog (and when there they were come), “Agadaga gagwejiig\! (pray, make trial — \*2pl imperative\*)” ogii-inaa’ (he said to them).

*pā‘tạnīnwäwitạmōᵍ / kītạgwicinuᵍ / owâbạmā iᵍi’ᵘ miskwâtäsiwaᵍ — the banked final-ᵍ family: subject plurals converted -oog/-og; the VTA object pair (owâbạmā … miskwâtäsiwaᵍ) drafted obviative-plural -a’ per \#45 precedent. ägipīpāgit — print ägi- where äji- is usual (carried, flagged light). mädwäna‘kumigutcin — morphology unparsed (madwe- + -nakamig- ground + ?), flagged.*

(9) Īᵍi’ᵘ dạc miskwātäsiwaᵍ tcāngā‘kwäniwaᵍ, mīnawā äji∙o∙ci‘kwänit miskwātäsiwaᵍ. “Mīsạgunākä∙a∙ī∙cīyāngibạn.”

**Igiw dash miskwaadesiwag jaangaakweniwag, miinawaa ezhi-ozhikwenid miskwaadesiwag. “Mii sa gonaa ge-a-izhiiyaangiban.”**

*And the Painted-Turtles lifted up their heads (from out of their shells), back into their shells the Painted-Turtles drew their heads. “This is just the way we would do.”*

Igiw dash miskwaadesiwag (and those Painted-Turtles) jaangaakweniwag (thrust out their heads — \*flagged\*), miinawaa ezhi-ozhikwenid (and back in drew their heads) miskwaadesiwag (the Painted-Turtles). “Mii sa gonaa (this is just) ge-a-izhiiyaangiban (the way we would do — \*exclusive we, preterite conjunct\*).”

*tcāngā‘kwäniwaᵍ / äji∙o∙ci‘kwänit — the paired neck-motion verbs (-kweni ‘move the head/neck’ final); first members unparsed, both flagged. Here the plural subject ᵍ forms are converted -wag (syntax-decides).*

(10) “Mīsa’ kīnawā tciwītciwīnạgug.”

**“Mii sa’ giinawaa ji-wiijiiwinagog.”**

*“Therefore then with you will I go.”*

“Mii sa’ giinawaa (therefore it is you) ji-wiijiiwinagog (whom I will take along — \*1→2pl\*).”

(11) Mīdạc iᵘ kā∙i∙jimādcāwāt. Nīc ogimāwiwạg ändạciwāt. Cigwadạc ạninibāwag. Päcig aᵍa’ᵘ wägimāwit inābạndạm: “Cigwạmī nimawīnạciwämin, mintimōyäyạgidạc kīcikạckimutāng nīmpītcwäbinigunānig.” Mīdạc igu iᵘ ạninạgamut ạnikuckusit:—  
“Micīⁿkäⁿwā, nintā‘tawänigomin nintinā‘pawe.”

**Mii dash iw gaa-izhi-maajaawaad. Niizh ogimaawiwag endashiwaad. Zhigwa dash ani-nibaawag. Bezhig a’aw wegimaawid inaabandam: “Zhigwa mii nimawinazhiwemin, mindimooyeyag idash giizhik-ashkimodaang nimbiinjwebinigonaanig.” Mii dash igo iw ani-nagamod ani-goshkozid:—  
“Micīⁿkäⁿwā, nintā‘tawänigomin nintinā‘pawe.”**

*Thereupon thence they departed. There were two chiefs among their number. And now, as they journeyed along, they slept by the way. One (of them) that was chief had a dream: “In the course of time we shall make an attack, and by the old women we shall be tossed into cedar-bark bags.” Thereupon then he began singing as he woke from sleep:—  
“O Snapping-Turtle\! that our fire was going out was what I dreamed.”¹*

Mii dash iw gaa-izhi-maajaawaad (thereupon thence they departed). Niizh ogimaawiwag (two were chiefs) endashiwaad (among their number). Zhigwa dash ani-nibaawag (and as they went they slept by the way). Bezhig a’aw wegimaawid (one of the chiefs) inaabandam (dreamed): “Zhigwa mii nimawinazhiwemin (in time we shall make an attack), mindimooyeyag idash (and by the old women) giizhik-ashkimodaang (into cedar-bark bags) nimbiinjwebinigonaanig (we shall be tossed).” Mii dash igo iw ani-nagamod ani-goshkozid (thereupon he sang as he woke):—  
“Micīⁿkäⁿwā, nintā‘tawänigomin nintinā‘pawe.” (O Snapping-Turtle\! that our fire was going out was what I dreamed.) (song, carried as printed)

*Jones’s footnote (marker on the song’s English; prints at the foot of p. 340): The going-out of fire is the symbol of the departure of life. — W.J. The song is carried as printed in every layer per the songs-carried ruling (M., 2026); note its first word — Micīⁿkäⁿ (mishiikenh), a second word for the snapping turtle beside the narration’s mikinaak.*

(12) Aᵘ mi‘kināk äci∙a∙mạtcimigut, mīdạc äcipạsiguntcisät; äwi∙i∙citạngickawāt, mīsa äcinicki∙ā∙t.

**Aw mikinaak ezhi-amajimigod, mii dash ezhi-bazigonjised; ewi-izhi-dangishkawaad, mii sa ezhi-nishki’aad.**

*When Snapping-Turtle was roused from sleep (by the song), he then leaped to his feet; going over to (the singer), he kicked him, whereupon he angered him.*

Aw mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) ezhi-amajimigod (being wakened by his singing), mii dash ezhi-bazigonjised (then leaped to his feet); ewi-izhi-dangishkawaad (going over and kicking him), mii sa ezhi-nishki’aad (thus he angered him).

*mi‘kināk — printed without the second ‘ (mi‘kinā‘k elsewhere); carried. Cf. mi‘kinâ‘k (§17), mi‘kinä‘k (§13) — the page spreads the turtle’s own name five ways.*

(13) Kīgīcäbitạc päjik īni’ᵘ wītci∙u∙gimān uganōnigôn: “Intawā kīwätā\!” Mīdạc kā∙i∙jikīwäwāt nīⁿc wītcōgimān, aᵘ mi‘kinä‘k idạc cigwa mādcā utänāng. Miskwâtäsiwạn nībiwa uwītcīwān. Cigwa otäbābandānâwa ō’o’ᵘ udäna. Sāga∙a∙midạc päjik a‘kiwäⁿzī, a‘panägu kābisā‘kwänig kīwi‘tāya∙ī∙; inābit, pạnägu miskwātäsiwaᵍ. Inändạmidạc: “Nimawinanigunānig māwīn.”

**Gigizheb idash bezhig iniw wiiji-ogimaan oganoonigoon: “Indawaa giiwedaa\!” Mii dash gaa-izhi-giiwewaad niinzh wiij-ogimaan, aw mikinaak idash zhigwa maajaa oodenaang. Miskwaadesiwan niibiwa owiijiiwaan. Zhigwa odebaabandaanaawaa o’ow oodena. Zaaga’ami dash bezhig akiwenzii, apane go gaa-bizaakwenig giiwitaaya’ii; inaabid, apane go miskwaadesiwag. Inendami dash: “Nimawinanigonaanig maawiin.”**

*So in the morning one of the chiefs was addressed by the other saying: “Therefore let us return home\!” And then, after the two that were chiefs together had departed, Snapping-Turtle kept straight on for the town. In company with many Painted-Turtles he went. In time they came in sight of this town. Now, out of doors came a certain old man, for there rose much shouting everywhere roundabout; as he looked, (he beheld) Painted-Turtles without number. And he thought: “We are being attacked, perhaps.”*

Gigizheb idash (so in the morning) bezhig (one of them) iniw wiiji-ogimaan (by his fellow-chief) oganoonigoon (was addressed): “Indawaa giiwedaa\! (therefore let us go home\!)” Mii dash gaa-izhi-giiwewaad (and after they had gone home) niinzh wiij-ogimaan (the two fellow-chiefs), aw mikinaak idash (Snapping-Turtle for his part) zhigwa maajaa oodenaang (kept on for the town). Miskwaadesiwan niibiwa owiijiiwaan (many Painted-Turtles went with him). Zhigwa odebaabandaanaawaa (in time they came in sight of) o’ow oodena (this town). Zaaga’ami dash (out of doors came) bezhig akiwenzii (a certain old man), apane go gaa-bizaakwenig (for there kept rising a clamor — \*flagged\*) giiwitaaya’ii (everywhere roundabout); inaabid (as he looked), apane go miskwaadesiwag (everywhere Painted-Turtles). Inendami dash (and he thought): “Nimawinanigonaanig maawiin (we are being attacked, it must be).”

*kābisā‘kwänig — Jones “there rose much shouting”; stem unparsed (bizaakwe-?), converted by character rules and flagged. uganōnigôn printed with ô. Sāga∙a∙midạc / Inändạmidạc — independent-order verbs run into idash (the liaison habit again); split in the Fiero layer.*

(14) Mīdạc iᵍi’ᵘ ki‘tci änigu‘k äjipīpāgit: “Ạnicināpätug\! miskwātäsiwạg kīmawinạnigunānig\! Aha’ᵘ, mindimoyätug\! kigīckạckimutāwān nawạtinamu‘k\! Pīndcwäpini‘k īgi’ᵘ miskwātäsiwag\!”

**Mii dash i’iw gichi-enigok ezhi-biibaagid: “Anishinaabedog\! miskwaadesiwag gii-mawinanigonaanig\! Aha’w, mindimooyedog\! gigiizhik-ashkimodaawaan nawadinamok\! Biinjwebinik igiw miskwaadesiwag\!”**

*Thereupon at the very top of his voice he called: “O ye people\! by the Painted-Turtles are we being attacked\! — Now, O ye old women\! make haste to get your cedar-bark bags\! Toss into them the Painted-Turtles\!”*

Mii dash i’iw gichi-enigok (thereupon with all his might) ezhi-biibaagid (he called aloud): “Anishinaabedog\! (O ye people\!) miskwaadesiwag gii-mawinanigonaanig\! (by the Painted-Turtles are we attacked\!) Aha’w, mindimooyedog\! (now, O ye old women\!) gigiizhik-ashkimodaawaan nawadinamok\! (seize your cedar-bark bags\!) Biinjwebinik igiw miskwaadesiwag\! (toss in those Painted-Turtles\!)”

(15) Ā‘taiyā\! Ā‘tā\! Ātaiyā, mindimōyäyạg sāgitcikwāskuniwạg\! Upīndcwäbināwa iᵍi’ᵘ miskwātäsiwaᵍ.

**Aataiyaa\! Aataa\! Aataiyaa, mindimooyeyag zaagijigwaashkoniwag\! Obiinjwebinaawaa’ i’iw miskwaadesiwa’.**

*Oh, I tell you what\! Oh, my\! Oh, but (how) the old women rushed out (of the wigwams)\! In they flung the Painted-Turtles.*

Aataiyaa\! Aataa\! Aataiyaa (oh, I tell you what\! oh, my\!), mindimooyeyag (the old women) zaagijigwaashkoniwag (came leaping out)\! Obiinjwebinaawaa’ (in they flung them) i’iw miskwaadesiwa’ (those Painted-Turtles).

*Upīndcwäbināwa iᵍi’ᵘ miskwātäsiwaᵍ — VTA with the ᵍ object: drafted obviative-plural -a’ (the banked family). The narrator’s three exclamations print three ways (Ā‘taiyā / Ā‘tā / Ātaiyā) — carried exactly; cf. \#45’s Hā‘a’ᵘ/Ha‘a’ᵘ pair.*

(16) Midạc imāⁿ wạnickwäkạmigatinik aᵘ mi‘kinā‘k äjipīndigāpa‘tōt i∙i∙mā cāpondawāning. Ogīwâbạmāᵍ ạbinōdcīyaᵍ. Päcig īni’ᵘ äcinawạtināt, äjikīckigwäpināt; utcicākānk äci∙a∙‘tōt iᵍi’wä utuctigwānim. Minawā päcig äcikīckikwäpināt ạbinōtcīyạn, mīnawā ācawaya∙ī∙ utcitcā‘kāng äjipīnawāt.

**Mii dash imaa wanishkwekamigadinig aw mikinaak ezhi-biindigebatood i’imaa zhaabondawaaning. Ogii-waabamaa’ abinoojiiya’. Bezhig iniw ezhi-nawadinaad, ezhi-giishkigwebinaad; ojijaakaang ezhi-atood i’iwe oshtigwaanim. Miinawaa bezhig ezhi-giishkigwebinaad abinoojiiyan, miinawaa aazhawaya’ii ojijaakaang ezhi-biinawaad.**

*And while there was din and tumult, Snapping-Turtle rushed into a long-lodge. He saw some children. On seizing one of them, he broke its neck; at his groin he placed its head. When he broke the neck of the other child, at his groin on the other side he put (it).*

Mii dash imaa wanishkwekamigadinig (and while the place was in tumult) aw mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) ezhi-biindigebatood (ran inside) i’imaa zhaabondawaaning (into the long-lodge there). *ina‘kwaciwät / kībītạgwạciwäwan — the -aazhiwe swim final, two members in one section; conversion flagged (a‘kw/agw prints). äcicagackāmikipạgisut — zhagashk- flat + -aamik- bottom + -bagizo sudden-move: ‘flung himself flat against the lake-bottom’ (the -bagizo ledger row, \#73). tibickotcaiya∙ī∙ here vs \#45’s tibickōtcaya∙ī∙ — one word, two stories, two prints.*

(27) Misa omāⁿ kinawatạmigut omạmaci∙ā∙ dac; äjimōckạmut aᵍa’ᵘ nigig. Kāmōckamutidạc pīpāgi: “Mi‘kināk ninta‘kwạmig\!” “Nīyānk\!” mạdwä∙i∙‘kito. “Kinạgāngina?” “Kāwīn, nīyānk.”

**Mii sa omaa gii-nawadamigod omamaazhi’aa dash; ezhi-mooshkamod a’aw nigig. Gaa-mooshkamod idash biibaagi: “Mikinaak nindakwamig\!” “Niiyaank\!” madwe-ikido. “Ginagaang ina?” “Gaawiin, niiyaank.”**

*And so here¹ (Otter) was bitten and he was overcome; then up to the surface came Otter. And when he came up, he called aloud: “By Snapping-Turtle am I bitten\!” “Where has he bitten you?” “At my penis\!”² he was heard saying. “At your penis?”² “No, at my penis\!”²*

Mii sa omaa (and so HERE) gii-nawadamigod (was he bitten by him) omamaazhi’aa dash (and he overcame him); ezhi-mooshkamod a’aw nigig (then up to the surface came the Otter). Gaa-mooshkamod idash biibaagi (and when he surfaced he called aloud): “Mikinaak nindakwamig\! (by Snapping-Turtle am I bitten\!)” “Niiyaank\!” madwe-ikido (“at my penis\!” he was heard saying — \*the archaic word\*). “Ginagaang ina? (at your penis? — \*the ordinary word\*)” “Gaawiin, niiyaank. (no — at my penis\!)”

*Jones’s footnotes: ¹ At the penis. ² There is a joke here which the translation does not bring out. It consists in a play on two words for “penis.” In the answer Otter uses an archaic word found only in story, and in the question the ordinary word is used; and, as the two words are nearly alike, the joke lies in the failure of the others to understand what Otter is saying, but at the same time to guess correctly. — W.J. Kinạgāngina — gi\[wii\]nag-aang ina suspected (the ordinary word wiinag; cf. \#74 §19) — print carried, segmentation flagged; nīyānk the story-archaism carried untouched in the Fiero layer (it is the joke).*

(28) Mīdạc iᵍi’ᵘ äjigīgitut ‘aᵍa’ᵘ mi‘kinā‘k: “Pāmā ạnimi‘kīg pītwäwitạmowāt kīgạpạgitạmin.” “Ānīc käkītotạmạnk?” i‘kitōwag īgi’ᵘ anicinābäg. Mi‘tigwaki‘kōnidạc udānumạdwäwāwān. “Kūwisu mi‘tigwa‘ki‘k.” Misa mīnawā kāskīgin utānubiti‘kubitōnāwa anicinābäg. “Kūwisu kāskīginīni.”

**Mii dash i’iw ezhi-giigidod ’a’aw mikinaak: “Baamaa animikiig biidwewidamowaad giga-bagidamin.” “Aaniish ge-gii-doodamang?” ikidowag igiw anishinaabeg. Mitigwakikoon idash odaano-madwewaawaan. “Goowisu mitigwakik.” Mii sa miinawaa gaashkiigin odaano-bidikobidoonaawaa anishinaabeg. “Goowisu gaashkiiginiini.”**

*Thereupon then spoke Snapping-Turtle, saying: “Not till the Thunderers come a-roaring will I let him go.” “What are we to do?” said the people. And upon the wooden (kettle-)drum they tried to beat, but in vain. “Impossible is the wooden (kettle-)drum.”³ And so next with (muslin) cloth⁴ did the people try in vain to make a noise. “Impossible is (muslin) cloth.”³*

Mii dash i’iw ezhi-giigidod ’a’aw mikinaak (thereupon spoke Snapping-Turtle): “Baamaa animikiig (not until the Thunderers) biidwewidamowaad (come sounding hither) giga-bagidamin (will I release him from my mouth).” “Aaniish ge-gii-doodamang? (what are we to do?)” ikidowag igiw anishinaabeg (said the people). Mitigwakikoon idash (and the wooden drum) odaano-madwewaawaan (they tried in vain to sound). “Goowisu mitigwakik. (impossible is the wooden drum — \*flagged\*)” Mii sa miinawaa gaashkiigin (and next with cloth) odaano-bidikobidoonaawaa (they tried in vain, ripping it,) anishinaabeg (the people). “Goowisu gaashkiiginiini. *Jones’s footnotes: ³ Said by Snapping-Turtle. ⁴ For a drum-head. — W.J. Kūwisu (×2) — the impossible-particle; unattested shape (gaawii-class suspected), converted by character rules and HARD-ish flagged. käkītotạmạnk — the ge-gii- family yet again (fifth print this story-pair). kīgạpạgitạmin — giga-bagidamin ‘I will release you/him from the mouth’ (bagid- release + -am by mouth): the bite grammar. biidwewidamowaad — biidwewidam verbatim (\#53/\#73/\#74 acoustic family).*

(29) Mīsa äjikīgitowāt anicinābäg: “Ānīc nā mīsa’ gägä‘t tcinisigut aᵍaᵘ nigig īni’ᵘ mi‘kinā‘kwan.” Päcigidạc aᵍaᵘ inini äjikīgitut: “Nīnsa nindākạcki‘tōn iᵍiwä tcipītwäwä‘tāwā‘pạn īgi’ᵘ ạnimi‘kīg.” Mīsa cigwa u‘pwāgạnạn kī∙a∙ī∙nā‘kunigät: “Ạmbäsanō, ạnimi‘kītug, pīcaiyu‘k\! nōndāgusiyu‘k omāⁿ kī∙a∙yāyānk\!” Kā∙i∙ckwā gāgīgitot ca∙ī∙gwa pitäbwäwitạmōᵍ.

**Mii sa ezhi-giigidowaad anishinaabeg: “Aaniish naa mii sa’ geget ji-nisigod a’aw nigig iniw mikinaakwan.” Bezhig idash a’aw inini ezhi-giigidod: “Niin sa nindaa-gashkitoon i’iwe ji-biidwewetaawaapan igiw animikiig.” Mii sa zhigwa opwaaganan gii-ayinaakoniged: “Ambesanoo, animikiidog, biijaayok\! noondaagoziyok omaa gii-ayaayaang\!” Gaa-ishkwaa-gaagiigidod zhayiigwa biidwewidamoog.**

*And then spoke the people, saying: “Perhaps it is true that Otter will be killed by Snapping-Turtle.” And one of the men spoke, saying: “I might be able to make the Thunderers come a-roaring.” Thereupon then he pointed his pipe toward the (various) directions: “Now, O ye Thunderers, come\! make the sound of your voices heard here where we are\!” After he had made an end of his talking, then came the sound (of the Thunderers).*

Mii sa ezhi-giigidowaad anishinaabeg (and then spoke the people): “Aaniish naa mii sa’ geget (perhaps then it is true) ji-nisigod (that he will be killed) a’aw nigig (the Otter) iniw mikinaakwan (by Snapping-Turtle).” Bezhig idash a’aw inini ezhi-giigidod (and one of the men spoke): “Niin sa nindaa-gashkitoon (I might be able to bring it about) i’iwe ji-biidwewetaawaapan (that they be heard coming) igiw animikiig (those Thunderers).” Mii sa zhigwa opwaaganan (thereupon his pipe) gii-ayinaakoniged (he pointed to the various directions): “Ambesanoo, animikiidog, biijaayok\! (now, O ye Thunderers, come\!) noondaagoziyok (make your voices heard) omaa gii-ayaayaang\! (here where we are\!)” Gaa-ishkwaa-gaagiigidod (after he had made an end of his talking) zhayiigwa biidwewidamoog (already came the sound of them approaching).

*kī∙a∙ī∙nā‘kunigät — ayinaakonige- ‘gesture/decree to the various directions’ (verbatim \#45 §16’s flagged stem — second attestation, with the pipe explicit); ca∙ī∙gwa — the zhayiigwa spelled-out print again (\#45’s Ca∙ī∙gwa, same narrator’s series); tcipītwäwä‘tāwā‘pạn — ji- + -waapan preterite-conjunct on the Thunderer verb, flagged light. animikiidog — the -dog vocative on the Thunderers: the same form the town-crier used for people and old women (§14); noted, never interpreted.*

(30) “Cigwa ạnimi‘kīg\!” udinān īni’ᵘ mi‘kinā‘kwan aᵍa’ᵘ nigig mägwā umindcimạmigōn. Cigwa imā titickōtcayā∙ī∙ pimitạnwäwitamōᵍ mīsa iᵍi’ᵘ äjipạgitạmigut. Änīci∙ạ∙gwā‘tāt awä nigig.

**“Zhigwa animikiig\!” odinaan iniw mikinaakwan a’aw nigig megwaa ominjimamigoon. Zhigwa imaa didishkoojayaa’ii bimi-danwewidamoog mii sa i’iw ezhi-bagidamigod. Eni-izhi-agwaataad awe nigig.**

*titickōtcayā∙ī∙ — this page’s third print of the directly-overhead word (tibickotcaiya∙ī∙ §26, tibickōtcaya∙ī∙ \#45) — one word, three printings; carried. pimitạnwäwitamōᵍ — bimi- + danwewidam ‘sound in passing’: the Thunderers go by; final ᵍ on the plural subject → -oog. minjimam- / bagidam- — held by mouth, released by mouth: the -am mouth final closes its own frame.*
