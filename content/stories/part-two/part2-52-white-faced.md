**WAABISHKIINGWENAAMOSH.**

WHITE-FACED.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ạnicinābäg a∙i∙ndāwạg. Pā‘tīnīnuwaᵍ unīdcānisaᵍ; nānạniwa utānisaᵍ, ugwisaᵍ ningutwā‘tciwaᵍ. Kägä‘t misawänimā pā‘tạnīninit i⁸i‘ᵘ unītcānisaᵍ. Ānīc ka‘kina uckinīgiwaᵍ. Ningutingigu minwänimā wâbickīngwänāmus, mīsa kägä‘t īni‘ᵘ päcig i‘kwäwạn äjiwītigämāt. “Kāwīn gạnạbạtc ni‘tānantawäntcigāsī,” inäntạm a‘kiwäⁿzī. Ickwạtcībạn midāsugun kāwīn mạci nạntawäntcigāsī. Kägä‘t kō‘pạtạsi. “Mīmāwīnigu iᵘ kạgä‘t kä∙i∙ci∙a∙yāt,” inändạm aᵘ a‘kiwänzi. Ānīc wī‘tān nạntawäntcigäckiwan, mīyä‘tạgu iᵘ anicā ạcạmint. “Ạmbäsa, wī‘kwạtci‘tōn tcinạntawäntcigäyạn\!” udinān aᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzī.

**Anishinaabeg ayindaawag. Baatiininowag oniijaanisa’; naananiwan odaanisa’, ogwisa’ ningodwaaswiwag. Geget misawenimaa baatiininid i’iw oniijaanisa’. Aaniish gakina oshkiniigiwag. Ningoding igo minwenimaa waabishkiingwenaamosh, mii sa geget iniw bezhig ikwewan ezhi-wiidigemaad. “Gaawiin ganabaj nin-da-nandawenjigesii,” inendam akiwenzii. Ishkwaajiban midaasogon gaawiin maji-nandawenjigesii. Geget goopadizi. “Miimawiin igo iw geget gaa-izhi-ayaad,” inendam aw akiwenzi. Aaniish wiidaa-nandawenjigeshkiwan, mii eta go iw anishaa ashamind. “Ambesa, wii-gwajitoon ji-nandawenjigeyan\!” odinaan aw akiwenzii.**

*Some people were living (there). Many were their children; five were their daughters, their sons were six. In truth, he¹ was much sought after, because many were his children. Now, all were young. And once White-Faced² was loved, whereupon truly one of the women he married. “Perhaps he is not good at hunting for game,” thought the old man. For the full space of ten days he had not gone on a hunt. In truth, he was good for nothing. “That probably is the way he will surely be,” thought the old man. Now, his brothers-in-law were fond of hunting for game, while he simply allowed himself to be fed. “Come, try to hunt for game\!” to him said the old man.*

Anishinaabeg ayindaawag (some people were living there — \*ayi-indaawag, dwelling-stem flagged\*). Baatiininowag oniijaanisa’ (many were their children); naananiwan odaanisa’ (five were their daughters), ogwisa’ ningodwaaswiwag (their sons were six). Geget misawenimaa baatiininid i’iw oniijaanisa’ (truly he was much sought after, because his children were many). Aaniish gakina oshkiniigiwag (now, all were young). Ningoding igo minwenimaa waabishkiingwenaamosh (once White-Faced was loved), mii sa geget iniw bezhig ikwewan ezhi-wiidigemaad (and so truly one of the women he married). “Gaawiin ganabaj nin-da-nandawenjigesii (“perhaps he is not good at hunting),” inendam akiwenzii (thought the old man). Ishkwaajiban midaasogon gaawiin maji-nandawenjigesii (for ten full days he had not hunted — \*ishkwaaj-/preterite stem flagged\*). Geget goopadizi (in truth he was good for nothing). “Miimawiin igo iw geget gaa-izhi-ayaad (“that surely is the way he will be),” inendam aw akiwenzi (thought the old man). Aaniish wiidaa-nandawenjigeshkiwan (now his brothers-in-law were fond of hunting — \*wiidaa- ‘brother-in-law’ + habitual, flagged\*), mii eta go iw anishaa ashamind (while he simply allowed himself to be fed). “Ambesa, wii-gwajitoon ji-nandawenjigeyan\!” (“come, try to hunt for game\!”) odinaan aw akiwenzii (to him said the old man).

*¹ Jones (p442): “The father of the children.” (the much-sought “he”). ² Jones (p442): “A story-name for the mink.” (Waabishkiingwenaamosh / White-Faced is the Mink.) ∙ VARIATION preserved: akiwenzii (akiwäⁿzī, long-ī + superscript ⁿ) \~ akiwenzi (akiwänzi, plain) within this section — narrator/print variation in the word for “old man,” not normalized. ∙ ᵍ-flags (HARD, obviative/plural -a’ vs -ag, syntax decides): oniijaanisaᵍ, odaanisaᵍ, ogwisaᵍ, ningodwaaswiwaᵍ, oshkiniigiwaᵍ — drafted -a’/-ag, banked for M.*

(2) Kigīcāb cigwa ocī‘tāwī‘ki∙o∙sät. Tcībā‘kwä aᵉa‘ᵘ mindimōyä īni‘ᵘ uzi‘kusisạn. Mī cigwa wīkitcipisut äjikīgitut a‘kiwäⁿzī: “Nạckänä, pạgitînisäyu‘k. Māgicā tagī‘kạtci waᵉaᵘ wāmādcāt.” Käga‘pīki‘tcibisut Wâbickīngwänāmus äcimạmōt ickutä; äcipīndōmut, kägä‘t tcāgisu. Ki‘twänigu ạnimādcā. Kumāgu a‘pī kā‘tagwicink ingutci utạni∙a∙‘pạgitōn. Kägä‘t kītcāgisō imāⁿ utcitcā‘kānk. Kāwīn ugạcki‘tōsīn ānawipimusät. Kā∙i∙nā‘kisut intawä äjikīwät; äcitạgwicink, kāwin kạnạgä pinäwan ubīnāsīn. Kägä‘t ubā‘pī∙i∙gō i⁸i‘ᵘ wī‘täᵍ. Ningutingigu mīnawā ōgạnōnigōn īni‘ᵘ wīwạn: “Māma‘kātc kāwin mạci kägō kīni‘tōsīn.” Wâbickīngwänāmus kīgito: “Nạckäsa, wâbạnk ninganisā aᵉa‘ᵘ ma‘kwa.”

**Gigizheb zhigwa ozhiitaa-wii-giiwose. Jiibaakwe a’aw mindimooye iniw ozhigosisan. Mii zhigwa wii-gichi-biizod ezhi-giigidod akiwenzii: “Naska ina, bagidiniseyok. Maagizhaa da-gikaaji a’aw waa-maajaad.” Gega-biiki-jiibizod Waabishkiingwenaamosh ezhi-maamood ishkode; ezhi-biindoomod, geget jaagizo. Gichi-twe igo ani-maajaa. Gomaa go apii gaa-dagwishing ingoji odani-apagidoon. Geget gii-jaagizo imaa ojijaakaank. Gaawiin ogashki’toosiin aanawi-bimosed. Gaa-izhi-naa’kizod indawaa ezhi-giiwed; ezhi-dagwishing, gaawiin gananage binewan obiinaasiin. Geget obaapi’igoo i’iw wiidaa’. Ningoding igo miinawaa oganoonigoon iniw wiiwan: “Maamakaaj gaawiin maji-gego gii-nitoosiin.” Waabishkiingwenaamosh giigido: “Naska sa, waabang ninga-nisaa a’aw makwa.”**

*In the morning, then he made ready to go on a hunt. Some food did the old woman who was his mother-in-law cook. When he made ready to put on his belt, then spoke the old man, saying: “Behold, (some of) you put wood on the fire. Maybe he who is going away will be cold.” At last, when White-Faced girdled on his belt, he then picked up a (live-coal of) fire; when he placed it in the bosom of his garment, he truly was burned. But nevertheless away he went. And when he was come a certain distance, he flung it away. In truth, he was burned down at the groin. Unable was he to walk, in spite of his efforts. Because of the burn he got, he therefore turned back; when he arrived, not so much as a ruffed grouse³ did he fetch home. To be sure, he was made fun of by his brothers-in-law. So one other time he was addressed by his wife saying: “It is strange that not a single thing have you yet killed.” White-Faced spoke, saying: “Behold, to-morrow I shall kill a bear.”*

*³ Jones (p443): “The ruffed grouse is the easiest of all game to get.” (attaches to “ruffed grouse,” obiinaasiin / binewan). ∙ Live-coal-in-the-bosom episode: ishkode = fire/live coal; the old man’s ‘put wood on the fire’ is the set-up for the trick. ∙ ‘wiidaa’ = wiidaa-/wiitaa- ‘brother-in-law’ (obv. pl. -a’ vs -ag flagged).*

*In the morning some food did the old woman cook. After she had finished cooking the meal, she spoke, and this was what she said: “The son-in-law perhaps may not have anything to warm his bosom, and that is why I made the soup.” Then before him was the soup placed; very hot it was. *In the morning then he departed, his clothes he carried upon his back. When he had come a certain distance, he saw a river. And then at a place where he saw that the water had not (yet) frozen he made camp. On the morrow he made a canoe and a paddle. When he had finished his work, he got into his canoe. When he was come a certain distance, in among the willows he saw some one busily eating. He crept up to it; when he shot at it, truly then he killed it. To be sure, he was pleased. Well, when he had picked up his knife, he cut a slice off the creature. “Lo, (there is) grease,” he thought; but it turned out to be a creature of white flesh only, and there was no fat on the ruffed grouse. In truth, strong was his wish to take it back to his wife. Then off he started. When he arrived at home, he spoke to his wife, saying: “Now, what I have killed is nothing but fat.”*

*Thereupon then away he went for good. Accordingly then on his way he went; as he went, he wept. Late in the evening he came to where his canoe was. And so at that place he slept.
