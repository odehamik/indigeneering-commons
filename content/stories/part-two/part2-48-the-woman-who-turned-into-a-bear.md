THE WOMAN WHO TURNED INTO A BEAR.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Aᵘ käkä‘k i‘kwäwạn uwīdigämān, ä‘pidci ucīngänimān, mī∙i∙‘ᵘ uwīpäcwābạmigōn; mī a‘panä kabätibi‘k udōpā∙i∙gōn. Kägä‘t kāwīn inändāⁿzī tcibäcwābạmāt īni‘kwäwạn. Ōsạn ayāwạn kayä īni‘ᵘ ugīn. Mīdạc iᵍi‘ᵘ, “Ạmbäsạnō, ningamādcā,” udinān ōsạn. “Kägä‘t nicīngänimā a∙i∙‘kwä. Kāwīn wība ningạtạgwiciⁿzī.” Kägä‘t äcimādcāt a∙i∙nini.

**Aw gekek ikwewan owiidigemaan, aapiji o-zhiingenimaan, mii iw owii-beshwaabamigoon; mii apane gabe-dibik odoopaa’igoon. Geget gaawiin inendanziin ji-beshwaabamaad iniw ikwewan. Oosan ayaawan gaye iniw ogiin. Mii dash igiw, “Ambesano, ninga-maajaa,” odinaan oosan. “Geget ni-zhiingenimaa a’aw ikwe. Gaawiin wiiba ningad-dagwizhinzii.” Geget ezhi-maajaad a’aw inini.**

*Bird-Hawk lived with a woman; very much he hated her, for attempt was made by her to be intimate with him; and so throughout the whole of every night he was kept awake by her. Truly no desire did he have to be intimate with the woman. He had a father and a mother. So then, “Therefore I am going away,” he said to his father. “Really do I hate the woman. Not soon will I return.” Truly thence departed the man.*

Aw gekek (Bird-Hawk; \*literally ‘the hawk’\*) ikwewan owiidigemaan (lived with / was married to a woman). Aapiji o-zhiingenimaan (very much he hated her), mii iw owii-beshwaabamigoon (for she would try to lie close beside him — \*stem beshwaab- ‘draw near’ flagged\*); mii apane gabe-dibik odoopaa’igoon (and so all night long she kept him awake — \*odoopaa’igoon ‘she keeps him from sleep’ flagged\*). Geget gaawiin inendanziin (truly he had no wish) ji-beshwaabamaad iniw ikwewan (to be intimate with that woman). Oosan ayaawan gaye iniw ogiin (he had a father and a mother). Mii dash igiw (and so then), “Ambesano, ninga-maajaa,” (“come now, I am going away,”) odinaan oosan (he said to his father). “Geget ni-zhiingenimaa a’aw ikwe (“truly I hate that woman). Gaawiin wiiba ningad-dagwizhinzii.” (not soon will I come back.”) Geget ezhi-maajaad a’aw inini (truly the man set off).

*uwīpäcwābạmigōn / ucīngänimān — the “draw near to be intimate” verb (beshwaab-) drafted; passive-inverse morphology read from Jones’s English, stem flagged. udōpā∙i∙gōn — “she keeps him awake / from sleep,” drafted odoopaa’igoon, segmentation flagged. ningạtạgwiciⁿzī — future “I will not return,” liaison future ningad- + dagwishin negative; the ⁿ before zī read nh/∅, drafted ningad-dagwizhinzii, flagged.*

(2) Mīdạc iᵍi‘ᵘ aᵍawi‘kwä nickādisit. Kīmādcānit īni‘ᵘ ininiwạn, “Tạga, ninganīsimān īniyōsạn kayä īni‘ᵘ ugīn.” Mīdạc äcima‘kōwit a∙i∙‘kwä.

**Mii dash igiw aw ikwe nishkaadizid. Gii-maajaanid iniw ininiwan, “Daga, ninga-nisimaan iniw oosan gaye iniw ogiin.” Mii dash ezhi-makoowid a’aw ikwe.**

*Thereupon then was the woman angry. When the man was gone, “Now, I am going to his father and mother.” Accordingly then into a bear the woman was changed.*

Mii dash igiw aw ikwe nishkaadizid (and so then the woman became angry). Gii-maajaanid iniw ininiwan (when that man had gone away), “Daga, ninga-nisimaan iniw oosan gaye iniw ogiin.” (“now, I will slay his father and his mother.”) Mii dash ezhi-makoowid a’aw ikwe (and so then the woman turned into a bear — \*makoowi- ‘become a bear’\*).

*äcima‘kōwit — “she turns into a bear,” makoo-wi- (bear + become); the transformation verb, read with confidence from Jones’s English, retained. ninganīsimān — “I will slay them,” ningan(d)-nisim-; future liaison drafted ninga-nisimaan, flagged.*

(3) Ānīc tci∙ō∙dänäng ayāwạg, wīnidạc udipändän iwudäna aᵍa‘ᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzi.

**Aaniish ji-oodenaang ayaawag, wiin idash odibendaan iw oodena a’aw akiwenzi.**

*Now, in a great town they lived, and the old man himself ruled over the town.*

Aaniish ji-oodenaang ayaawag (now, in a town they were living — \*tci∙ō∙dänäng ‘at a/the town’; the tci- prefix here flagged\*), wiin idash odibendaan iw oodena (and he himself ruled / owned that town) a’aw akiwenzi (that old man).

*tci∙ō∙dänäng — “at a town / in a great town,” the tci- + oodenaang form is unusual (Jones renders ‘in a great town’); parse flagged. udipändän — “he rules / owns it,” odibendaan, retained.*

(4) Mīdạc iᵍi‘ᵘ äcimādcād a∙i∙‘kwä mī∙i∙wä kīkạcki‘tōt iᵍi‘ᵘ ma‘kūwit; ā‘pidcigu kīki‘tciminditō. Mīdạc iwiti ä‘kwä‘kwāni‘k kāpi∙u∙ndcipīpāgit, ka‘kina‘ äcisāga∙ạ∙muwāt īgi‘ᵘ ạnicinābäg. Cigwa päcu‘ pātạgwicing ma‘kwa, pīndigäsäwạg anicinābäg. Udä‘pinạmowāt upäckisigạniwān kayä īni‘ᵘ o wâgā‘kwạtōwān māwinäwāwāt.

**Mii dash igiw ezhi-maajaad a’aw ikwe mii iwe gii-gashki’tood igiw makoowid; aapiji go gii-gichi-mindido. Mii dash iwidi e’kwekwaanig gaa-bi-onji-biibaagid, gakina ezhi-zaaga’amowaad igiw anishinaabeg. Zhigwa besho ba-dagwishing makwa, biindigesewag anishinaabeg. Odaapinamowaad obaashkizigani-waan gaye iniw o-waagaakwadowaan mawinewaawaad.**

*And so then away went the woman, whereupon she then brought it about that she became a bear; and ever so big was she. Thereupon, when from yonder place at the edge of the forest she called aloud, then out came all the people. When near by the bear was come, then into (their wigwams) hastened the people. They seized their guns and their axes to go to attack (the bear).*

Mii dash igiw ezhi-maajaad a’aw ikwe (and so then the woman set off) mii iwe gii-gashki’tood igiw makoowid (whereupon she managed to become a bear); aapiji go gii-gichi-mindido (and very big indeed was she). Mii dash iwidi e’kwekwaanig (and so over there at the edge of the woods — \*ä‘kwä‘kwāni‘k flagged\*) gaa-bi-onji-biibaagid (when she called out from there), gakina ezhi-zaaga’amowaad igiw anishinaabeg (all the people came outside). Zhigwa besho ba-dagwishing makwa (when the bear came near), biindigesewag anishinaabeg (the people rushed indoors). Odaapinamowaad obaashkiziganiwaan (they seized their guns) gaye iniw o-waagaakwadowaan (and their axes) mawinewaawaad (to go attack her — \*mawinewaa- ‘rush upon’\*).

*ä‘kwä‘kwāni‘k — “at the edge of the woods,” reduplicated/locative; parse flagged. pātạgwicing / pīndigäsäwạg — ba-dagwishing, biindigese- (rush in) retained. māwinäwāwāt — mawinewaawaad “they rush upon / attack,” retained.*

(5) Mīgū īni‘ᵘ ni‘tạm a‘kiwäⁿzīyạn iwägimāwinit näsāt. Mīdạc imāⁿ ānawipa‘kitä∙u∙nt wâgā‘kwātōn kāwäsā unisāsiwāwān. Ka‘kina udānumāmawō‘kawāwān änupäckiswāwāt, kāwīn pigwäckōsusī. Ānind änōcimōwạg pōtc ka‘kina äjinisāt. Ga‘kina kānisāt mī cigwa nō‘pinạnät īni‘ᵘ cängänimigut īni‘ᵘ ininiwạn. mạdwäsāsā‘kwänit ạnicinābäᵍ; kuniginīn, owâbạmāᵍ ạnicinābäᵍ pā‘tạnīninit wäyābạmigut.

**Mii go iniw nitam akiwenziiyan iwe-gimaawinid nesaad. Mii dash imaa aanawi-bakite’ond waagaakwadoon gaawesaa onisaasiwaawaan. Gakina odaano-maamawo’kawaawaan aano-baashkiswaawaad, gaawiin bigwashkozosii. Aniind anoozhimowag boorch gakina ezhi-nisaad. Gakina gaa-nisaad mii zhigwa noopinanaad iniw zhiingenimigod iniw ininiwan. Madwesaasaa’kwenid anishinaabe’; goniginiin, owaabamaa’ anishinaabe’ baadaniininid weyaabamigod.**

*And the first she slew was the old man that was chief. And though she was then beaten with axes, yet they could not kill her. All of them together tried going against her, in vain shooting at her (with guns), but she was impervious. Some tried to flee, but in spite of that she slew them all. After she had slain them all, she then followed after the man who hated her. When a certain distance she had come on the trail (along which) she was following him, she suddenly caught the sound of some people whooping; lo, she beheld some people, many in number, looking at her.*

Mii go iniw nitam akiwenziiyan (and so first that old man) iwe-gimaawinid nesaad (the one who was chief she slew — \*iwägimāwinit ‘the one who is chief’ flagged\*). Mii dash imaa aanawi-bakite’ond waagaakwadoon (and though she was struck there with axes) gaawesaa onisaasiwaawaan (they could not kill her — \*gaawesaa ‘in vain / not at all’\*). Gakina odaano-maamawo’kawaawaan (all of them together tried to set on her) aano-baashkiswaawaad (shooting at her in vain), gaawiin bigwashkozosii (she was not pierced — \*bigwashkozo- ‘be pierced’ flagged\*). Aniind anoozhimowag (some fled) boorch gakina ezhi-nisaad (yet she slew them all). Gakina gaa-nisaad (after she had slain them all) mii zhigwa noopinanaad (then she pursued) iniw zhiingenimigod iniw ininiwan (that man who hated her). Madwesaasaa’kwenid anishinaabe’ (she heard people whooping — \*madwesaasaa’kwe- ‘sound of whooping’ flagged\*); goniginiin (lo and behold), owaabamaa’ anishinaabe’ (she saw people) baadaniininid weyaabamigod (many of them looking at her).

*iwägimāwinit — “the one who was chief,” drafted iwe-gimaawinid, prefix iwä- flagged. unisāsiwāwān / pigwäckōsusī — “they could not kill her / she was not pierced,” inverse + negative; bigwashkozo- flagged. mạdwäsāsā‘kwänit — “whooping is heard,” madwe- (sound of) + saasaa’kwe-, drafted, flagged. Word-final raised ᵍ on anicinābäᵍ / owâbạmāᵍ (obviative/plural) rendered as trailing ’ per corpus convention. This long paragraph spans printed pages 398–400.*

(6) Mī cigwa pimawinạnint, pāckizigạnạn änupāckisunt; mīgu iᵍi‘ᵘ ana‘kōtäbibinät änibō‘kutcībinät. Kägä‘t nībiwa unisāᵍ. Abā‘pic ga‘kina kānisāt, kāwīn awiya īni‘ᵘ ininiwạn īni‘ᵘ gācingänimigut; mīdạc iᵘ mādci‘tād aninantwa∙a∙tcigät, mīsa kāwīn ningutci omi‘kwa∙ạ∙nāsīn. Käga‘pīgu icpîmink mi‘tigunk ānu∙i∙nābi, ningutingigu ugīwâbạmān ạgōsinit. “ ‘Ä, nōngum ä‘ta kibimādis\!” Äci∙a∙‘kwạndawät ‘aᵍa‘ᵘ ma‘kwa; payäcwâbạmāt ugạnōnigōn: “Kigītimāgis nindānisā änänimiwạnän.”

**Mii zhigwa bimawinanind, baashkiziganan aano-baashkizond; mii go igiw ana-gotebibinaad eni-bookojibinaad. Geget niibiwa o-nisaa’. Abaapii gakina gaa-nisaad, gaawiin awiya iniw ininiwan iniw zhaagenimigod; mii dash iw maajii’taad ani-nandawaa-jiged, miisa gaawiin ningoji o-mikwaa’anaasiin. Gegapii go ishpiming mitigong aano-inaabi, ningoding igo ogii-waabamaan agoozinid. “ ‘E, noongom eta gi-bimaadiz\!” Ezhi-akwandawed a’aw makwa; baa-beshwaabamaad oganoonigoon: “Gidimaagiz nindaanisaa enenimiwanen.”**

*Then was she pursued, then with guns was she shot at, but to no purpose; whereupon as fast as she could catch them she tore them in two. In truth, many she slew. In a while, after she had slain them all, there was nothing (to be seen) of the man who hated her; whereupon then she set to work looking for (the signs of his trail), but nowhere could she find his tracks. At last up a tree she looked, and of a sudden she saw him perched upon (a limb). “Ah, this is the only moment left you to live\!” Then up the tree climbed the Bear; when getting near to him, she was addressed by him saying: “You are a poor fool to be possessed of the thought that you can kill me.”*

Mii zhigwa bimawinanind (then she was pursued), baashkiziganan aano-baashkizond (with guns she was shot at in vain); mii go igiw ana-gotebibinaad eni-bookojibinaad (whereupon as she caught them she tore them in two — \*gotebibinaa-/bookojibinaa- ‘seize and break/tear apart’ flagged\*). Geget niibiwa o-nisaa’ (truly many she slew). Abaapii gakina gaa-nisaad (when she had slain them all), gaawiin awiya iniw ininiwan iniw zhaagenimigod (there was no sign of the man who hated her); mii dash iw maajii’taad ani-nandawaa-jiged (and so she began searching for his trail — \*nandawaa-jige- ‘seek tracks’ flagged\*), miisa gaawiin ningoji o-mikwaa’anaasiin (but nowhere could she find his tracks). Gegapii go ishpiming mitigong aano-inaabi (at last she looked up into a tree in vain), ningoding igo ogii-waabamaan agoozinid (and suddenly she saw him perched up there). “ ‘E, noongom eta gi-bimaadiz\!” (“ah, only now have you left to live\!”) Ezhi-akwandawed a’aw makwa (then the bear climbed up the tree); baa-beshwaabamaad oganoonigoon (when she drew near him she was addressed by him): “Gidimaagiz nindaanisaa enenimiwanen.” (“you are pitiful, that you should suppose you can kill me” — \*nindaanisaa flagged; Jones: ‘you are a poor fool…’\*).

*ana‘kōtäbibinät änibō‘kutcībinät — “as she seized them she tore/broke them in two,” a paired -bibinaa- (seize) construction; both stems flagged. mādci‘tād aninantwa∙a∙tcigät — “she set to searching (his trail),” drafted maajii’taad ani-nandawaa-jiged, flagged. omi‘kwa∙ạ∙nāsīn — “she could not find his tracks,” mikwaa’an- (find by track), retained. Kigītimāgis nindānisā änänimiwạnän — the bear-quote; nindānisā is opaque (Jones ‘poor fool’), flagged.*

(7) “Mi\[ᵉ\]tcaya∙i∙ kīgamīgätimin.” “Kāwīn, mīgu∙o∙māⁿ wāta‘pinạninān.” Umi‘tigwābīn äji∙ō∙tā‘pinät kayä upikwa‘k a∙i∙nini. Pīdäntawänit nāwā‘kigạn äcipimwāt, kīsängängäsinini iᵘ pikwa‘k. Kägāgu pạngicinōn. Mīnawā päcig ubikwa‘k umạmōn; äjipimwāt wawīngä ucāpunawān; nāgạtcigu äjipạngicininit. Mīsagu iᵍi‘ᵘ kägä‘t kī∙ạ∙ninibunit äcinīsāndawät. Mi‘tigōn äci∙ō∙‘kwä‘kwisitōt; kā∙u∙tā‘pinạnk īni‘ᵘ ubīkwa‘kōn, nībiwa ugī∙a∙‘tōnạn īni‘ᵘ misạn. Kā∙i∙jisa‘kawāt inābit, ōdäna pạnägu kābō‘kutcīcininit anicinābäᵍ. Umi‘tigwābīn udōdā‘pinän kayä obikwa‘k. Äjīcpāngwa∙ā∙t pīpāgi tci∙ä∙nigu‘k: “Anicinābätug\! nimbikwa‘k kibisi‘kāgunāwā\!”

**“Mii zhaye’ii ji-gagamiigaadimin.” “Gaawiin, mii go omaa waa-dakwaninaan.” Omitigwaabiin ezhi-odaapinaad gaye obikwak a’aw inini. Biidentawenid naawakigan ezhi-bimwaad, gii-zaangaangesinini iw bikwak. Gegaa go bangizhinoon. Miinawaa bezhig obikwak omamoon; ezhi-bimwaad wawiinge ozhaaponawaan; naagaj igo ezhi-bangizhininid. Mii sa go igiw geget gii-ani-nibonid ezhi-niisaandawed. Mitigoon ezhi-okwaakwisitood; gaa-odaapinang iniw obikwakoon, niibiwa ogii-atoonan iniw misan. Gaa-izhi-zaka’waad inaabid, oodena banaa go gaa-bookojishininid anishinaabe’. Omitigwaabiin odoodaapinaan gaye obikwak. Ezhi-ishpaangwa’aad biibaagi ji-enigok: “Anishinaabedog\! nimbikwak gibizikaagonaawaa\!”**

*“Down on the ground let us fight with each other\!” “No, right in this very place do I intend to kill you.” Then the man took his bow and arrow. As (the Bear) came climbing up, then in the centre of the chest was where he shot her, up as far as the feathers went the arrow. And almost did (the Bear) fall. Another arrow he took; when he shot her, clear on through he sent the arrow; and in a little while down fell (the Bear). And so, when in truth (the Bear) was dead, then down the tree he climbed. A heap of wood he then piled up; after he had picked up his arrows, he put on a great deal of fire-wood. After he had set fire to (the Bear), he looked, (and saw that) all the people of the town had been torn apart. He picked up his bow and arrow. As he shot into the air, he called aloud: “O ye people\! by my arrow will you be struck\!”*

“Mii zhaye’ii ji-gagamiigaadimin.” (“down on the ground let us fight each other” — \*mi\[ᵉ\]tcaya∙i∙ and kīgamīgätimin both flagged; superscript ambiguous\*). “Gaawiin, mii go omaa waa-dakwaninaan.” (“no, right here I mean to kill you” — \*wāta‘pinạninān flagged\*). Omitigwaabiin ezhi-odaapinaad gaye obikwak a’aw inini (the man took up his bow and his arrow). Biidentawenid naawakigan ezhi-bimwaad (as she came climbing up he shot her in the middle of the chest), gii-zaangaangesinini iw bikwak (the arrow sank up to the feathers — \*zaangaangesin- flagged\*). Gegaa go bangizhinoon (she nearly fell). Mii sa go igiw geget gii-ani-nibonid (and so when she had truly died) ezhi-niisaandawed (he climbed down). Mitigoon ezhi-okwaakwisitood (he piled up a heap of wood); gaa-odaapinang iniw obikwakoon (after picking up his arrows), niibiwa ogii-atoonan iniw misan (he heaped on much firewood). Gaa-izhi-zaka’waad inaabid (after he set fire to her he looked), oodena banaa go gaa-bookojishininid anishinaabe’ (the whole town of people lay torn apart). Omitigwaabiin odoodaapinaan gaye obikwak (he took up his bow and arrow). **Onishkaabi’idiwag; geget onishkaabi’idiwa’ anishinaabe’. Mii sa naasaab gaa-bimaadizini’ban, neyaab gii-mino-ayaanid. Inendam a’aw inini: “Mii maawiin iw gii-nisaagwen iniw noosan. *Up they quickly rose together; in truth, up quickly rose the people together. Thereupon back to the same life as before they came, back to the same state of well-being they returned. Thought the man: “It is possible that she may have slain my father. Therefore thither I will go.” In truth, he departed; when on his way he came in sight of the town, not a single person was there alive. When he looked towards the edge of the woods, some smoke was lifting (there). Then, going stealthily up to (the smoke), close by he lay down. And in a little while out of doors came the youngest of his sisters, covered all over with sores; and hardly could he make out (who she was), for the little girl, his younger sister, presented a dreadful sight. And unable to see was the little girl. “Would that over in this direction she would come\!” willed the man. Sure enough, in his direction came his little sister, working her way along.
