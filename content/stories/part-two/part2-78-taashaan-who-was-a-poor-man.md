*(TĀCĀⁿ KĀ‘KITIMĀGISIT ININI).*

**(TAASHAANH GAA-GIDIMAAGIZID ININI).**

(TĀSHĀⁿ), WHO WAS A POOR MAN.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk and/or Midāsugaⁿj (Series IV; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (“either … or both”; not credited on the story page).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Tācāⁿ ōsạn pimādisiwạn kayä īni‘ᵘ ugīn; nīciwaᵉ iᵉi‘ᵘ usayä̃ⁿyaᵉ. Mīsa‘ kägä‘t ā‘pidci kitimāgisiwag. Ki‘tigäwạg, nībiwa ki‘tigäwạg. Mīdạc ‘iᵉi‘ᵘ ayābi‘ta nībininig ā‘pitcigu mi‘kugininiwạn ini‘ᵘ ugi‘tigāniwān. A‘pidcigu minwäntạm aᵉa‘ᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzī mi‘kuginini‘k ini‘ᵘ ki‘tigānạn. Ningutingigu a‘pidci kigicäp owâbạndānạn ugi‘tigānạn, mīsa‘ iᵉi‘ᵘ kigimōtimint. “Awägwänigic äcictcigät?” inändạm. Kāwīn pimi‘kawäsiwạn. Täbi‘kạtinig ubāckisigan udōdā‘pinān; äji∙a∙kamawāt kämōtimigut. Cigwa pītābanini äjinibāt. Kwäckusit pabā‘inābi ugi‘tigāning; mīsa‘ mīnawā ki‘kimōtimint oki‘tigān. Intawä kiwä tibātcīmu‘tawāt iᵉi‘ᵘ unītcānisaᵉ: “Mīsa iᵉi‘ᵘ mīnawā kī‘kimōtimigōyạnk, intawā kini‘tamawā a‘kandō‘käg.”

**Taashaanh oosan bimaadiziwan gaye iniw ogiin; niizhiwa’ i’iw osayenhya’. Mii sa geget aapiji gidimaagiziwag. Gitigewag, niibiwa gitigewag. Mii dash i’iw ayaabita niibining aapiji go mikoginiwan iniw ogitiganiwaan. Aapiji go minwendam a’aw akiwenzii mikoginig iniw gitigaanan. Ningoding igo aapiji gigizheb owaabandaanan ogitigaanan, mii sa i’iw gii-gimoodimind. “Awegwenigizh ezhichiged?” inendam. Gaawiin bimikawesiwan. Dabi’adinig obaashkizigan odoodaapinaan; ezhi-akamawaad gemoodimigod. Zhigwa biidaabanini ezhi-nibaad. Gweshkozid babaa-inaabi ogitigaaning; mii sa miinawaa gii-gimoodimind ogitigaan. Indawaa giiwenh dibaajimotawaad iniw oniijaanisa’: “Mii sa i’iw miinawaa gii-gimoodimigooyang, indawaa ginitamawaa akandookeg.”**

*Tashan’s father was living, and so was his mother; two were his elder brothers. And now, in truth, they were very poor. They tilled the ground, and much they tilled. And so, when the summer was halfway gone, exceedingly fine did their fields look. And highly pleased was the old man, that the (crops of the) fields looked (so) promising. Now, very early one morning he looked at his fields, whereupon (he found that) he had been robbed. “Who in the world did it?” he thought. (He saw) no sign of any tracks. When night came on, he took up his gun; then he lay in wait for the one that had robbed him. The dawn was beginning to appear when he fell asleep. On waking from sleep, he wandered about his field, looking; and so again (he found that) he had been robbed (of things in) his field. Accordingly back home he went to tell the news to his children. “And now again we have been robbed, so therefore it is your turn to keep watch.”*

Taashaanh oosan bimaadiziwan (Tashan’s father was alive) gaye iniw ogiin (and his mother too); niizhiwa’ i’iw osayenhya’ (two were his elder brothers). Mii sa geget aapiji gidimaagiziwag (and so, truly, they were very poor). Gitigewag, niibiwa gitigewag (they farmed, much they farmed). Mii dash i’iw ayaabita niibining (and so when the summer was half over) aapiji go mikoginiwan iniw ogitigaaniwaan (their fields looked very fine — \*mikogin- “appear fine/promising,” stem flagged\*). Aapiji go minwendam a’aw akiwenzii (very pleased was the old man) mikoginig iniw gitigaanan (that the fields looked so promising). Ningoding igo aapiji gigizheb (then once, very early in the morning) owaabandaanan ogitigaanan (he looked at his fields), mii sa i’iw gii-gimoodimind (whereupon he had been robbed). “Awegwenigizh ezhichiged?” inendam (“who in the world did it?” he thought). Gaawiin bimikawesiwan (there was no track of anyone). Dabi’adinig obaashkizigan odoodaapinaan (when night fell he took up his gun); ezhi-akamawaad gemoodimigod (then he lay in wait for the one robbing him). Zhigwa biidaabanini ezhi-nibaad (when dawn was breaking he fell asleep). Gweshkozid babaa-inaabi ogitigaaning (on waking he looked about his field); mii sa miinawaa gii-gimoodimind ogitigaan (and again his field had been robbed). Indawaa giiwenh dibaajimotawaad iniw oniijaanisa’ (so, they say, he went home to tell his children the news): “Mii sa i’iw miinawaa gii-gimoodimigooyang (“and now again we have been robbed), indawaa ginitamawaa akandookeg.” (so it is your turn to keep watch.”)

*mi‘kugininiwạn / mi‘kuginini‘k — “the fields looked fine/promising,” a -gin- “appear, look” verb; stem and segmentation flagged (H/soft). nīciwaᵉ … usayä̃ⁿyaᵉ — the word-final superscript ᵉ marks plural/obviative; read niizhiwa’ … osayenhya’.*

(2) Kägä‘t änitibi‘kạtinig a‘i‘nini mādcā. Mīsa‘ nāsāb kayä win äcinibāt pātābaninig. Skwạtcīban ka‘kina igi‘ᵘ ininiwag mīsa‘ untcita nibāwāt ānu∙a∙‘kandōwāt.

**Geget ani-dibikadinig a’i inini maajaa. Mii sa naasaab gaye wiin ezhi-nibaad baadaabaning. Ishkwaajiban gakina igiw ininiwag mii sa onjida nibaawaad aanawi-akandoowaad.**

*In truth, when night came on, the man departed. And then in the same manner he also fell asleep at the coming of dawn. In due course of time all the men did the very same thing, falling asleep when they tried in vain to keep watch.*

Geget ani-dibikadinig a’i inini maajaa (truly, when night came on, the man set out). Mii sa naasaab gaye wiin ezhi-nibaad baadaabaning (and just the same, he too fell asleep at the coming of dawn). Ishkwaajiban gakina igiw ininiwag (in the end all the men — \*ishkwaaj- “finally/in turn,” flagged\*) mii sa onjida nibaawaad aanawi-akandoowaad (slept just so, though they tried in vain to keep watch).

(3) Mīdạc ä‘ta Tācāⁿ, kāwīn ma‘ci a‘kandōsi. Cigwa änitibi‘kạtinig mādcā awi∙a∙kantōt. Cigwa pīdābanim, kāwīn kanagä ugi‘kändansin tciwinibāt. Ninguting inābit awiya owâbamān pītcisänit, mīdạc iᵉi‘ᵘ äcipōnīnit imāⁿ ki‘tigāning. Äji∙i∙cāt päcu äcipāskiswāt. Pasigwa∙ō∙wan; äjipagitcwäbinang upāckisigan äjinō‘pinanāt. Kägä‘t kicī‘kā. Kumāgu a‘pi kanawâbamāt animisänit; intigu winipangicin äcinawāt. Kägä‘t änicipangicininit äjimādcīpa‘tōt, ki‘tci änigu‘k mādcība‘tō. Cigwa owâbandān kā∙i∙cipangicininit; kuniginin mīgu itu kā∙i∙cipō‘kiwäbickạminit iᵉi‘ a‘ki; mīgu ju kayä win äcipīndcikwāckwạnit. Kumāgu a‘pi papimagōtcink owâbandān tibickō wāsäyānig. Inābit animibisōwạn. “Kāwīn nintapōni∙ā∙sī,” inäntạm. Cigwa udäbābandān päjik iᵉi‘ᵘ a‘ki. Cigwa ändạci‘pạngicinowāt, mīgu imāⁿ ōdäna ayānig. Mīsa‘ äcita‘kunint imā ändạci∙a∙‘kakacä‘käng; mī∙i∙māⁿ ucictcigātänig iᵉi‘ᵘ a‘kạ‘kajä kī‘pạngicink. Mīsa kīmīntcima‘pinit.

**Mii dash eta Taashaanh, gaawiin mashi akandoosii. Zhigwa ani-dibikadinig maajaa awi-akandood. Zhigwa biidaabanim, gaawiin ganage ogikendanziin ji-wii-nibaad. Ningoding inaabid awiya owaabamaan biijisenid, mii dash i’iw ezhi-booniinid imaa gitigaaning. Ezhi-izhaad besho ezhi-baashkiswaad. Bazigwa’oowan; ezhi-bagijwebinang obaashkizigan ezhi-noopinanaad. Geget gizhiikaa. Gomaa go apii ganawaabamaad animisenid; indigo wii-nibangishin ezhinawaad. Geget ani-bangishininid ezhi-maajiibatood, gichi-enigok maajiibatoo. Zhigwa owaabandaan gaa-izhi-bangishininid; goniginiin mii go idi gaa-izhi-bookiwebishkaminid i’iw aki; mii go zhe gaye wiin ezhi-biinjikwaashkwanid. Gomaa go apii babimagoojing owaabandaan dibishkoo waaseyaanig. Inaabid animibisoowan. “Gaawiin nindaa-booni’aasii,” inendam. Zhigwa odebaabandaan bezhig i’iw aki. Zhigwa endazhi-bangishinowaad, mii go imaa oodena ayaanig. Mii sa ezhi-dakonind imaa endazhi-akakazhekeng; mii imaa ozhichigaadenig i’iw akakazhe gii-bangishing. Mii sa gii-miinjima’binid.**

*There was Tashan, he had not yet watched. When night came on, he departed to go keep watch. When the dawn came, he had no desire at all to sleep. By and by, while looking about, he saw something come flying into (the field), and then (saw it) alight in the field. On going up close to it, he shot at it. (He saw) it fly up; then, flinging away his gun, he chased after it. Truly with speed it went. Watching it for some time, (he beheld it) as it went flying away; as if it would fall was the way it seemed to him. In truth, when (he saw) it coming down to the ground, he started to run; with full speed he started running. In a while he saw where it descended; behold, it was a place where it had gone straight through the earth; accordingly then he too leaped in. While for some time he was dropping through space, he saw something that seemed like light. As he looked, (he saw something) falling through the air. “I would not give him up,” he thought. In a while he came in sight of another country. Now, at the place where they alighted, right there was a town. Thereupon he was made captive at a place where charcoal was being made; for at the place where charcoal was made he fell. And then he was bound with cords.*

Mii dash eta Taashaanh, gaawiin mashi akandoosii (and there was Taashaanh, he had not yet watched). Zhigwa ani-dibikadinig maajaa awi-akandood (when night fell he set out to go watch). Zhigwa biidaabanim, gaawiin ganage ogikendanziin ji-wii-nibaad (when dawn came he had no wish to sleep at all). Ningoding inaabid awiya owaabamaan biijisenid (once, looking about, he saw something come flying), mii dash i’iw ezhi-booniinid imaa gitigaaning (and it alighted there in the field). Ezhi-izhaad besho ezhi-baashkiswaad (going up close, he shot at it). Bazigwa’oowan (it flew up); ezhi-bagijwebinang obaashkizigan ezhi-noopinanaad (flinging away his gun, he chased it). Geget gizhiikaa (truly fast it went). Gomaa go apii ganawaabamaad animisenid (watching it a while as it flew off); indigo wii-nibangishin ezhinawaad (as if it would fall, it seemed to him). Geget ani-bangishininid ezhi-maajiibatood (truly, as it came down he started to run), gichi-enigok maajiibatoo (with all his might he ran). Zhigwa owaabandaan gaa-izhi-bangishininid (then he saw where it had come down); goniginiin mii go idi gaa-izhi-bookiwebishkaminid i’iw aki (behold, it had broken right through the earth there — \*bookiwebishk- flagged\*); mii go zhe gaye wiin ezhi-biinjikwaashkwanid (and so he too leaped in). Gomaa go apii babimagoojing (while he was dropping through space a while) owaabandaan dibishkoo waaseyaanig (he saw something like light). Inaabid animibisoowan (looking, something was floating/falling away). “Gaawiin nindaa-booni’aasii,” inendam (“I would not give him up,” he thought). Zhigwa odebaabandaan bezhig i’iw aki (then he came in sight of another land). Zhigwa endazhi-bangishinowaad, mii go imaa oodena ayaanig (where they came down, right there was a town). Mii sa ezhi-dakonind imaa endazhi-akakazhekeng (and so he was seized where charcoal was being made); mii imaa ozhichigaadenig i’iw akakazhe gii-bangishing (it was there where charcoal is made that he fell). Mii sa gii-miinjima’binid (and so he was bound with cords).

*kā∙i∙cipō‘kiwäbickạminit — “where it had broken/gone straight through the earth,” long fused verb, parse drafted (bookiwebishk-), flagged. animibisōwạn — “floating/falling through the air,” stem flagged. a‘kạ‘kajä / a‘kakacä‘käng — “charcoal / where charcoal is made,” akakazhe; preserved spelling.*

(4) Cigwa äjikạgwätcimint aᵉa‘ᵘ pinäsi kīpāskisunt. “Anicāgu ningīpāskisu‘k.” Mīdạc iᵉi‘ᵘ äjikạnōnint: “Kāwīn kanabạtc anicā kītābāskisugusī aᵉa‘ᵘ Tācāⁿ′.”

**Zhigwa ezhi-gagwejimind a’aw binesi gii-baashkizond. “Anishaa go ningii-baashkizog.” Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonind: “Gaawiin ganabaj anishaa gii-daa-baashkizogosii a’aw Taashaanh.”**

*In a while the bird was asked how he came to be shot at with a gun. “For no cause I was shot.” Thereupon he was told: “Not without cause, perhaps, would you have been shot at by Tāshāⁿ.” “Come, let him be sent for\!”*

Zhigwa ezhi-gagwejimind a’aw binesi gii-baashkizond (in a while the bird was asked how he came to be shot). “Anishaa go ningii-baashkizog.” (“for no reason I was shot.”) Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonind (thereupon he was told): “Gaawiin ganabaj anishaa (“not without cause, perhaps) gii-daa-baashkizogosii a’aw Taashaanh.” (would Taashaanh have shot at you.”)

*Tācāⁿ′ — the raised tick ′ after the name is Jones’s variant/footnote marker (see footnote ¹ at §7), not a glottal; preserved. This section’s closing English line “Come, let him be sent for\!” renders Ojibwe carried into the next sentence; here kept with §4 per Jones’s paragraph.*

(5) “Tạga, tanāsi‘kawā.” Cigwa kī‘pīndigäwạg äjikạgwätcimint: “Wägunän kā∙u∙ntcipāskiswạt waᵉa‘ᵘ pinäsi?”

**“Daga, da-naazikawaa.” Zhigwa gii-biindigewag ezhi-gagwejimind: “Wegonen gaa-onji-baashkiswad wa’aw binesi?”**

*“Come, let him be sent for\!” Presently in were they come, when he was asked: “For what reason did you shoot at the bird?”*

“Daga, da-naazikawaa.” (“come, let him be sought / sent for.”) Zhigwa gii-biindigewag (presently they came in) ezhi-gagwejimind (when he was asked): “Wegonen gaa-onji-baashkiswad wa’aw binesi?” (“why did you shoot at this bird?”)

*Section split note: Jones prints “Come, let him be sent for\!” at the foot of one English paragraph and “Presently in were they come …” as a new paragraph; the Ojibwe Tạga, tanāsi‘kawā opens the next Ojibwe sentence-group. Kept here for parallelism; nāsi‘kawā drafted da-naazikawaa “go to / fetch him,” flagged.*

(6) “Kā, nībiwa kimōti ki‘tigan, mīdạc iᵉi‘ᵘ kā∙u∙ntcipāskiswạk.”

**“Gaa, niibiwa gimoodi gitigaan, mii dash i’iw gaa-onji-baashkiswag.”**

*“Why, much he stole from the field, and for that reason I shot at him.”*

“Gaa, niibiwa gimoodi gitigaan (“why, much he stole from the field), mii dash i’iw gaa-onji-baashkiswag.” (and that is why I shot at him.”)

(7) Udigōn ugimān Tācāⁿ′: “Mī gwaya‘k kīpāskiswạt. Nōngum ano‘kātcigan kīgamīnigō kayä aᵉa‘ᵘ cōniyā.”

**Odigoon ogimaan Taashaanh: “Mii gwayak gii-baashkiswad. Noongom anokaajigan gigad-miinigoo gaye a’aw zhooniyaa.”**

*By the chief was Tasha” told: “It was right that you shot at him. This day shall you be given goods and silver.”*

Odigoon ogimaan Taashaanh (Taashaanh was told by the chief): “Mii gwayak gii-baashkiswad (“it was right that you shot at him). Noongom anokaajigan gigad-miinigoo (today you will be given goods) gaye a’aw zhooniyaa.” (and silver too.”)

(8) Mīsa cigwa; anic ā‘pidci kitimāgisi, pīguckāniwạn uta∙i∙īmạn. Äcigītcikunayä∙i∙nt, äjipīsi‘kamōnint ini‘ᵘ pīsikiganạn, ā‘pitci minwäntạm. Kägä‘t nībiwa mīnā ạno‘kātcigan kayä īni‘ᵘ cōniyān. Äjikạnōnint: “Tācāⁿ′, mī∙i∙‘ᵘ kīcpîn kacki‘tōyạn tcikiwäyạn icikiwän.”

**Mii sa zhigwa; aaniish aapiji gidimaagizi, biigoshkaaniwan oda’iiman. Ezhi-giijigonayenind, ezhi-biizikamonind iniw biizikiganan, aapiji minwendam. Geget niibiwa miinaa anokaajigan gaye iniw zhooniyaan. Ezhi-ganoonind: “Taashaanh, mii iw giishpin gashki’toyan ji-giiweyan izhi-giiwen.”**

*So thus it happened; for he was very poor, in tatters were his garments. When he was made to undress (and) was then arrayed in the clothes, very happy he was. Truly much was he given in goods and silver. Then he was addressed: “Tasha”, therefore, if you are now able to return home (by the way you came), you may go back.”*

Mii sa zhigwa (and so it now happened); aaniish aapiji gidimaagizi (for he was very poor), biigoshkaaniwan oda’iiman (in tatters were his clothes). Ezhi-giijigonayenind (when he was undressed), ezhi-biizikamonind iniw biizikiganan (he was dressed in the garments), aapiji minwendam (very glad was he). Geget niibiwa miinaa anokaajigan gaye iniw zhooniyaan (truly much was he given in goods and silver). Ezhi-ganoonind (then he was addressed): “Taashaanh, mii iw giishpin gashki’toyan ji-giiweyan (“Taashaanh, then, if you are able to return home) izhi-giiwen.” (go back so.”)

(9) Kägä‘t kiwä, mīyä‘ta ucōniyāmạn kāwäwināt. Kā‘tạgwicink ōsạn ändānit mīsa iⁿ ominạn ini‘ ō‘sạn ini‘ᵘ cōniyān. Cigwa uganōnigōn usayä̃ⁿyạn: “Tcīcāⁿ′,¹ kanabạtc kigīkimōtinạn aᵉa‘ᵘ cōniyā.” Mīsa‘ tibātcimut kīmīnint īni‘ᵘ cōniyān. Kāwäsa; mīsagōntcita iᵉi‘ᵘ nicki∙a∙t iᵉi‘ usayä̃ⁿya.

**Geget giiwe, mii eta ozhooniyaaman gaa-wewinaad. Gaa-dagwishing oosan endaanid mii iw ominaan iniw oosan iniw zhooniyaan. Zhigwa oganoonigoon osayenhyan: “Tciishaanh, ganabaj gigii-gimoodinan a’aw zhooniyaa.” Mii sa dibaajimod gii-miinind iniw zhooniyaan. Gaawesa; mii sa gonjida i’iw niishki’aad i’iw osayenhya.**

*To be sure, back he went, and only his silver he took home. When he was come at where his father dwelt, then he gave his father the silver. Presently he was addressed by his elder brother saying: “Tcīshaⁿ, perhaps you stole the silver.” Accordingly then he related how the silver had been given him. But (the story would) not (pass); furthermore, it was fated that he should anger his elder brothers.*

Geget giiwe (sure enough, he went home), mii eta ozhooniyaaman gaa-wewinaad (only his silver he carried home). Gaa-dagwishing oosan endaanid (when he came where his father lived) mii iw ominaan iniw oosan iniw zhooniyaan (he gave his father the silver). Zhigwa oganoonigoon osayenhyan (presently his elder brother addressed him): “Tciishaanh, ganabaj gigii-gimoodinan a’aw zhooniyaa.” (“Tciishaanh, perhaps you stole the silver.”) Mii sa dibaajimod gii-miinind iniw zhooniyaan (so he told how he had been given the silver). Gaawesa (but to no avail); mii sa gonjida i’iw niishki’aad i’iw osayenhya (and so he was fated to anger his elder brothers — \*niishki’- “anger someone,” direction flagged\*).

*Tcīcāⁿ′,¹ — the name printed here with the variant tick AND the footnote ¹ reference (see §7 footnote). Name variation (Tcīcāⁿ / Tācāⁿ / Tājāⁿ) is preserved, not normalized.*

(10) Wäyābạninig mädcāwaᵉ usaⁿyäyaᵉ, kabägījik inäntōwaᵉ. Mīnangwana kitciwāni‘kān kī∙ō∙ci‘tōnit. Kigīcäp, “Tācāⁿ,” utigōᵉ usayä̃ⁿyaᵉ, “mādcātā tciki∙ō∙säyạnk\!” Kumāgu a‘pi tägwuci, nuwāt owâbandān iⁿ ki‘tciwāni‘kān. Äji∙u∙‘ti‘tinint Tācāⁿ′, usayä̃ⁿyaᵉ udōti‘tinigō; äcipīndcwäbinigut iᵉi‘māⁿ wāni‘kāning. Kägä‘t unāwâbandān iᵉi‘ᵘ kā‘pạngicink; ānutā‘tạganābit, kägä‘t nāwi∙i∙nagwạtini. Pā‘kạtc icinīnkwa‘kạmigā∙i∙kätänig i∙i∙witi kā∙u∙ntcipīndcipisut. Mīsa panä mädwä∙i∙cimatcānit, ānuwi‘kwạtci∙o∙; äᵉ\! kāwäsa kacki∙u∙sī. Mīdạc iwiti tibātcimowāt, īni∙ō∙siwān tibātcimu‘tawāwāt: “Mīsā‘pan aᵉa‘ᵘ nicīmänān kiwani∙a∙ngit.”

**Wayaabaninig maajaawa’ osayenhya’, gabe-giizhig inendoowa’. Miinangwana gichi-waanikaan gii-oozhitoonid. Gigizheb, “Taashaanh,” odigoo’ osayenhya’, “maajaadaa ji-giiwosayang\!” Gomaa go apii dagwishin, \[a\]nawaad owaabandaan iw gichi-waanikaan.
