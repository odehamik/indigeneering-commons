**(AYAASE.)**

FILCHER-OF-MEAT.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Aᵘ Āyāsä nijō‘kwäwä; päjikowạn ogwisạn kāwīn wīdigäsiwạn kwaya‘k ijiwäbisiwạn. Ki‘tci∙ō∙däna udibändānāwa, mī nīciwāt ugimāwiwāt.

**Aw Ayaase niizho’kwewe; bezhigowan ogwisan gaawiin wiidigesiwan gwayak izhiwebizimwan. Gichi-oodena odibendaanaawaa, mii niizhiwaad ogimaawiwaad.**

*Filcher-of-Meat had two wives; he had one son who was not married, (and) who was leading an upright life. Over a large town they ruled, for both of them were chiefs.*

Aw Ayaase (Filcher-of-Meat) niizho’kwewe (had two wives); bezhigowan ogwisan (he had one son) gaawiin wiidigesiwan (who was not married) gwayak izhiwebizimwan (who lived rightly — \*upright-life stem flagged\*). Gichi-oodena odibendaanaawaa (a great town they ruled), mii niizhiwaad ogimaawiwaad (for both of them were chiefs).

*Foot ¹ (p380): “That is, never transgressing in anything that would get him in ill grace with the manitous.” Attaches to kwaya‘k ijiwäbisiwạn (“leading an upright life”). — Title printed English-first with Ojibwe parenthetical subtitle (Áyâsä); titleJones taken from that paren.*

(2) Ningutingigu kägä‘t inändạm Āyāsä wī∙ạ∙ndawābạtạmuwāt wāwạnūn kītcisāga∙i∙gạning imāⁿ ändāwāt; nāwạgām tạgwạnini i∙i∙wä ki‘tciminisābi‘k. Kigicäb sāga∙ạ∙m Āyāsä äjipīpāgit: “Mīsa cigwa tci∙ạ∙ntawâbantamạnk ini‘ᵘ wâwạnūn\!” Cigwa dạc udạbwi kā∙u∙dā‘pinạng kīpipīndigäwạn īni‘ wīwạn pigīgitōwạn: “A‘kawä awipäckisu‘k pinä.”

**Ningoding igo geget inendam Ayaase wii-andawaabandamowaad waawanoon gichi-zaaga’iganing imaa endaawaad; naawagaam dagwanini i’iwe gichi-minisaabik. Gizhiseb zaaga’am Ayaase ezhi-biibaagid: “Mii sa zhigwa ji-andawaabandamang iniw waawanoon\!” Zhigwa dash odabwi gaa-odaapinang gii-bi-biindigewan iniw wiiwan bi-giigidowan: “Akawe awi-baashkizo’k bine.”**

*Now, once truly thought Filcher-of-Meat that he would go look for eggs at a great lake where they lived; far out on the water was a great island of rock. In the morning out of doors went Filcher-of-Meat, when he then cried aloud: “It is now time that we go look for the eggs\!” And when he took up his paddle, in entered his wives, who came saying: “Before you depart, go shoot a ruffed grouse.”*

Ningoding igo geget inendam Ayaase (once truly Filcher-of-Meat thought) wii-andawaabandamowaad waawanoon (that they would go look for eggs) gichi-zaaga’iganing imaa endaawaad (at the great lake where they lived); naawagaam dagwanini i’iwe gichi-minisaabik (far out on the water lay a great rocky island). Gizhiseb zaaga’am Ayaase (in the morning Filcher-of-Meat went out of doors) ezhi-biibaagid (then he called aloud): “Mii sa zhigwa ji-andawaabandamang iniw waawanoon\!” (“now it is time we go look for the eggs\!”) Zhigwa dash odabwi gaa-odaapinang (and when he took up his paddle) gii-bi-biindigewan iniw wiiwan (his wives came in) bi-giigidowan (saying): “Akawe awi-baashkizo’k bine.” (“first go shoot a ruffed grouse.”)

(3) Äjikạnōnāt īniyōgwisạn: “Ningwisis, a‘kawä awipāskisᵘ aᵘ binä.”

**Ezhi-ganoonaad iniw ogwisan: “Ningwizis, akawe awi-baashkizo aw bine.”**

*Then he spoke to his son, saying: “My dear son, before you depart, go shoot the ruffed grouse.”*

Ezhi-ganoonaad iniw ogwisan (then he spoke to his son): “Ningwizis, akawe awi-baashkizo aw bine.” (“my son, first go shoot that ruffed grouse.”)

(4) Kā∙u∙dā‘pinạng a∙ī∙nini ‘iˢi’ᵘ pāskisigạn, “Ānīndi dạc ạyāt?” “Iwiti,” utigōn īniᵘ unucäyạn.

**Gaa-odaapinang a’aw inini i’iw baashkizigan, “Aaniindi dash ayaad?” “Iwidi,” odigoon iniw onozhayan.**

*When the man took up his gun, “And where is it?” (he said). “At yonder place,” he was told by his step-mother.*

(7) Kägä‘t ugiwâbamān nạmạdabinit ini‘ᵘ pinäwạn, mīsa imāⁿ kīpāckiswāt. Käcitinä änicigīwäpa‘tōt aˢawininī; utạnigạnōnan ini‘yōsan: “Mii cigwa tci∙a∙nimādcāyank, kinạgata‘u∙gōmin.” Mīnangwana iⁿ nicki∙ā∙t īni∙ō∙san. Mīdac iˢi’ᵘ äjipōsiwāt äjimādcīkwaciwäwāt. Mīgu iwiti a‘pi tägwicinuwāt iⁿ ki‘tciminisābi‘k, mīgu iˢi’ᵘ anind pimädcānit iˢi’ᵘ anicinābä. Änicigābaba‘tōwāt; käcitinä omōjiginānāwān ini‘ᵘ wâwanōn, kayāckwāwanōn. Aja ka‘kina mādcāwa anicināba anikiwänit. Mīdac iˢi’ᵘ uganōnigōn ōsan:

**Geget ogii-waabamaan namadabinid iniw binewan, mii sa imaa gii-baashkizwaad. Gezhitine ani-zhi-giiwebatood a’aw inini; oda-ani-ganoonan iniw osan: “Mii zhigwa ji-ani-maajaayang, gi-nagada’ogoomin.” Miinangwana i’iw nishki’aad iniw osan. Mii dash i’iw ezhi-booziwaad ezhi-maajii-gwaazhiwewaad. Mii go iwidi apii dagwishinowaad i’iw gichi-minisaabik, mii go i’iw anind bimaajaanid i’iw anishinaabe. Ani-zhi-gaababatoowaad; gezhitine omoozhiginaanaawaan iniw waawanoon, gayaashkwaawanoon. Azha gakina maajaawa anishinaabe ani-giiwenid. Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonigod osan:**

*Sure enough, he saw the ruffed grouse seated (there), whereupon there he shot it. Straightway back home went the man running. Immediately he spoke to his father, saying: “It is now time that we were starting on our way, (for) we are left behind (by the other canoes).” Now, as a matter of fact, he had angered his father. And when they got into (their canoe), they then went paddling away. Accordingly, when at yonder big rocky island they were come, already then were some of the people coming away. When they went ashore, away they ran; at once they went to gathering the eggs, gull-eggs. Already had all the people started on their homeward way. Accordingly he was addressed by his father saying:*

Geget ogii-waabamaan namadabinid iniw binewan (sure enough he saw the ruffed grouse sitting), mii sa imaa gii-baashkizwaad (and there he shot it). Gezhitine ani-zhi-giiwebatood a’aw inini (straightway home the man ran); oda-ani-ganoonan iniw osan (he spoke to his father): “Mii zhigwa ji-ani-maajaayang, gi-nagada’ogoomin.” (“now it is time we set out, we are being left behind.”) Miinangwana i’iw nishki’aad iniw osan (as it happened he had angered his father). Mii dash i’iw ezhi-booziwaad ezhi-maajii-gwaazhiwewaad (then they got in and paddled off). Mii go iwidi apii dagwishinowaad i’iw gichi-minisaabik (and when they reached the big rocky island) mii go i’iw anind bimaajaanid i’iw anishinaabe (already some of the people were leaving). Ani-zhi-gaababatoowaad (they ran ashore); gezhitine omoozhiginaanaawaan iniw waawanoon, gayaashkwaawanoon (quickly they gathered the eggs, gull-eggs). Azha gakina maajaawa anishinaabe ani-giiwenid (already all the people had set off home). Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonigod osan (then his father spoke to him):

*kīpāckiswāt / awipäckisu‘k / baashkizo — the shoot verb. nạgata‘u∙gōmin / gi-nagada’ogoomin ‘we are left behind (by canoe)’, stem flagged. The English foot ² (p381) explains the step-mother’s motive; it sits on the English page only, recorded in the flags file.*

(8) “Tạga, ā‘pitci wänicicingin ini‘ᵘ wâwạnōn kā∙o∙‘kusitōyānin, awimamōn.”

**“Daga, aapiji wenizhishingin iniw waawanoon gaa-o’ozhitooyaanin, awi-mamoon.”**

*saying: “Now, there are some very nice eggs that I have gathered in a pile, them do you go fetch.”*

“Daga, aapiji wenizhishingin iniw waawanoon (“now, the very nice eggs) gaa-o’ozhitooyaanin (that I have piled up), awi-mamoon.” (go fetch them.”) — \*o’ozhitoo- ‘pile up / make ready’ flagged\*

(9) Mīdạc iˢi’ᵘ utānapwāmigūn īni∙ō∙sạn; änīcikunga‘kī∙u∙t. Mīnawā äcinātạgāmiwäpa∙ạ∙minit. “Mānū, ningwisis\! nāsi‘kạn ini‘ wâwạnūn.”

**Mii dash i’iw odaanawaa’amigoon iniw osan; ani-zhi-gondaki’od. Miinawaa ezhi-naadagaamiwepa’aminid. “Maanoo, ningwizis\! naazikan iniw waawanoon.”**

*Whereupon he was unwilling to go when asked by his father; then away from the shore he pushed the canoe (with his paddle). Back again to the shore did (his father) shove (the canoe). “Please, O my little son\! do go get the eggs.”*

Mii dash i’iw odaanawaa’amigoon iniw osan (whereupon he refused when his father asked); ani-zhi-gondaki’od (then he pushed the canoe off from shore — \*stem flagged\*). Miinawaa ezhi-naadagaamiwepa’aminid (again his father shoved it back toward shore). “Maanoo, ningwizis\! naazikan iniw waawanoon.” (“please, my son\! go to the eggs.”)

*änīcikunga‘kī∙u∙t / utānapwāmigūn — ‘he refused; he pushed the canoe off’; both stems flagged. nātạgāmiwäpa‘aminit ‘shove it ashore’, flagged.*

(10) Äcikwāckunit a∙i∙nini, mādcība‘tō. Kä∙i∙citinä udawimamōnạn īni‘ᵘ wâwạnūn. Änisāgābikiba‘tōt ugīwâbạmān ōsạn āja micawạgām. Ānīc äjipīpāgimāt: “Anin wīn wändcinạgata∙o∙yạn?” Mēdạc iˢi’ᵘ udạbwīni äjigwā‘kwābạbwīyānigut. “Kī‘nwänj kīwâwīwinin.”

**Ezhi-gwaashkonid a’aw inini, maajiibatoo. Gezhitine oda-awi-mamoonan iniw waawanoon. Ani-zaagaabikibatood ogii-waabamaan osan azha mishawagaam. Aaniish ezhi-biibaagimaad: “Aaniin wiin wenji-nagada’oyan?” Mii dash i’iw odabwiin ezhi-gwekwaababwiiyaanigod. “Giinwenzh gii-waawiiwinin.”**

*When out leaped the man, he started on a run. Immediately went he to gathering up the eggs. When he ran out into open view (of the lake), he saw that his father was already far out on the water. Then he called aloud to him: “Why are you leaving me behind?” Whereupon then with the paddle was water splashed at him (by his father). “For a long while have I been calling you by name.”*

Ezhi-gwaashkonid a’aw inini, maajiibatoo (when the man leaped out, he ran). Gezhitine oda-awi-mamoonan iniw waawanoon (quickly he went to gather the eggs). Ani-zaagaabikibatood (when he ran out into open view) ogii-waabamaan osan azha mishawagaam (he saw his father already far out on the water). Aaniish ezhi-biibaagimaad (then he called aloud to him): “Aaniin wiin wenji-nagada’oyan?” (“why are you leaving me behind?”) Mii dash i’iw odabwiin ezhi-gwekwaababwiiyaanigod (whereupon with the paddle water was splashed back at him). “Giinwenzh gii-waawiiwinin.” (“for a long while I have been calling your name.”)

*gwā‘kwābạbwīyānigut / gwekwaababwii- ‘splash water with the paddle’, fused verb, flagged.*

(11) Äᵉ, mīsā‘pạnä, kīnạgata‘u∙nt. Intāwā äjimādci‘tāt ạsinīˢ äcikiwi‘tayapi‘kicimāt. Mīsagunā iᵘ wāwīgiwāmit mīdāsugun kā∙a∙yāt imāⁿ minisābi‘kunk, kāwin kägō omītcisīn. Ānīc kāwin ninkutci pima‘kạmigāsinini. Kigīcabitạc ā‘pitci mīca‘kwatini. Äjikiwitāckạnk ningutingigu ogīwâbaman awiya tcä‘kicininit. Äjināⁿzi‘kawāt, kuniginīn miciginäbigōn. Kägä‘t minditōwạn. Mēdạc iˢi’ᵘ äjikạnōnāt: “Nimicō, tạga āja∙ō∙cin\!”

**Ee, mii sa pane, gii-nagada’ond. Indawaa ezhi-maajiitaad asiniin ezhi-giiwita-ayaabiki-zhimaad. Mii sa gonaa iw waa-wiigiwaamid midaaso-gon gaa-ayaad imaa minisaabikong, gaawiin gegoo omiijisiin. Aaniish gaawiin ningoji bima’kamigaasinini. Gizhiseb idash aapiji mizhaakwadini. Ezhi-giiwitaashkang ningoding igo ogii-waabamaan awiya jiigishininid. Ezhi-naazikawaad, goniginiin mishiginebigoon. Geget minditoowan. Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonaad: “Nimishoo, daga aazhawa’oozhin\!”**

*Well, now was (his father) gone, he was left behind. Therefore he set to work piling up the stones (and) laying them in a circle; that was going to be his wigwam. While he was ten days at the rocky island, nothing did he have to eat. Now, nowhere was the shore line in view. And in the morning very clear was the sky. While going round the (island), he suddenly saw some creature upon the shore. On going up to it, behold, it was a big Serpent. Truly big it was. Accordingly then he spoke to it, saying: “O my grandfather\! do take me across the water\!”*

Ee, mii sa pane, gii-nagada’ond (well, now he was left behind). Indawaa ezhi-maajiitaad asiniin (so he set to work with the stones) ezhi-giiwita-ayaabiki-zhimaad (laying them in a circle); mii sa gonaa iw waa-wiigiwaamid (that was to be his wigwam). midaaso-gon gaa-ayaad imaa minisaabikong (ten days he was at the rocky island), gaawiin gegoo omiijisiin (nothing did he have to eat). Aaniish gaawiin ningoji bima’kamigaasinini (nowhere was the shore in sight). Gizhiseb idash aapiji mizhaakwadini (and in the morning the sky was very clear). Ezhi-giiwitaashkang (while going round the island) ningoding igo ogii-waabamaan awiya jiigishininid (suddenly he saw someone lying at the shore). Ezhi-naazikawaad, goniginiin mishiginebigoon (when he went up to it — behold, a great Serpent\!). Geget minditoowan (truly it was big). Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonaad (then he spoke to it): “Nimishoo, daga aazhawa’oozhin\!” (“my grandfather, do take me across the water\!”)

*Äᵉ ‘well/yes’ — interjection, carried. asini-/ayaabiki-zhimaa- ‘lay stones in a circle’, fused, flagged. aazhawa’oozhin ‘take me across by boat/back’ stem flagged.*

**“Aw, noozhis\! Nandawaabam asin zhaayaagawaabikizid ji-nisi-biwebinadigo.”**

(13) Cigwasa omi‘kawān. Äjiwâbanda∙ā∙t, “Äᵘ, ōnagōsîn imā niⁿkwäganānk.” Kägä‘t äci∙ō∙nagōsit imā ukwäkanāning. “Nōcis, mi icapa‘ki‘tä∙u∙cin ‘aˢa’ᵘ gitasinīm änigu‘k.” Äjipagi‘täwāt, ā‘tā, ki‘tcitatā‘tabīnit, änigu‘k mādcānit. Ayābi‘tawagam oganōnigōn. “Nōcis, windamawicin pīyāna‘kwato‘kāg. Ā‘pitci ningusāg īgi’ᵘ animi‘kīg; mī∙i∙’ᵘ ijipagi‘tä∙u∙cin.”

**Zhigwa sa omikawaan. Ezhi-waabanda’aad, “Aw, oonagoozhin imaa ninkwegananaang.” Geget ezhi-oonagoozhid imaa okwegananing. “Noozhis, mii izhi-bakite’oozhin a’aw gidasiniim enigok.” Ezhi-bagite’waad, aataa, gichi-dadaadabiinid, enigok maajaanid. *In a while he found one. On showing it to (the Serpent), “All right\! Mount upon my neck.” Truly then got he upon its neck. “My grandson, now strike me a hard blow with your stone.” When he struck (the Serpent), ah, the speed (with which) it moved when it travelled through the water, with full speed it went. Part way out upon the water, he was addressed by it saying: “My grandson, let me know when a cloud is coming up. Very much am I in fear of the Thunderers; then do you hit me.”*

Zhigwa sa omikawaan (in a while he found one). Ezhi-waabanda’aad (on showing it to the Serpent), “Aw, oonagoozhin imaa ninkwegananaang.” (“all right, mount upon my neck.”) Geget ezhi-oonagoozhid imaa okwegananing (truly he got up onto its neck). “Noozhis, mii izhi-bakite’oozhin a’aw gidasiniim enigok.” (“my grandson, now strike me hard with your stone.”) Ezhi-bagite’waad, aataa (when he struck it — ah\!), gichi-dadaadabiinid (how fast it sped), enigok maajaanid (with full speed it went). Ayaabita-wagaam oganoonigoon (part way out it spoke to him): “Noozhis, windamawishin bi-aanakwadokaag (“my grandson, tell me when a cloud comes up). Mīndcimigu päcwâbandamuwāt i∙i∙ya‘ki cigwa’ onōtawāˢ ‘iˢi’ᵘ animi‘kī. Cigwa anagwāsäwan, äjigwāckunit aˢawinini. Pitcīnagigu kā‘kwāckunit āca nawatinimān ūmicōmisan. Inābit icpiming kī∙a∙nibabiskibiwan; äciki‘tcimawit. Intawa utawipāpagunan iⁿ wīgwās; ä‘kubīgatinig äji∙a∙‘tōd; äjinanāmadapit. Wī‘kā äwi∙i∙ci∙i∙nābit, ābidink dac pangi‘kānig miskwi kī∙a∙‘täni. Mīnawā äwi∙i∙ci∙u∙nābit, kabäya∙i∙dac namatabi. Mīsa äwici∙i∙nābit mīnāwā, kāwīn dac kägō kīpangi‘käsinini. Mīsa iˢi’ᵘ undcita abiding ä‘ta kīpangi‘kānik. Intāwā äcimamōt, äcikackackwämaginang. Intawā äji∙u∙ci‘tōt imāⁿsa wīnibāt.

**Ezhi-bagite’waad. Gwetchi go go ji-mishagaamebizowaad owaabandaan a’aw inini bi-aanakwadowaninig. “Gaa maawiin nind-andawiidamawaasii,” inendam. Minjimi go beshwaabandamowaad i’iw aki zhigwa onoodawaan iniw animikiin. Zhigwa anaakwadiwan, ezhi-gwaashkonid a’aw inini. Bijiinaag igo gaa-gwaashkonid azha nawadinimaan omishoomisan. Inaabid ishpiming gii-ani-babiiskibiiwan; ezhi-gichi-mawid. Indawaa oda-awi-baabaagonan iw wiigwaas; ekobiigadinig ezhi-atood; ezhi-nanaamadabid. Wiikaa ewi-izhi-inaabid, aabiding dash bangii gaanig miskwi gii-atenig. Miinawaa ewi-izhi-onaabid, gabeyaa’ii dash namadabi. Mii sa ewizhi-inaabid miinawaa, gaawiin dash gegoo gii-bangiigaasinini. Mii sa i’iw onjida aabiding eta gii-bangiigaanig. Indawaa ezhi-mamood, ezhi-gashkashkwemaginang. Indawaa ezhi-oozhitood imaa sa wii-nibaad.**

*Then he hit (the Serpent). Just as they were arriving at the shore, the man saw a cloud coming up. “I doubt if I shall need to tell it,” he thought. And just as they were drawing into easy view of the land, then he heard the Thunderers. When it slowly drew up to shore, then off leaped the man. The moment that he jumped, then already was his grandfather seized. On looking up (into the air), there went (the Serpent) wriggling; then he began to cry. Accordingly went he and pulled off some birch-bark; by the edge of the water he placed it; then he sat down. When later he went to look, there was a drop of blood. Again he went and sat down, and a long while he sat. And then he went to look again, but not a single drop fell. And so for a purpose but a single drop had fallen. Accordingly, when he picked it up, he then wrapped it in the bark. Then he prepared a place where he intended to sleep.*
