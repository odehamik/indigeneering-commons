THE ROLLING SKULL.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ạnicinābäg a∙i∙ndāwạg, inini kayä wīwạn kayä päcig kwīwisäns. Cigwadạc tạgwāginini; kīyusä mōcạg a∙i∙nini. Ningutingigu omayạgänimān īni‘ᵘ wīwạn; kāwīn mạnisäsīwạn. Mīgu∙i∙‘ᵘ pitcīnạg ānubimạnisät. Ningutingigu kā∙ạ∙nimādcāt a∙i∙nini māminunäntam aᵘ gwīwisäns; pitcīnạg mīgu‘ku animādcānitcini īniyōsạn mī cigwa ki‘tciwāwäci∙u∙nit ini‘ᵘ ugīn, mīsa‘ kạbägīcig untäntōwạn īniyōgīn. “Nạmạntcigicsa änano‘kīgwän aᵍa‘ᵘ nīnga\!” inändạm. Patạgwicininit, käcitinä kītci‘kunayäwạn. Cigwa mīnawā tạgwicin a∙i∙nini kik‘īyusät, mīsa untcita kāwīn kägō mici‘. Käya‘pī ugạnōnān ini‘ᵘ wīwạn: “Anīn änano‘kīyạn kayä kīn mạnisäsīwạn?”

**Anishinaabeg a-indaawag, inini gaye wiiwan gaye bezhig gwiiwizens. Zhigwa dash dagwaagini; giiyose moozhag a-inini. Ningoding igo omayaagenimaan iniw wiiwan; gaawiin manisesiiwan. Mii go iw bijinaag aanawi-bi-manised. Ningoding gaa-ani-maajaad a-inini maaminonendam aw gwiiwizens; bijinaag mii go go ani-maajaanijin iniw oosan mii zhigwa gichi-waawezhi’onid iniw ogiin, mii sa gabe-giizhig ondendowan iniw oogiin. “Namanj igosa enanokiigwen a’aw ninga\!” inendam. Ba-dagwishininid, gezhitine gichi-gonayewan. Zhigwa miinawaa dagwishin a-inini gii-giiyosed, mii sa onjida gaawiin gegoo mizhi’. Geyaabi oganoonaan iniw wiiwan: “Aaniin enanokiiyan gaye giin manisesiiwan?”**

*Some people were living (there), a man and his wife and one boy; and one autumn was coming on; then on the hunt always was the man. Now, in course of time he perceived a strange behavior in his wife; she gathered no fire-wood. Accordingly, whenever (he came home), against his will would he go after the fire-wood. And once, after the man had gone away, the boy got to thinking of things; for always, as soon as his father was gone, would his mother then get into gay attire, whereupon all day long would she then be absent from home. “Wonder what my mother is up to\!” he thought. When she returned, straightway would she remove her (gay) attire. Now, another time back came the man from a hunt for game, and still there was no fire-wood. At last he spoke to his wife, saying: “And what are you so busied with, that you do not gather any fire-wood?”*

Anishinaabeg a-indaawag (some people were living there), inini gaye wiiwan gaye bezhig gwiiwizens (a man and his wife and one boy). Zhigwa dash dagwaagini (and now autumn was coming on); giiyose moozhag a-inini (always on the hunt was the man). Ningoding igo omayaagenimaan iniw wiiwan (now in time he found a strangeness in his wife — \*stem flagged\*); gaawiin manisesiiwan (she gathered no firewood). Mii go iw bijinaag aanawi-bi-manised (so then at last, against his will, he himself would go for firewood — \*flagged\*). Ningoding gaa-ani-maajaad a-inini (and once when the man had gone off) maaminonendam aw gwiiwizens (the boy fell to pondering); bijinaag mii go go ani-maajaanijin iniw oosan (for as soon as his father went off) mii zhigwa gichi-waawezhi’onid iniw ogiin (then his mother would dress herself up grandly), mii sa gabe-giizhig ondendowan iniw oogiin (and so all day long his mother would be away). “Namanj igosa enanokiigwen a’aw ninga\!” (“I wonder what my mother is up to\!”) inendam (he thought). Ba-dagwishininid (when she came home), gezhitine gichi-gonayewan (quickly she would take off her fine clothes). Zhigwa miinawaa dagwishin a-inini gii-giiyosed (and now again the man came home from hunting), mii sa onjida gaawiin gegoo mizhi’ (and there was on purpose nothing — no firewood — \*stem flagged\*). Geyaabi oganoonaan iniw wiiwan (at last he spoke to his wife): “Aaniin enanokiiyan gaye giin manisesiiwan?” (“what are you so busy at that you gather no firewood?”). Note: gwiiwizens here printed with g-; cf. kwīwisäns elsewhere — both preserved.

After she had come forth, then away she started. Then, following after her, he secretly stole upon her. When some distance on their way they were come, lo, up to a big tree went the woman. Now, she had an axe in her hand; as she struck the tree, “Your old woman has come,” she said.*

*It happened to be a serpent-tree; and forthwith out proceeded a host of serpents, so many (that) not at all could his wife be seen. To be sure, angry was the man. Bullets two (in number) he quickly put into his gun. When closer up he went, even closer still, why, the place was alive with serpents; not visible was his wife when he shot at them. Of a truth, many he killed; and as many of the snakes as he did not kill, the same hastened quickly back into the tree. Then, flinging away his gun, he rushed for his wife. While his wife was yet struggling to rise to her feet, he then dealt her a blow with the axe. He severed the (head from her) neck. After he had severed the (head from her) neck, in motion still was her head. “Wonder what I shall do with it\!” he thought. He grabbed it, then he tied it fast to a tree. After tying it fast (to the tree), he started off on a run, to where he lived he ran; a net he went to get. Back again he came running, he went to where her head was. On coming in sight of it, (he saw) that it already had gotten loose. He grabbed it, whereupon then in the net he rolled it. When he tied it fast (to a tree), even yet of its own accord did it keep moving. Accordingly he began chopping down the serpent-tree. After the tree had been felled, he began chopping it; as he began cutting (the tree) up into billets, he then went on to chopping in pieces the serpents. Accordingly all of them he killed.*

*He started away on the run, to where he (and the others) dwelt he ran, passing speedily inside. Even yet was his beloved son there. *“Mī∙i∙‘ᵘ pinäwitcīt kī∙ạ∙gōtä.” — the FULL-FORM binewi-jiid closing (mii iw binewi-jiid gii-agoode, the hanging-verb on the page), with the ’mii iw’ demonstrative; cf. the full forms \#45/\#72/\#73/\#74 (kī∙ạ∙gōtä), \#20 gizzard, \#71 (-nig).
