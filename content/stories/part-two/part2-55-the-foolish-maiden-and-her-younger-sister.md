**MAJI-IKWEWIS MIINAWAA OSHIIMEYAN.**

THE FOOLISH MAIDEN AND HER YOUNGER SISTER.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Mīsa mī a∙ī∙ntāwag i‘kwäwạg, nīciwạg; pācik ạnimōcạn udayāwāwạn, mīsa mī imi‘ᵘ näntawäntcigätamāguwāt. Ci‘gwadạc wī‘pipōnini, mīsa kiⁿwäⁿ mämäckwat wītciwāwāt. Ningutingidạc unisāni īni‘ᵘ wâwâckäciwạn. Kägä‘t kạbäya∙ī∙ udamwāwān. Abā‘pic cigwa kätạmwāwāt, mīsa mī mīnawā mādci‘tāwāt nạntawäntcigäwāt; mämäckwat wītcīwâwāt. Ninguting unisāwān mīnawa pācik īni‘ᵘ wâwâckäciwạn, ā‘pidci wininōwạn. Mīdạc i∙i∙‘ᵘ täyābi‘tapibōninig tcibwākitạmwāwāt; cigwa kā‘kitạmwāwāt kāwīn ni‘tägäsīwạn udayayänsiwān. “Intawā mādcātā\!” i‘kitowạg. Äcikusiwät sāga∙i∙gạning, mätābīwāt; micawi‘kwạm tägwucinuwāt, uwâbamāwaᵉ awiya mawinạnigōwāt. Kuniginīn, umawinanigōwān. Mīdạc äjimādciyạnk Matci‘ki‘kwäwis:—

**Mii sa mii ayi-indaawag ikwewag, niizhiwag; bezhig animoshan odayaawaawan, mii sa mii iniw nandawenjigetamaagowaad. Zhigwa dash wii-biboonini, mii sa giiwenh memeshkwad wiijiiwaawaad. Ningoding idash onisaani iniw waawaashkeshiwan. Geget gabeya’ii odamwaawaan. Abaapizh zhigwa gaa-damwaawaad, mii sa mii miinawaa maajii’taawaad nandawenjigewaad; memeshkwad wiijiiwaawaad. Ningoding onisaawaan miinawaa bezhig iniw waawaashkeshiwan, aapiji wiininowan. Mii dash i’iw dayaabi’-dabibooninig jibwaa-damwaawaad; zhigwa gaa-damwaawaad gaawiin ni’tagesiiwan odayayaansiwaan. “Indawaa maajaadaa\!” ikidowag. Ezhi-gozhiwed zaaga’iganing, madaabiiwaad; mishawikwam dagwishinowaad, owaabamaawaa’ awiya mawinanigowaad. Goniginiin, omawinanigowaan. Mii dash ezhi-maajiyang Maji-ikwewis:—**

*And so there lived some women,¹ they were two in number; a dog they had, and by it did they have their hunting done. It was now growing winter, whereupon it is said they took turns in going along with (the dog). And once it killed a deer. In fact, for a long while they had it to eat. In due course of time they ate it up, whereupon they made ready again to go hunting for game; they took turns going along with the dog. By and by they killed another deer, very fat it was. It was halfway along through the winter before they ate it up; when they had eaten it all, no (more) killing did their pet do. “Therefore let us go away\!” they said. When they moved camp, out upon a lake they came; when far out upon the ice they were come, they saw that by some one they were pursued. Behold, they were being attacked. And thus the Foolish Maiden then began singing:—*

Mii sa mii ayi-indaawag ikwewag (and so there lived some women), niizhiwag (they were two); bezhig animoshan odayaawaawan (a dog they had), mii sa mii iniw nandawenjigetamaagowaad (and by it their hunting was done — \*stem flagged\*). Zhigwa dash wii-biboonini (it was now growing winter), mii sa giiwenh memeshkwad wiijiiwaawaad (whereupon, they say, they took turns going with it — \*memeshkwad ‘in turns’ flagged\*). Ningoding idash onisaani iniw waawaashkeshiwan (and once it killed a deer). Geget gabeya’ii odamwaawaan (for a long while they had it to eat). Abaapizh zhigwa gaa-damwaawaad (in due course, when they had eaten it up), mii sa mii miinawaa maajii’taawaad nandawenjigewaad (they made ready again to go hunting); memeshkwad wiijiiwaawaad (taking turns going with it). Ningoding onisaawaan miinawaa bezhig iniw waawaashkeshiwan (by and by they killed another deer), aapiji wiininowan (very fat it was). Mii dash i’iw dayaabi’-dabibooninig jibwaa-damwaawaad (it was halfway through the winter before they ate it up — \*dayaabi’-dabiboon- ‘mid-winter’ flagged\*); zhigwa gaa-damwaawaad gaawiin ni’tagesiiwan odayayaansiwaan (when they had eaten it all, no more killing did their pet do — \*ni’tagesii- flagged\*). “Indawaa maajaadaa\!” ikidowag (“therefore let us go away\!” they said). Ezhi-gozhiwed zaaga’iganing (when they moved camp, to a lake), madaabiiwaad (they came out); mishawikwam dagwishinowaad (when far out upon the ice they came), owaabamaawaa’ awiya mawinanigowaad (they saw they were pursued by someone). Goniginiin, omawinanigowaan (behold, they were being attacked — \*mawinan- ‘attack/charge’ flagged\*). Mii dash ezhi-maajiyang Maji-ikwewis:— (and thus the Foolish Maiden began singing:— \*maajii- ‘begins’ flagged\*)

(3) Mīdąc iⁱⁿ äjinagiskāgänit iniⁿ uta∙i∙wān, mīsa‘ kīnisimint iniⁿ uta∙i∙wān. Mīdąc iⁱⁿ änumawinąnāwāt, äji∙u∙cimunit. Kāwäsa utątimāsiwāwa, mīsągu kīnągąniguwāt. Kägä‘t umintcinawäsināwān iniⁿ uta∙i∙wān. Intawā äjimādcāwāt; äjikąbäciwāt, misa‘ kāwäsa kägō umīdcisīnāwa. Intawā äjimādcāwāt, ąnōtcigupagwata‘kamig icāwāt. Ningutingiku pąpimusäwāt kāgwąn äjimi‘kawāwāt. Mądci‘ki‘kwäwis äjikīgitut: “Kägä‘tigu kā∙u∙yāwānānig igiⁿ kāwayąg.”

**Mii dash iw ezhi-nagishkaagenid iniw odayi’wan, mii sa giinisimind iniw odayi’wan. Mii dash iw anu-mawinananaawaad, ezhi-ozhimonid. Gaawesa odadimaasiwaawaa, mii sa go giinaganigowaad. Geget ominjinawezinaawaan iniw odayi’wan. Indawaa ezhi-maajaawaad; ezhi-gabeshiwaad, mii sa gaawesa gegoo omiijisiinaawaa. Indawaa ezhi-maajaawaad, anooj go bagwadakamig izhaawaad. Ningoding igo babaa-bimosewaad gaagwan ezhi-mikawaawaad. Maji-ikwewis ezhi-giigidod: “Geget igo gaa-oyaawaanaanig igiw gaawayag.”**

*And then to meet them went their pet, whereupon their dog was slain. Accordingly then they tried attacking the wolves, (but) they fled. They were not able to overtake them, and so they were left behind. Truly sad they felt over the loss of their pet. Accordingly then they started on; when they made camp, then not a thing did they have to eat. Therefore then on they went, off to a lonely region they went. And once, when walking about, they found a porcupine. The Foolish Maiden then spoke, saying: “And in truth we will keep some of the quills.”*

Mii dash iw ezhi-nagishkaagenid iniw odayi’wan (and so their pet went to meet them — \*stem flagged\*), mii sa giinisimind (and so it was killed). Mii dash anu-mawinananaawaad (then they tried attacking them in vain), ezhi-ozhimonid (they fled). Gaawesa odadimaasiwaawaa (they could not overtake them — \*stem flagged\*), mii sa go giinaganigowaad (and so they were left behind). Geget ominjinawezinaawaan iniw odayi’wan (truly they grieved their pet). Indawaa ezhi-maajaawaad (so they set off); ezhi-gabeshiwaad (when they camped), mii sa gaawesa gegoo omiijisiinaawaa (they had nothing to eat). Indawaa ezhi-maajaawaad (so they went on), anooj go bagwadakamig izhaawaad (to a lonely wild region they went — \*stem flagged\*). **Aaniish aapiji waabishkiziwan. Zhigwa Maji-ikwewis oganawaabamaan waabigaagwan, “Niyaa, geget igo wii-gaagezha dawawinaakwawe\!” Mii dash iw ezhi-ganoonigowaad: “Gishpin da-bidiyeniyeg da-onizhishiwag igiw ningaawayag.” “Niyaa, nishiime, ambesanoo giin-idam da-bidiyeniin\!” “Nimisenh, gaawesa nin-da-bidiyenisii.” Mii sa ezhi-zhaagwenimowaad, gaawiin awiya inendanzii memeshkwad anu-gagwejindiwaad. Aapiji go wewinizhishiwaad ningad-ayaawaag.” Megwaa go danawewe’towaad, “Gezhitinaa\!” odigowaan. Mii dash geget Maji-ikwewis ezhi-dabidiyenid. “Nawaj besho,” odigoon.**

*Now, very white was (the porcupine). While the Foolish Maiden was looking at the white porcupine, “Oh, would that his quills be nice and straight\!” Thereupon they were addressed by him saying: “If you put your buttocks (into the hole of the tree), nice will be these quills of mine.” “Ah, my little sister\! please do you first put in your buttocks\!” “My elder sister, not for anything would I put in my buttocks.” Whereupon then they became disinclined, for neither one was willing when back and forth they asked each other in vain. And then finally the Foolish Maiden said: “Very well, then, I myself will put in my buttocks, and for so doing I myself will have all the quills, and the ones that are very nice I will keep.” And while they were chattering away about it, “Hurry up\!” they were told. Thereupon truly the Foolish Maiden put in her buttocks. “Still closer,” she was told.*

Aaniish aapiji waabishkiziwan (now very white it was). Zhigwa Maji-ikwewis oganawaabamaan waabigaagwan (the Foolish Maiden looked at the white porcupine — \*waab-gaag, white porcupine\*), “Niyaa, geget igo wii-…dawawinaakwawe\!” (“Oh, would that his quills be straight\!” — \*stem flagged\*). Mii dash ezhi-ganoonigowaad (then they were addressed by him): “Gishpin da-bidiyeniyeg (if you put in your buttocks) da-onizhishiwag igiw ningaawayag (nice will be these quills of mine).” “Niyaa, nishiime, ambesanoo giin-idam da-bidiyeniin\!” (“Ah little sister, do you first put in your buttocks\!”) “Nimisenh, gaawesa nin-da-bidiyenisii.” (“Elder sister, not for anything would I.”) Mii sa ezhi-zhaagwenimowaad (so they were reluctant), gaawiin awiya inendanzii (neither was willing) memeshkwad anu-gagwejindiwaad (asking each other back and forth in vain). *Truly as far as she could she extended her buttocks. Presently was she then slapped across the buttocks with his tail. Poor thing\! truly out of sorts at the buttocks became the Foolish Maiden. Therefore, leaving him behind, they then near by went into camp. Very sick, in truth, was the Foolish Maiden. In a while down went some of the swelling of her buttocks, she was able to get out some of the quills. In time a little better grew the Foolish Maiden. “My little sister, therefore now do you bear me hence, do you draw me on the toboggan.”*

**Atawaa, mii sa ezhi-ombishkaawaad, ezhi-bangishinowaad wasiswaning. Atawaa\! oshiimeyan geget onishki’aan. “Gaa-maa wiin wiika nindaa-gashki’oosiimin,” inendam a ikwe. *Behold, then were they lifted into the air, then they alighted in the nest. Ah\! truly did she anger her little sister. “No doubt we shall never be able (to get down),” thought the woman. Once, while up there, they then saw a Lynx come walking along; then to him spoke the Foolish Maiden, saying: “O Lynx\! please come take us down the tree\! If you can take us down the tree, you may have us for wives.” Truly hitherward came he to the tree; when he slipped and fell, Lynx was heard to say: “I cannot possibly do it. I have no claws.” Accordingly, when on his way went Lynx, the Foolish Maiden waved at him maliciously with her fingers. “Old thing\! Wonder why he did so, he whose penis is like the chain that holds up (the kettle)\!”*

Atawaa, mii sa ezhi-ombishkaawaad (lo, they were lifted up), ezhi-bangishinowaad wasiswaning (and alighted in the nest). Atawaa\! oshiimeyan geget onishki’aan (ah, truly she angered her little sister). “Gaa-maa wiin wiika nindaa-gashki’oosiimin,” inendam a ikwe (“We shall never be able \[to get down\],” thought the woman).
