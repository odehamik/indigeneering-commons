KWĪWISÄNS KĀMĀDCĪNIGUT MA‘KWAN.

**GWIIWIZENS GAA-MAAJIINIGOD MAKWAN.**

THE BOY THAT WAS CARRIED AWAY BY A BEAR.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ningudingsa tāgwābạn anicinābäᵉg; päᵉjikidạc a‘kiwäⁿzī nībiwa udaiyāwaᵍ unīdcānisaᵍ, īni’ᵘ idạc päᵉjik ogwisạn mōⁿjạg opagi‘täᵒwān, ạgāⁿjīyi ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kwīwisäns. Nīnguding minawā ugīpapaki‘tä∙o∙wān, ‘aᵍa’widạc kwīwisäns kīmādcība‘tō nō‘piming. Ningudcidạc mägwä cingubī‘kā ani∙a∙ba‘tōd ki‘tcipäᵉcu’ ma‘kwạn owâbạmān. Mīgo∙i∙’ᵘ āja kītäbibinigut; ‘aᵍawidạc kwīwijäns sägizit pīpāgi. “Îyā\!” inwä. Mägwā pīpāgit mīgū∙i∙’ᵘ kīwạnänimāt ōᵘsan ugīn gayä; mīgō∙i∙’ᵘ āja mäckut kīsāgi∙ā∙t īni’ᵘ ma‘kwan obicawäninigōn; kāwīn ogīnîsigusīn.

**Ningoding sa \[daawaaban\] anishinaabeg; bezhig idash akiwenzii niibiwa odayaawaa’ oniijaanisa’, iniw idash bezhig ogwisan moonzhag obakite’waan, agaashiinyi a’aw gwiiwizens. Ningoding miinawaa ogii-babakite’waan, a’aw idash gwiiwizens gii-maajiibatoo noopiming. Ningoji idash megwaa zhingobiikaa ani-batood gichi-besho’ makwan owaabamaan. Mii go iw azha gii-debibinigod; a’aw idash gwiiwizens zegizid biibaagi. “Iyaa\!” inwe. Megwaa biibaagid mii go iw gii-wanenimaad oosan ogiin gaye; mii go iw azha meshkwad gii-zaagi’aad iniw makwan \[obizhawenimigoon\]; gaawiin ogii-nisigosiin.**

*Once on a time there were dwelling some people; and a certain old man had many children, and one of his sons was he continually flogging; small was the boy. Once again he chastised him thoroughly, and the boy started away on the run into the forest. And presently, while running along through a balsam-grove, very close by he saw a bear. Thereupon then was he seized; and the boy, becoming alarmed, cried out with a loud voice. “Iyā\!” he exclaimed. While calling aloud, he thereupon lost the memory of his father and his mother; accordingly, then, instead he became slain by it.*

Ningoding sa (once upon a time) \[daawaaban\] (there dwelt; \*27-H1 — dwelling-verb, stem uncertain\*) anishinaabeg (people); bezhig idash akiwenzii (and a certain old man) niibiwa odayaawaa’ oniijaanisa’ (had many children), iniw idash bezhig ogwisan (and one of his sons) moonzhag obakite’waan (he kept flogging), agaashiinyi a’aw gwiiwizens (small was the boy). Ningoding miinawaa (once again) ogii-babakite’waan (he flogged him thoroughly; \*reduplicated\*), a’aw idash gwiiwizens (and the boy) gii-maajiibatoo noopiming (started running off into the forest). Ningoji idash (and presently) megwaa zhingobiikaa (while in a balsam-grove) ani-batood (as he ran along) gichi-besho’ makwan owaabamaan (very close by he saw a bear). Mii go iw azha (thereupon now) gii-debibinigod (he was seized by it); a’aw idash gwiiwizens (and the boy) zegizid (being afraid) biibaagi (cried out). “Iyaa\!” inwe (he exclaimed). Megwaa biibaagid (while crying out) mii go iw (thereupon) gii-wanenimaad oosan ogiin gaye (he forgot his father and his mother); mii go iw azha meshkwad (and so now instead) gii-zaagi’aad iniw makwan (he came to love the bear), \[obizhawenimigoon\] (\*27-H2 — verb unparsed; Jones: “he became slain by it”\*); gaawiin ogii-nisigosiin (he was not killed by it).

(2) Mēdac kīmādcīnigut nō‘pîmīng, ā‘pidci ozāgi∙i∙gōn. “Nōjis,” mōⁿjạg udigōn. Mīgu a‘pạnä mōⁿjạg pạpāwīdcīwigut; ạnōdc kägōn umīdcināwa, ka‘kina kägōn nä‘tāwiging mīnạn umīdciwāwān. Ningudīngidạc udigōn: “Ambä, ōwidi ijādā. Mōⁿjạg anicinābäᵉg imāⁿ kägō uda‘tōnāwa mīdcîm. Awigîmōdata\!” i‘kido ‘aᵍa’ᵘ ma‘kwa.

**Mii dash gii-maajiinigod noopiming, aapiji ozaagi’igon. “Noozhis,” moonzhag odigoon. Mii go apane moonzhag babaa-wiijiiwigod; anooj gegoon omiijinaawaa, gakina gegoon netaawiging miinan omiijiwaawaan. Ningoding idash odigoon: “Ambe, oowidi izhaadaa. Moonzhag anishinaabeg imaa gegoo oda’toonaawaa miijim. Awi-gimoodadaa\!” ikido a’aw makwa.**

*Thereupon he was carried away into the forest, very much was he loved (by the bear). “My grandson,” continually was he called. And so all the while, when roaming about, he was ever in the company (of the bear); various kinds of things they ate, all kinds of things in the way of berries that grew in the ground they ate. Now, once he was told: “Come, let us go over in this direction\! Ever are the people putting away some kind of food there. Let us go steal it\!” said the Bear.*

Mii dash (and then) gii-maajiinigod noopiming (he was led away into the forest), aapiji ozaagi’igon (very much he was loved by it). “Noozhis,” (my grandson) moonzhag odigoon (he was always called). Mii go apane moonzhag babaa-wiijiiwigod (and so always he went about in its company); anooj gegoon omiijinaawaa (various things they ate), gakina gegoon netaawiging miinan omiijiwaawaan (all kinds of berries that grow in the ground they ate). Ningoding idash odigoon (and once he was told): “Ambe, oowidi izhaadaa (Come, let us go this way). Moonzhag anishinaabeg imaa gegoo oda’toonaawaa miijim (the people always store some food there). Awi-gimoodadaa\!” (Let us go steal it\!) ikido a’aw makwa (said the bear).

(3) A‘pī∙i∙dạc wädi‘tạmuwād îmāⁿ atänik asạndcigun, minisäns tcīgibīg a‘täni; pāgwā îmāⁿ cībaiyāg. “Mī∙u∙māⁿ ayān,” udigōn. “Ninganāsi‘kān ‘iᵍi’ᵘ asạndcigun.”

**Apii idash weditamowaad imaa atenig asanjigon, minisens jiigibiig ateni; baagwaa imaa zhiibaayaag. “Mii omaa ayaan,” odigoon. “Ninga-naazikaan i’iw asanjigon.”**

*Now, when they came to the place where the cache was, there was a small island off from the water’s edge; shallow was the channel in between. “In this place do you remain,” he was told. “I will go fetch the (contents of the) cache.”*

Apii idash weditamowaad (and when they reached) imaa atenig asanjigon (where the cache was), minisens jiigibiig ateni (a small island lay off the shore); baagwaa imaa zhiibaayaag (shallow was the channel between). “Mii omaa ayaan,” odigoon (Here you stay, he was told). “Ninga-naazikaan i’iw asanjigon” (I will go to the cache).

(4) Mīdac kī‘pîmādạgāzī ‘aᵍa’ᵘ ma‘kwa ijāt minisänsing. Unūndān ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kwīwisäns mạmạdwäpitōd wīgwās agwana∙i∙gātänig ‘iᵍi’ᵘ usạndcigun. Nāgạdcidạc ci’gwa pimạdāpī imāⁿ minisänsing ‘aᵍa’ᵘ ma‘kwa, upita‘kunān ma‘ka‘kuckwämak. Mī∙i∙dạc kīmādcītōd nō‘pîmīng. “Wībatạbitạgwicinōg anicinābäᵉg imāⁿ a‘tägibạn asạndcigun.”

**Mii dash gii-bimaadagaazii a’aw makwa izhaad minisensing. Onoondaan a’aw gwiiwizens memadwebidood wiigwaas agwana’igaadenig i’iw asanjigon. Naagaji dash zhigwa bimadaabii imaa minisensing a’aw makwa, obidakonaan makakoshkwemak. Mii dash gii-maajiidood noopiming. “Wiiba da-bi-dagwishinoog anishinaabeg imaa ate-giban asanjigon.”**

*Accordingly into the water waded the Bear as he went over to the islet. A noise did the boy hear (of the Bear) tearing up the birch-bark that covered the cache. Then after a while forth from the island down to the water came the Bear, he came holding in his arms a birch-bark box. Thereupon he started off into the forest with it: “In a little while will the people be coming to the place where the cache used to be.”*

Mii dash gii-bimaadagaazii a’aw makwa (and so the bear waded along) izhaad minisensing (going to the little island). Onoondaan a’aw gwiiwizens (the boy heard) memadwebidood wiigwaas (the noise of him tearing the birch-bark) agwana’igaadenig i’iw asanjigon (that covered the cache). Naagaji dash zhigwa (and after a while now) bimadaabii imaa minisensing a’aw makwa (the bear came into view from the island), obidakonaan makakoshkwemak (carrying a birch-bark box). Mii dash gii-maajiidood noopiming (and then he started off into the forest with it). “Wiiba da-bi-dagwishinoog anishinaabeg (Soon the people will come) imaa ate-giban asanjigon (to where the cache used to be).”

(5) Wâsadạc kā∙i∙jāwād, “Mī∙u∙māⁿ tạjiwīsinidā\!” udigōn. Mī∙i∙dạc kī‘pigopidōt ‘iᵍi’ᵘ maka‘kuckwämak. Ā‘pidci wäniciciwād nạmä‘tägwạg imāⁿ pīzōwạg; pîmidä gayä pīndäni. Mīdạc kīwīsiniwād. Kā∙i∙ckwā wīsiniwād, “Nibādā\!” udigōn. Ā‘pidci kicā‘täni.

**Waasa dash gaa-izhaawaad, “Mii omaa daji-wiisinidaa\!” odigoon. Mii dash gii-biigobidood i’iw makakoshkwemak. Aapiji wenizhishiwaad \[namedegwag\] imaa biizowag; bimide gaye biindeni. Mii dash gii-wiisiniwaad. Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisiniwaad, “Nibaadaa\!” odigoon. Aapiji gizhaateni.**

*And when a long way off they had gone, “In this place let us eat\!” (the boy) was told. Whereat he broke up the birch-bark box. Very nice were the fishes dried by roasting that were in (the box); some tallow, too, was inside. Thereupon they ate. After they had eaten, “Let us go to sleep\!” (the boy) was told. Exceedingly warm was it.*

Waasa dash gaa-izhaawaad (and when they had gone far), “Mii omaa daji-wiisinidaa\!” odigoon (Here let us eat\! he was told). Mii dash gii-biigobidood i’iw makakoshkwemak (and then he broke open the birch-bark box). Aapiji wenizhishiwaad \[namedegwag\] (very nice were the fire-dried fishes; \*namedeg-family, Jones: fishes dried by roasting\*) imaa biizowag (that were inside); bimide gaye biindeni (some tallow too was within). Mii dash gii-wiisiniwaad (and so they ate). Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisiniwaad (when they had eaten), “Nibaadaa\!” odigoon (Let us sleep\! he was told). Aapiji gizhaateni (exceedingly warm was it).

(6) Mī∙i∙dec a‘pī ka‘kina kā∙ạ∙mwāwād nạmä‘tägwạn kī∙ạ∙nimādcāwạg; ạnōdc kägō obạbāmīdcināwa. A‘pī∙i∙dạc kägā änibibōnk, “Ạmbä nạndawâbạndạdā tcī∙a∙yāyạnk\!” ‘Aᵍawidạc ma‘kwa kī∙ạ∙nimi‘ku‘tā kīnạntuki‘kändạnk mini‘k anicinābäᵉn kädicānit tcîbibōng. Ningudcidạc ogī∙o∙nābạndān ‘aᵍa’ᵘ ma‘kwa. “Mīsaᵍ omā kāwīn umāⁿ tạbimusäsī ạnicinābä kabäbîbōn.” Mīdạc imāⁿ kī∙u∙ji‘tōd owāc mägwä kījikänsi‘kāng. A‘pī∙i∙dạc päbōnk mī∙i∙māⁿ kīpīndigäwād.

**Mii idash apii gakina gaa-amwaawaad namedegwan gii-ani-maajaawag; anooj gegoo obabaa-miijinaawaa. Apii idash gegaa ani-biboong, “Ambe nandawaabandadaa ji-ayaayang\!” A’aw idash makwa \[gii-ani-mikwa’taa\] gii-nando-gikendang minik anishinaaben \[ged-izhaanid\] ji-biboong. Ningoji idash ogii-onaabandaan a’aw makwa. “Mii sa omaa gaawiin omaa da-bimosesii anishinaabe gabe-biboon.” Mii dash imaa gii-ozhitood owaazh megwaa gizhikensikaang. Apii idash baboong mii imaa gii-biindigewaad.**

*And so, when they had eaten up all of the fish that had been dried by the fire, they started upon their way; all sorts of things they ate as they wandered about. Now, when it was getting well on into the winter, “Come, let us seek for a place where we are to stay\!” So the Bear rolled over upon his face and belly, in order to find out in his mind how many people would be passing by during the winter. So on in a certain place did the Bear seek for a spot. “Now, by this place will no person pass throughout the entire winter.” Accordingly he made his lair there, in a grove of little cedars. So, when winter came, it was into that place they went.*

Mii idash apii gakina gaa-amwaawaad namedegwan (and when they had eaten up all the dried fishes) gii-ani-maajaawag (they started on their way); anooj gegoo obabaa-miijinaawaa (all sorts of things they ate as they roamed). Apii idash gegaa ani-biboong (and when it was nearly winter), “Ambe nandawaabandadaa ji-ayaayang\!” (Come, let us seek a place to stay\!). A’aw idash makwa \[gii-ani-mikwa’taa\] (and the bear lay prone; \*Jones: rolled over upon his face and belly — stem soft, light flag\*) gii-nando-gikendang minik anishinaaben (to learn how many people) \[ged-izhaanid\] ji-biboong (would pass by during the winter; \*future, light flag\*). Ningoji idash ogii-onaabandaan a’aw makwa (and somewhere the bear chose a spot by looking). “Mii sa omaa gaawiin omaa da-bimosesii anishinaabe gabe-biboon” (By this place no person will pass all winter long). Mii dash imaa gii-ozhitood owaazh (and there he made his den) megwaa gizhikensikaang (amid a grove of little cedars). Apii idash baboong (and when winter came) mii imaa gii-biindigewaad (it was there they went in).

(7) Ningudīng ānawi kwaiya‘k pīdāsạmusä ‘aᵍa’ᵘ anicinābä; päᵉjik udūdā‘pinān nạmä‘tägwạn; mīdạc äjisāgitciwäpinād pînäng idạc ijināguzi ‘aᵍ’ᵘ nạmä‘täg. Mīdạc äjiwạcki‘kād ‘aᵍa’ᵘ anicinābä nōdci∙ā∙d pînäwạn; mīdạc ningudci pîmī∙i∙jāt ‘aᵍa’ᵘ anicinābäᵉ.

**Ningoding aanawi gwayak biidaasamose a’aw anishinaabe; bezhig odoodaapinaan namedegwan; mii dash ezhi-zaagijiwebinaad \[bineng\] idash izhinaagozi a’aw namedeg. Mii dash \[ezhi-waaki’kaad\] a’aw anishinaabe noojii’aad binewan; mii dash ningoji bimi-izhaad a’aw anishinaabe.**

*Sometimes a person would in fact be coming straight (to where they were); one piece of fish that had been dried by the fire would (the Bear) take; and when he flung it out, then into the form of a ruffed grouse would the dried smoked fish become. Thereupon would the man turn off his course to follow after the ruffed grouse; and so into another direction would the person go.*

Ningoding aanawi gwayak biidaasamose a’aw anishinaabe (sometimes a person would come walking straight toward them); bezhig odoodaapinaan namedegwan (one of the dried fishes he would take); mii dash ezhi-zaagijiwebinaad (and as he flung it out) \[bineng\] idash izhinaagozi a’aw namedeg (the dried fish would appear as a ruffed grouse; \*bineng “as a grouse,” light flag\*). Mii dash \[ezhi-waaki’kaad\] a’aw anishinaabe (and so the man would turn aside; \*light flag\*) noojii’aad binewan (chasing after the grouse); mii dash ningoji bimi-izhaad a’aw anishinaabe (and so off in another direction the man would go).

(8) Kạbäpîbōn nīpā ‘aᵍa’ᵘ ma‘kwa, uwī‘pämān ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kwīwisäns. Ningudingi‘ku ogạnōnigōn: “Nōjis, kipa‘kạdäna?” “Äyeᵍ,” udinān. “Ckumāⁿ imāⁿ inābin nimpi‘kwanāng.” Pạngī∙i∙dac kwänibi‘tā aᵘ ma‘kwa. Mīdạc inābit aᵘ gwīsiwäns ā‘pidci wänicicing mīdcîm owâbạndān. Ka‘kina kā∙i∙nạndcigäwād nībînung mī∙i∙māⁿ ka‘kina a‘tänik. “Wīsînin nōjis\!” udigōn. Gäᵉgä‘t kīwīsini ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kwīwisäns.

**Gabe-biboon nibaa a’aw makwa, owiipemaan a’aw gwiiwizens. Ningoding igo oganoonigoon: “Noozhis, gibakade na?” “Eya’,” odinaan. “\[Ishkomaa\] imaa inaabin nimbikwanaang.” Bangii idash \[gwekibi’taa\] aw makwa. Mii dash inaabid aw gwiiwizens aapiji wenizhishing miijim owaabandaan. Gakina gaa-inanjigewaad niibinong mii imaa gakina atenig. “Wiisinin noozhis\!” odigoon. Geget gii-wiisini a’aw gwiiwizens.**

*All winter long would (the Bear) sleep, he slept with the boy. Once in a while he was addressed by his grandfather: “My grandson, are you hungry?” “Yes,” he said to him. “Just you look there at my back.” So slightly over would the Bear turn. And when the boy looked, very nice was the food he saw. Everything which they had eaten during the summer before was all there. “Do you eat, my grandson\!” he was told. Truly did the boy eat.*

Gabe-biboon nibaa a’aw makwa (all winter the bear slept), owiipemaan a’aw gwiiwizens (he slept with the boy). Ningoding igo oganoonigoon (once in a while he was addressed): “Noozhis, gibakade na?” (My grandson, are you hungry?) “Eya’,” odinaan (Yes, he said to him; \*Äyeᵍ — 2nd corpus print, feeds the \#41 HARD flag\*). “\[Ishkomaa\] imaa inaabin nimbikwanaang” (Just look there at my back; \*exhortative particle, light flag\*). Bangii idash \[gwekibi’taa\] aw makwa (and a little the bear turned himself; \*light flag\*). Mii dash inaabid aw gwiiwizens (and when the boy looked) aapiji wenizhishing miijim owaabandaan (very nice food he saw). Gakina gaa-inanjigewaad niibinong (everything they had eaten last summer) mii imaa gakina atenig (was all there). “Wiisinin noozhis\!” odigoon (Eat, my grandson\! he was told). Geget gii-wiisini a’aw gwiiwizens (truly the boy ate).

(9) Mīgō∙i∙’ᵘ kā‘tōdạminit kạbäbibōn kī∙ạ∙camigut. Nāningutinū ‘aᵍa’ᵘ ma‘kwa i‘kido: “Ānawi nījawänimāg anicinābäᵉg, kāwīndạc nimīnāsīg nī∙i∙yaᵘ. Uzām kidāpạnādci∙i∙n kīcpîn nisigōyān.”

**Mii go iw gaa-doodaminid gabe-biboon gii-ashamigod. Naaningodinoo a’aw makwa ikido: “Aanawi nizhawenimag anishinaabeg, gaawiin idash nimiinaasiig niiyaw. Onzaam gidaapanaaji’in giishpin nisigoyaan.”**

*So that was what (the Bear) did throughout the winter when feeding (the boy). Sometimes the Bear would say: “Even though I take pity upon people, yet I do not (always) give them of my body. Too much harm would I do you if I should be killed.”*

Mii go iw gaa-doodaminid gabe-biboon (so that was what he did all winter) gii-ashamigod (in feeding him). Naaningodinoo a’aw makwa ikido (now and then the bear would say): “Aanawi nizhawenimag anishinaabeg (Even though I take pity on people), gaawiin idash nimiinaasiig niiyaw (yet I do not give them my body). Onzaam gidaapanaaji’in giishpin nisigoyaan (Too much harm would I do you if I were killed).”

(10) A‘pī∙i∙dạc kägā äninībing, käyābi pạngī kōni‘kānig, mī∙i∙’ᵘ kīsāga∙ạ∙mowād. Mojạg ugi‘känimān anicinābäᵉn käbimi∙i∙jānit, kāwīndec imāⁿ ayāsīwạg. A‘pī∙i∙dạc wawīngä kānībing, “Ạmbä, nōjis, ōwidi ijādā\! Kīⁿgōⁿyạg iwidi ayāwạg sībīng. Mī∙i∙māⁿ mōjạg äjāyān sāgwạngîn.”

**Apii idash gegaa ani-niibing, geyaabi bangii goonikaanig, mii iw gii-zaaga’amowaad. Moozhag ogikenimaan anishinaaben ge-bimi-izhaanid, gaawiin dash imaa ayaasiiwag. Apii idash wawiinge gaa-niibing, “Ambe, noozhis, oowidi izhaadaa\! Giigoonyag iwidi ayaawag ziibiing. Mii imaa moozhag ezhaayaan \[ziigwaning\].”**

*And when it was getting well on towards the summer, while there was yet a little snow on the ground, then out they came. Always did (the Bear) know where the people would be passing, so there would they not remain. And after the summer had fully come, “Now, my grandson, over this way let us go\! Some fishes are in a river over there. It is there I always stay during the spring.”*

Apii idash gegaa ani-niibing (and when it was nearly summer), geyaabi bangii goonikaanig (while yet a little snow lay), mii iw gii-zaaga’amowaad (then out they came). Moozhag ogikenimaan anishinaaben ge-bimi-izhaanid (always he knew where the people would pass), gaawiin dash imaa ayaasiiwag (so there they would not stay). Apii idash wawiinge gaa-niibing (and when summer had fully come), “Ambe, noozhis, oowidi izhaadaa\! (Come, my grandson, let us go this way\!) Giigoonyag iwidi ayaawag ziibiing (Fishes are in a river yonder). Mii imaa moozhag ezhaayaan \[ziigwaning\] (It is there I always go in the spring; \*light flag\*).”

(11) Ạnicinābäᵉg mōjạg imāⁿ ijāwạg wīnisāwād ma‘kwạn. Āja ugī∙u∙ji‘tōnāwān tạsōnāgạnạn. A‘pī∙i∙dạc wädi‘tạmuwād a‘pidci nībawa ayāwaᵍ kīⁿgōⁿyaᵍ. Mīdạc wâbạndạmuwād wạnī∙i∙gạnạn mīgō∙i∙’ᵘ ki‘kändạnk ‘aᵍa’ᵘ ma‘kwa wägunän a‘tänig ‘iᵍi’ᵘ gu‘käya; kāwīndec uwīdā‘pạnāⁿzīn. *People were always going to the place to kill bears. Already had they set the dead-falls. And when they got to the place, very many were the fishes there. (12) Ningudingidạc ogạnōnigōn umicōmisạn: “Ānīc, nōjis, mīsaᵍ nindawā tcikīwäwininān. Uzām kạckändạmōg kinīki∙i∙gōg. Ạmbä, iwidi ijādāᵉ ayāwād\!” Mī∙i∙dec kī∙ạ∙nimādcīnigut. Mī∙i∙dạc ägut kwä‘kā‘tig, mīdạc ägut: “Kīcpîn ninguding pạ‘kadäyạn kạnōjicin. Kīkạdajạmin.”

**Ningoding idash oganoonigoon omishoomisan: “Aaniish, noozhis, mii sa nindawaa ji-giiwewininaan. Onzaam gashkendamoog ginikii’igoog. Ambe, iwidi izhaadaa ayaawaad\!” Mii idash gii-ani-maajiinigod. Ningoding oganoonigoon: “Mii omaa besho’ ayaamagak zaaga’igan, mii imaa endaawaad koos giga gaye.” Jiigibiig gii-ani-izhaa a’aw makwa. Bezhig mitigoon ogii-ani-naazikawaan jiigibiig betakizonid. *Now, once he was addressed by his grandfather saying: “Well, my grandchild, now therefore will I take you back home. Too sorrowful are your parents. Come, thither let us go where they are\!” Accordingly was he then carried away. By and by he was addressed (by the Bear) saying: “Now, nigh to this place is a lake, and there dwell your father and your mother.” Along by the edge of the water travelled the Bear. (13) A‘pī∙i∙dạc ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kwīwisäns kwä‘kā‘tig kā∙i∙jāt mī∙i∙’ᵘ kīwạnänimāt umicōmisạn. Mīdạc kā∙i∙jināzibīt ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kwīwisäns imāⁿ sāga∙i∙ganīng mi‘tāwạngānik imādec u‘pîmäya∙ī∙ inābit owābạndānạn tcīmānạn a‘tänig; kī∙ạ∙nī∙i∙jāt owâbạmāᵍ i∙i∙māⁿ i‘kwäwaᵍ tạji‘tānit. *And when the boy went over behind the tree, then lost he all thought of his grandfather. *And when they ran down to the water, they saw a bear swimming along. And the boy hurried over to get his little war-club, he too got into a canoe.
