65\. MIGHTY-ONE, BLACK-TAIL-OF-A-FISH, AND THE MYSTIC RITE.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk and/or Midāsugaⁿj (Series IV; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (“either … or both”; not credited on the story page).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ānīcnä äniwä‘k ōcisạn udaiyāwän ‘aᵉa‘ᵘ Cōngä‘pāⁿ, pīnicidạc ki‘tcī‘kwäwiwạn. Ānic, mii‘ᵘ cigwa a‘pidci agāwā kacki‘tōt pimusät; cigwadạc ätawisa‘ku∙ᵘ Cōngä‘pāⁿ. Ningutingidạc mādcā awi i‘kwä mạnisät. Cacingicink Cōngä‘pāⁿ mạdwä∙i∙säwạn ōcisạn. “Ambäsanō ningawikackitciwi∙i∙cāyān\! Ninganagatān ‘iᵉi‘ᵘ nīyaᵘ, nōcisidạc ningamina.” Cigwa kāwutā‘pinạnk usa‘ka∙u∙nạn, —ānic nīⁿj ininiwạn ini‘ᵘ usa‘ka∙u∙nạn,— mādcā icāt īni‘ᵘ ōcisạn mạnisänit. Mägwāgu tạcimạnisät i‘kwä pīdāsạmusäwạn omicōmisạn. “Ambäsa, nōjis, pisindawicin wâ∙i∙ninān\! Mīsa‘ cigwa wīnaganinān, niwīmādca. Mäckwạt kānaganinān, kiwīnagatạmōn ‘aᵉa‘ᵘ kwīwisäns.”

**Aaniish ina aniwe’k oozhisan odayaawaan a’aw Zhaangwe’paa(nh), biinish idash gichi-ikwewiwan. Aaniish, mii iw zhigwa aapiji agaawaa gashkitood bimosed; zhigwa idash edawisaka’oo Zhaangwe’paa(nh). Ningoding idash maajaa awi-ikwe manised. Zhazhingishing Zhaangwe’paa(nh) madwe-isewan oozhisan. “Ambegizhanoo ninga-wii-gashki’ewizi-izhaayaan\! Ninga-nagadaan iw niiyaw, noozhis idash ninga-miinaa.” Zhigwa gaa-odaapinang osaka’onan, —aaniish niizh ininiwan iniw osaka’onan,— maajaa izhaad iniw oozhisan manisenid. Megwaa go dazhi-manised ikwe biidaasamosewan omishoomisan. “Ambegizh, noozhis, bizindawishin waa-ininaan\! Mii sa zhigwa wii-naganinaan, niwii-maajaa. Meshkwad gaa-naganinaan, gi-wii-nagadamoon a’aw gwiiwizens.”**

*Now, several grandchildren did Mighty-One have, and some had come to be grown-up women. Well, it was then that he could scarcely even walk; and in a while with a cane on either hand walked Mighty-One. Now, once departed (one of) the women to gather fire-wood. While lying (upon his pallet), Mighty-One (heard) the sound of his grandchild chopping wood. “Would that I might be able to go over there\! I am going to leave my body here, to my grandchild will I leave it.” Presently, taking up his canes, — for two were those canes of his, — he set out to go to where his grandchild was gathering fire-wood. And hither to the place, while the woman was gathering fire-wood, came her grandfather walking along. “Come, my grandchild, listen to what I shall say to you\! The time has now come when I shall leave you, I intend to go away. But, notwithstanding my leaving you, I shall leave with you a boy.”*

Aaniish ina (now) oozhisan odayaawaan a’aw Zhaangwe’paa (Mighty-One / Black-Tail-of-a-Fish had grandchildren — \*name flagged 65-H2\*), biinish idash gichi-ikwewiwan (and some had become grown women). Aaniish (well), mii iw zhigwa aapiji agaawaa gashkitood bimosed (it was then that he could scarcely walk); zhigwa idash edawisaka’oo (and then he walked with a cane in each hand — \*stem flagged\*). Ningoding idash maajaa awi-ikwe manised (once a woman set off to gather firewood). Zhazhingishing (as he lay) Zhaangwe’paa madwe-isewan oozhisan (Mighty-One heard the sound of his grandchild). “Ambegizh ninga-gashki’ewizi-izhaayaan iwidi\! (would that I could go over there\!) Ninga-nagadaan iw niiyaw (I shall leave my body here), noozhis idash ninga-miinaa.” (and to my grandchild I shall give it.) Zhigwa gaa-odaapinang osaka’onan (then, taking up his canes), —aaniish niizh iniw osaka’onan— (for two were his canes,) maajaa izhaad iniw oozhisan manisenid (he set off to where his grandchild was gathering firewood). Megwaa go dazhi-manised ikwe (while the woman was gathering firewood) biidaasamosewan omishoomisan (her grandfather came walking toward her). “Ambegizh, noozhis, bizindawishin waa-ininaan\! (come, my grandchild, listen to what I shall say to you\!) Mii sa zhigwa wii-naganinaan (the time has come when I shall leave you), niwii-maajaa (I intend to go away). Meshkwad gaa-naganinaan (but though I leave you), gi-wii-nagadamoon a’aw gwiiwizens.” (I shall leave you a boy.)

*Cōngä’pāⁿ — the protagonist’s name ’Black-Tail-of-a-Fish’ (the title pairs it with Mä’kadäcigwạn ’Mighty-One’; the two names are used across the story — see 65-H3). Word-final superscript ⁿ carried as printed (drafted -paa(nh), flagged). edawisa’ku∙ᵘ — ’with a cane on either hand,’ stem drafted edawisaka’oo, flagged. The grandfather/grandchild address (omicōmis / nōjis) runs throughout; the woman is his grandchild (oozhisan).*

(2) Ānic ki‘tcinānāgatawäntạm ‘a‘a‘ᵘ i‘kwä. Kāwin wi‘kā ugi‘känimāsīn omicōmisạn kägō wi‘kā tcipimatci∙i∙nänimāt. Äjiganōnāt: “Anin, nimicōmis, wâ∙i∙cictcigäyạn ‘ii‘ᵘ winagatạmawiyạn kwiwisäns?”

**Aaniish gichi-naanaagadawendam a’aw ikwe. Gaawiin wiikaa ogikenimaasiin omishoomisan gegoo wiikaa ji-bimaaji’inenimaad. Ezhi-ganoonaad: “Aaniin, nimishoomis, waa-izhichigeyan iw wii-nagadamawiyan gwiiwizens?”**

*Now, in deep meditation was the woman. Never had she known of her grandfather in an evil-minded way towards her. Then she spoke to him, saying: “My grandfather, how are you going to bring it about to leave with me a boy?”*

Aaniish gichi-naanaagadawendam a’aw ikwe (now, in deep thought was the woman). Gaawiin wiikaa ogikenimaasiin omishoomisan (never had she known her grandfather) gegoo wiikaa ji-bimaaji’inenimaad (to think of her in any evil way — \*stem flagged\*). Ezhi-ganoonaad (then she spoke to him): “Aaniin, nimishoomis (“my grandfather), waa-izhichigeyan iw wii-nagadamawiyan gwiiwizens?” (how will you bring it about to leave me a boy?”)

(3) “Nōjis, kägu‘ anwä‘tawici‘kän, mīguca iᵘ ickwātc kaganōninān; mīgu omā tcinibuyān. Kä∙i∙cictcigäyạn tcinibuyān: Kinibuyānidạc mō‘kumān dạc kamạnōn kagīckicān o‘o‘wä, picicigidạc ningawanamaniⁿ. Miziwä kāwīn ningutci kīgawäbinisi ‘ii‘ᵘ nīyaᵘ. Midac iᵘ käpạmi∙i∙guyäg, wạnạmạn kä∙i∙cini‘kādạnk ‘aa‘ᵘ ạnicinābä. Wa‘a widạc kwiwisäns tamanidōwi. Ayāngwāmisin, nōjis, wäwäni icictcigäyu‘k. Miziwä tanōntạm ‘a‘a‘ᵘ ạnicinābä. Ayangwāmisin, wäwäni wīni‘tāwigi aᵘ kwīwisäns.” Misạgu cigwa picigwātci∙ā∙t ōcisạn, misa‘ kī∙ā∙‘pitwäwäsigät.

**“Noozhis, gego anwe’tawishiken, mii go sa iw ishkwaaj ge-ganooninaan; mii go omaa ji-niboyaan. Ge-izhichigeyan ji-niboyaan: Gi-niboyaan idash mookomaan dash ga-manoon ga-giishkizhan o’ow, bizhishig idash ninga-onamani(nh). Miziwe gaawiin ningoji giga-webinisii iw niiyaw. Mii dash iw ge-bami’igoyeg, onaman ge-izhi-nikaadang a’aw anishinaabe. Wa’aw idash gwiiwizens da-manidoowi. Ayaangwaamizin, noozhis, weweni izhichigeyok. Miziwe da-noondam a’aw anishinaabe. Ayaangwaamizin, weweni wii-nitaawigi’a aw gwiiwizens.” Mii sa go zhigwa bizigwaaji’aad oozhisan, mii sa gii-aabita-weweziged.**

*“My grandchild, do not fail to heed my words, for this is the last time that I shall speak with you; it is now that I should die. (What) you shall do when I die (is this): now, when I die, do you take a knife (and) cut here, for I shall be changed wholly into magic paint. No part of my whole body shall you fling away. It is by this that you (and all) shall be sustained, magic paint is what the people shall call it. And this boy shall be a manitou being. Be heedful, my grandchild, with care do you (and the others) act upon it. Everywhere shall the people hear about it. Be zealous, in the right way do you bring up the boy.” Thereupon he then had carnal knowledge of his grandchild, and while having it he wasted his life away.*

“Noozhis, gego anwe’tawishiken (“my grandchild, do not disregard me), mii go sa iw ishkwaaj ge-ganooninaan (for this is the last time I shall speak with you); mii go omaa ji-niboyaan (it is now that I shall die). Ge-izhichigeyan ji-niboyaan: (what you shall do when I die:) Gi-niboyaan idash mookomaan dash ga-manoon (when I die take up a knife) ga-giishkizhan o’ow (and cut here), bizhishig idash ninga-onaman (for I shall become wholly magic paint — onaman). Miziwe gaawiin ningoji giga-webinisii iw niiyaw (nowhere shall you fling away any part of my body). Mii dash iw ge-bami’igoyeg (it is by this that you shall be sustained), onaman ge-izhi-nikaadang a’aw anishinaabe (onaman / magic paint is what the people shall call it). Wa’aw idash gwiiwizens da-manidoowi (and this boy shall be a manidoo being). Ayaangwaamizin, noozhis (be careful, my grandchild), weweni izhichigeyok (act upon it rightly). Miziwe da-noondam a’aw anishinaabe (everywhere the people shall hear of it). Ayaangwaamizin, weweni wii-nitaawigi’a aw gwiiwizens.” (be zealous, raise the boy well.) Mii sa go zhigwa bizigwaaji’aad oozhisan (thereupon he had carnal knowledge of his grandchild — \*stem flagged\*), mii sa gii-aabita-weweziged (and as he did so he wasted his life away — \*stem flagged\*).

*wạnạmạn / onaman — ’magic paint’ (the corpus form onaman ’red ochre / magic paint’; the rite-frame term in the corpus is ’onamani-midewiwin’ ’magic-paint mystic-rite’). Jones’s gloss ’magic paint’ carried in the gloss as onaman per Translation Ethics (no missionary frame). picigwātci∙ā∙t — ’had carnal knowledge of,’ Jones’s plain rendering; stem drafted bizigwaaji’aad, flagged. kī∙ā∙’pitwäwäsigät — ’wasted his life away,’ long fused verb, parse uncertain, flagged. FIREWALL: the dying-into-onaman and the manidoo-boy are stated as Jones records them, not interpreted.*

(4) Ätawā, i‘kwä mī∙i∙‘ᵘ kīnibunit īni‘ᵘ omicōmisạn. Wägunäniwinän ubäcicwān i∙i∙mã unintcīning, mīdạc ‘iᵉi‘ᵘ kumiginin kägä‘t picicig wạnạmạnạn unintcīning. Wägunänīwinạn ugīwäwạnänạn. Mīsa cayīgwa kīmādci‘kawāt īni‘ᵘ omicōmisạn. Kitạci‘kawāt mīziwä, mīgu iᵘ pijicikwạnạmạnạn. Mīdạc iᵘ äjipāsạnk ‘iᵉi‘ᵘ wiyawini. Kägä‘t ki‘tcimāma‘kādäntạm iᵘ inạmanisutawāt. Miziwä uda‘tōn umacki‘kīm macki‘ki‘kānāt īni‘ᵘ omicōmisạn. Cigwadạc kā‘kīcictcigät mīsa‘ iᵘ mādcāt, icāt Ma‘kadäcigwanạn. Kā‘tagwicin äjikanōnāt: “Māma‘kāte mīsa a‘panä kī∙i∙ckwāmicōmisiyān.” Äjiganōnigut: “Nōjis, kägu‘ ningutci wäbinä‘kän ‘aa‘ᵘ kimicōmis, mīginini∙i∙‘ miziwä wa‘täbisäg änigu‘kwāg a‘ki. Pitcīnạg tabā‘tinīni känạntutamōk.”

**Edawaa, ikwe mii iw gii-nibonid iniw omishoomisan. Wegonen iniwen obeshizhwaan i’imaa oninjiining, mii dash i’iw go miinangini-nin geget bizhishig onamanan oninjiining. Wegoneniwinan ogii-wewanenan. Mii sa zhayiigwa gii-maaji-gawaad iniw omishoomisan. Gidazhi-gawaad miziwe, mii go iw bizhishig onamanan. Mii dash iw ezhi-baasang i’iw wiyawini. Geget gichi-maamakaadendam iw inaa-manisotawaad. Miziwe oda’toon omashki’kiim mashki’kiikaanaad iniw omishoomisan. Zhigwa idash gaa-giizhichiged mii sa iw maajaad, izhaad Makadeshigwanan. Gaa-dagwishin ezhi-ganoonaad: “Maamakaadi mii sa apane gii-ishkwaa-mishoomisiyaan.” Ezhi-ganoonigod: “Noozhis, gego ningoji webinaaken a’aw gimishoomis, mii ginini’iw miziwe wa’-debiseg enigokwaag aki. Bijiinag da-baatiinini ge-nandodamoog.”**

*Alas\! now dead was the woman’s grandfather. What should she do but cut a slice off his finger, whereupon to her surprise there really was nothing but magic paint (to be seen) upon his hand. What should she then do but carry him home upon her back. It was then that she went to work upon her grandfather. After she had finished with him all over, then (she beheld) absolutely nothing but magic paint. Thereupon she then put his body in place to dry. Of a truth, greatly amazed was she to see that she had turned him into magic paint. In every place she put her medicine while engaged in drawing the remedy from her grandfather. And in a while, when she had finished, she then departed, she went to where Black-Tail-of-a-Fish was. When she got there, she spoke to him, saying: “It is strange since my grandfather is no more.” Then was she addressed by him saying: “My grandchild, do not fling away (any part of) your grandfather, for from that is there to be (magic paint) enough for the whole earth throughout its length and breadth. In time to come many shall they be that will ask it of you.”*

Edawaa (alas), ikwe mii iw gii-nibonid iniw omishoomisan (now the woman’s grandfather was dead). Wegonen obeshizhwaan oninjiining (she cut a slice off his finger), mii dash geget bizhishig onamanan oninjiining (and truly there was nothing but onaman / magic paint on his hand). Ogii-wewanenan (she carried him home on her back). Mii sa zhayiigwa gii-maaji-gawaad iniw omishoomisan (then she went to work on her grandfather). Gidazhi-gawaad miziwe (after she had worked him over all over), mii go iw bizhishig onamanan (then there was nothing but onaman). Mii dash iw ezhi-baasang iw wiyawini (then she put his body to dry). Geget gichi-maamakaadendam (truly she was greatly amazed) iw inaa-manisotawaad (at what she had drawn from him — \*stem flagged\*). Miziwe oda’toon omashki’kiim (everywhere she put her medicine) mashki’kiikaanaad iniw omishoomisan (drawing the medicine from her grandfather). Zhigwa idash gaa-giizhichiged mii sa iw maajaad (then, when she had finished, she set off), izhaad Makadeshigwanan (going to Black-Tail-of-a-Fish). Gaa-dagwishin ezhi-ganoonaad (when she got there she spoke to him): “Maamakaadi mii sa apane gii-ishkwaa-mishoomisiyaan.” (“it is strange now that my grandfather is no more.”) Ezhi-ganoonigod (then he addressed her): “Noozhis, gego ningoji webinaaken a’aw gimishoomis (“my grandchild, do not fling away any of your grandfather), mii ginini’iw miziwe wa’-debiseg enigokwaag aki (for from that there is enough for the whole earth in all its breadth). Bijiinag da-baatiinini ge-nandodamoog.” (in time many will be those who ask it of you.)

*Ma’kadäcigwạn / Ma’kadäcigwanạn — ’Black-Tail-of-a-Fish’ (the title’s first name ’Mighty-One’ is paired with this; in this story Ma’kadäcigwạn is the elder Black-Tail-of-a-Fish to whom the woman goes — see 65-H3 on the two names). inạmanisutawāt / macki’ki’kānāt — ’drawing the remedy/medicine from him,’ stems drafted, flagged.*

(5) Mīsa‘ cigwa wī‘kīwät ‘aᵉa‘ᵘ i‘kwä, “Nōjis, ạmbäsạnō, ayāngwāmisin\!” udigōn. Änijimādcāt, “Pitcīnạg tabā‘tạnīni ‘aᵉa‘ᵘ känạntutamōk,” udigon.

**Mii sa zhigwa wii-giiwed a’aw ikwe, “Noozhis, ambegizhanoo, ayaangwaamizin\!” odigoon. Ani-izhi-maajaad, “Bijiinag da-baatiinini a’aw ge-nandodamoog,” odigon.**

*And So, when the woman was about to come away, “My grandchild, I beg of you, do show zeal\!” she was told. Then, as she set forth upon her way, “In time to come many shall they be that will ask it of you,” she was told.*

Mii sa zhigwa wii-giiwed a’aw ikwe (and so, as the woman was about to go home), “Noozhis, ambegizhanoo, ayaangwaamizin\!” odigoon (“my grandchild, I beg of you, be zealous\!” she was told). Ani-izhi-maajaad (as she set off), “Bijiinag da-baatiinini a’aw ge-nandodamoog,” odigon (“in time many will be those who ask it of you,” she was told).

(6) Mīsa‘ kägä‘t cigwa mādcī‘kawint nạntutamawint īni‘ᵘ wạnạmạnạn. Anawidạc päpangi migiwä, mibō‘tc nōndäsänit. Anawigu päpangi migiwä, kägätsa nibiwa udaiyạn anō‘kätcigan mini‘k mānint. Aba‘pic ningō‘ki‘kinōniwin äni∙ā∙waninik mi cigwa kītäbisänik mōskinänik ‘iᵉi‘ᵘ äntāwāt anoʻkātcigan. Kumāgu a‘pi cigwa kägō ici∙ā∙yāt ugi‘känimān abinotcīyạn ayāwāt. “Mimāwīni∙i∙‘ᵘ nimicōmis kā∙i∙cit, cigwa äci∙ai∙yāyān,” inäntạm. Ningutingigu abinōtciyạn owâbamān. **Mii sa geget zhigwa maajii-gawind nandodamawind iniw onamanan. Anawi idash baabange migiwe, miibood noondesenid. Anawi go baabange migiwe, geget sa niibiwa odayaan anokaajigan minik maanind. Aba’pii ningo-gikinooniwin ani-aawaninig mii zhigwa gii-debisenig mooshkinenig i’iw endaawaad anokaajigan. Goma go a’pii zhigwa gegoo izhi-ayaad ogikenimaan abinoojiiyan ayaawaad. “Mii maawiin iw nimishoomis gaa-izhid, zhigwa ezhi-ayaayaan,” inendam. Ningoding go abinoojiiyan owaabamaan. Aapiji ozhaawenimaan gaye iw weweni dodawaad; ningoding sa zhigwa onitaawigi’aan. Mii sa zhayiigwa gii-debweyendang. Mii sa geget e-ikidonid gaa-izhi-webadinig.**

*Thereupon truly did they then begin upon her, going to her for the magic paint. Even though but little at a time she gave away, yet of necessity there was not enough to go around. Even though but little at a time she gave away, yet truly much she had in the way of goods that were given her in plenty. By the time that a cycle of the seasons came round, was when there were goods enough to fill the place where she (and others) dwelt. In due course of time, when feeling something the matter with her, she knew that she was with child. “This is no doubt according to what my grandfather had foretold of me, this state that I now am in,” she thought. So by and by she bore a child. Very affectionate was she with (the boy), and tenderly she cared for him; and in the time that went on she reared him. And so now did she believe what (her grandfather had said) when he told of what would happen.*

Mii sa geget zhigwa maajii-gawind (and so truly they began upon her) nandodamawind iniw onamanan (being asked for the onaman / magic paint). Anawi idash baabange migiwe (though she gave but a little at a time), miibood noondesenid (yet there was not enough to go around). Anawi go baabange migiwe (though she gave but a little at a time), geget sa niibiwa odayaan anokaajigan minik maanind (truly she had much in goods that were given her). Aba’pii ningo-gikinooniwin ani-aawaninig (when a cycle of the seasons came round) mii zhigwa gii-debisenig mooshkinenig iw endaawaad anokaajigan (there were goods enough to fill the place where she dwelt). Ningoding go abinoojiiyan owaabamaan (by and by she bore a child). Kīcpindạc win awägwän a‘pidci käbicigwätisigwän, mi aᵘ abiding ä‘ta tayanda‘ki. Intawā dạc mānō ayāⁿ ‘aa‘ᵘ kigwisis mītiⁿ icikīwän.”
