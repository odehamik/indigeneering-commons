A MOOSE AND HIS OFFSPRING.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Mōᵃs wīʼpibōnici, kayä iniʼᵘ wīwạn. Nīciwa unitcānisaᵉ, kayä päcigowạn iniʼᵘ udōckinawämạn; mīᵃiᵃ∙ʼᵘ nānạniwāt. Mīsaʼ äjikuʼtätciwāt, iniʼᵘ anicinābän kusāwāt. Āʼpidcisa wâwâsa icā aᵘ uckinawä, mīsāʼpanā änutạcimāt aʼkiwäⁿzī. “Kigạmicāᵃ∙ạ∙nik ạnicinābä.” Kāwīn ạnicīʼtạnzī. Ninguting pimiʼkawäwạn paʼkān mōᵃsōn; ugiʼkänimän iʼkwäwinit. Mīsaʼ mādāᵃ∙ạ∙nāt, mīsaʼ wâbạmāt iniᵘ kīwiwit.

**Mooz wii-biboonizhi, gaye iniw wiiwan. Niizhiwa onijaanisa’, gaye bezhigowan iniw odoshkinaweman; mii i’iw nananiwaad. Mii sa ezhi-godatchiwaad, iniw anishinaaben gosaawaad. Aapijisa waawaasa izhaa aw oshkinawe, mii sa-ban enootachimaad akiwenzii. “Gigamizhaa-anik anishinaabe.” Gaawiin anishi’tanzii. Ningoding bimi’kawewan bakaan moozoon; ogii’kenimaan ikwewinid. Mii sa madaa’anaad, mii sa waabamaad iniw giiwiwid.**

*The Moose was about to go into camp for the winter, and also his wife. Two (in number) were their children, and there was a youth among them; therefore they were five. It was so that they were in fear, of people they were in fear. On very long journeys frequently went the youth, whereupon continually was the old man trying to dissuade him (not to go so far). “Upon your trail might come the people.” But (the youth) paid no heed. Once (he saw) the tracks of another Moose; he knew it was a cow. Accordingly he followed after her, whereat, on seeing her, he took her to wife.*

Mooz wii-biboonizhi (the Moose was about to winter — \*stem flagged\*), gaye iniw wiiwan (and his wife too). Niizhiwa onijaanisa’ (two were their children), gaye bezhigowan iniw odoshkinaweman (and one of them was a youth); mii i’iw nananiwaad (so they were five — \*naanan ‘five’; stem flagged\*). Mii sa ezhi-godatchiwaad (and so they were fearful), iniw anishinaaben gosaawaad (of people they were afraid). Aapijisa waawaasa izhaa aw oshkinawe (very far away the youth would go), mii sa-ban enootachimaad akiwenzii (whereupon the old man kept trying to dissuade him — \*anood-/aanawendam-? stem flagged\*). “Gigamizhaa-anik anishinaabe.” (“the people might come upon your trail.”) Gaawiin anishi’tanzii (but he paid no heed — \*stem flagged\*). Ningoding bimi’kawewan bakaan moozoon (once he came on the tracks of another moose); ogii’kenimaan ikwewinid (he knew it was a cow). Mii sa madaa’anaad (and so he followed her trail), mii sa waabamaad iniw giiwiwid (and so, on seeing her, he took her to wife — \*wiidigemaa-; stem flagged\*).

*wīʼpibōnici / wii-biboonizhi “about to go into camp for the winter” — biboon- ‘winter’ + the going-into-winter-camp stem, flagged. nānạniwāt — “they were five (in number),” naanan ‘five’ + a being-so-many verb; stem/segmentation flagged. änutạcimāt aʼkiwäⁿzī — Jones “the old man trying to dissuade him,” stem flagged (cf. ānugi- ‘in vain’ family). ạnicīʼtạnzī — “he paid no heed,” stem flagged. Last clause “he took her to wife” fused into Jones’s next page (p497); kīwiwit / giiwiwid the marriage stem, flagged.*

(2) Cigwagu māgu miniʼk wāwit, paʼkān awīya iniʼᵘ mōᵃzōn udōdisikuwān; nāsāb wāwītigämigut; kägäʼt wītigämāt. Mīdac iᵉiʼᵘ nīⁿc ayāwāt iʼkwäwaᵉ.

**Zhigwa go maagu minik waawid, bakaan awiya iniw moozoon odoodisigowaan; naasaab wawiidigemigod; geget wiidigemaad. Mii dash i’iw niizh ayaawaad ikwewa’.**

*During this time that he had her for wife, by another Moose were they visited; and by her, as by the other, was he desired for a husband; to be sure, he married her. Therefore two were the wives he had.*

Zhigwa go maagu minik waawid (now during the while he had her to wife), bakaan awiya iniw moozoon odoodisigowaan (by another moose they were visited); naasaab wawiidigemigod (likewise was he desired for a husband by her — \*stem flagged\*); geget wiidigemaad (sure enough, he married her). Mii dash i’iw niizh ayaawaad ikwewa’ (and so two were the wives he had).

*māgu / maagu — particle; drafted, flagged. udōdisikuwān “they were visited by (another moose),” odoodis- ‘visit’; flagged. nāsāb / naasaab ‘likewise, the same way’.*

(3) Kägäʼt wawība mīgātiwaᵉ. Ningutingigu mādcā, ōsạn icāt. Kāʼtagwicing äjikanōnat iniʼᵘ ōsan: “Kägäʼt, nōs, nīⁿc nintayāwāg igiʼᵘ iʼkwäwạg.” Ugạnōnigōn: “Ningwisis, käguʼ icictcigäʼkän tcinīciwāt tci∙a∙yāwạtwā igiʼᵘ iʼkwäwạg. Kạnạbạtc tạbāʼtā∙i∙tiwạg.”

**Geget wawiiba miigaadiwa’. Ningoding igo maajaa, oosan izhaad. Gaa-dagwishing ezhi-ganoonaad iniw oosan: “Geget, noos, niizh nindayaawaag igiw ikwewag.” Oganoonigoon: “Ningwisis, gego izhichigekken ji-niizhiwaad ji-ayaawadwaa igiw ikwewag. Ganabaj daa-baataa’idiwag.”**

*In truth, very frequently did they fight. And once he went away, to his father he went. After he was come, he spoke to his father, saying: “Verily, my father, two (are) the women I have.” He was addressed by him saying: “My son, do not bring it about that there be two women for you to have. Perhaps they might do harm to each other.”*

Geget wawiiba miigaadiwa’ (truly, very often they fought). Ningoding igo maajaa (and once he set off), oosan izhaad (going to his father). Gaa-dagwishing (after he arrived) ezhi-ganoonaad iniw oosan (he spoke to his father): “Geget, noos, niizh nindayaawaag igiw ikwewag.” (“truly, father, two are the women I have.”) Oganoonigoon (he was addressed by him): “Ningwisis, gego izhichigekken (“my son, do not bring it about) ji-niizhiwaad ji-ayaawadwaa igiw ikwewag (that you have two women). Ganabaj daa-baataa’idiwag.” (perhaps they might harm one another — \*baataa’idi- ‘harm each other’ stem flagged\*.)

*tạbāʼtā∙i∙tiwạg / daa-baataa’idiwag “they might harm each other,” reciprocal; the harm-stem flagged.*

(4) “Äyeᵉ,” udinān ōsạn. Mīdạc iᵘ wäyābạninig äjikīwät; cigwa tạgwicin ändāwāt. Mīsaʼ kägäʼt kīnisîmint päjig iniʼᵘ wīwạn.

**“Aye’,” odinaan oosan. Mii dash iw wayaabaninig ezhi-giiwed; zhigwa dagwishin endaawaad. Mii sa geget gii-nisimind bezhig iniw wiiwan.**

*“Ay,” he said to his father. And then on the morrow he went back home; in a while he arrived at where they dwelt. Whereupon, sure enough, (he found) that one of his wives had been killed.*

“Aye’,” odinaan oosan (“ay,” he said to his father). Mii dash iw wayaabaninig (and then on the morrow) ezhi-giiwed (he went home); zhigwa dagwishin endaawaad (in a while he arrived where they lived). Mii sa geget gii-nisimind bezhig iniw wiiwan (and sure enough, one of his wives had been killed).

(5) Ningutingigu tạgwicinuᵉ mōⁿzūᵉ nīc. Cigwa kạnōnā: “Ānīc wäntcinīcōʼkwäwäyạn? Kāwin kitāgīcictcigäsī.”

**Ningoding igo dagwishinow moozoo’ niizh. Zhigwa ganoonaa: “Aaniish wenji-niizhookwaweyan? Gaawiin gidaa-gii-izhichigesii.”**

*And once there arrived two other Moose. Presently they spoke to him, saying: “Why did you have two wives? You should not have done so.”*

Ningoding igo dagwishinow moozoo’ niizh (and once two other moose arrived). Zhigwa ganoonaa (presently he was spoken to): “Aaniish wenji-niizhookwaweyan? (“why did you have two wives? — \*niizhookwe- ‘have two women’ stem flagged\*) Gaawiin gidaa-gii-izhichigesii.” (you should not have done so.”)

*mōⁿzūᵉ nīc / moozoo’ niizh — “two (other) moose,” with the obviative-plural raised glyph on moozoo (flagged for verification). wäntcinīcōʼkwäwäyạn “why you had two wives,” niizho-ikwe- stem flagged.*

(6) Ānīc kīmōtc waᵉaʼᵘ uckī∙i∙nini äjipaʼkwạtcibināt iᵉiʼᵘ uniciwa, kā∙i∙ji∙a∙ʼpagināt ningābī∙ạ∙nung iji gwayaʼk.

**Aaniish giimooj wa’aw oshki-inini ezhi-bakwajibinaad i’iw onizhiwa, gaa-izhi-apaginaad ningaabii’anong izhi gwayak.**

*Now, in secret the youth had plucked out his testes, afterwards he flung them straight toward the west.*

Aaniish giimooj wa’aw oshki-inini (now, in secret the youth) ezhi-bakwajibinaad i’iw onizhiwa (plucked out his testes — \*bakwaji-bin- ‘pluck/tear out by hand’\*), gaa-izhi-apaginaad (then he flung them) ningaabii’anong izhi gwayak (straight toward the west).

*uniciwa / onizhiwa — “his testes,” drafted; flagged. ningābī∙ạ∙nung / ningaabii’anong ‘in/toward the west’.*

(7) Mīdạc äʼkitowāt īgiʼᵘ iʼkwäwạg: “Indawā, ninōʼpinanānānig igiʼᵘ kiniciwạg.”

**Mii dash ekidowaad igiw ikwewag: “Indawaa, nino’pinananaanig igiw ginizhiwag.”**

*And then said the women: “Therefore we will follow after your testes.”*

Mii dash ekidowaad igiw ikwewag (and then said the women): “Indawaa, nino’pinananaanig igiw ginizhiwag.” (“so then, we will follow after your testes.” — \*noopinan- ‘follow after’; possessor reading of ginizhiwag flagged\*)

*ninōʼpinanānānig — “we will follow after (them),” noopinan- ‘pursue’; the exact inflection flagged. kiniciwạg / ginizhiwag “your testes” (2nd-person possessor), flagged.*

(8) Mīdac iᵉiʼᵘ āʼpidci āʼkusi, agāwa ugạckiʼtōn. Kīwät ijāt ōsạn. Cigwa pīndigä, mīsa cigwa māsumigut iniyōsạn. “Mī∙i∙ʼᵘ ānugī∙u∙ndci∙i∙ninān. Wâwiyạg kītōntci∙ā∙ʼkus. Mī intawā pîsān ici∙ā∙yān.”

**Mii dash i’iw aapiji aakozi, agaawaa ogashki’toon. Giiwed izhaad oosan. Zhigwa biindige, mii sa zhigwa maazomigod iniyoosan. “Mii i’iw aanugii-onji-i’ininaan. Waawiyazh gii-doonji-aakos. Mii indawaa biisaan izhi-ayaan.”**

*Thereupon he became exceedingly ill, hardly was he able to go back to his father. In time he arrived within (the wigwam), whereupon then he began to undergo treatment from his father. “Such was the reason why I tried to dissuade you from your purpose. Because of this disobedience you became sick. Therefore now you should remain quietly by.”*

Mii dash i’iw aapiji aakozi (thereupon he became very ill), agaawaa ogashki’toon (he could hardly manage it). Giiwed izhaad oosan (he went home to his father). Zhigwa biindige (in time he came inside), mii sa zhigwa maazomigod iniyoosan (whereupon his father began to treat him — \*maazom-/mashkiki- treatment stem flagged\*). “Mii i’iw aanugii-onji-i’ininaan (“that is why in vain I told you so — \*fused; flagged\*). Waawiyazh gii-doonji-aakos (because of (this) wrongdoing you became sick — \*stem flagged\*). Mii indawaa biisaan izhi-ayaan.” (so now you should stay quietly by.”)

*māsumigut / maazomigod — “he was treated (by his father),” the doctoring/treatment stem flagged. ānugī∙u∙ndci∙i∙ninān — heavily fused ‘in vain I told you so’ (aanu(g)- + onji- + i(n)- ‘say to’); segmentation flagged. Wâwiyạg / waawiyazh ‘wrongly, amiss’ + the becoming-sick clause, flagged.*

(9) Cigwa āʼpitapibōnini. Ningutci pạbāʼtaciʼkäwạg aya∙āⁿ∙sạg. Sōgiʼponinig nạgạmōwạg igiʼᵘ ạbinōtcīⁿyạg mōzōⁿsạg. Kägäʼt minwäntạmōg zōgiʼpōninig:—

**Zhigwa aabitabiboonini. Ningoji babaa-dazhi-gewag aya-aanhsag. Zoogiponinig nagamowag igiw abinoojiinyag moozoonsag. Geget minwendamog zoogiponinig:—**

*By this time the winter was halfway gone. In certain places roundabout where they lived wandered the calves. When it snowed, (then) sang the young Moose. Truly happy they were when it snowed:—*

Zhigwa aabitabiboonini (by now the winter was half gone — \*aabita-biboon- ‘mid-winter’\*). Ningoji babaa-dazhi-gewag aya-aanhsag (here and there roundabout wandered the calves — \*stem flagged\*). Zoogiponinig (when it snowed) nagamowag igiw abinoojiinyag moozoonsag (the young moose-calves sang). Geget minwendamog zoogiponinig (truly happy they were when it snowed):—

*pạbāʼtaciʼkäwạg aya∙āⁿ∙sạg — “the calves wandered about (in places),” babaa-dazhi- distributive; the calf-word aya∙āⁿ∙sạg and the stem flagged. zōgiʼpoᵒn- / zoogipon- ‘it snows’.*

(11) Mī äna∙ạ∙mawāt igiʼᵘ mōzōⁿsạg. Unōntaguwān ugiwān äjigạnōniguwāt: “Käguʼ ina∙ạ∙ngägun, kạnabạtc kagwatcinīwana∙a∙ʼgō unābạnaʼk kīcpîn nibiwa zōgiʼpuk.”

**Mii ena’amawaad igiw moozoonsag. Onoondagowaan ogiwaan ezhi-ganoonigowaad: “Gego ina’angegon, ganabaj gagwajiniiwana’gowaa-ban onaabana’k giishpin niibiwa zoogipog.”**

*Thus sang the young Moose. They were heard by their mother, by whom they were then addressed: “Do not sing such a song, lest perhaps you be laid low with a club on the hardened crust, if much snow falls.”*

Mii ena’amawaad igiw moozoonsag (thus sang the young moose — \*ina’amaw- ‘sing thus’ stem flagged\*). Onoondagowaan ogiwaan (they were heard by their mother) ezhi-ganoonigowaad (and were addressed by her): “Gego ina’angegon (“do not sing such a song), ganabaj gagwajiniiwana’gowaa-ban onaabana’k (lest you be clubbed down on the hard snow-crust — \*long fused verb; stem flagged\*) giishpin niibiwa zoogipog.” (if much snow falls.”)

*äna∙ạ∙mawāt / ena’amawaad — the singing/chanting stem ina’am-; flagged. kagwatcinīwana∙a∙ʼgō unābạnaʼk — “lest you be struck down with a club on the crust,” a long fused passive; segmentation flagged. unābạnaʼk ‘on the snow-crust (onaaban)’; flagged.*

(13) Ningutingigu āʼpitcigu ạniminu∙a∙ya aᵘ uckinawä. Mī∙i∙cigwa mādcāt ạbāgagwätusät; āʼpitcigu minōpạpimusä. Ningutingigu owâbạndān ayagawäʼtäyāsininig iᵉiʼᵘ ānaʼkwat; kägäʼt umānäntān tcinạgạtangibạn. Mīdạc iᵉiʼᵘ äcigạgwätcikạtank āʼpidci tciwâsa unạgạtān. Kägäʼt minwäntạm nạgạtank anaʼkwat. Äni∙i∙cigiwät; kā pīndigät ändāwāt, ugạnōnān ōsạn: “Nōs, kägäʼt kikīwạnîm äʼkidoyạn kicīʼkā aᵉaʼᵘ anicinābä kī∙i∙ʼkitoyạn. Nōngum kīʼkīcigaʼk ningīgạgwätcikạtān iᵉiʼᵘ ānaʼkwat, wâsa ningīnạgạtān. Mādcīna tā∙a∙pīsiʼkä waᵉaᵘ anicinābä.” Mīdac i∙i∙ᵘ äcikạnōnigut ōsạn: “Ningwisis, kägäʼt kigitcikidimägis wimänänimāt aᵉaʼᵘ ạnicinābä. Mạnidōwⁱ anicinābäʼk. Nōngum kigạgiʼkändān kīcpîn wawâsa wi∙i∙cāyạn wäntcimanitōwit anicinābä. Udawäᵉ kägäʼkwaᵉ kayä wäᵃwäᵉ, mī∙i∙ʼᵘ wändcigiciʼkād aᵉaᵘ anicinābä.”

**Ningoding igo aapiji ani-mino-ayaa aw oshkinawe. Mii i’iw zhigwa maajaad abaagagwedosed; aapiji go mino-babimose. Ningoding igo owaabandaan ayagawe-deyaasininig i’iw aanakwad; geget omaanendaan ji-nagadang-iban. Mii dash i’iw ezhi-gagwejigadang aapiji ji-waasa onagadaan. Geget minwendam nagadang aanakwad. Ani-izhi-giiwed; gaa-biindiged endaawaad, oganoonaan oosan: “Noos, geget gigiiwanim ekidoyan gizhiikaa a’aw anishinaabe gii-ikidoyan. Noongom gii-giizhigak ningii-gagwejigadaan i’iw aanakwad, waasa ningii-nagadaan. Maajiina daa-bisikaa wa’aw anishinaabe.” Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonigod oosan: “Ningwisis, geget gigichi-gidimaagiz wii-menenimaad a’aw anishinaabe. Manidoowi anishinaabeg. Noongom giga-gikendaan giishpin waawaasa wii-izhaayan wenji-manidoowid anishinaabe. Odawaa’ gegekwa’ gaye wewe’, mii i’iw wenji-gizhiikaad a’aw anishinaabe.”**

*And in course of time to very much better health was the youth restored. Therefore then he started off, trying to see how he could travel; and very comfortably did he walk along. And once he saw where the cloud had cast a shadow; in truth, he believed that he could outstrip it. Accordingly, when he ran it a race, a very great distance behind he left it. Truly pleased was he to have outrun the cloud. Then on his homeward way he went. When he entered into where they lived, he spoke to his father, saying: “My father, of a truth, you deceived me when you said that speedy is a human being. On this day now past I raced with the cloud, far behind I outran it. Not so swift as that would a human being be.” Thereupon he was addressed by his father saying: “My dear son, of a truth, you are greatly to be pitied for regarding with contempt a human being. Of the nature of a manitou is a human being. To-day you shall learn, if very far you intend to go, how it is that a human being is of the nature of a manitou. He makes use of bird-hawks and swans, and on that account speedy is a human being.”*

Mii dash i’iw ezhi-gagwejigadang (so he raced it) aapiji ji-waasa onagadaan (and left it far behind). Geget minwendam nagadang aanakwad (truly pleased to have outrun the cloud). Ani-izhi-giiwed (then he went home); gaa-biindiged endaawaad (when he came inside) oganoonaan oosan (he spoke to his father): “Noos, geget gigiiwanim (“father, truly you deceived me) ekidoyan gizhiikaa a’aw anishinaabe gii-ikidoyan (when you said the human being is swift). Noongom gii-giizhigak ningii-gagwejigadaan i’iw aanakwad (today I raced the cloud), waasa ningii-nagadaan (far behind I left it). Maajiina daa-bisikaa wa’aw anishinaabe.” (not even that swift would a human being be — \*bizikaa-? stem flagged\*) Mii dash i’iw ezhi-ganoonigod oosan (then his father addressed him): “Ningwisis, geget gigichi-gidimaagiz (“my son, you are greatly to be pitied) wii-menenimaad a’aw anishinaabe (for holding the human being in contempt — \*meneni m- ‘scorn’ stem flagged\*). Manidoowi anishinaabeg (of manitou-nature is the human being). Odawaa’ gegekwa’ gaye wewe’ (he makes use of bird-hawks and swans), mii i’iw wenji-gizhiikaad a’aw anishinaabe.” (that is why the human being is swift.”)
