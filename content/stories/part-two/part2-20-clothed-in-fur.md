WÄMĪSĀ‘KWĀ.

**WEMIISAAKWAA.**

CLOTHED-IN-FUR.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ninguding ayāwạg päjik kwīwizäns omisäⁿyạn oni‘tāwīgi∙i∙gōn; Wämīsā‘kwa ijini‘kāso ‘aᵍa’ᵘ kwīwisäns. Ā‘pitci ni‘tanandawändcigä; a‘pī∙i∙dạc änimindidot unisāᵍ wāwāckäciwaᵍ, pîmwād adi‘kwaᵍ gayä. Mīdạc kī∙ạ∙nōnād omisäyạn opạpīsi‘kawāgạn,—mīsā‘kwayānạn,—tcipāpīs∙i∙‘kawāgạnigäd. Mī∙i∙dạc kägä‘t kī∙u∙ji‘tōd ‘iᵍi’ᵘ papīsi‘kawāgạn ‘aᵍa’ᵘ uskinīgi‘kwä.

**Ningoding ayaawag bezhig gwiiwizens omisenyan onitaawigi’igoon; Wemiisaakwaa izhinikaazo a’aw gwiiwizens. Aapiji nitaa-nandawenjige; apii idash eni-mindidod onisaag waawaashkeshiwag, bimwaad adikwag gaye. Mii dash gii-anoonaad omiseyan obabiisikawaagan — miisaakwayaanan — ji-baabiisikawaaganiged. Mii idash geget gii-ozhitood i’iw babiisikawaagan a’aw oshkiniigikwe.**

*Once on a time there lived a boy and his elder sister, by whom he was reared; Clothed-in-Fur was the name of the boy. He was a very good hunter of game; and when he was growing up, he killed deer, and he shot caribou also. Accordingly he had his elder sister make a coat, — a coat of fur, — that he might have it to wear. It was true that the maiden made the coat.*

Ningoding (once) ayaawag (there lived) bezhig gwiiwizens (a boy) omisenyan (his elder sister; \*obviative\*) onitaawigi’igoon (by her was he reared; \*inverse\*); Wemiisaakwaa izhinikaazo (Clothed-in-Fur was he named) a’aw gwiiwizens (that boy). Aapiji nitaa-nandawenjige (he was very skilled at hunting); apii idash eni-mindidod (and as he was growing up) onisaag waawaashkeshiwag (he killed deer), bimwaad adikwag gaye (and shot caribou too). Mii dash gii-anoonaad omiseyan (then he engaged his elder sister) obabiisikawaagan (for a coat) — miisaakwayaanan (a fur robe) — ji-baabiisikawaaganiged (that she make the coat). Mii idash geget gii-ozhitood (and truly she made) i’iw babiisikawaagan (that coat) a’aw oshkiniigikwe (that young woman).

(2) Ningudingidạc apī kînwäⁿj äyāwād ogạnōnān omîsäⁿyạn: “Tạga, nîmîsäⁿ\! Ningabạbāmādes.” Mēdạc kägä‘t kipạgitinigut omîsäⁿyạn. Mī kīmādcād ‘aᵍa’ᵘ Wämīsā‘kwā.

**Ningoding idash apii ginwenzh eyaawaad oganoonaan omisenyan: “Daga, nimisenh\! Ninga-babaamaades.” Mii dash geget gii-bagidinigod omisenyan. Mii gii-maajaad a’aw Wemiisaakwaa.**

*Now once, after they had been continuing there for a long while, he spoke to his elder sister, saying: “I say, my elder sister\! I am going off on a journey.” Whereupon truly was he granted leave by his elder sister. So away went Clothed-in-Fur.*

Ningoding idash (now once) apii ginwenzh eyaawaad (after they had long been there) oganoonaan omisenyan (he spoke to his elder sister): “Daga, nimisenh\! (“I say, my elder sister\!) Ninga-babaamaades.” (I am going off on a journey.”) Mii dash geget gii-bagidinigod (whereupon truly was he given leave by her; \*inverse\*) omisenyan (his elder sister). Mii gii-maajaad (so departed) a’aw Wemiisaakwaa (that Clothed-in-Fur).

(3) Ningudingidạc odōditān ōdäna, mīdạc kīpindigät wigiwāmänzig mindimōⁿyäⁿyạn imāⁿ tāwạn. Mīdạc wīndạmāgut: “Ogowä anicinābäg mōⁿjạg odạminōwạg. Kayä gīn kīgawījāmigō. Ạnōdc igitcigäwạg: pāgā∙ā∙dowäwag, i‘kwäwạg gayä pasi‘kawäwạg; nāningutinōⁿ māmawi udạminōwạg.”

**Ningoding idash ododitaan oodena, mii dash gii-biindiged wiigiwaamensing mindimoonyenyan imaa daawan. Mii dash wiindamaagod: “Ogowe anishinaabeg moozhag odaminowag. Gaye giin giga-wiijaamigoo. Anooj izhichigewag: baagaa’adowewag, ikwewag gaye basikawewag; naaningodinoonh maamawi odaminowag.”**

*Now, once he came to a town, whereupon he entered into a small wigwam where an old woman was abiding. And this was what she said to him: “These people are always playing at games. You too will be asked (to join in play). All kinds of things they do: they play ball, and the women play the double-ball game; sometimes all play together.”*

Ningoding idash (now once) ododitaan oodena (he came to a town), mii dash gii-biindiged (whereupon he entered) wiigiwaamensing (into a small wigwam) mindimoonyenyan imaa daawan (where an old woman was abiding; \*obviative\*). Mii dash wiindamaagod (and this was she told him; \*inverse\*): “Ogowe anishinaabeg (these people) moozhag odaminowag (are always playing at games). Gaye giin giga-wiijaamigoo (you too will be asked to join). Anooj izhichigewag (all kinds of things they do): baagaa’adowewag (they play ball), ikwewag gaye basikawewag (and the women play the double-ball game); naaningodinoonh maamawi odaminowag (sometimes all play together).”

(4) Wayābạnīnig idạc nīⁿj uskînawän kī‘pījāwạn kī‘pikạnōnigut: “Ambä nīdci\! Wīdcī∙i∙cinām odạminoyāng\!”

**Wayaabaninig idash niinzh oshkinawen gii-biizhaawan gii-bi-ganoonigod: “Ambe niijii\! Wiijii’ishinaam odaminoyaang\!”**

*And on the morrow by two youths who came over he was addressed: “Come hither, my friend\! Join with us in the games we play\!”*

Wayaabaninig idash (and on the morrow) niinzh oshkinawen (two youths; \*obviative\*) gii-biizhaawan (came over) gii-bi-ganoonigod (and he was addressed by them; \*inverse\*): “Ambe niijii\! (Come hither, my friend\!) Wiijii’ishinaam (join with us; \*imperative, exclusive us\*) odaminoyaang\! (in the games we play\!)”

(5) Mēdạc kägä‘t kī∙ạ∙niwīdcīwād; ijād kayäwīn ändạci∙o∙daminung. Kạbägicig kī∙u∙dạminōwạg. Nīⁿjidạc uskinīgi‘kwäᵍ umī‘kidcī∙i∙gōᵍ,—mạdcīkī‘kwäwisaᵍ; kāwīndạc ominwänimāsīn. Mīdạc gī‘kiwäd wänāgucig ō‘kumisạn ändānit. “Tạga, nō‘kumis\! kīdạsạp kīwi‘tāyābīgin omāⁿ ändāyạn\! māgija tabījāwạg mạdci‘kikwäwisạg; ningimī‘kidcī∙i∙gōg,” i‘kido Wämīsā‘kwā. Täbi‘kạdinik kī‘pījāwạg īgi’ᵘ uskinīgi‘kwäg. Ā‘pidci unīcici ‘aᵍaᵘ’ Wämīsā‘kwā. Kāwīn ogīkạskitōsīnāwa tcipīndigäwād, ạsạbīn kibābīgạmōnid; gägādạc weyābang kī‘kīwäwạg īgi’ᵘ uskinīgi‘kwäg.

**Mii dash geget gii-ani-wiijiiwaad; izhaad gaye wiin endazhi-odaminong. Gabe-gizhig gii-odaminowag. Niinzh idash oshkiniigikweg omiikijii’igoog — maji-kiikwewisag; gaawiin idash ominwenimaasiin. Mii dash gii-giiwed wenaagoshig ookomisan endaanid. “Daga, nookomis\! giidasab giiwitaayaabiigin omaa endaayan\! maagizhaa da-biizhaawag maji-kikwewisag; ningii-miikijii’igoog,” ikido Wemiisaakwaa. Debikadinig gii-biizhaawag igi’w oshkiniigikweg. Aapiji onishishi a’aw Wemiisaakwaa. Gaawiin ogii-gashkitoosiinaawaa ji-biindigewaad, asabiin gii-baabiigamoonid; gegaa idash weyaabang gii-giiwewag igi’w oshkiniigikweg.**

*Whereupon truly he went, joining in with them; he too went to where the play was going on. All day long they played. Now, by two maidens was he annoyed, — by the Foolish Maidens; and he did not like them. Thereupon back he went in the evening to where his grandmother lived. “I say, my grandmother\! coil your net about this place where you live\! for perhaps hither may come the Foolish Maidens; I was annoyed by them,” said Clothed-in-Fur. When it was night, then hither came the maidens. Very handsome was Clothed-in-Fur. They were not able to enter, for the net was in their way; and when it was nearly morning, back home went the maidens.*

Mii dash geget gii-ani-wiijiiwaad (whereupon truly he went along with them); izhaad gaye wiin (he too going) endazhi-odaminong (to where the play was). Gabe-gizhig gii-odaminowag (all day long they played). Niinzh idash oshkiniigikweg (now, two young women) omiikijii’igoog (annoyed him; \*inverse\*) — maji-kiikwewisag (the Bad/Foolish Maidens); gaawiin idash ominwenimaasiin (and he did not like them). Mii dash gii-giiwed wenaagoshig (so back he went in the evening) ookomisan endaanid (to where his grandmother lived). “Daga, nookomis\! (I say, my grandmother\!) giidasab giiwitaayaabiigin (coil your net about) omaa endaayan\! (this place where you live\!) maagizhaa da-biizhaawag (for perhaps hither may come) maji-kikwewisag (the Bad/Foolish Maidens); ningii-miikijii’igoog (I was annoyed by them),” ikido Wemiisaakwaa (said Clothed-in-Fur). Debikadinig (when it was night) gii-biizhaawag (hither came) igi’w oshkiniigikweg (those maidens). Aapiji onishishi (very handsome was) a’aw Wemiisaakwaa (that Clothed-in-Fur). Gaawiin ogii-gashkitoosiinaawaa ji-biindigewaad (they were not able to enter), asabiin gii-baabiigamoonid (for the net was in their way); gegaa idash weyaabang (and when it was nearly morning) gii-giiwewag (back home went) igi’w oshkiniigikweg (those maidens).

(6) Kāwâbạngidạc mīnawā kī‘pinạndomā aᵘ skinawä; kạbägījik mänäwā kī∙o∙dạminum. Mīgu mīnawā kā∙i∙jimī‘kidci∙i∙ngut ‘iᵍi’ᵘ mạdcīki‘kwäwisaᵍ. Mīnawā gī‘kiwä wänāgucig ō‘kumisạn ändānit. Mīnawā ugī∙i∙nān o‘kumisạn: “Kidạsạp kīwi‘taiyābīgin omāⁿ ändaiyạn\!” / Mī gägä‘t kā‘tōdạng ‘aᵍa’ᵘ mindimōyäⁿ.

**Gaa-waabang idash miinawaa gii-bi-nandomaa aw shkinawe; gabe-giizhig miinawaa gii-odaminom. Mii go miinawaa gaa-izhi-miikijii’ingod i’iw maji-kikwewisag. Miinawaa gii-giiwe wenaagoshig ookomisan endaanid. Miinawaa ogii-inaan okomisan: “Gidasab giiwitaayaabiigin omaa endaayan\!” / Mii geget gaa-doodang a’aw mindimooyenh.**

*And on the morrow they came again to invite the youth; all day long again they played at games. Thereupon again he was annoyed by the Foolish Maidens. Again back he went in the evening to where his grandmother lived. He repeated to his grandmother: “Coil your net about this place where you live\!” / That truly was what the old woman did.*

Gaa-waabang idash (and on the morrow) miinawaa gii-bi-nandomaa (again was invited) aw shkinawe (that youth); gabe-giizhig miinawaa gii-odaminom (all day long again was there playing). Mii go miinawaa (thereupon again) gaa-izhi-miikijii’ingod (was he annoyed by) i’iw maji-kikwewisag (the Bad/Foolish Maidens). Miinawaa gii-giiwe wenaagoshig (again back he went in the evening) ookomisan endaanid (to where his grandmother lived). Miinawaa ogii-inaan okomisan (he repeated to his grandmother): “Gidasab giiwitaayaabiigin (coil your net about) omaa endaayan\! (this place where you live\!)” Mii geget gaa-doodang (that truly was what she did) a’aw mindimooyenh (that old woman).

(7) Mīnawā kī‘pi∙i∙jāwag īgi’ᵘ i‘kwäwạg; kạbädibi‘k kīnạgāyābī∙i∙cinōg ạsabīng īgi’ᵘ i‘kwäwạg. Wayābạninig kägā kimạdwäkīwäwạg.

**Miinawaa gii-bi-izhaawag igi’w ikwewag; gabe-dibik gii-nagaayaabii’ishinoog asabiing igi’w ikwewag. Wayaabaninig gegaa gii-madwe-giiwewag.**

*Again hither came the women; all night long were the women bothered with the net. When it was nearly morning, they could be heard going away.*

Miinawaa gii-bi-izhaawag (again hither came) igi’w ikwewag (those women); gabe-dibik (all night long) gii-nagaayaabii’ishinoog asabiing (were they held up by the net; \*the net-line blocked them\*) igi’w ikwewag (those women). Wayaabaninig gegaa (when it was nearly morning) gii-madwe-giiwewag (they could be heard going home).

(8) Mīdạc änād ō‘kumisạn: “Nindawā\! ningamādcā,” i‘kido ‘aᵍaᵘ Wämīsā‘kwā.

**Mii dash enaad ookomisan: “Nindawaa\! ninga-maajaa,” ikido a’aw Wemiisaakwaa.**

*Thereupon he said to his grandmother: “Confound it\! I am going (back home),” said Clothed-in-Fur.*

Mii dash enaad ookomisan (thereupon he said to his grandmother): “Nindawaa\! (Confound it\!/I might as well\!) ninga-maajaa (I am going),” ikido a’aw Wemiisaakwaa (said that Clothed-in-Fur).

(9) Mēdạc ägut o‘kumisạn: “Kigidimāgis nōjis. Kāwīn kitānạgạnāsīg, ā‘pidci kijī‘kāwạg.”

**Mii dash egod okomisan: “Gigidimaagis noozhis. Gaawiin gidaa-naganaasiig, aapiji gizhiikaawag.”**

*Whereupon he was told by his grandmother: “You are to be pitied, my grandson. You could not leave them behind, so exceedingly fast do they walk.”*

Mii dash egod okomisan (whereupon he was told by his grandmother; \*inverse\*): “Gigidimaagis (you are to be pitied) noozhis (my grandson). Gaawiin gidaa-naganaasiig (you could not leave them behind), aapiji gizhiikaawag (so very fast do they walk).”

(10) “Mānu nīngamādcā,” i‘kido ‘aᵍaᵘ uskinawä. Mēdạc kägä‘t kīmādcād ‘aᵍaᵘ inini, kạbägījik pimibạ‘tō. Ninguding unōndawān awīya pigāgigidōnit udānāng. Mī īni’ᵘ mạdcīgi‘kwäwisaᵍ ubidājimigōn. “Kidimāgizi Wämīsā‘kwā ‘nīndānạgajiwä’ änädạng. Käwīn mi‘tcāsinōn owä a‘ki.”

**“Maanoo ninga-maajaa,” ikido a’aw oshkinawe. Mii dash geget gii-maajaad a’aw inini, gabe-giizhig bimibatoo. Ningoding onondawaan awiya bi-gaagiigidonid odaanaang. Mii ini’w maji-giikwewisag o-bidaajimigon. “Gidimaagizi Wemiisaakwaa ‘nindaa-naganaajiwe’ enendang. Gaawiin michaasinoon owe aki.”**

*“But nevertheless I am going,” said the youth. It was true that away started the man, all day long he went running. Suddenly he heard the approaching sound of somebody talking behind him. It was the Foolish Maidens who came talking about him. “To be pitied is Clothed-in-Fur if ‘I can leave them behind’ he thinks. Not large is this earth.”*

“Maanoo (nevertheless/anyway) ninga-maajaa (I am going),” ikido a’aw oshkinawe (said that youth). Mii dash geget gii-maajaad (it was true that away started) a’aw inini (that man), gabe-giizhig bimibatoo (all day long he ran). Ningoding onondawaan (suddenly he heard) awiya bi-gaagiigidonid (somebody approaching talking) odaanaang (behind him). Mii ini’w maji-giikwewisag (it was the Bad/Foolish Maidens) o-bidaajimigon (bringing word about him; \*inverse\*). “Gidimaagizi Wemiisaakwaa (to be pitied is Clothed-in-Fur) ‘nindaa-naganaajiwe’ enendang (if ‘I can outrun-and-leave them’ he thinks). Gaawiin michaasinoon (not large is) owe aki (this earth).”

(11) Änigu‘k ānupimibạ‘tō; äckạm päcu’ pigāgīgidiwaᵍ. Mī∙i∙dec äji∙a∙‘kwādawäbạ‘tōd kistciwīgwāsing ā‘pidci pāgwäpạgạdinig. Päjikidạc ạnībīc ugī∙o∙dā‘pinān; mīdạc i∙i∙māⁿ kī∙ạ∙nimindcimīt ạnībīcing, wāsa’dạc kījiwäbāsin ạnībīc. Mīdạc mīnawā kī∙ạ∙ni∙u∙ndcimādcād.

**Enigok aano-bimibatoo; eshkam besho’ bi-gaagiigidiwag. Mii idash ezhi-akwaandawebatood gichi-wiigwaasing aapiji baagwebagadinig. Bezhig idash aniibiish ogii-odaapinaan; mii dash i’imaa gii-ani-minjimiid aniibiishing, waasa’ idash gii-zhiwebaasin aniibiish. Mii dash miinawaa gii-ani-onji-maajaad.**

*All the faster he then tried to run; yet nearer still they came talking. Thereupon he climbed a tall birch which was very thick with foliage. Now a single leaf he took; whereupon away he went clinging to the leaf, and a long way off was the leaf wafted by the wind. Thereupon from there he again started on his way.*

Enigok aano-bimibatoo (with all his might he vainly ran; \*aano- in-vain\*); eshkam besho’ (nearer still) bi-gaagiigidiwag (they came talking). Mii idash ezhi-akwaandawebatood (thereupon he climbed at a run) gichi-wiigwaasing (a great birch) aapiji baagwebagadinig (very thick with foliage). Bezhig idash aniibiish (a single leaf) ogii-odaapinaan (he took); mii dash i’imaa gii-ani-minjimiid aniibiishing (whereupon he went clinging to the leaf), waasa’ idash gii-zhiwebaasin aniibiish (and far away was the leaf wafted by the wind). Mii dash miinawaa gii-ani-onji-maajaad (thereupon from there he again started on his way).

(12) Mīnawādec mạdcigi‘kwäwisạg, tägucinowād imāⁿ wīgwās pạda‘kidänig i‘kidōwạg: “Mī omāⁿ a‘pạnä kī∙a∙‘kwādawäd kinābäminān.” Mē∙i∙dạc ä‘kidōwād: “Kawā∙ạ∙ndā owä wīgwās\!” Pepäjik wâgā‘kwạdōzạn udaiyānāwān. Mī∙i∙dec kawā∙a∙mō∙o∙wād ‘iᵍi’ᵘ wīgwās. A‘pī∙i∙dạc käwisäg ‘iᵍi’ᵘ wīgwās kī∙a∙‘pa∙i∙diwạg, kāwīndạc ugīmi‘kawāsīwān. Mī∙i∙dạc ạndawâbạndạmowād tcipimi‘kawänit, kāwīndec ōmi‘kawāsiwāwān. Mī∙i∙dạc änād ucīmäyạn: “Taga, nicīmä\! ạgīndadā ändasobạga‘k o∙o∙ wīgwās\!” Kägä‘tidạc kā∙a∙gindạmowād, päjik kāwīn gägō ạnībīc. Mī∙i∙dec kīnạndawâbạndạmowād ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ạnībīc; nawạdc wāsaᵉ mī∙i∙dạc kīmi‘kạmowād ạnībīc. Mīdạc imāⁿ äni∙ō∙ndcipimi‘kawäd ‘aᵍaᵘ Wämīsā‘kwā. Mī∙i∙dạc mīnawā nō‘pinanāwād.

**Miinawaa dash maji-gikwewisag, dagoshinowaad imaa wiigwaas badakidenig ikidowag: “Mii omaa apane gii-akwaandawed ginaabeminaan.” Mii idash ekidowaad: “Gawa’andaa owe wiigwaas\!” Bebezhig waagaakwadoozan odayaanaawaan. Mii idash gawa’amoowaad i’iw wiigwaas. Apii idash gewiseg i’iw wiigwaas gii-apa’idiwag, gaawiin idash ogii-mikawaasiiwaan. Mii idash andawaabandamowaad ji-bimikawenid, gaawiin idash omikawaasiwaawaan. Mii idash enaad oshiimeyan: “Daga, nishiime\! agindadaa endaso-bagak o’o wiigwaas\!” Geget idash gaa-agindamowaad, bezhig gaawiin gegoo aniibiish. Mii idash gii-nandawaabandamowaad i’iw aniibiish; nawaj waasa mii idash gii-mikamowaad aniibiish. Mii dash imaa eni-onji-bimikawed a’aw Wemiisaakwaa. Mii idash miinawaa noopinanaawaad.**

*And as for the Foolish Maidens, when they came to the place where the birch was standing, they said: “It is up here where our husband has climbed and disappeared.” Whereupon they said: “Let us cut down this birch\!” Each had a small axe. So then they cut down the birch. And when down the birch fell, they ran to it at the same time, but they did not find him. And then they looked to see if he had left any tracks, but they did not find any trace of him. And then (the elder) said to her younger sister: “Come, my little sister\! let us count how many leaves there are upon this birch\!” And truly, after they had counted them, there was one leaf missing. Whereupon they started looking for that leaf; farther on the way was where they found the leaf. Thereupon from there was where Clothed-in-Fur began leaving the sign of his trail. Whereupon once more they pursued him.*

Miinawaa dash maji-gikwewisag (and as for the Bad/Foolish Maidens), dagoshinowaad imaa (when they arrived where) wiigwaas badakidenig (the birch stood) ikidowag (they said): “Mii omaa apane gii-akwaandawed (it is up here that climbed and was gone) ginaabeminaan (our husband).” Mii idash ekidowaad (whereupon they said): “Gawa’andaa owe wiigwaas\! (Let us cut down this birch\!)” Bebezhig waagaakwadoozan odayaanaawaan (each had a small axe; \*reduplicated each-one\*). Mii idash gawa’amoowaad (so they cut down) i’iw wiigwaas (that birch). Apii idash gewiseg (and when it fell) i’iw wiigwaas (that birch) gii-apa’idiwag (they ran to it together; \*reciprocal\*), gaawiin idash ogii-mikawaasiiwaan (but they did not find him). Mii idash andawaabandamowaad (then they looked for) ji-bimikawenid (whether he had left tracks), gaawiin idash omikawaasiwaawaan (but they found no trace of him). (91) Mīsa pînäwidis kīwạgōdä.

**Mii sa binewidis gii-agoode.**

*And so the gizzard of the ruffed grouse now hangs aloft.*

Mii sa binewidis (and so the gizzard of the ruffed grouse) gii-agoode (now hangs aloft) *(the closing that leaves the story to the listener’s wondering)*.

*“Mīsa pînäwidis kīwạgōdä.” — fuller forms: Mii sa binewi-jiid akoote / Mii sa binewidis akoote, “and thus the rump / the gizzard of the ruffed grouse hangs aloft.” This closing marks a story that asks for the listener’s own wondering and interpretation — it hints without imposing. \#20 prints the closing in full — the hanging-verb stands on the page (kīwạgōdä, gii-agoode, preterite) as in \#45, \#72, \#73, and \#74, where \#53/\#54 print a trailing ellipsis; this is the first full-form printing in binewidis (gizzard), and it carries no demonstrative and no ellipsis. (Per M., 2026.)*
