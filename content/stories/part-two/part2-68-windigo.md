WINDIGO.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk and/or Midāsugaⁿj (Series IV; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (“either … or both”; not credited on the story page).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ānīc tāwạg ạnicinābäg unītcānisiwā ä‘tạgu. Ānīc pibōnini. Kägä‘t inändạm ‘aᵉa’ᵘ inini ku‘tātcit nōtci∙ā∙t ạmi‘kwạn. Kägä‘tsa nīsānäntạm awiya ugi‘känimān. Mīdạc ningutingiku a∙i∙nạno‘kīt nōtci∙ā∙t īni’ᵘ ạmi‘kwạn, ningudingiku cigwa ‘aᵉa’ᵘ i‘kwä pāmāgu awiya mädā‘kunit wayä‘kwāgạmīng sāga∙i∙gạnīng ändāwāt. “Pabā‘pinisiwāgạn kinibūmin\!” udināⁿ unītcānisạ. Kägä‘t minditōwạn; kāwīn kạnagä ābi‘ta äyä‘kwä‘kusinit mi‘tigōᵍ ạgōsisīwạn. Mīdạc ini’ᵘ windigōn wâdisiguwāt. Mīsa‘ bīdā‘tạgā‘kōwạn, mīsa‘ nājibība‘tō waᵉa’ᵘ i‘kwä. Kākī‘tcikunayät pingwācāgit. Awinạgickawāt pidātạgā‘kunit, näyāgigu ogạnōnān: “Nōs\! kitạgwicinina?” udinān.

**Aaniish daawag anishinaabeg oniijaanisiwaa e-dagoo. Aaniish biboonini. Geget inendam a’aw inini gotaajid noojii’aad amikwan. Geget sa niisaanendam awiya ogii-kenimaan. Mii dash ningoding igo a-i-nanoki’d noojii’aad iniw amikwan, ningoding igo zhigwa a’aw ikwe baamaa go awiya medaa’koonid wayiikwaagamiing zaaga’igaaning endaawaad. “Babaa’binisiwaagan giniboomin\!” odinaan oniijaanisa. Geget mindidowan; gaawiin ganage aabita eyiikwe’koosinid mitigoog agoozisiiwan. Mii dash iniw wiindigoon waadizigowaad. Mii sa biidaa’dagaakowan, mii sa naajiibaabatoo a’aw ikwe. Gaa-giizhi-goonayed bingwaazhagid. Awi-nagishkawaad bidaadagaa’koonid, neyaag igo oganoonaan: “Noos\! gidagwishinina?” odinaan.**

*Now, there were abiding some people with only their children. Now, it was winter. Truly in the mind of the man rose thoughts of fear while on the hunt for beaver. Truly cautious he became of some creature (whose presence) he felt. And so once, while he was busily engaged catching beaver, at that time there suddenly (appeared to) the woman some one that was travelling over the ice from the other end of the lake, (and coming to) where she (and her family) lived. “Alas\! and now we are going to die\!” she said to her children. Truly big was the creature; not even half so tall (as he) were the trees, (such) was how high he reached. It was by the Windigō that they were visited. And as he came hitherward over the ice, then to the lake ran the woman (to meet him). Removing all her clothes, she had nothing on. On her way to meet him as he came along on the ice, and while yet some distance away, she spoke to him, saying: “O my father\! have you now returned home?” she said to him.*

Aaniish daawag anishinaabeg (now, there were some people living) oniijaanisiwaa e-dagoo (with only their children present). Aaniish biboonini (now it was winter). Geget inendam a’aw inini (truly the man had it in mind) gotaajid (being afraid; \*gotaaji- ‘be afraid/cautious’\*) noojii’aad amikwan (while hunting beaver). Geget sa niisaanendam (truly uneasy was he) awiya ogii-kenimaan (he sensed someone/something). Mii dash ningoding igo (and so once) a-i-nanoki’d noojii’aad iniw amikwan (while he was busy hunting that beaver), ningoding igo zhigwa a’aw ikwe (once, now, that woman —) baamaa go awiya medaa’koonid (suddenly someone travelling over the ice — \*stem flagged\*) wayiikwaagamiing zaaga’igaaning (from the far end of the lake) endaawaad (to where they lived). “Babaa’binisiwaagan (“alas\! — \*interjection flagged, 68-H5\*) giniboomin\!” (we are going to die\!”) odinaan oniijaanisa (she said to her children). Geget mindidowan (truly big was he); gaawiin ganage aabita (not even half) eyiikwe’koosinid mitigoog (so tall as the trees stood) agoozisiiwan (such was how high he reached — \*stem flagged\*). Mii dash iniw wiindigoon (and so by that Windigo) waadizigowaad (they were visited). Mii sa biidaa’dagaakowan (and so as he came over the ice toward them), mii sa naajiibaabatoo a’aw ikwe (the woman ran out to meet him). Gaa-giizhi-goonayed bingwaazhagid (having taken off her clothes, she was bare). Awi-nagishkawaad bidaadagaa’koonid (going to meet him as he came over the ice), neyaag igo oganoonaan (while yet some way off she spoke to him): “Noos\! gidagwishinina?” (“O my father\! have you come home?”) odinaan (she said to him).

(2) Mīdạc ä‘kitunit: “Kāwīn ningi‘kändạⁿzī tci∙o∙dānisiminān.”

**Mii dash ekidonid: “Gaawiin ningikendanzii ji-oodaanisiminaan.”**

*Thereupon said the other: “I don’t know if you are a daughter of mine.”*

Mii dash ekidonid (thereupon said the other; \*obviative\*): “Gaawiin ningikendanzii (“I do not know) ji-oodaanisiminaan.” (whether you are a daughter of mine.”)

(3) “Nōs\! kitạgwicinina?” udinān mīnawā. Äjikawicimōpạgisunit, äcitcāngitiyä‘kisunit kạnawâbạmigut.

**“Noos\! gidagwishinina?” odinaan miinawaa. Ezhi-gawishimobagizonid, ezhi-jaangidiye’kizonid ganawaabamigod.**

*“O my father\! have you now come home?” she said to him again. When he dropped down and lay (on the ice), when he lay resting on his hands and knees, then by him was she observed.*

“Noos\! gidagwishinina?” (“O my father\! have you come home?”) odinaan miinawaa (she said to him again). Ezhi-gawishimobagizonid (when he dropped down and lay; \*stem flagged\*), ezhi-jaangidiye’kizonid (when he settled on his hands and knees; \*stem flagged\*) ganawaabamigod (then she was looked over by him).

(4) Ānīc ugạnōnigōn: “Nindānis, mī‘i∙’ᵘ ijikiwän, ningasägi∙ā∙g nōcicäⁿyạg,” udinān īni’ᵘ. Mīsa‘ kägä‘t äjikīwät. “Oᵉō’ᵘ ici’: ‘Kimicōmisiwa kitạgwicin’, ici nōcicäⁿyạg.”

**Aaniish oganoonigoon: “Nindaanis, mii iw izhi-giiwen, ninga-zegi’aag noozhishenyag,” odinaan iniw. Mii sa geget ezhi-giiwed. “O’ow izhi: ‘Gimishoomisiwaa gidagwishin’, izhi noozhishenyag.”**

*Now, she was addressed by him saying: “My daughter, therefore go you home, lest I perhaps scare my little grandchildren,” he said to her. Thereupon truly back home she went. “This do you say to them: ‘Your grandfather has come home,’ say you to my little grandchildren.”*

Aaniish oganoonigoon (now she was addressed by him): “Nindaanis, mii iw izhi-giiwen (“my daughter, so then go home), ninga-zegi’aag noozhishenyag,” (lest I scare my little grandchildren,”) odinaan iniw (he said to her). Mii sa geget ezhi-giiwed (and so truly she went home). “O’ow izhi (“this say): ‘Gimishoomisiwaa gidagwishin’, (‘your grandfather has come home,’) izhi noozhishenyag.” (say to my little grandchildren.”)

(5) Cigwasa ạnipīndigä i∙i∙mā äntawat. “Nītcānisitug\! mạnitō nintäwänimā,” udinān ini’ᵘ unītcānisan. Mīsa‘ äcimạngạ‘kitōt ‘iᵉi’ᵘ äntāt. “ ‘Au, mī∙i∙’ᵘ icipīndigän, nōs\!” udinān.

**Zhigwa sa ani-biindige i’imaa endawaad. “Niijaanisitog\! manidoo nindenwenimaa,” odinaan iniw oniijaanisan. Mii sa ezhi-mangaki-tood i’iw endaad. “Aw, mii iw izhi-biindigen, noos\!” odinaan.**

*So she went (and) entered the place where she (and her family) lived. “O my children\! a manitou do I take him to be,” she said to her children. And then she enlarged the size of her home. “All right, now come on in, my father\!” she said to him.*

Zhigwa sa ani-biindige (so then she went in) i’imaa endawaad (where they lived). “Niijaanisitog\! (“O my children\!) manidoo nindenwenimaa,” (a manitou I take him to be,”) odinaan iniw oniijaanisan (she said to her children). Mii sa ezhi-mangaki-tood i’iw endaad (and so she made her dwelling bigger; \*stem flagged\*). “Aw, mii iw izhi-biindigen, noos\!” (“all right, so come in, my father\!”) odinaan (she said to him).

(6) Cigwasa gägä‘t pīndigä∙ō∙täwạn īni’ᵘ ōsạn. Ānīc mīsa‘ udōtcīmāni unītcānisaᵍ. Kägä‘t kicäwādisiwạn; mīsạgu a‘pạnä ta‘kunānit ‘iᵉi’ᵘ unītcānisaᵍ.

**Zhigwa sa geget biindige’otaawan iniw oosan. Aaniish mii sa odoojiimaani oniijaanisa. Geget gizhewaadiziwan; mii sa go apane dakonaanid i’iw oniijaanisa.**

*Then truly in came crawling that father of hers. So thereupon he kissed her children. Truly gentle was he, and it was then all the while that he had those children of hers in his hands.*

Zhigwa sa geget biindige’otaawan (then truly in he crept) iniw oosan (that father of hers). Aaniish mii sa odoojiimaani oniijaanisa (so then he kissed her children). Geget gizhewaadiziwan (truly gentle was he); mii sa go apane (and so all the while) dakonaanid i’iw oniijaanisa (he held those children of hers).

*pīndigä∙ō∙täwạn — “in he came crawling/creeping,” the biindige- + -’o- (move-self) verb; drafted, flagged light.*

(7) Kägä‘t minwäntạm ‘aᵉa’ᵘ i‘kwä. Cigwa ogạnōnigōn ōsạn: “Nindānis, ạmbä, tạga, awinagiskaᵘ na∙ā∙ngic\! Cigwa indigu pīdādạgā‘ku. Mīsa‘ gägä‘t oᵉō’ᵘ ici’, kägu’ wīn mīnawā inā‘kän ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kāgī∙i∙nạtwā īgi’ᵘ kinītcānisạg ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kā∙i∙nạtwā ‘ Mạnitō nindāwänimā ’ kī∙i∙‘kitoyạn. Mīdạc ici’ ‘aᵉa’ᵘ na∙ā∙ngic: ‘ Nōs kītạgwicin ’, ici’, māgicā ningasägi∙ā∙’’, udinān īni’ᵘ udānisan.

**Geget minwendam a’aw ikwe. Zhigwa oganoonigoon oosan: “Nindaanis, ambe, daga, awi-nagishk aw ningwanzh\! Zhigwa indigo biidaadagaa’ko. Mii sa geget o’ow izhi, gego wiin miinawaa inaaken i’iw gaa-gii-inadwaa igiw giniijaanisag i’iw gaa-inadwaa ‘ Manidoo nindenwenimaa ’ gii-ikidoyan. Mii dash izhi a’aw ningwanzh: ‘ Noos gidagwishin ’, izhi, maagizhaa ninga-zegi’aa,” odinaan iniw odaanisan.**

*To be sure, pleased was the woman. Presently she was addressed by her father saying: “My daughter, come, I say, go meet (my) son-in-law\! It seems that now he is coming along on the ice. Therefore truly this do you say to him, and do not repeat to him what you said to your children, when you told them that ‘A manitou do I take him to be.’ But this say you to (my) son-in-law: ‘My father has come home,’ (thus) say you to him, lest perhaps I should frighten him,” he said to his daughter.*

Geget minwendam a’aw ikwe (truly pleased was the woman). Zhigwa oganoonigoon oosan (presently she was addressed by her father): “Nindaanis, ambe, daga, awi-nagishk aw ningwanzh\! (“my daughter, come, go meet my son-in-law\!) Zhigwa indigo biidaadagaa’ko (it seems he is now coming over the ice). Mii sa geget o’ow izhi (so truly say this), gego wiin miinawaa inaaken (do not say to him again) i’iw gaa-gii-inadwaa igiw giniijaanisag (what you said to your children) i’iw gaa-inadwaa ‘ Manidoo nindenwenimaa ’ gii-ikidoyan (when you said ‘a manitou I take him to be’). Mii dash izhi a’aw ningwanzh (but say this to my son-in-law): ‘ Noos gidagwishin ’, izhi (‘my father has come home,’ say), maagizhaa ninga-zegi’aa,” (lest I scare him,”) odinaan iniw odaanisan (he said to his daughter).

(8) Mīdạc gägä‘t aninạgickawāt īni’ᵘ unābämạn änāt: “Mạnitō nindāwänimā,” udinān. Mīsa‘ kīwītcīwāt īni’ᵘ unābämạn. Cayīgwa kīpīndigäwạg, cigwa ogạnōnigōn: “Nindānis, ānīn winā kī∙i∙‘kitoyạn, ‘ Mạnitō nindāwänimā,’ kī∙i∙‘kitoyạn?”

**Mii dash geget ani-nagishkawaad iniw onaabeman enaad: “Manidoo nindenwenimaa,” odinaan. Mii sa giiwijiiwaad iniw onaabeman. Zhayiigwa gii-biindigewag, zhigwa oganoonigoon: “Nindaanis, aaniin wina gii-ikidoyan, ‘ Manidoo nindenwenimaa,’ gii-ikidoyan?”**

*Thereupon, truly going to meet her husband, she said to him: “A manitou do I consider him to be,” she said to him. Accordingly then she went along with her husband. When they entered, then was she addressed: “My daughter, pray, why did you say \[when you said\], ‘A manitou do I consider him to be’?”*

Mii dash geget ani-nagishkawaad iniw onaabeman (so truly going to meet her husband) enaad (saying to him): “Manidoo nindenwenimaa,” (“a manitou I take him to be,”) odinaan (she said to him). Mii sa giiwijiiwaad iniw onaabeman (and so she went along with her husband). Zhayiigwa gii-biindigewag (when they had gone in), zhigwa oganoonigoon (then she was addressed): “Nindaanis, aaniin wina gii-ikidoyan (“my daughter, why did you say), ‘ Manidoo nindenwenimaa,’ gii-ikidoyan?” (‘a manitou I take him to be,’ that you said?”)

(9) Ānīc kä∙i∙nāt? Kāwīn ogạnōnāsīn. Mīsa‘ cigwa udānawi∙ạ∙cạmān, kāwīn wīsinisīwạn. “Mīgū i’ᵘ nindānis\!” udinān. “Kāwīn nīn ā‘pidci wawība niwīsinisī,” udinān īni’ᵘ udānisan. Kägä‘t mīsa‘ kāwī‘kā wīsinisīwạn wâwītigämāwāt. Mīsa ä‘kitunit: “Kägu’ wīn, na∙ā∙ngic, nạnīsānändạgän,” udinān.

**Aaniish ge-inaad? Gaawiin oganoonaasiin. Mii sa zhigwa odaanawi-ashamaan, gaawiin wiisinisiiwan. “Mii go iw nindaanis\!” odinaan. “Gaawiin niin aapiji waawiiba niwiisinisii,” odinaan iniw odaanisan. Geget mii sa gaawiikaa wiisinisiiwan waawiidigemaawaad. Mii sa ekidonid: “Gego wiin, ningwanzh, naniisaanendagen,” odinaan.**

*What was she to say to him? She did not speak to him. Thereupon she then offered to feed him, but he would not eat. “Never mind, my daughter\!” he said to her. “Very seldom do I ever eat,” he said to his daughter. True it was that never did he eat while he was living with them. And now he said: “Don’t you, son-in-law, don’t entertain any fear (of me),” he said to him.*

Aaniish ge-inaad? (what was she to say to him?) Gaawiin oganoonaasiin (she did not speak to him). Mii sa zhigwa odaanawi-ashamaan (then she tried to feed him), gaawiin wiisinisiiwan (but he would not eat). “Mii go iw nindaanis\!” (“never mind, my daughter\!”) odinaan (he said to her). “Gaawiin niin aapiji waawiiba niwiisinisii,” (“very seldom do I ever eat,”) odinaan iniw odaanisan (he said to his daughter). Geget mii sa gaawiikaa wiisinisiiwan (truly never did he eat) waawiidigemaawaad (while he dwelt with them). Mii sa ekidonid (and so he said): “Gego wiin, ningwanzh, naniisaanendagen,” (“do not, son-in-law, be afraid,”) odinaan (he said to him).

(11) Mīdạc kä∙i∙nāt ninguding wạnāgucininig: “Kāwīnīnā ingutci ạmi‘kwạg, indānis, wī‘kā tciwâbạmāt na∙ā∙ngic?” udinān. “Kägä‘t o∙o∙mā päcu’ tāwạg ạmi‘kwạg, nicwäwān,” udinān.

(15) “Ōᵘⁿ, kāwīn wīn ningī∙i∙dạⁿzīn. Ingutci wīcing tci∙i∙nạtînāg nindạcigạgwätwä. Kägä‘t mī∙i∙mā sāga∙i∙gānsing wâbandạmān inạtînāg. Tạga‘pī wâbạnǵ ijā‘kạnk\!” udinān.

(16) Mīsa‘ kägä‘t weyābạninig mādcāwạg. Cigwa udōdi‘tānāwa kāgagīpōnạminit udōnini. “Kägätsa pā‘ta∙ī∙nowạg,” udigōn. “Aya∙ā∙nsạg ayātugänig,” udigōn. Cigwa udạnināⁿzi‘kạmini sāgiskusininik. Zhigwa odani-naanzhi’kamini zaagishkozininig. Aaniish ozaawaabik wesaka’oonid; ezhi-bakite’aminid, banaa go gaaziigijisenid. “ ‘Aw, ningwanzh\! giwazhishkomag\!”**

*Thereupon truly on the morrow they departed. When they got to the place, then (with surprise) did (the man) hold his hand over his mouth. “Truly many are they,” he was told. “Young (beavers) they must be,” he was told. Presently the (Windigō) went to where (the dwelling) was projecting out above the ice.
