NOTES ON THE MYSTIC RITE.

*(Narrator not credited on the page — left for M.; no series header sighted in this range.)*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ningạtibādcîm kā∙i∙jinōndạmān äjiwäba‘k ī∙i∙’ᵘ midēwiwin. Nī‘tạm ‘aᵉa’ᵘ kā∙u∙ji‘tōd ī∙i∙’ᵘ mîdēwiwin ‘aᵉa’ᵘ ạnicinābä. Awiya ningī‘pi∙u∙disī‘k, ningīwîndạmāk äjiwäbạtogwän midēwiwin. Ningīki‘kinō∙a∙māg ka‘kina äjiwäba‘k midēwiwin tcigaski‘tōyān midēwiwin.

**Ningad-dibaajim gaa-izhi-noondamaan ezhiwebak i’iw midewiwin. Niitam a’aw gaa-ozhi’tood i’iw midewiwin a’aw anishinaabe. Awiya ningii-bi-odisik, ningii-wiindamaag ezhiwebadogwen midewiwin. Ningii-gikinoo’amaag gakina ezhiwebak midewiwin ji-gashki’tooyaan midewiwin.**

*I will give an account¹ of what I have learned concerning the way of the mystic rite. The one that in the beginning founded the mystic rite was a man. By a certain one was I visited, by him was imparted to me the knowledge of what the mystic rite meant. By him was I taught everything appertaining to the mystic rite, to the end that I might become a member of the mystic society.*

Ningad-dibaajim (I shall relate it) gaa-izhi-noondamaan (what I have come to understand) ezhiwebak i’iw midewiwin (of how the midewiwin goes). Niitam (first of all) a’aw gaa-ozhi’tood i’iw midewiwin (the one who made the midewiwin) a’aw anishinaabe (was a person). Awiya ningii-bi-odisik (a certain one came to visit me), ningii-wiindamaag (he told me of it) ezhiwebadogwen midewiwin (however the midewiwin might be; \*dubitative\*). Ningii-gikinoo’amaag (he taught me) gakina ezhiwebak midewiwin (all that pertains to the midewiwin) ji-gashki’tooyaan midewiwin (that I might be able to carry the midewiwin).

*¹ This narrative deals with general information on the mystic rite, and with the narrator’s experience as a member of the Society. Both are interwoven without discrimination; and the whole discourse goes with a birch-bark chart in the American Museum of Natural History, New York City (Cat. No. 50: 5663. See Plate I; diagram, pp. 322, 323). — editor’s note, foot of p. 320. ningad-dibaajim — the dibaajim- “I relate” frame opens the account (genre dibaajimowin; recurs §9, §12). mîdēwiwin — printed with î here beside midēwiwin elsewhere in the line; both kept. ezhiwebadogwen — dubitative -dogwen, drafted.*

(2) Nackä mī∙u∙wä a‘ki ka‘kina änigu‘kwāg, mī∙u∙’ midämạnidō kā∙i∙dunk. Kayädạc owä misiwä änigu‘kwāg kistcigạmi mī ‘aᵉa’ᵘ midämạnidō ningī∙i∙‘k. Mīnawä uwä kīcig mīgu u täbicink midämạnido, mī∙i∙’ᵘ gā∙i∙cit; o∙o∙mā‘ⁿ dạc änigo‘kwāg, o gījik. Mīziwä ayāwạn wändänimak mī∙i∙mā‘ⁿ ayāwād mạnitōg,— wâbạnunk, minawä cãwạnunk, minawä nīngībī∙a∙nunk, minawä gīwädinunk; mīwaninu’ nīwin undinōn awacimā mä‘tcāgin. Mīnawä nîsawaya∙i∙ kīwädinunk wâbạnunk, mīnawä nisawaya∙i∙ wâbạnunk cãwạnunk, minawä cãwanunk nīngābi∙a∙nunk nisawaya∙i∙, minawä nīngābi∙a∙nunk kīwädinunk nisawaya∙i∙. Mī∙i∙’ᵘ cwäswi undinūn ayāmạga‘kin, päpa‘kān ijiwäbạdōn; ānawi pājigwạn u∙u∙ a‘ki.

**Nashke mii uwe aki gakina enigokwaag, mii uw mide-manidoo gaa-idoong. Gaye dash owe misiwe enigokwaag gichigami mii a’aw mide-manidoo, ningii-ig. Miinawaa uwe giizhig miigo o dabishkinh mide-manidoo, mii iw gaa-izhi-id; o’omaa dash enigokwaag, o giizhig. Misiwe ayaawan wendenimag mii imaa ayaawaad manidoog,— waabanong, miinawaa zhaawanong, miinawaa ningaabii’anong, miinawaa giiwedinong; miiwaninw niiwin ondinoon awashime me’jaagin. Miinawaa nisawayi’ii giiwedinong waabanong, miinawaa nisawayi’ii waabanong zhaawanong, miinawaa zhaawanong ningaabii’anong nisawayi’ii, miinawaa ningaabii’anong giiwedinong nisawayi’ii. Mii iw nishwaaswi ondinoon ayaamagakin, baapakaan izhiwebadoon; aanawi bezhigwan o’ow aki.**

*Behold, this earth in all its length and breadth, such is what is meant as the mystic manitou.² So likewise this sea throughout its whole extent, it is the mystic manitou, so I was told. Furthermore, this sky, that too does the mystic manitou overspread, such was what he told me; and this is the extent (of the mystic rite), as wide as the limits of this sky. At every place from whence blow the winds is where the manitous are,—at the east, and at the south, and at the west, and at the north; these are the four sources of the wind that are more manifest. Next is the region between the north and east, then between the east and south, then between the south and west, then between the west and north.³ These are the eight regions from whence blow the winds, each different from the other; while, on the other hand, this earth is as one.*

Nashke (behold) mii uwe aki gakina enigokwaag (this whole earth, the full extent of it), mii uw mide-manidoo gaa-idoong (that is what is called the mide-manidoo). Gaye dash owe misiwe enigokwaag gichigami (and so too this whole sea throughout) mii a’aw mide-manidoo (is the mide-manidoo), ningii-ig (so was I told; \*inverse\*). Miinawaa uwe giizhig miigo o dabishkinh mide-manidoo (and this sky too the mide-manidoo overspreads), mii iw gaa-izhi-id (so he told me); o’omaa dash enigokwaag, o giizhig (and here is its extent, this sky). Misiwe ayaawan wendenimag (everywhere they are, those I think on) mii imaa ayaawaad manidoog (there is where the manidoog are),— waabanong (at the east), miinawaa zhaawanong (and at the south), miinawaa ningaabii’anong (and at the west), miinawaa giiwedinong (and at the north); miiwaninw niiwin ondinoon (those are the four from which they come) awashime me’jaagin (the more manifest ones). Miinawaa nisawayi’ii giiwedinong waabanong (and in between north and east), miinawaa nisawayi’ii waabanong zhaawanong (and in between east and south), miinawaa zhaawanong ningaabii’anong nisawayi’ii (and in between south and west), miinawaa ningaabii’anong giiwedinong nisawayi’ii (and in between west and north). Mii iw nishwaaswi ondinoon ayaamagakin (those are the eight from which they come), baapakaan izhiwebadoon (each different one from another); aanawi bezhigwan o’ow aki (yet this earth is as one).

*² Another way of saying that all nature is manito (1–8, diagram). — editor’s note, foot of p. 320. ³ This is a characteristic passage, and illustrates a type of expression much employed by members of the Mystic Rite Society. The neophyte may want to know wherein regions, aside from direction, differ from another, and just what is meant by saying that the earth is as one. To get an answer one must pay, and here is where it pays to be a priest. — editor’s note, foot of pp. 320–321. mide-manidoo — “the mystic manitou”; the midä- (mide) element compounded on manidoo, drafted, flagged. gaa-idoong / gaa-izhi-id / ningii-ig — the impersonal/inverse “as it is said / he told me / I was told,” drafted. dabishkinh “overspreads/reaches” (§2) — stem uncertain, flagged. miiwaninw niiwin ondinoon — “those four are the sources (of wind),” drafted; me’jaagin “the more manifest” flagged. nishwaaswi “eight” (printed cwäswi) — the four cardinals + four inter-cardinals; cf. the diagram (Plate I, 1–8).*

(3) Mīzawädạc a‘kīng icpîming gayä gayä kistcigạming ki∙ạ∙sāwạg mạnidōg käbisindawātcig ạnicinābän. Ka‘kina gayä a‘kīng äyāmạga‘k udacindānāwa īgi’ᵘ mädäwiwād anicinābäg, mämindagä iwidi wâbạnung, mī∙i∙wide ayāmaga‘k kä‘i‘pi∙u∙ndcipād ‘aᵉa’ᵘ kā‘piki‘kinō∙a∙mawägät; mī∙i∙widi nībawit midämạnido, mī∙i∙diw pā∙u∙ndci māda‘kidäk madōdusạnạn kāyābạdcitōwād anicinābäg wāmidēwiwādcin.

**Mii zawe dash akiing ishpiming gaye gaye gichigamiing gii-asaawag manidoog ge-bizindawaajig anishinaaben. Gakina gaye akiing eyaamagak odazhindaanaawa igi’w medewiwaad anishinaabeg, memindage iwidi waabanong, mii iwide eyaamagak ge-bi-onjibaad a’aw gaa-bi-gikinoo’amawiget; mii iwidi niibawid mide-manidoo, mii idiw baa-onji madaki’idek madoodiswanan gaa-aabajitoowaad anishinaabeg waa-midewiwaadin.**

*Now, everywhere on the earth, and up above, and in the sea, have been placed the manitous that shall listen to the people. And about everything that is on the earth do the people speak when they perform the mystic rite, especially of yonder place at the east, for it was from thence that came the one⁴ who taught (the mystic rite); at that place⁵ stands a mystic manitou, it is from thence (that come) the line of sweat-lodges which the people use whenever they perform the mystic rite.*

Mii zawe dash akiing ishpiming gaye gaye gichigamiing (now everywhere on the earth, and above, and in the sea) gii-asaawag manidoog (the manidoog have been placed) ge-bizindawaajig anishinaaben (those who shall listen to the people). Gakina gaye akiing eyaamagak (and all that is on the earth) odazhindaanaawa igi’w medewiwaad anishinaabeg (the people speak of, when they perform the midewiwin), memindage iwidi waabanong (especially over at the east), mii iwide eyaamagak ge-bi-onjibaad (that is the place whence comes) a’aw gaa-bi-gikinoo’amawiget (the one who came to teach it); mii iwidi niibawid mide-manidoo (there is where a mide-manidoo stands), mii idiw baa-onji madaki’idek madoodiswanan (and from there extends the row of sweat-lodges) gaa-aabajitoowaad anishinaabeg waa-midewiwaadin (which the people use whenever they would hold the midewiwin).

*⁴ Referring to the mystic Otter (10, diagram), that was sent to teach the mystic rite; or, as the Ojibwas put it symbolically, “to bring life.” — editor’s note, foot of p. 321. ⁵ The top of the four worlds are above another; from the first, a bottom world, came the mystic rite. — editor’s note, foot of p. 321. (foot of p. 325) the lodge of the mystic rite; and the path of life, the path followed by the mystic Otter bringing the rite, is lined with sweat-lodges. gayä gayä — “gaye” printed twice in succession (dittography suspect; preserved). madaki’idek / madoodiswanan — the sweat-lodge run, drafted; ge-bi-onjibaad / gaa-bi-gikinoo’amawiget the come-from / came-to-teach changed-conjuncts, drafted. §3 spans p320 ll.17–20 + p324 ll.1–2 (the diagram, Plate I, is printed between in pp. 322–323).*

(4) Minawä mi‘tig päda‘kizut, midäwä‘tig; mī∙i∙dac imā‘ⁿ cigwa ändạguk midēwigạmik, miziwä inạmun mī‘kạna i∙i∙mā‘ⁿ wīgiwâmink. Imā‘ⁿ dec ābi‘tawind mī∙i∙mā‘ⁿ ka‘kina ạsindwā midäwayānạg, mīgísạg, kạckibidāgạnạg, asämā, kayä anōtcigägō wänicicink; oda‘tōnāwa inā pạgidcigäwinạn.

**Miinawaa mitig badaki’izod, midewaatig; mii dash imaa zhigwa endagok midewigamig, misiwe inamon miikana i’imaa wiigiwaaming. Imaa dash aabita’ond mii imaa gakina asindwaa midewayaanag, miigisag, gashkibidaaganag, asemaa, gaye anooji-gego wenizhishing; oda’toonaawa ina bagijigewinan.**

*And there is a post that stands upright, a mystic post; and now at that place is where the mystic lodge is, from every direction leads a path to that lodge. Now at the centre of the lodge (52) are laid all mystic skins, wampum beads, magic pouches, tobacco, and every kind of thing that is precious; they place them there as offerings.*

Miinawaa mitig badaki’izod (and a post that stands upright), midewaatig (a mide-post); mii dash imaa zhigwa endagok midewigamig (and there now is where the mide-lodge stands), misiwe inamon miikana i’imaa wiigiwaaming (from every way leads a path to that lodge). Imaa dash aabita’ond mii imaa gakina asindwaa (and there at its centre are placed all) midewayaanag (the mide-skins), miigisag (the megis shells), gashkibidaaganag (the tobacco-pouches), asemaa (tobacco), gaye anooji-gego wenizhishing (and every kind of thing that is good); oda’toonaawa ina bagijigewinan (they set them down there as offerings).

*midewaatig — the mide-post (mitig “tree/post” + the mide- prefix). midewigamig — the mide-lodge. midewayaanag — the mide-skins/bags; miigisag — the megis (cowry) shells; gashkibidaaganag — the tobacco-pouches: the offering-set, drafted. aabita’ond “at the centre” (52 in the diagram) — drafted, flagged. bagijigewinan “offerings” (cf. bagijiganan, diagram 45–48).*

(5) Igiwidạc nāgānizidcig gāgīgitōwạg; miziwä udạcindānāwa änigu‘kwāg uwä a‘ki kījig gayä kistcigạmi ka‘kina gayä kīwi‘tāgījik wä∙u∙ndānima‘k. Ānind gayä awäsīyaᵍ udacimāwān kī‘ⁿgō‘ⁿyạn gayä. Minawä päjig mạnidōwininiwan kayä dac, Nänabujūn udạjimāwān; mī‘ᵉa’ᵘ ga‘kina gägō kā∙u∙ji‘tōd kī∙i∙‘kidowād; kayädac uda‘tōnāwān pạgidcigạnạn. Mīnawä nībiwa mīdcîm ogīzizānāwa wī‘kundiwād, ka‘kina kägō wänicicink mīdcîm.

**Igiw idash naagaanizijig gaagiigidowag; misiwe odazhindaanaawa enigokwaag uwe aki giizhig gaye gichigami gakina gaye giiwitaa-giizhig waa-ondaanimak. Aanind gaye awesiiyag odazhimaawaan giigoonyan gaye. Miinawaa bezhig manidoo-wininiwan gaye dash, Nanabozhoon odazhimaawaan; mii a’aw gakina gegoo gaa-ozhi’tood gii-ikidowaad; gaye dash oda’toonaawaan bagijiganan. Miinawaa niibiwa miijim ogii-zizaanaawa wii-gondiwaad, gakina gegoo wenizhishing miijim.**

*And they that take the lead discourse at length; they talk about everything that is contained in all the length and breadth of this earth and sky and the whole sea and the entire vault of heaven from whence blow the winds. And some speak of the big game-folk and fishes. And also about another manitou man, about Nänabushu, do they talk; for it was he who created everything, so they have said; and so they place offerings for him. Furthermore, much food do they cook when they feast together, every kind of food that is nice.*

Igiw idash naagaanizijig gaagiigidowag (and they who take the lead, they discourse); misiwe odazhindaanaawa enigokwaag uwe aki giizhig gaye gichigami (everywhere they speak of the whole extent of this earth and sky and the sea) gakina gaye giiwitaa-giizhig waa-ondaanimak (and all the round of the heavens whence the winds blow). Aanind gaye awesiiyag odazhimaawaan giigoonyan gaye (and some speak of the big game-beings and the fishes). Miinawaa bezhig manidoo-wininiwan gaye dash (and of another manidoo-man too), Nanabozhoon odazhimaawaan (Nanabozhoo, they speak of him); mii a’aw gakina gegoo gaa-ozhi’tood (for he it is who made everything) gii-ikidowaad (so they have said); gaye dash oda’toonaawaan bagijiganan (and so they set down offerings for him). Miinawaa niibiwa miijim ogii-zizaanaawa wii-gondiwaad (and much food they cook to feast upon), gakina gegoo wenizhishing miijim (every kind of food that is good).

(6) Kayä dạc ānawi ogīki‘känimāwān īni’ᵘ kijämạnidōn, mī∙a∙’ᵘ ka‘kina kägō täbā‘kunạng; mīdạc ī∙i∙’ᵘ i‘kidōwād wägonän pägwisändạmowād. Mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā‘pạgwisändạmowād, kînwä‘ⁿj tcîbimādisiwād, kunimāgayä tcîminupimādisiwād tci∙ā∙‘kusisigwa, kumā gayä nawạtcimānäwād awä‘ⁿsīyag kī‘ⁿgōyạg kayä, kumā‘ⁿ gayä tcîminu∙i∙jiwäba‘k, tcîni‘tāwiging ạnōtc mīnạn, kunimā‘ⁿ tcîminū∙i∙jiwäbisiwād. Mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙u∙ndcipagidinigäwād; kāyä wäwäni tcîni‘tāwīgī∙ā∙wād unīdcānisiwā tcînibwā‘kānit gayä. Tibickō ningōgījik ogī∙i∙ⁿwādānāwa ningobîbōn. Mī∙i∙’u kāwī‘kwạdci‘tōwāg: “A‘pägic kabäpîmādisiyān\!” kī∙i∙nändạmowād. Mī∙i∙dac ī∙i∙’ᵘ kä‘gä‘t kā∙i∙jiwäbisiwād ạnicinābäg, mō‘ⁿjạg awiya ki‘kạbäpimādisi. Äniwäk gayä änind kīnîbwā‘kāwạg ạnicinābäg. Kāwin gayä ā‘pidci mō‘ⁿjạg awiya kī∙ā∙‘kusisi. Mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙i∙jipạgwisändạmowād.

**Gaye dash aanawi ogii-gikenimaawaan ini’w gichi-manidoon, mii a’aw gakina gegoo debaakonang; mii dash i’iw ikidowaad wegonen baagwisendamowaad. Mii iw gaa-bagwisendamowaad, ginwenzh ji-bimaadiziwaad, gonimaa gaye ji-mino-bimaadiziwaad ji-aakozisigwaa, gomaa gaye nawajimaanewaad awesiiyag giigoonyag gaye, gomaa gaye ji-mino-izhiwebak, ji-nitaawiging anooj miinan, gonimaa ji-mino-izhiwebiziwaad. Mii iw gaa-onji-bagidinigewaad; gaye waawiini ji-nitaawigi’aawaad oniijaanisiwaa ji-nibwaakaanid gaye. Dibishkoo ningo-giizhig ogii-inwaadaanaawa ningo-biboon. Mii iw gaa-wiikwajitoowaag: “Apegish gabe-bimaadiziyaan\!” gii-inendamowaad. Mii dash i’iw geget gaa-izhiwebiziwaad anishinaabeg, moozhag awiya gii-gabe-bimaadizi. Eniwek gaye aanind gii-nibwaakaawag anishinaabeg. Gaawiin gaye aapiji moozhag awiya gii-aakozisii. Mii iw gaa-izhi-bagwisendamowaad.**

*And yet, withal, they know about the great manitou, for he it is who passes judgment upon everything; therefore they speak of what they desire to get by prayer. Now, this is what they wish to obtain by prayer, that long life they may have, or that they may have good health and not be sick, or that more abundant may become the big animal-folk and fishes, or that the weather may be fair, that in plenty may grow all kinds of berries, or that they may live upright lives. Thus accordingly do they plant seed (in the ground); and (thus it is) that they are careful to bring up their children so that they too may be wise. As of a day do they refer to a winter. Therefore this is what they try to obtain: “Would that I might live a long life\!” (thus) do they wish. And this is what actually happens to the people, often does one live a long life. And some few of the people too are wise. And not very frequently is any one sick. Such is what they ask for (in their prayers).*

Gaye dash aanawi ogii-gikenimaawaan ini’w gichi-manidoon (and yet withal they know that great manidoo), mii a’aw gakina gegoo debaakonang (he it is who judges everything); mii dash i’iw ikidowaad wegonen baagwisendamowaad (and so they say what it is they pray for). Mii iw gaa-bagwisendamowaad (this is what they pray for), ginwenzh ji-bimaadiziwaad (that long they may live), gonimaa gaye ji-mino-bimaadiziwaad ji-aakozisigwaa (or that they live well and not be sick), gomaa gaye nawajimaanewaad awesiiyag giigoonyag gaye (or that the game-beings and fishes be the more plentiful), gomaa gaye ji-mino-izhiwebak (or that the weather be fair), ji-nitaawiging anooj miinan (that all kinds of berries grow well), gonimaa ji-mino-izhiwebiziwaad (or that they live upright lives). Mii iw gaa-onji-bagidinigewaad (this is why they make offerings); gaye waawiini ji-nitaawigi’aawaad oniijaanisiwaa ji-nibwaakaanid gaye (and carefully they raise their children, that they too be wise). Dibishkoo ningo-giizhig ogii-inwaadaanaawa ningo-biboon (as one day do they reckon a winter). Mii iw gaa-wiikwajitoowaag (this is what they strive for): “Apegish gabe-bimaadiziyaan\!” (would that I might live a long life\!) gii-inendamowaad (so they wish). Mii dash i’iw geget gaa-izhiwebiziwaad anishinaabeg (and truly this is what befell the people), moozhag awiya gii-gabe-bimaadizi (often did one live a long life). Eniwek gaye aanind gii-nibwaakaawag anishinaabeg (and a good few of the people were wise). Gaawiin gaye aapiji moozhag awiya gii-aakozisii (and not very often was anyone sick). Mii iw gaa-izhi-bagwisendamowaad (such is what they prayed for).

*gichi-manidoo / debaakonang — “the great manidoo who judges all” (the dipen-/debaakon- judging root, cf. \#37 debaakonang). baagwisendam- / bagwisendam- “pray for, desire by prayer” — drafted, the prayer-verb of the section. nawajimaanewaad “that they be more plentiful” (the game/fish increase) — drafted, flagged. inwaadaanaawa “they reckon (a day as a winter)” — drafted, flagged. apegish gabe-bimaadiziyaan “would that I might live long” — the long-life prayer at the heart of the rite (cf. the mystic Otter “to bring life,” fn ⁴).*

(7) A‘pī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙ckwāwīsiniwādcin ni‘tạm mī∙i∙’wa‘pī pạzigwīwād midäwiwād, pîmājagāmäwạg imā‘ⁿ wīgiwâming, udōtā‘pināwān midäwayānaᵍ mīgisaᵍ kayä. Mämäckut īdạc nima∙u∙dīwạg, pạngijîni dac ‘aᵉa’ᵘ wayäbinamawint. Ugōdānāwān īni’ᵘ mīgisạn, mī∙i∙dac mādcīsät ‘aᵉa’ᵘ mīgis. Kāwin mō‘ⁿjạg mīgisạn pạgidādisīwạg. Ạnicāgu udạminōwạg. Pānimādạc iskwā‘tāwād, minawä ka‘kina una∙i∙nāwān ugackipidāgạniwān mīgisạn gayä. Pānimā‘ⁿ minawä kīningopipōn mī∙i∙’ᵘ mīnawa a‘pī ijitcigäwād. Ānāwi nāningutinō nạgamōwạg ạnicāgu minwändamōg nạgamowād kāwin kā∙u∙ntcipạpāmänimāsiwāwāt udcistcāguwān.

**Apii idash gaa-ishkwaa-wiisiniwaajin niitam mii iw waapii bazigwiiwaad midewiwaad, bimaajagaamewag imaa wiigiwaaming, odoodaapinaawaan midewayaanag miigisag gaye. Memeshkod idash nima’odiiwag, bangii-jinh dash a’aw wayaabinamawind. Ogoodaanaawaan ini’w miigisan, mii dash maajiised a’aw miigis. Gaawiin moozhag miigisan bagidaadisiiwag. Anishaa go odaminowag. Baanimaa dash ishkwaataawaad, miinawaa gakina ona’inaawaan ogashkibidaaganiwaan miigisan gaye. Baanimaa miinawaa gii-ningo-biboon mii iw miinawaa apii izhichigewaad. Aanawi naaningodinoo nagamowag anishaa go minwendamoog nagamowaad gaawiin gaa-onji-babaamenimaasiwaawaad odijichaagowaan.**

*Now, when they finish eating, then first to their feet rise they who are performing the mystic rite, they circle about in the lodge, they pick up the mystic skins and the wampum. And one after another they go through the motion of hitting at one another (with the magic pouches), and down falls the one that is struck at. They blow upon the wampum, whereupon away starts the wampum in its flight. Not often do they fling wampum at one another. And later on, when they finish, they again lay away all their magic pouches and wampum. Not till another winter has passed do they then perform again. Yet nevertheless they frequently sing, simply because they like to sing, and not on account of any desire they have for the good of their souls.*

Apii idash gaa-ishkwaa-wiisiniwaajin (and when they have finished eating) niitam mii iw waapii bazigwiiwaad midewiwaad (then first do they rise who perform the midewiwin), bimaajagaamewag imaa wiigiwaaming (they pass round within the lodge), odoodaapinaawaan midewayaanag miigisag gaye (they take up the mide-skins and the megis). Memeshkod idash nima’odiiwag (and turn by turn they make the striking motion at one another), bangii-jinh dash a’aw wayaabinamawind (and down goes the one struck at). Ogoodaanaawaan ini’w miigisan (they blow upon the megis), mii dash maajiised a’aw miigis (and away it starts in flight). Gaawiin moozhag miigisan bagidaadisiiwag (not often do they cast the megis at one another). Anishaa go odaminowag (in mere play they do it). Baanimaa dash ishkwaataawaad (and afterward, when they finish), miinawaa gakina ona’inaawaan ogashkibidaaganiwaan miigisan gaye (they put away again all their pouches and the megis). Baanimaa miinawaa gii-ningo-biboon (not until another winter) mii iw miinawaa apii izhichigewaad (do they perform again). Aanawi naaningodinoo nagamowag (yet now and then they sing), anishaa go minwendamoog nagamowaad (simply because they like to sing), gaawiin gaa-onji-babaamenimaasiwaawaad odijichaagowaan (not because they are minding the good of their souls).

*bimaajagaame- “pass round (within the lodge)” · nima’odii- “make the striking motion at one another” (the shooting-rite, reciprocal -di-) · wayaabinamawind “the one struck/cast at” — drafted, flagged. odoodaapinaawaan midewayaanag miigisag — the mide-skins and the megis taken up; the megis “shot” and recovered. odijichaagowaan “their souls” (ojichaag) — Jones’s English “the good of their souls”; the gloss carries the word, not a doctrine. anishaa go “in mere play / merely” — the narrator’s own framing of the singing, preserved.*

(8) Mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙i∙‘kitōwad: “Kīcpîn awiya nibut tcibaya‘kạmigonk ta∙i∙jā,” kā∙i∙‘kitōwāt; “mī∙i∙widi ändạci∙ō∙dä‘tōwād tcîbayạg.” Nānīngutinō awiya kānibutcin, kī∙ā∙pitcībā. Mīdạc iᵘ kā∙i∙nādcimut: “Ningī∙i∙jā iwidi tcîbayạg ayāwād.” Kitîbādcimo äjināgwa‘k tcîbaya‘kạmigōnk.

**Mii iw gaa-ikidowaad: “Giishpin awiya nibod jiibayakamigong da-izhaa,” gaa-ikidowaad; “mii iwidi endazhi-oodetoowaad jiibayag.” Naaningodinoo awiya gaa-nibojin, gii-aabijiibaa. Mii dash iw gaa-inaajimod: “Ningii-izhaa iwidi jiibayag ayaawaad.” Gii-dibaajimo ezhinaagwak jiibayakamigong.**

*This is what they have said: “When any one dies, then to the world of ghosts one will go,” so they have said; “for it is there that the ghosts have a town.” Many a time, when one has died, from the dead has one risen. Whereupon then of this does one tell: “I have been to the place where the ghosts dwell.” One relates how it is in the land of ghosts.*

Mii iw gaa-ikidowaad (this is what they have said): “Giishpin awiya nibod (if anyone dies) jiibayakamigong da-izhaa (to the land of ghosts will one go),” gaa-ikidowaad (so they have said); “mii iwidi endazhi-oodetoowaad jiibayag (for it is there the ghosts have their town).” Naaningodinoo awiya gaa-nibojin (now and then one who has died), gii-aabijiibaa (has come back to life). Mii dash iw gaa-inaajimod (and then this one tells): “Ningii-izhaa iwidi jiibayag ayaawaad (I have gone yonder where the ghosts dwell).” Gii-dibaajimo ezhinaagwak jiibayakamigong (one relates how it looks in the land of ghosts).

*jiibayakamigong “in the land of ghosts” · jiibayag “the ghosts” · aabijiibaa- “come back to life” (the returned-from-death witness) · inaajim-/dibaajim- “relate.” The ghost-land testimony is carried as the narrator gives it (named, not interpreted).*
