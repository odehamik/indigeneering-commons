KĪCKWA∙Ä∙.

**GWIISHKWA’E.**

CHIRPER.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Kwĭckwa∙ä∙ ō‘kumisạn ogāⁿwaⁿjiguc mīsa iᵘ äjini‘kāsut ātisō‘kān. Cigwasa tạgwāginini. “Ạmbäsa, nō‘kumis\! nōdcikīⁿgōⁿ∙i∙wätā, mägica kīgapa‘katämin.”

**Gwiishkwa’e ookomisan Ogaanwanjigozh mii sa iw ezhinikaazod aadizookaan. Zhigwa sa dagwaagini. “Ambe sa, nookomis\! noojigiigoonyiiwetaa, maagizhaa giga-bakademin.”**

*Chirper’s grandmother was old Squaw-Duck, for such is the name of the story. It was now growing autumn. He spoke to his grandmother, saying: “Come, my grandmother\! let us try to get some fish, lest perhaps we become in need of food.”*

Gwiishkwa’e (Chirper; \*name flagged\*) ookomisan (his grandmother) Ogaanwanjigozh (old Squaw-Duck; \*her name\*) mii sa iw ezhinikaazod aadizookaan (and so this is what the sacred story is called). Zhigwa sa dagwaagini (now it was becoming autumn). “Ambe sa, nookomis\! (Come, my grandmother\!) noojigiigoonyiiwetaa (let us go after fish), maagizhaa giga-bakademin (else perhaps we shall be hungry).”

*¹ Jones: “A name for the robin.” The protagonist’s name prints two ways — Kīckwa∙ä∙ (title, no w) and Kwĭckwa∙ä∙ (body, with w, breve ĭ); rendered Gwiishkwa’e (Jones plain k = lenis g; the lexicon’s \#34 attestation is kwiishkwa’e). Name reading flagged for M. The grandmother’s name Ogaanwanjigozh echoes the song’s derision-word Ugāⁿwaⁿjiguc (p708 fn). Genre is named on the page: ātisō‘kān = aadizookaan.*

(2) Kägä‘t cigwa mādci‘tāwạg pagitawānōtawāwāt adi‘kamägwạn. Kägä‘tigu nībä‘tibik onōdci∙ā∙wän; äbiding tibi‘kạtinig ningutwā‘k unisāwān adi‘kamägwạn. Mīsa iᵘ äji∙ạ∙gōnāwāt ạdcitạgōnāwāt.

**Geget zhigwa maajiitaawag bagidawaanootawaawaad adikamegwan. Geget igo niibaa-dibik onoojii’aawen; aabiding dibikadinig ningotwaak onisaawaan adikamegwan. Mii sa iw ezhi-agoonaawaad adjidagoonaawaad.**

*In truth, they began to setting out nets for whitefish. And verily, in the silence of the night they sought for (the whitefish); in a single night one hundred whitefishes they slew. Therefore then they hung them up, they hung them up with the heads down.*

Geget zhigwa maajiitaawag (truly now they began) bagidawaanootawaawaad (setting nets for them) adikamegwan (the whitefish). Geget igo niibaa-dibik (verily in the dead of night) onoojii’aawen (they hunted them); aabiding dibikadinig (one night) ningotwaak (a hundred) onisaawaan adikamegwan (they killed, the whitefish). Mii sa iw ezhi-agoonaawaad adjidagoonaawaad (and so they hung them up, hung them head-downward).

(3) A‘pidạc mädāsugunagatinik mī∙i∙ᵘ cigwa ki‘tcinībiwa agōnāwāt. Kägä‘t minwäntạmōg. Kwĭckwa∙ä∙ äjikạnōnāt ō‘kumisạn: “Nō‘kō, mīsa iᵘ tcitäbisäwāt käyä‘pītcipibōnk tci∙ạ∙mōngwā.”

**Apii dash midaasogonagadinig mii iw zhigwa gichi-niibiwa agoonaawaad. Geget minwendamoog. Gwiishkwa’e ezhi-ganoonaad ookomisan: “Nookoo, mii sa iw ji-debisewey(?) gaye bgo gabe-biboon ji-amongwaa.”**

*And in the course of ten days a great many did they have hanging up. Truly pleased were they. Chirper then spoke to his grandmother, saying: “My grandmother, therefore now will there be enough for us to eat as long as the winter lasts.”*

Apii dash midaasogonagadinig (and when ten days had passed) mii iw zhigwa gichi-niibiwa agoonaawaad (now a great many they had hanging). Geget minwendamoog (truly they were glad). Gwiishkwa’e ezhi-ganoonaad ookomisan (Chirper thus spoke to his grandmother): “Nookoo (my grandmother), mii sa iw ji-debise- (now there will be enough) gabe-biboon ji-amongwaa (to eat all winter long).”

*² tcitäbisäwāt “there will be enough (to eat)” — the verb (debise-/debisii-) is drafted; reading flagged.*

(4) Wāgunänīwinän äjiki‘tci∙ā∙bawänik, mīsa iᵘ äjinigiskānit i∙i∙ᵘ ogīⁿgōⁿ∙i∙miwāᵍ; mīgū iᵘ ạnipạnādisinit. Kägä‘t mindcinawäsināwän; kägä‘t mindcinäⁿj mawiwạg. Ā‘pitcigu sägisiwạg tcinōntayābaniciwāt, kāwīn kägō pa‘kän udạyāsīnāwā kämīdciwād.

**Wegonen-iiwinen ezhi-gichi-aabawenig, mii sa iw ezhi-nigiskaanid iniw ogiigoonyimiwaa; miigo iw ani-banaadizinid. Geget minjinawezinaawen; geget minji-nezh(?) mawiwag. Aapiji go zegiziwag ji-noondaage-banaajichiwaad(?), gaawiin gego bakaan odayaasiinaawaa ge-miijiwaad.**

*What should take place but a great thaw, whereupon were their fishes rendered soft; and so then they began to spoil. To be sure, they were disheartened concerning their fish; verily, they both wept. And they were very much alarmed lest they might not live through the winter, for they had nothing else to eat.*

Wegonen-iiwinen ezhi-gichi-aabawenig (what but a great thaw set in) mii sa iw ezhi-nigiskaanid iniw ogiigoonyimiwaa (and so their fish went soft); miigo iw ani-banaadizinid (and so they began to spoil). Geget minjinawezinaawen (truly they grieved over them); geget mawiwag (truly they wept). Aapiji go zegiziwag (very much they feared) gaawiin gego bakaan odayaasiinaawaa ge-miijiwaad (they had nothing else to eat).

*³ Wāgunänīwinän “whatever creatures/things” and tcinōntayābaniciwāt (fear-clause) are drafted; readings flagged.*

(5) Ningutingigu owâbạmāwāᵍ ugīⁿgōⁿ∙i∙miwāᵍ wawīngä mīgu iᵘ kā∙i∙jipīsi‘kānit.

**Ningoding igo owaabamaawaa ogiigoonyimiwaa wawiinge miigo iw gaa-izhi-biisikaanid(?).**

*Then by and by they beheld their fishes go completely to decay.*

Ningoding igo owaabamaawaa ogiigoonyimiwaa (by and by they saw their fish) wawiinge (completely) miigo iw gaa-izhi- (thus they had gone) — gone to decay.

*⁴ kā∙i∙jipīsi‘kānit (the decay-verb) drafted; reading flagged.*

(6) Ānīc kī‘tcigamīng i∙i∙mä tāwạg. Ningutinigu kwĭckwa∙ä∙ kigicāp ājimādcāt. Ānīc awạnini. Mī‘tāwagāni dạc imä. Ājimädcāt, mī‘tāwagāng idạc ạnipapimusä. Cigwadạc awīya äjinōndawāt nạgamunit:—

**Aaniish gichigamiing i’imaa daawag. Ningoding igo Gwiishkwa’e gigizheb ezhi-maajaad. Aaniish awanini. Mii dawaagani(?) dash imaa. Ezhi-maajaad, mii dawaagaang idash ani-babaamose. Zhigwa dash awiya ezhi-noondawaad nagamonid:—**

*Now, by the sea over there were they abiding. So one morning Chirper went away. Now, it was foggy. And the beach was sandy over there. When he departed, along the sandy beach he went walking. And presently he heard the sound of some one singing:—*

Aaniish gichigamiing i’imaa daawag (now, there by the sea they lived). Ningoding igo Gwiishkwa’e gigizheb ezhi-maajaad (one morning Chirper went off). Aaniish awanini (now it was foggy). Mii dawaagani dash imaa (and the beach was sandy there). Ezhi-maajaad, mii dawaagaang idash ani-babaamose (setting out, along the sandy shore he walked). Zhigwa dash awiya ezhi-noondawaad nagamonid (and now he heard someone singing):—

(7) “Tcītu∙ä∙wa, Tcītu∙ä∙wa\! pīpisạnānowāgä kītānamägumāgä.  
Tcītu∙ä∙wa, Ugāwạⁿjiguc¹, Ugāwạⁿjiguc\!”

**“Tcītu∙ä∙wa, Tcītu∙ä∙wa\! pīpisạnānowāgä kītānamägumāgä.  
Tcītu∙ä∙wa, Ugāwạⁿjiguc, Ugāwạⁿjiguc\!”**

*“O Snipe, O Snipe\! all to decay have gone your catfishes.  
O Snipe, O old Squaw-Duck, O old Squaw-Duck\!”*

“O Snipe, O Snipe\! all to decay have gone your catfishes. O Snipe, O old Squaw-Duck, O old Squaw-Duck\!” (*the song is carried as printed, not converted*).

*¹ Jones (p708): “Name applied in derision of the robin” (on Ugāwạⁿjiguc). The song is excluded from lexicon mining (Batch-2 ruling, M.).*

(8) Ā‘pidcisa nickādisi Kwĭckwa∙ä∙. Indawā äjikīwät ō‘kumisạn āndāwāt. Ạnipīndigä. Anicādạc omaiyạgänimigōn īniyō‘kumisạn kuckwāwādisit.

**Aapiji sa nishkaadizi Gwiishkwa’e. Indawaa ezhi-giiwed ookomisan endaawaad. Ani-biindige. Aaniish dash omayagenimigoon iniw ookomisan goshkwaawaadizid.**

*Exceedingly angry was Chirper. Accordingly back he went to where he and his grandmother lived. He went right on in. And purely on account of his silence he drew the attention of his grandmother.*

Aapiji sa nishkaadizi Gwiishkwa’e (very angry was Chirper). Indawaa ezhi-giiwed ookomisan endaawaad (so back he went to where he and his grandmother lived). Ani-biindige (in he went). Aaniish dash omayagenimigoon iniw ookomisan (and his grandmother thought it strange of him) goshkwaawaadizid (his being so silent).

(9) Kāwīn kayä änu∙a∙cạmigut ō‘kumisạn wīsinisī kabägīcik; mīgu iᵘ pīnic kītibi‘kạtinig tcibwāwīsinit. Cigwadạc wâbạnini kigicäp änu∙a∙cạmāt ōcisạn, kāwīndac wīsinisīwạn. Mīgu iᵘ a‘pi tcibwāgīgitōnit.

**Gaawiin gaye anooj(?) aacamigod ookomisan, wiisinisii gabe-giizhig; miigo iw biinish gii-dibikadinig jibwaa-wiisinid. Zhigwa dash waabanini gigizheb anooj aacamaad oozhisan, gaawiin dash wiisinisiiwan. Miigo iw apii jibwaa-giigidonid.**

*And he would not eat throughout the whole day, despite the attempt of his grandmother to feed him; and it was not till the night came on that he would eat. And in time came the morning of the next day, when she tried in vain to feed her grandson, but he would not eat. And during all this while he would not speak.*

Gaawiin gaye aacamigod ookomisan (nor would he be fed by his grandmother), wiisinisii gabe-giizhig (he ate not all day); miigo iw biinish gii-dibikadinig jibwaa-wiisinid (not until night fell did he eat). Zhigwa dash waabanini gigizheb (and when next morning came) anooj aacamaad oozhisan (in vain she fed her grandson), gaawiin dash wiisinisiiwan (but he would not eat). Miigo iw apii jibwaa-giigidonid (and all that while he would not speak).

*⁵ änu∙a∙cạmigut / änu∙a∙cạmāt carry the in-vain preverb aano-/anoo-; the prefix vowel is drafted.*

(10) Kānicisāga∙ạ∙minit kägä‘t ōgīmāma‘kādändạm aᵘ mindimōyä. “Wägutugwänigic wändcikīgitōsik a nōjis?” inändạm.

**Gaa-nizhi-zaaga’aminid(?) geget ogii-mamakaadendam aw mindimooye. “Wegonen-dogwen-ish wenji-giigidosig a noozhis?” inendam.**

*When afterwards he went out of doors, truly did the old woman marvel. “Why in the world is it that my grandson does not speak?” she thought.*

Gaa-nizhi-zaaga’aminid (when he had gone outdoors) geget ogii-mamakaadendam aw mindimooye (truly the old woman marvelled). “Wegonen-dogwen-ish wenji-giigidosig a noozhis? (why in the world does my grandchild not speak?)” inendam (she thought).

*⁶ Kānicisāga∙ạ∙minit (going-outdoors conjunct) drafted; reading flagged.*

(11) Cigwadạc mīnawā äjimādciyāwạngusät. Mīsa käyābi awạnini ‘iᵉi’ᵘ ki‘tcigạmi. Mīnawā onōndạwān mạdwänạgạmunit awīya, mīsa nāsāb mädwä∙i∙na∙ạ∙minit:—

**Zhigwa dash miinawaa ezhi-maajii’ayaawangozed(?). Mii sa gaye-bi awanini i’iw gichigami. Miinawaa onoondawaan madwe-nagamonid awiya, mii sa naasaab madwe-inaaminid:—**

*So another time off he went walking along the sandy beach. There was yet a fog over the sea. And he heard the sound of some one singing, it was the same voice singing as before:—*

Zhigwa dash miinawaa (now once more) ezhi-maajii’ayaawangozed (off he went over the sandy reach). Mii sa gaye-bi awanini i’iw gichigami (still there was fog upon the sea). Miinawaa onoondawaan madwe-nagamonid awiya (again he heard someone singing), mii sa naasaab madwe-inaaminid (the same voice sounding as before):—

(13) Intawā äjikīwät. Mīdạc kägä‘t äjinickādisit kwĭckwa∙ä∙. Cigwasa änijipīndigät ändāwāt, owābạmān ō‘kumisạn nạmạdabinit. Mī gucagu iᵘ äjiki‘tcimawit kwĭckwa∙ä∙, kayä wīn mindimōyä äjimawit. Wī‘kā kā∙i∙jikīckuwäwāt. Ajigạnōnigut iniyō‘kumisạn: “Nōjis\! wägunäni iᵘ wändcimawiyạn?”

**Indawaa ezhi-giiwed. Mii dash geget ezhi-nishkaadizid Gwiishkwa’e. Zhigwa sa ani-zhi-biindiged endaawaad, owaabamaan ookomisan namadabinid. Mii go zha(?) iw ezhi-gichi-mawid Gwiishkwa’e, gaye wiin mindimooye ezhi-mawid. Wiikaa gaa-izhi-giishkwewewaad. Ezhi-ganoonigod iniw ookomisan: “Noozhis\! wegonen iw wenji-mawiyan?”**

*Whereupon back home he then went. And then truly angry was Chirper. Presently, when he entered into where he and his grandmother lived, he saw her seated there. Then it was that bitterly Chirper wept, and his grandmother also wept. It was a long while before they ceased crying. Then he was addressed by his grandmother, saying: “O my grandson\! for what reason are you crying?”*

Indawaa ezhi-giiwed (so home he went). Mii dash geget ezhi-nishkaadizid Gwiishkwa’e (and truly angry was Chirper). Mīdạc ‘aᵉa’ᵘ wägimāwit, aᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzī, ōdānisạn ā‘pidci ki‘kādänimōwạn. “Nōjis, ā‘pidcigu kāwīn wī‘kā awiya usäsigä∙i∙gusīn ‘aᵉa’ᵘ uckinīgi‘kwä; nībiwa awiya udānuwīpäcwâbạmigōn. **Aaniish ningii-gikenimaag anishinaabeg endaawaad, agaami-gichigamiing oodenawi’idiwag. *“Why, I know of a place where some people dwell, on the farther shore of the sea do they live together in a town. Aaniish ningii-gikenimaag anishinaabeg endaawaad (now, I know of people who dwell), agaami-gichigamiing oodenawi’idiwag (across the sea they have their town together). Mii dash a’aw wegimaawid, aw akiwenzii (and he who is chief, an old man), odaanisan aapiji gii-gikaadenimoowan (his daughter is held in great esteem). “Noozhis (my grandchild), gaawiin wiikaa awiya ozhaazigi’igosiin a’aw oshkiniigikwe (never has anyone won that young woman); niibiwa awiya odaanowii-...-waabamigoon (by many in vain has she been courted). Gaawiin ina gidaa-gashki’toosiin waawiyazh ji-doodawadiban a’aw oshkiniigikwe? *“My grandson, I myself will do something.” Then, turning about in her seat, a small pouch she drew forth from the place; it was a small pouch. “This is what you shall take with you, and a trick do you play on her who invited us because of our misfortune when our fishes were ruined. “Noozhis, niin gego ninga-izhichige (my grandchild, I myself will do something).” Zhigwa gwekitaawan (turning in her place), mashkimodens ezhi-mikonaminid (a little pouch she drew out); aapiji agaasaani gashkibijiganens (a very small tobacco-pouch). “Mii sa o ge-maajitooyan (this you shall take along), waawiyazh igo doodaw a’aw nenaabagaansominang (and play a trick on her who mocked us) i’iw isa gii-banaadiziwaad giigoonyiminaanig (when our fish were spoiled). Ambesanoo, noozhish, izhaan iwidi agaami-gichigamiing (go yonder across the sea) ayaawaad igiw anishinaabeg (where the people are). Mii dash o’o ge-maajitooyan (and this you shall carry).
