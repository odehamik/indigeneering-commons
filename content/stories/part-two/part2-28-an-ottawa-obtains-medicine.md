UDĀWĀ WÄDITẠNK MASKI‘KI.

**ODAAWAA WEDITANG MASHKIKI.**

AN OTTAWA OBTAINS MEDICINE.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ningudingsa kīwäⁿ Udāwāg kī∙i∙dāwạg ạnīcinābēg,—päjik inini päjikayä i‘kwä; nīⁿjîn gayä abînōdcīyạn unīdcānesiwān. Ningudingidạc wänāgucininîg kägō unūndānāwa mạdwäsininig, mīdạc kīsägesiwād. Mīnawādec wayâbạng wänāgucig unūndānāwa mīnawa mạdwäsininig, nawạdcidạc päcu mạdwäsīnini; mēdạc kägä‘t sägiswād. Mīnawādec wayābạninig wänāgucig unūndānawa ā‘pidcidạc pä’cu; ā‘pidcidạc sägisiwạg; pîsān ki∙a∙yāwạg. Wayābạngidạc mī∙i∙we wâbạmāwād ạnicinābän pidāsạmosäni; kägōn upîmōndān ‘aᵍaᵘ ạnicinābä,—piwābi‘k pạgạmāgạn, asāwābi‘k. “Ā, bōjo, bōjo,¹ nīdci\!” udigōn īni’ᵘ pạdạgwicininid.

**Ningoding sa giiwenh Odaawaag gii-daawag anishinaabeg,—bezhig inini bezhig gaye ikwe; niinzhin gaye abinoojiiyan oniijaanisiwaan. Ningoding idash wenaagoshininig gegoo onoondaanaawaa madwesininig, mii dash gii-zegiziwaad. Miinawaa dash weyaabang wenaagoshig onoondaanaawaa miinawaa madwesininig, nawaj idash beshoo madwesinini; mii dash geget zegiziwaad. Miinawaa dash weyaabaninig wenaagoshig onoondaanaawaa aapiji dash beshoo; aapiji dash zegiziwag; bizaan gii-ayaawag. Weyaabang idash mii iwe waabamaawaad anishinaaben bidaasamosenid; gegoon obimoondaan a’aw anishinaabe,—biiwaabik bagamaagan, ozaawaabik. “Aa, boozhoo, boozhoo, niijii\!” odigoon iniw bedagwishininid.**

*Now, once on a time, they say, the Ottawa people were abiding there, — one man and a woman; and two babes, their children. And once in the evening they heard the sound of something making a noise, whereupon they became afraid. And on the evening of the next day they heard the sound going on again, even nearer was the sound being made; whereupon truly they were afraid. And so on the evening of the next day they heard the noise ever so close; then they were very much afraid; quietly they remained. And on the next day they then saw a person come walking along; something in the hand the person had as he came, — a war-club of metal, copper. “Ah, good day, good day,¹ my friend\!” they were told by him on his arrival.*

Ningoding sa giiwenh (now, once on a time, they say), Odaawaag gii-daawag anishinaabeg (the Ottawa people were abiding there),—bezhig inini bezhig gaye ikwe (one man and one woman); niinzhin gaye abinoojiiyan (and two babes), oniijaanisiwaan (their children). Ningoding idash wenaagoshininig (and once in the evening), gegoo onoondaanaawaa madwesininig (they heard the sound of something), mii dash gii-zegiziwaad (whereupon they were afraid). Miinawaa dash weyaabang wenaagoshig (and on the evening of the next day), onoondaanaawaa miinawaa madwesininig (they heard the sound again), nawaj idash beshoo madwesinini (even nearer was the sound); mii dash geget zegiziwaad (then truly they were afraid). Miinawaa dash weyaabaninig wenaagoshig (and on the next evening), onoondaanaawaa aapiji dash beshoo (they heard it ever so close); aapiji dash zegiziwag (very much were they afraid); bizaan gii-ayaawag (quietly they remained). Weyaabang idash (and on the next day), mii iwe waabamaawaad anishinaaben (they saw a person), bidaasamosenid (come walking hither; *obviative*); gegoon obimoondaan a’aw anishinaabe (something on his back that person bore),—biiwaabik bagamaagan (a war-club of metal), ozaawaabik (of copper). “Aa, boozhoo, boozhoo, niijii\!” (ah, good day, good day, my friend\!), odigoon iniw bedagwishininid (they were told by the one arriving).

*¹ The Ojibwa form of salutation is from the French bon jour. — Jones’s note as printed, p278 foot; the marker prints at bōjo in the Ojibwe and at “good day,¹” in the English. \*\*Per M., 2026: the greeting comes from Nanaboozhoo.\*\* Jones’s French derivation is preserved above as his 1919 claim and is not imported (Translation Ethics — the corpus and its living speakers outrank the collector’s etymology).*

(2) Mīdac pạgidōndạng, mī∙i∙dac kistcimạdwäsininig; mīnạngwanạ∙i∙we i‘kᵘ kānōndạmowād, ā‘pictisa kusigwạnini. Mī∙i∙dạc kī‘pīndigät îmāⁿ wīgiwāming. Ogī∙ạ∙cạmāwān idạc māndāminạn. Kā∙i∙ckwāwīsinit idạc ogạnōnān īni’ᵘ kā∙u∙idisād: “Kāwīnina, nīdci, kidāwīdcīwēsī? Ō witi nindijā kistci∙a∙gāming; mī∙i∙widi ayāmạga‘k mạski‘ki ạnōdc änō∙ā∙däg,” i‘kido.

**Mii dash bagidoondang, mii dash gichi-madwesininig; miinangwana iwe iko gaa-noondamowaad, aapiji sa gozigwaninig. Mii dash gii-biindiged imaa wiigiwaaming. Ogii-ashamaawaan idash mandaaminan. Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid idash oganoonaan iniw gaa-odisaad: “Gaawiin ina, niijii, gidaa-wiijiiwesii? O’owidi nindizhaa gichi-agaaming; mii iwidi ayaamagak mashkiki anooj enaadeg,” ikido.**

*And so, when he put down his burden, it then made a great noise; and that was the thing which they had been hearing all the while, it was exceedingly heavy. And then he entered the wigwam there. Now he was given corn to eat. And after he was done eating, he addressed him to whom he had come: “Will you not, my friend, come along with me? I am going from here across the sea; for over there is a medicine which is good for all kinds of ills,” he said.*

Mii dash bagidoondang (and when he put down his burden), mii dash gichi-madwesininig (it made a great noise); miinangwana iwe iko gaa-noondamowaad (so that was what they had been hearing all the while), aapiji sa gozigwaninig (so exceedingly heavy it was). Mii dash gii-biindiged imaa wiigiwaaming (and then he entered the wigwam there). Ogii-ashamaawaan idash mandaaminan (they fed him corn). Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid idash (and after he had eaten), oganoonaan iniw gaa-odisaad (he addressed the one to whom he had come): “Gaawiin ina, niijii, gidaa-wiijiiwesii? (will you not, my friend, come along with me?), O’owidi nindizhaa gichi-agaaming (over there I am going, across the great water); mii iwidi ayaamagak mashkiki (over there is a medicine), anooj enaadeg (potent for all kinds of ills),” ikido (he said).

(3) “Ānīj kawīdciwinisa’.”

**“Aaniish, giga-wiijiiwin isa.”**

*“Of course I will go with you.”*

“Aaniish (of course), giga-wiijiiwin isa (I will go with you).”

(4) Mī∙i∙dạc wayābạninik kīmādcāwād. A‘pī∙i∙dạc wänāgucik käbäciwād ogībạgidciwäbinān ‘iᵍi’ᵘ kistcipạgạmāgạn, ā‘pitci kistcikijīwäwäsin. Kînwänj ki∙ạ∙nipîmosäwạg, mädāsugunạgạdinig idạc mī∙i∙’ᵘ kīmạdābīwād kistcikitcigạming. Mī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙ji∙o∙ji‘towād pîndasāgạn wī∙a∙jāwa∙o∙wād ki‘tcigạming; ạbwīn gayä ogi∙o∙ji‘tōnāwan wā∙a∙bạdci‘tōwād. Mīdạc kībōsiwād; pạgạmāgạn gayä obōzi‘tōnāwa.

**Mii dash weyaabaninig gii-maajaawaad. Apii idash wenaagoshig gabeshiwaad ogii-bagijiwebinaan i’iw gichi-bagamaagan, aapiji gichi-gizhiiwewesin. Ginwenzh gii-ani-bimosewag, medaasogonagadinig idash mii i’iw gii-madaabiiwaad gichi-gichigamiing. Mii dash gaa-izhi-ozhitoowaad bindasaagan wii-azhawa’owaad gichigamiing; abwiin gaye ogii-ozhitoonaawan waa-aabajitoowaad. Mii dash gii-boziwaad; bagamaagan gaye obozitoonaawaa.**

*And so on the morrow they set out. And in the evening, when they camped, he flung down the huge war-club, making an exceedingly loud noise. A long while they walked as they went, and on the tenth day they came out upon the great sea. Thereupon they built a raft in order to cross the sea; paddles too they made, which they were to use. Accordingly they embarked; and the war-club they also put aboard.*

Mii dash weyaabaninig gii-maajaawaad (and on the morrow they set out). Apii idash wenaagoshig gabeshiwaad (and in the evening when they camped), ogii-bagijiwebinaan i’iw gichi-bagamaagan (he flung down the huge war-club), aapiji gichi-gizhiiwewesin (an exceedingly loud noise it made). Ginwenzh gii-ani-bimosewag (a long while they walked along), medaasogonagadinig idash (and on the tenth day), mii i’iw gii-madaabiiwaad gichi-gichigamiing (they came out upon the great sea). Mii dash gaa-izhi-ozhitoowaad bindasaagan (thereupon they made a raft), wii-azhawa’owaad gichigamiing (to cross the sea by water); abwiin gaye ogii-ozhitoonaawan (paddles too they made), waa-aabajitoowaad (which they were to use). Mii dash gii-boziwaad (accordingly they embarked); bagamaagan gaye obozitoonaawaa (and the war-club they put aboard).

(5) Mēdạc kīmādcāwād, kwaya‘k wâbạnung ijāwạg. Kāwīn kī∙u∙ndci nibāsīwạg, kägạbädibi‘k kîstcitcimäwag; nībawa tạsing kī∙ạ∙nitibi‘kạdini. Ningudingidạc kigijäb mī∙i∙we wâbạmāwād a’wīya nạmạdạpinit, ki‘tcima‘kwạn. “Misa’∙a∙we äjāyān,” i‘kido ‘aᵍaᵘ Odāwa. “Mī∙a∙’we näyābi‘kạng ‘iᵍi’ᵘ mạckimut, ạnōdcigägo äno∙ā∙däg mạski‘ki îmāⁿ pīndä. Nībawa ạnincinābäg onisigowān ānawi‘kwạdcidōwād ‘iᵍi’ᵘ mạskimud. Kīcpîn kästcigîcā‘tägin mī‘ku∙i∙’we nibāt. Mīgisạpi‘kạn unābi‘kān, mī∙i∙’māⁿ ta‘kupidäg ‘iᵍi’ᵘ maskimut. Kīcpîn nîbāt mī∙i∙’ᵘ kädijināsi‘kawạg; ningagītābīginān ‘iᵍi’ᵘ mīgisạpi‘kạn. Mī∙u∙māⁿ āgumudā\! pānimā wâbạng kīgadijāmin.”

**Mii dash gii-maajaawaad, gwayak waabanong izhaawag. Gaawiin gii-onji-nibaasiiwag, gegabe-dibik gii-gichi-jiimewag; niibawa dasing gii-ani-dibikadini. Ningoding idash gigizheb mii iwe waabamaawaad awiya namadabinid, gichi-makwan. “Mii sa a’awe ezhaayaan,” ikido a’aw Odaawaa. “Mii a’awe neyaabikang i’iw mashkimod, anooj gegoo enaadeg mashkiki imaa biinde. Niibawa anishinaabeg onisigowaan aanawi-gwajitoowaad i’iw mashkimod. Giishpin gechi-gizhaategin mii iko i’we nibaad. Miigis-apikan onaabikaan, mii imaa dakobideg i’iw mashkimod. Giishpin nibaad mii i’iw ged-izhi-naazikawag; ninga-giitaabiiginaan i’iw miigis-apikan. Mii omaa aagomodaa\! baanimaa waabang giga-dizhaamin.”**

*Thereupon they started away, straight towards the dawn they went. Nowhere they slept, during the whole of every night hard they paddled; many a night the journey took. And it was one morning when they saw some creature that was seated, a huge Bear. “To that one yonder am I going,” said the Ottawa. “Yonder being has about his neck the bag, and the medicine that can heal all sorts of ills is there inside. Many people have been slain in a vain attempt to get that bag. Whenever the weather is extremely warm, then is when he generally sleeps. A tump-line (studded) with wampum beads he has about his neck, and from there hangs the bag. When he falls asleep, then shall I go to him; I shall remove from him the tump-line of wampum beads. Let us go floating along\! not till to-morrow shall we go there.”*

Mii dash gii-maajaawaad (thereupon they started away), gwayak waabanong izhaawag (straight towards the dawn they went). Gaawiin gii-onji-nibaasiiwag (nowhere did they sleep), gegabe-dibik gii-gichi-jiimewag (the whole of every night hard they paddled); niibawa dasing gii-ani-dibikadini (many a night came on). Ningoding idash gigizheb (and one morning), mii iwe waabamaawaad awiya namadabinid (they saw some being that was seated), gichi-makwan (a huge Bear; *obviative*). “Mii sa a’awe ezhaayaan (to that one yonder am I going),” ikido a’aw Odaawaa (said the Ottawa). “Mii a’awe neyaabikang i’iw mashkimod (yonder being wears the bag about his neck), anooj gegoo enaadeg mashkiki (medicine potent for all sorts of ills), imaa biinde (is there inside). Niibawa anishinaabeg onisigowaan (many people have been slain by him), aanawi-gwajitoowaad i’iw mashkimod (vainly trying for that bag). Giishpin gechi-gizhaategin (whenever it is extremely hot), mii iko i’we nibaad (that is when he generally sleeps). Miigis-apikan onaabikaan (a tump-line of miigis beads he wears about the neck), mii imaa dakobideg i’iw mashkimod (and there is tied the bag). Giishpin nibaad (when he sleeps), mii i’iw ged-izhi-naazikawag (then shall I go to him); ninga-giitaabiiginaan i’iw miigis-apikan (I shall draw off him the bead tump-line). Mii omaa aagomodaa\! (here let us float\!), baanimaa waabang giga-dizhaamin (not till to-morrow shall we go there).”

(6) Mī∙i∙dạc äjisạgạswāwād. “Tawī kitcimīja‘kwạt wâbạng\!” i‘kido ‘aᵍaᵘ Udāwa.

**Mii dash ezhi-zagaswaawaad. “Daa-wii-gichi-mizhakwad waabang\!” ikido a’aw Odaawaa.**

*Thereupon they began smoking. “May there be a very clear sky to-morrow\!” said the Ottawa.*

Mii dash ezhi-zagaswaawaad (thereupon they smoked). “Daa-wii-gichi-mizhakwad waabang\! (may there be a very clear sky to-morrow\!; *the daa-wii- optative*)” ikido a’aw Odaawaa (said the Ottawa).

(7) Kägä‘t idạc weyābạninik kistcimija‘kwạtini. Mē∙i∙dạc wâbạmāwād īni’ᵘ kistcima‘kwạni; tibîskōgu āna‘kwạd kī∙u∙mbạgōdäg mī∙i∙we äjināgusininit, ä‘pī‘tci mindidonit. Mī∙i∙dạc äskạm päcu’ kī∙ạ∙ni∙i∙jāwād. Kāwīn kawicîmosī nîbād, mi∙ä∙‘tạgu nawạgi‘kwänit. Ā‘pitci sägis ‘aᵍaᵘ päjîk ạnicinābä. Cigwạdạc änināwa‘kwänig, mī∙i∙’ᵘ wâbạmāwād nawạgi‘kwäninit. “ᵍAᵘ, nīdci, änigu‘k tcīmän\!”

**Geget idash weyaabaninig gichi-mizhakwadini. Mii dash waabamaawaad iniw gichi-makwan; dibishkoo go aanakwad gii-ombagoodeg mii iwe ezhinaagozininid, aapiji mindidonid. Mii dash eshkam beshoo gii-ani-izhaawaad. Gaawiin gawishimosii nibaad, mii eta go nawagikwenid. Aapiji zegizi a’aw bezhig anishinaabe. Zhigwa dash eni-naawakweg, mii i’iw waabamaawaad nawagikwenininid. “Aw, niijii, enigok jiimen\!”**

*And truly on the morrow there was a very clear sky. Thereupon they beheld the great Bear; like a cloud that hangs above, such was the look of the being, ever so huge was (the Bear). Accordingly nearer they went towards (him). He did not recline while sleeping, he simply bowed the head. Very much afraid was one of the men. And when it was about noon, then they beheld (the Bear) letting his head droop farther over. “Now, then, my friend, paddle hard\!”*

Geget idash weyaabaninig (and truly on the morrow), gichi-mizhakwadini (there was a very clear sky). Mii dash waabamaawaad iniw gichi-makwan (thereupon they beheld the great Bear); dibishkoo go aanakwad gii-ombagoodeg (just like a cloud hung aloft), mii iwe ezhinaagozininid (such was his look), aapiji mindidonid (ever so huge was he). Mii dash eshkam beshoo gii-ani-izhaawaad (and nearer and nearer they went). Gaawiin gawishimosii nibaad (he did not lie down while sleeping), mii eta go nawagikwenid (he simply bowed the head). Aapiji zegizi a’aw bezhig anishinaabe (very much afraid was the one man). Zhigwa dash eni-naawakweg (and when it was coming on noon), mii i’iw waabamaawaad nawagikwenininid (they beheld him letting his head droop farther). “Aw, niijii, enigok jiimen\! (now, then, my friend, paddle hard\!)”

(10) Odāwādec odābiskubidōn pạgạmāgạn mīdạc änād uwīdciwâgạnạn: “Wäwäni odā‘kän.” Wīnidạc Odāwa kīnībawi nīgān pindisāgạning ta‘kunạng upạgumāgan. Kwaya‘k ijisäwạg imāⁿ ma‘kwạn äyānit. Ā‘pī∙i∙dạc pägạmisäwād îmāⁿ ma‘kwạn udōning, mī∙i∙’ᵘ pa‘ki‘täwād ustigwānining; mī∙i∙dạc kīkīwạskwägạnāmād.

**Odaawaa dash odaabiskobidoon bagamaagan mii dash enaad owiijiwaaganan: “Weweni odaaken.” Wiin idash Odaawaa gii-niibawi niigaan bindasaaganing dakonang obagamaagan. Gwayak izhisewag imaa makwan eyaanid. Apii idash begamisewaad imaa makwan odooning, mii i’iw bakite’waad oshtigwaaning; mii dash gii-giiwashkwe-ganaamaad.**

Odaawaa dash odaabiskobidoon bagamaagan (and the Ottawa unbound his war-club), mii dash enaad owiijiwaaganan (and said to his companion): “Weweni odaaken (steer with care).” Wiin idash Odaawaa gii-niibawi niigaan bindasaaganing (he, the Ottawa, stood at the front of the raft), dakonang obagamaagan (holding his war-club). Gwayak izhisewag imaa makwan eyaanid (straight they sped to where the Bear was). Apii idash begamisewaad imaa makwan odooning (and when they came rushing at the Bear’s very mouth), mii i’iw bakite’waad oshtigwaaning (he struck him on the head); mii dash gii-giiwashkwe-ganaamaad (and knocked him dizzy with the blow).

(11) Mī∙i∙dec mīnawa kisisāgidcidciwạng iᵘ nibi. Mī∙i∙dạc mīnawa wāsa’ kī∙i∙nâbuguwād. Änābiwādidac ma‘kwạn ayānit mī∙i∙’ᵘ gī∙ạ∙nibạzigwīnit, nō‘pîming ki∙ạ∙ni∙i∙jānit. “ ‘Aᵘ, nīdci, änigu‘k tcīmän\! mīsa’ kāwīn mīnawā kīgạbabāmänimigusinān.”

**Mii dash miinawaa gii-zaagijijiwang iw nibi. Mii dash miinawaa waasa’ gii-inaabogowaad. Enaabiwaad idash makwan ayaanid mii i’iw gii-ani-bazigwiinid, noopiming gii-ani-izhaanid. “ ’Aw, niijii, enigok jiimen\! mii sa gaawiin miinawaa giga-babaamenimigosinaan.”**

Mii dash miinawaa gii-zaagijijiwang iw nibi (then out rushed the water once more), mii dash miinawaa waasa’ gii-inaabogowaad (and far away again were they carried on the flood; *-aabogo: by current*). Enaabiwaad idash makwan ayaanid (and as they looked back to where the Bear was), mii i’iw gii-ani-bazigwiinid (up he rose), noopiming gii-ani-izhaanid (and off inland he went). “ ’Aw, niijii, enigok jiimen\! (now, my friend, paddle hard\!), mii sa gaawiin miinawaa giga-babaamenimigosinaan (for never again will he trouble us).”

(12) Mīsa kīpimādisiwād. Mī∙i∙dạc kī‘pikīwäwād pīnic kī‘pimijạgāwād gayädạc kī‘pipîmosäwād. A‘pī∙i∙dạc kā‘tạgwicinowād îmāⁿ ka∙u∙ndciwīdcīndiwād mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙i∙jiwâbạndạmowād ‘iᵍiᵘ maski‘ki ạnōdc änwādäg. Ābi‘tadac ogīmīnān uwīdciwāgạnạn gayä wīndạc ābi‘ta ogi∙a∙yān. Mīdạc änād: “Mīsa’ uwä kädayāyạn gayä nīndạc uᵍuwe ningạdayān. Nādowä¹ gīn gigạtigo, nīndạc Odāwa ningạtigo. Kīnāwind ā‘pitci kīgạgiki‘kändāmin maski‘ki.”

**Mii sa gii-bimaadiziwaad. Mii dash gii-bi-giiwewaad biinish gii-bi-mizhagaawaad gaye dash gii-bi-bimosewaad. Apii idash gaa-dagwishinowaad imaa gaa-onji-wiijiindiwaad mii i’iw gaa-izhi-waabandamowaad i’iw mashkiki anooj enwaadeg. Aabita dash ogii-miinaan owiijiwaaganan gaye wiin dash aabita ogii-ayaan. Mii dash enaad: “Mii sa owe ged-ayaayan gaye niin dash o’owe ninga-dayaan. Naadowe giin giga-digo, niin dash Odaawaa ninga-digo. Giinawind aapiji giga-gikendaamin mashkiki.”**

*Therefore were they safe. And then homeward they came, (keeping on) till they came ashore, and where they took their time as they walked. And when they arrived at the place from whence they had started away together, then they beheld the medicine that was good for every ill. And half he gave to his companion, and for himself half he retained. And then he said to him: “Now, this shall you yourself have, and this will I myself have. An Adder¹ shall you be called, and I an Ottawa will be called. You and I shall have great knowledge of medicine.”*

Mii sa gii-bimaadiziwaad (therefore were they alive-safe). Mii dash gii-bi-giiwewaad (and homeward they came), biinish gii-bi-mizhagaawaad (till they came ashore), gaye dash gii-bi-bimosewaad (and walked their way back). Apii idash gaa-dagwishinowaad (and when they arrived) imaa gaa-onji-wiijiindiwaad (at the place whence they had set out together), mii i’iw gaa-izhi-waabandamowaad i’iw mashkiki (then they beheld the medicine), anooj enwaadeg (potent for every ill). Aabita dash ogii-miinaan owiijiwaaganan (half he gave his companion), gaye wiin dash aabita ogii-ayaan (and half he kept himself). Mii dash enaad (and he said to him): “Mii sa owe ged-ayaayan (this shall you have), gaye niin dash o’owe ninga-dayaan (and this shall I have). Naadowe giin giga-digo (an Adder shall you be called), niin dash Odaawaa ninga-digo (and I an Ottawa shall be called). Giinawind aapiji giga-gikendaamin mashkiki (you and I shall know medicine deeply).”
