MẠTCININIWÂGÄ.

**MAJININIWAAGE.**

VAGABOND.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ānīc Mạtcininiwāgä äjini‘kāsut. Kāwīn ningutci owīgiwām ayāsinini; mōjạg mīyä‘ta pạnä papimusät. Ninguting pạpimusät sāga∙i∙gạn owâbạndān; kāmạtāpīt iᵘ sāga∙i∙gạn, äja∙ī∙nābit, pạcu’ owâbạmāᵍ ạnicinābäᵍ utạminonit. Ki‘tcipā‘tinīnōwa; ānīnd pāga∙ā∙towäwaᵍ; kayä pasi‘kawäwaᵍ iᵍiwisa i‘kwäwaᵍ; ānind kayä kwāckwāckwänạtowäwaᵍ; mīnawā ānint pä‘päcinitiwaᵍ.

**Aaniish Majininiwaage ezhinikaazod. Gaawiin ningoji owiigiwaam ayaasinini; moozhag miiy eta apane babimosed. Ningoding babimosed zaaga’igan owaabandaan; gaa-madaabiid iw zaaga’igan, ezhi-ayinaabid, besho owaabamaa’ anishinaabe’ odaminonid. Gichi-baatiininowa; aanind baaga’adowewag; gaye basikawewag igiw isa ikwewag; aanind gaye gwaashkwaashkwanadowewag; miinawaa aanind bebezhinidiwag.**

*Now, Vagabond was the name by which he was called. Nowhere did he have a wigwam; all the while was he simply walking about. Once, while walking about, he saw a lake; when he came out upon the lake, (and) while looking about, he saw not far away some people at play. They were in great number; some were playing ball; and at the double-ball game played the women; and some were at play jumping, and some at tagging the head.¹*

Aaniish Majininiwaage (now, Vagabond) ezhinikaazod (was the name he was called). Gaawiin ningoji (nowhere) owiigiwaam ayaasinini (did he have a wigwam); moozhag miiy eta apane (all the while, simply, always) babimosed (he walked about). Ningoding babimosed (once, while walking about) zaaga’igan owaabandaan (he saw a lake); gaa-madaabiid iw zaaga’igan (when he came out upon that lake), ezhi-ayinaabid (as he looked about), besho (not far away) owaabamaa’ anishinaabe’ (he saw people) odaminonid (at play). Gichi-baatiininowa (they were in great number); aanind baaga’adowewag (some were playing ball); gaye basikawewag igiw isa ikwewag (and those women were playing double-ball); aanind gaye gwaashkwaashkwanadowewag (and some were at the jumping game); miinawaa aanind bebezhinidiwag (and some were tagging one another).

*¹ A game of tag. The players draw sticks from a bundle held in the hand of a certain one. The one getting the longest stick is “it;” the one he tags becomes his partner, and his side increases in number as fast as the players are tagged; and the last one left is then “it.” Thus the game continues of itself. \[Jones’s note; the marker stands on “tagging the head” in his English (p. 349), and the note is printed at the foot of the Ojibwe page 348, continuing on 349.\]*

(2) Mīdạc iᵍiᵘ änīcimādcāt, utạnināsi‘kawaᵍ; payäcwābamāt, mīcigwa iᵍiᵘ ạniku‘pīnit; ā‘pitcigu payäcwâbạmāt mī cigwa iᵘ ka‘kina kī∙a∙nigu‘pinīt. “Ānīc wini∙i∙ᵘ wäntcitōtamuwāt?” inändạm. “Mīnōtcisạgu ningaku‘pī.” Änīcigu‘pi‘pa‘tōd i∙i∙ᵘ umī‘kạnāni. Kā∙ạ∙gwitā‘kīwät ugīwâbandānạn īni’ᵘ wīgiwāmạn. Iᵍi’wä nä‘tạmickạng iᵘ wīgiwām äcita‘pāpit, kāwīn gạnagä awiya owâbạmāsīn. Mīgu’ iᵍi’ᵘ äcipijijiwa‘tänig, intawā änīcimādcāt. Cigwa ugīwâbandān cāpundawān; äjita‘pābit, kāwīn gạnạgä mīnawā awiya abisīwạn. Inābit pīndik anā‘kanạn mīgu i’ᵘ äcitạtạngisininig. Kägätsa omisawinānạn. “Ạmbäsạnō, ningakīmōt.” Äcipīndigät. Ā‘pitcidạc wänicicininig äji∙o∙dā‘pinạng. Ānīc mīdạc iᵘ äcipạgitciwạnät īni’ᵘ udạsämān pämōmāt; mīdạc imāⁿ äcitatibä∙i∙gināt imāⁿ utanā‘kaning, äci∙ō∙cowani‘kānāt; äci∙ō∙mbīwạnät mīnawā, änīcisāga∙ạ∙nk. Nawạtcigwa animādcīpa‘tō. Päcu änitạgwicing, awīya onōndawān: “Ä∙ē∙e∙’ᵍ\! Mạtcininiwāgä kitanā‘kaniminān kīkimotcimigunān\! Ha‘a’ᵘ, piminicawātā\!”

**Mii dash i’iw eni-izhi-maajaad, odani-naazikawaa’; bayeshwaabamaad, mii zhigwa i’iw ani-gopiinid; aapiji go bayeshwaabamaad mii zhigwa iw gakina gii-ani-gopiinid. “Aaniish wiin i’iw wenji-doodamowaad?” inendam. “Miinooj sa go ninga-gopii.” Eni-izhi-gopiibatood i’iw omiikanaani. Gaa-agwidaakiiwed ogii-waabandaanan iniw wiigiwaaman. I’iwe netamishkang iw wiigiwaam ezhi-dapaabid, gaawiin ganage awiiya owaabamaasiin. Mii go i’iw ezhi-bizhizhiwatenig, indawaa eni-izhi-maajaad. Zhigwa ogii-waabandaan zhaabondawaan; ezhi-dapaabid, gaawiin ganage miinawaa awiiya abisiiwan. Inaabid biindig anaakanan mii go iw ezhi-dadangisininig. Geget sa omisawinaanan. “Ambesanoo, ninga-giimood.” Ezhi-biindiged. Aapiji dash wenishishininig ezhi-odaapinang. Aaniish mii dash iw ezhi-bagijiwaned iniw odasemaan bemoomaad; mii dash imaa ezhi-dadibe’iginaad imaa odanaakaning, ezhi-oozhowanikaanaad; ezhi-oombiiwaned miinawaa, eni-izhi-zaaga’ang. Nawaj igwa ani-maajiibatoo. Besho ani-dagwishing, awiiya onoondawaan: “Ä∙ē∙e∙’ᵍ\! Majininiwaage gidanaakaniminaan gigiimoojimigonaan\! Ha’aw, biminizhawaadaa\!”**

*Accordingly, when he continued his way, he started over to where they were; when he was in near view of them, they were then going up from the lake; when he was very close upon them, then were they all on the way up from the lake. “Why are they doing this?” he thought. “Nevertheless I will go up from the lake.” Then up from the lake he went, running along their path. When he was come at the top of the hill, he saw the wigwams. Into the first lodge that was in his way he peeped, but not a single one he saw. Since it was empty, he therefore continued on. Presently he saw a long-lodge; on peeping in, not a single one again was there. As he looked inside, (he saw) reed mats lying edge to edge (the full length of the lodge). Truly eager was he to possess them. “Well, I will steal (them).” Then in he went. And the one that was exceedingly beautiful he picked up. So thereupon he put down his pack of tobacco that he had on his back; and then, wrapping it up in his reed mat, he made a pack of it; then, lifting it upon his back again, he went out of doors. He then set out upon a running gait. When a short distance upon his way he was come, he heard some one (saying): “Alas\! by Vagabond have we been robbed of our reed mats\! Come, let us pursue after him\!”*

Mii dash i’iw eni-izhi-maajaad (accordingly, as he continued his way), odani-naazikawaa’ (he went toward them); bayeshwaabamaad (when he was in near view of them), mii zhigwa i’iw ani-gopiinid (they were then going up from the lake); aapiji go bayeshwaabamaad (when he was very close upon them) mii zhigwa iw gakina gii-ani-gopiinid (then they had all gone up from the lake). “Aaniish wiin i’iw wenji-doodamowaad?” (“why are they doing this?”) inendam (he thought). “Miinooj sa go ninga-gopii.” (“nevertheless I will go up from the lake.”) Eni-izhi-gopiibatood (then up he went, running) i’iw omiikanaani (along their path). Gaa-agwidaakiiwed (when he was come at the top of the hill) ogii-waabandaanan iniw wiigiwaaman (he saw the wigwams). I’iwe netamishkang iw wiigiwaam (into the first lodge in his way) ezhi-dapaabid (he peeped), gaawiin ganage awiiya owaabamaasiin (not a single one he saw). Mii go i’iw ezhi-bizhizhiwatenig (since it was empty), indawaa eni-izhi-maajaad (he therefore continued on). Zhigwa ogii-waabandaan zhaabondawaan (presently he saw a long-lodge); ezhi-dapaabid (on peeping in), gaawiin ganage miinawaa awiiya abisiiwan (not a single one again was at home). Inaabid biindig (as he looked inside) anaakanan mii go iw ezhi-dadangisininig (reed mats lay edge to edge). Geget sa omisawinaanan (truly eager was he to possess them). “Ambesanoo, ninga-giimood.” (“well, I will steal.”) Ezhi-biindiged (then in he went). Aapiji dash wenishishininig (and the one exceedingly beautiful) ezhi-odaapinang (he picked up). Aaniish mii dash iw ezhi-bagijiwaned (so thereupon he put down his pack) iniw odasemaan bemoomaad (the tobacco he carried on his back); mii dash imaa ezhi-dadibe’iginaad (and then he wrapped him up) imaa odanaakaning (in his reed mat), ezhi-oozhowanikaanaad (making a pack of him); ezhi-oombiiwaned miinawaa (then lifting the pack again), eni-izhi-zaaga’ang (he went out of doors). Nawaj igwa ani-maajiibatoo (he then set out running). Besho ani-dagwishing (when a short way along), awiiya onoondawaan (he heard someone): “Ä∙ē∙e∙’ᵍ\! (alas\! — cry, carried as printed) Majininiwaage (by Vagabond) gidanaakaniminaan (of our reed mats) gigiimoojimigonaan\! (have we been robbed\!) Ha’aw, biminizhawaadaa\!” (“come, let us pursue after him\!”)

*asemaa (tobacco) is animate throughout this passage — Vagabond’s mat-wrapping and pack-making verbs take animate objects: the tobacco is a him, not an it.*

(3) Äjimādcāt ki‘tci änigu‘k, pạnägu pizāzā‘kwānit; ạnimādcā ki‘tci änigu‘k. Kägä‘t käga‘pī∙i∙gu päcu’ ābạnābit, owâbạmāᵍ ininiwaᵍ; misa cigwa sägisit. Intawā unạntawâbamān mi‘tigōn tciwīmbini‘kisinit; päcu’ ogīwâbạmān mi‘tigōn wīmbini‘kisinit, äcipīndigäpa‘tōt. Kā‘pīndigät mīsa iᵍi’ᵘ äci∙ā∙ba∙a∙nk iᵍi’ᵘ pimiwạnān; utasämān äcikipicimāt iwiti kāpi∙u∙ndcipīndigät; ācawa∙ī∙ utạnā‘kạnim äcī∙i∙jīkisitōt.

**Ezhi-maajaad gichi-enigok, apane go bizaazaakwaanid; ani-maajaa gichi-enigok. Geget gegapii igo besho aabanaabid, owaabamaa’ ininiwa’; mii sa zhigwa zegizid. Indawaa onandawaabamaan mitigoon ji-wiimbinikizinid; besho ogii-waabamaan mitigoon wiimbinikizinid, ezhi-biindigebatood. Gaa-biindiged mii sa i’iw ezhi-aaba’ang i’iw bimiwanaan; odasemaan ezhi-gibishimaad iwidi gaa-bi-onji-biindiged; aazhawa’ii odanaakanim ezhi-izhiigisidood.**

*When off he started at full speed, then continually did they whoop (at him) as they came; on his way he continued at full speed. In truth, when (they were) near at hand (and) he looked back, he saw some men; thereupon then was he scared. Accordingly he sought for a tree that was hollow; near by he saw a tree that was hollow, then in he ran. When he got inside, he then untied his pack; with his tobacco he closed up the place by way of which he came in; then on the other side he hung up his mat spread out.*

Ezhi-maajaad gichi-enigok (when off he started at full speed), apane go bizaazaakwaanid (continually they came whooping); ani-maajaa gichi-enigok (on his way he went at full speed). Geget gegapii igo (in truth, at last) besho (near at hand) aabanaabid (as he looked back), owaabamaa’ ininiwa’ (he saw some men); mii sa zhigwa zegizid (thereupon was he scared). Indawaa onandawaabamaan mitigoon (accordingly he sought for a tree) ji-wiimbinikizinid (that would be hollow); besho ogii-waabamaan mitigoon wiimbinikizinid (near by he saw a hollow tree), ezhi-biindigebatood (then in he ran). Gaa-biindiged (when he got inside) mii sa i’iw ezhi-aaba’ang i’iw bimiwanaan (he untied his pack); odasemaan ezhi-gibishimaad (with his tobacco he closed it up) iwidi gaa-bi-onji-biindiged (there where he had come in); aazhawa’ii (on the other side) odanaakanim (his mat) ezhi-izhiigisidood (he hung up spread out).

(4) Mīdạc īgi’ᵘ anicinābäg ä‘kitowāt: “Mīsa’ imāⁿ tcīwanawạnk. Hā‘a’ᵘ nātciwāgāgwạtwäg\!”

**Mii dash igiw anishinaabeg ekidowaad: “Mii sa imaa ji-wanawang. Haa’aw naajiwaagaakwadweg\!”**

*And then the people said: “It is there that we shall pound him to death. Come, go get your axes\!”*

Mii dash igiw anishinaabeg (and then the people) ekidowaad (said): “Mii sa imaa (it is there) ji-wanawang (that we shall club him to death). Haa’aw (come\!) naajiwaagaakwadweg\! (go get your axes\!)”

(5) Kägä‘t äcinātciwāgāgwạtwäwāt. Cīgwasa’ opīdōnāwān īni’ᵘ wâgā‘kwatōn; ci’gwa mādcigawā aᵍa’ᵘ mi‘tig.

**Geget ezhi-naajiwaagaakwadwewaad. Zhigwa sa obiidoonaawaan iniw waagaakwadoon; zhigwa maajigawaa a’aw mitig.**

*Truly then went they after their axes. In a while they fetched the axes; presently they began to fell the tree.*

Geget (truly) ezhi-naajiwaagaakwadwewaad (then went they after their axes). Zhigwa sa obiidoonaawaan (in a while they fetched) iniw waagaakwadoon (the axes); zhigwa maajigawaa (presently began to be felled) a’aw mitig (that tree).

*ci’gwa (third word of the last clause) is printed with an apostrophe inside the word, against cigwa / Cīgwasa’ elsewhere on the same page — preserved as printed; a printer’s slip is suspected.*

(6) “Mīmāwīni’ᵘ kägä‘t tcinisigōyān,” inändạm. Otasämān äcimockina∙ā∙t īni’ᵘ otō‘pwāgạn. Äci∙a∙ī∙nā‘kunigät mīdạc äjikīkitut: “Ambäsạnō ogawipwāwīgawāwān\!”

**“Mii maawiin i’iw geget ji-nisigooyaan,” inendam. Odasemaan ezhi-mooshkina’aad iniw odoopwaagan. Ezhi-ayinaakoniged mii dash ezhi-giigidod: “Ambesanoo oga-wii-bwaawigawaawaan\!”**

*“Perhaps now I shall surely be slain,” he thought. With tobacco then he began filling his pipe. Then he pointed toward the various directions, and this he spoke, saying: “Now, may they not (be able to) cut down the tree\!”*

“Mii maawiin i’iw (perhaps it is that) geget (surely) ji-nisigooyaan (I shall be slain),” inendam (he thought). Odasemaan (with his tobacco) ezhi-mooshkina’aad (he filled) iniw odoopwaagan (his pipe). Ezhi-ayinaakoniged (then he gestured toward the various directions) mii dash ezhi-giigidod (and this he spoke, saying): “Ambesanoo (now, pray) oga-wii-bwaawigawaawaan\! (may they fail to cut him down\!)”

(7) Kägä‘t īgi’ᵘ anicinābäg utā‘pitcipwāwigawāwān. “Ānīcitug nā kạgī‘tōtạmạng?”

**Geget igiw anishinaabeg odaapiji-bwaawigawaawaan. “Aaniish idog naa ge-gii-doodamang?”**

*Verily, the people were unable to fell the tree. “Pray, what shall we do with it?”*

Geget (verily) igiw anishinaabeg (the people) odaapiji-bwaawigawaawaan (utterly failed to fell him). “Aaniish idog naa (pray, what then) ge-gii-doodamang? (shall we do with it?)”

(8) “Tạgapina mi‘tig paciba∙u∙‘k.”

**“Daga bina mitig bazhiba’ok.”**

*“Why, just shove something into the tree.”*

“Daga bina (why, just) mitig (the tree) bazhiba’ok. (pierce him.)”

(9) Ca∙ī∙gwạdạc äcipạciba‘∙u∙nt. Kägō owâbạndānâwa īgi’ᵘ anicinābäg. Mīdạc äjikīgitōwāt: “Mīnāwini∙i∙ᵘ iᵍiyo‘pạn. Mīsa’ iᵍi’ᵘ gīnisạnk.”

**Zhayiigwa dash ezhi-bazhiba’ond. Gegoo owaabandaanaawaa igiw anishinaabeg. Mii dash ezhi-giigidowaad: “Mii maawiin i’iw opan. Mii sa i’iw gii-nisang.”**

*And when it was pierced, something did the people see. Whereupon they said: “Perhaps it is his lungs. Therefore we have killed him.”*

Zhayiigwa dash (and presently) ezhi-bazhiba’ond (he was pierced). Gegoo owaabandaanaawaa (something did they see) igiw anishinaabeg (the people). Mii dash ezhi-giigidowaad (whereupon they said): “Mii maawiin i’iw opan. (perhaps it is his lung.) Mii sa i’iw gii-nisang. (therefore we have killed him.)”

*Mīnāwini∙i∙ᵘ is printed with n where the §6 parallel (Mīmāwīni’ᵘ, “perhaps”) prints m — read here as maawiin and flagged for M.; the print is preserved in the Jones layer.*

(10) Asämāndạc wīnini’ᵘ.

**Asemaan dash wiin iniw.**

*But it was the tobacco.*

Asemaan dash (but the tobacco) wiin iniw (it was that).

(11) “Mīsa iᵍi’ᵘ ạni∙i∙cikīwätā\!” i‘kitowạg. Änijigīwäwāt, mīsa cigwa kīsāgitōtät. Äcimādcāt, mīnawā sāga∙i∙gạn owâbạndān; inābit, nāwạgām mīnisinatāwạngā owâbạndān. “Tạga, ninga∙ī∙cā,” inändạm. Kägä‘t äji∙i∙jāt äjiwâbandạnk awīya pimi‘kawänit. “Ạmạntcigic wīwâbạmạg awägwän ayāwigwän\!” Mīsa iᵍi’ᵘ näyāb iwiti äcī∙i∙cāt iwiti kā‘pi∙u∙ndatābīt. Äjikāsut, mīdạc äji∙a∙‘kạndōt; wīpạgu ȧwīya untcimōckạmuwạn, wâbickisiwạn. Kuniginīn micībicīn\! Äciyāba‘wāt īni’ᵘ otasämān äcimōckina∙ā∙t. “Ạmbäsano, tạwi ki‘tcipōsāngwāmut\! Kayä dạc tawikisinā, nigu∙u∙tuni‘k tawī∙a∙‘pi‘tatin\! Mī∙i∙’ᵘ pitcīnạg kä∙i∙cikuskusit.”

**“Mii sa i’iw ani-izhi-giiwedaa\!” ikidowag. Eni-izhi-giiwewaad, mii sa zhigwa gii-saagidooded. Ezhi-maajaad, miinawaa zaaga’igan owaabandaan; inaabid, naawagaam miinisinataawangaa owaabandaan. “Daga, ninga-izhaa,” inendam. Geget ezhi-izhaad ezhi-waabandang awiiya bimikawenid. “Amanj igish wii-waabamag awegwen ayaawigwen\!” Mii sa i’iw neyaab iwidi ezhi-izhaad iwidi gaa-bi-ondadaabiid. Ezhi-gaazod, mii dash ezhi-akandood; wiiba go awiiya onji-mooshkamowan, waabishkiziwan. Goniginiin mishiibizhiin\! Ezhi-aaba’waad iniw odasemaan ezhi-mooshkina’aad. “Ambesano, da-wii-gichi-boozaangwaamod\! Gaye dash da-wii-gisinaa, ningodonik da-wii-apiitadin\! Mii iw bijiinag ge-izhi-goshkozid.”**

*“Accordingly let us go home\!” they said. When back home they went, then it was that out he crawled. When he departed thence, another lake he saw; while looking about, he saw an island of sand far out upon the water. “Well, I am going over there,” he thought. Verily, when thither he went, he then saw where something had left the sign of its footprints. “Wonder if I shall see who that may be\!” Accordingly back he went to the place from whence he had come forth upon the view. Hiding himself, he then lay in wait; and in a little while (he saw) something come up to the surface of the water, it was something white. Behold, it was a great Lynx\!¹ Untying his tobacco, he then filled his pipe. “Now, let him fall into a very heavy sleep\! And may it be cold, to the depth of an arm may (the water) freeze\! It is then that he may wake from sleep.”*

“Mii sa i’iw ani-izhi-giiwedaa\!” (“accordingly let us go home\!”) ikidowag (they said). Eni-izhi-giiwewaad (as they went back home), mii sa zhigwa gii-saagidooded (then it was that out he crawled). Ezhi-maajaad (when he departed), miinawaa zaaga’igan owaabandaan (another lake he saw); inaabid (while looking about), naawagaam (far out upon the water) miinisinataawangaa (an island of sand) owaabandaan (he saw). “Daga, ninga-izhaa,” (“well, I am going over there,”) inendam (he thought). Geget ezhi-izhaad (verily, when thither he went) ezhi-waabandang (he then saw) awiiya bimikawenid (where something had left its footprints). “Amanj igish wii-waabamag (wonder if I shall see) awegwen ayaawigwen\!” (whoever he may be\!) Mii sa i’iw neyaab iwidi ezhi-izhaad (so back over there he went) iwidi gaa-bi-ondadaabiid (whence he had come forth upon the view). Ezhi-gaazod (hiding himself), mii dash ezhi-akandood (he then lay in wait); wiiba go (in a little while) awiiya onji-mooshkamowan (something came up to the surface), waabishkiziwan (it was something white). Goniginiin mishiibizhiin\! (behold, a Great Lynx\!) Ezhi-aaba’waad iniw odasemaan (untying his tobacco) ezhi-mooshkina’aad (he filled his pipe). “Ambesano, da-wii-gichi-boozaangwaamod\! (now, let him fall into a very heavy sleep\!) Gaye dash da-wii-gisinaa (and may it be cold), ningodonik da-wii-apiitadin\! (to the depth of an arm may it freeze\!) Mii iw bijiinag ge-izhi-goshkozid. (only then may he wake from sleep.)”

*¹ The water-monster. \[Jones’s note; the marker stands on “great Lynx” in his English (p. 353), and the note is printed at the foot of the Ojibwe page 352.\] — In the print of this section, awīya carries a stray dot over the a (ȧwīya); a flaw in the type is suspected and the reading awiiya kept.*

(12) Kägä‘t iᵍi’ᵘ anīgạskatāgamạtinini iᵘ sāga∙i∙gạn, ki‘tcinībiwa anigackatinini; nōntāgusi mī‘kwạm. Kägä‘t iᵍi’ᵘ äji∙a∙‘pītatink.
