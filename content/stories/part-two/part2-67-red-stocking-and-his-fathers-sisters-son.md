KĀ‘U‘MISKWĀCIGANIT KAYÄ INI‘ᵘ WĪ‘TĀWISẠN.

**GAA-OZHI-MISKWAACHIGANID GAYE INIW WIITAAWISAN.**

RED-STOCKING AND HIS FATHER’S SISTER’S SON.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk and/or Midāsugaⁿj (Series IV; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (“either … or both”; not credited on the story page).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Mīsai Kā‘u‘miskwāciganit wī‘tāwisạn uwītigämān, mī yä‘ta nīciwāt. Mōⁿjạg nạntawäntcigäwạg; nībiwa wāwäckäciwạn unisāwān kayä utāwāgạn uni‘tōnāwa. Mīdạc iᵘ ä‘ta äjiwawānisiwāt kāwīn awiya tcibā‘kwäsiwạn, kayä käbạgitinamwāt kāwīn udāsīnāwa; ānīc kägạbägīcig inäntōwạg nạntawäntcigäwāt. Ninguting änitibi‘kạtinig, kīgitu aᵉa∙i∙nini; kāwīn wīna aᵘ Kāmiskwāciganit, ini‘ᵘ wī‘tāwisạn udigōn: “Ạmbäsa, kägu wī‘kā wītigäsītā\!” Utānōntci∙ā∙n ini‘ᵘ uwī‘tāwisạn aᵉaᵘ Kā‘u‘miskwāciganit.

**Miinsai Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid wiitaawisan owiidigemaan, mii eta niizhiwaad. Moonzhag nandawenjigewag; niibiwa waawaashkeshiwan onisaawaan gaye odaawaagan oni’toonaawaa. Mii dash iw eta ezhi-wawaanisiwaad gaawiin awiya ji-jiibaakweziwan, gaye gaa-bagidinamwaad gaawiin odaasiinaawaa; aaniish gabe-giizhig inendoowag nandawenjigewaad. Ningoding eni-dibikadinig, giigido a’aw inini; gaawiin wiin aw Gaa-miskwaachiganid, iniw wiitaawisan odigoon: “Ambesa, gego wiikaa wiidigesiidaa\!” Odaani-noondi-zhi-aan iniw owiitaawisan a’aw Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid.**

*And now Red-Stocking was living with his cousin (father’s sister’s son), and they were only two. At the time they hunted for game; many deer they slew, and fur-bearing animals they killed. And so the only thing they lacked was some one to cook food for them, and some one to get wood for them they did not have; for throughout the whole of every day they were away hunting for game. Once, when night was coming on, up spoke one of the men; it was not Red-Stocking himself, but it was by his cousin he was told: “Now, let us never be married\!” In vain did Red-Stocking try to dissuade his cousin.*

Miinsai Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid wiitaawisan owiidigemaan (now Red-Stocking was living with his cross-cousin — \*name stem flagged 67-H1; wiitaawis = father’s-sister’s-son, 67-H2\*), mii eta niizhiwaad (just the two of them). Moonzhag nandawenjigewag (always they hunted); niibiwa waawaashkeshiwan onisaawaan (many deer they killed) gaye odaawaagan oni’toonaawaa (and fur-animals they slew). Mii dash iw eta ezhi-wawaanisiwaad (the one thing they lacked) gaawiin awiya ji-jiibaakweziwan (was anyone to cook), gaye gaa-bagidinamwaad gaawiin odaasiinaawaa (and a wood-gatherer they had not); aaniish gabe-giizhig (for all day long) inendoowag nandawenjigewaad (they were bent on hunting). Ningoding eni-dibikadinig (once as night came on), giigido a’aw inini (a man spoke); gaawiin wiin aw Gaa-miskwaachiganid (not Red-Stocking himself), iniw wiitaawisan odigoon (by his cousin he was told): “Ambesa, gego wiikaa wiidigesiidaa\!” (“come, let us never marry\!”) Odaani-noondi-zhi-aan (in vain he tried to dissuade — \*stem flagged\*) iniw owiitaawisan a’aw Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid (Red-Stocking his cousin).

*wī‘tāwisạn — \*wiitaawis\* cross-cousin (Jones “father’s sister’s son”); marriage-prohibition hinge flagged (67-H2). Kāmiskwāciganit (no -u-) here beside Kā‘u‘miskwāciganit — both preserved.*

Äjiwâbạmigut mīgu‘ kayä wīn äjibä‘pi∙ā∙t. Ā‘pidci payäcwâbạmāt icpiming icini‘käniwạn, mīgu‘ änīci∙ō∙mbickānit. Kāpickunāgusinit, wäwäni kawicimwäpạgisō änigu‘k äjimawit. Ānīc ā‘pidci inändạm. “Ạmbägic wīdigämạg\!” inändạm. Ā‘pidci tayämawit intawā äcikīwät. Tägwicink pīndigät äntāt. Äjikawicimut nibāt.

**… zhiibayaani eni-izhaad; gwayak eni-izhaad. Ikwewan owaabamaad dazhi-binaakwe’onid, geget mikawaadiziniwan. Zhigwa ezhi-beshwaabamaad bii-inaabiwan ezhi-baapi’igod. Ezhi-waabamigod miigo gaye wiin ezhi-baapi’aad. Aapiji bayeshwaabamaad ishpiming izhini’geniwan, miigo eni-izhi-oombishkaanid. Gaa-bishkonaagozinid, weweni gawishimwebagizo enigok ezhi-mawid. Aaniish aapiji inendam. “Ambegizh wiidigemag\!” inendam. Aapiji dayemawid indawaa ezhi-giiwed. Dagwishing biindiged endaad. Ezhi-gawishimod nibaad.**

*On the morrow thence departed the man. By and by, while walking along, very dense was the forest of tall trees through which he was going; straight on his way he kept. A woman he saw who was combing her hair in a place there, truly pretty she was. When he came into easy view of her, he was met with a look and a smile. When seen by her, he therefore smiled at her in return. When very close to her he was come, then up she raised an arm, whereupon she ascended into the air. After she had disappeared from view, forthwith down (upon the ground) he flung himself, weeping as hard as he could. For very (deep) was his feeling (for her). “Would that I might marry her\!” he wished. When he had had a surfeit of crying, he accordingly went back home. On his arrival, he then entered into where he lived. Then he lay down to go to sleep.*

…zhiibayaani eni-izhaad (dense was the wood he went through); gwayak eni-izhaad (straight on he kept). Ikwewan owaabamaad dazhi-binaakwe’onid (a woman he saw combing her hair — \*binaakwe’o- flagged 67-H4\*), geget mikawaadiziniwan (truly pretty she was). Zhigwa ezhi-beshwaabamaad (coming near in view) bii-inaabiwan ezhi-baapi’igod (she met him with a look and smile). Ezhi-waabamigod (seen by her) miigo gaye wiin ezhi-baapi’aad (he smiled back too). Aapiji bayeshwaabamaad (when very close), miigo eni-izhi-oombishkaanid (up into the air she rose). Gaa-bishkonaagozinid (after she vanished), weweni gawishimwebagizo (gently he flung himself down) enigok ezhi-mawid (weeping with all his might). Aaniish aapiji inendam (deep was his feeling). “Ambegizh wiidigemag\!” (“would I might marry her\!”) inendam (he wished). Aapiji dayemawid (sated with crying) indawaa ezhi-giiwed (he went home). Dagwishing biindiged endaad (arriving he entered his lodge). Ezhi-gawishimod nibaad (he lay down to sleep).

(4) Ā‘pidci wänāgucininig, tagwicin Kā‘u‘miskwāciganit. Ä‘tawā\!, nibāwạn wī‘tāwisạn. Kā‘kīsisä‘kwät äjikạnōnāt: “Nī‘tāwis, ạmbä, wīsinin\!”

**Aapiji wenaagoshinig, dagwishin Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid. Etawaa\!, nibaawan wiitaawisan. Gaa-giizisekwed ezhi-ganoonaad: “Niitaawis, ambe, wiisinin\!”**

*When it was late in the evening, home came Red-Stocking. Poor thing\! there asleep was his cousin. After he had cooked a meal, he then spoke to him saying: “My cousin, come, eat some food\!”*

Aapiji wenaagoshinig (very late in the evening), dagwishin Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid (Red-Stocking came home). Etawaa\! (poor thing\!) nibaawan wiitaawisan (his cousin was asleep). Gaa-giizisekwed (after cooking a meal) ezhi-ganoonaad (he spoke to him): “Niitaawis, ambe, wiisinin\!” (“my cousin, come, eat\!”)

(5) Pimayāwīwạn wī‘tāwisạn. Unisi‘tawināwān kīkitcimawinit, ā‘pidci kagībwābimowạn. Wīsiniwāt kägä‘t kāwīn ugạnōnigusīn. Intawā äjikawicimuwāt nibāwāt tibi‘kạtinig. Kigīcäp mạdwäwạnickāwạn wī‘tāwisạn, ā‘pidci kigīcäp; ugạnōnigōn: “Nī‘tāwis, ạmbä, wīsinin\!”

**Bimayaawiiwan wiitaawisan. Onisidawinaawaan giikijimawinid, aapiji gagiibwaabimowan. Wiisiniwaad geget gaawiin oganoonigosiin. Indawaa ezhi-gawishimowaad nibaawaad dibikadinig. Gigizheb madwewanishkaawan wiitaawisan, aapiji gigizheb; oganoonigoon: “Niitaawis, ambe, wiisinin\!”**

*Forth from bed then came his cousin. (Red-Stocking) knew that he had been weeping bitterly, (for he saw that) very small were his eyes by (reason of much) weeping. While they ate, he really got no word from him. Therefore then went they to bed, to sleep when night came on. In the morning he heard the sound of his cousin rising from bed, very early in the morning. He was addressed by him saying: “My cousin, come, eat some food\!” Then up from bed rose Red-Stocking.*

Bimayaawiiwan wiitaawisan (out from bed came his cousin). Onisidawinaawaan giikijimawinid (he knew he had wept bitterly), aapiji gagiibwaabimowan (very small were his eyes from weeping — \*stem flagged\*). Wiisiniwaad geget gaawiin oganoonigosiin (as they ate, no word from him). Indawaa ezhi-gawishimowaad (so they lay down) nibaawaad dibikadinig (to sleep at nightfall). Gigizheb madwewanishkaawan wiitaawisan (in the morning he heard his cousin rise), aapiji gigizheb (very early); oganoonigoon (he was told): “Niitaawis, ambe, wiisinin\!” (“my cousin, come, eat\!”)

(6) Tcibwā∙i∙ckwāwīsinit āca mi∙i∙‘ᵘ kīmādcānit. Mīsa‘ mīnawā iwiti äni∙i∙cāt aᵉa∙i∙nini, iwiti kīwâbạmāt ini‘ᵘ i‘kwäwạn. Kägä‘t täbābạndạnk imāⁿ kīwâbạmāt ini‘ᵘ i‘kwäwạn ạnī∙i∙nābit, käyābi nạmạdạpiwạn. Äni∙i∙jināsi‘kawāt; cigwa äcipäcwâbạmāt ā‘pidci ubā‘pi∙i∙gōn kayä wīn ubā‘pi∙ā∙n. Mīnawā i‘pimīng icini‘käniwạn, äc∙i∙ōmbickānit; kāpickunāgusinit, mīdạc kägä‘t tci∙ä∙nigu‘k äjimawit. Ā‘pidci kā‘tämawit äjikīwät. Kā‘tagwicink, ändāwāt mōⁿcạg mawi.

**Jibwaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid aazha mii iw gii-maajaanid. Mii sa miinawaa iwidi eni-izhaad a’aw inini, iwidi gii-waabamaad iniw ikwewan. Geget debaabandang imaa gii-waabamaad iniw ikwewan ani-inaabid, geyaabi namadabiwan. Eni-izhi-naazikawaad; zhigwa ezhi-beshwaabamaad aapiji obaapi’igoon gaye wiin obaapi’aan. Miinawaa ishpiming izhini’geniwan, ezhi-oombishkaanid; gaa-bishkonaagozinid, mii dash geget ji-enigok ezhi-mawid. Aapiji gaa-demawid ezhi-giiwed. Gaa-dagwishing, endaawaad moonzhag mawi.**

*Before (his cousin) had finished eating, even then did he depart. And so again to yonder place then went the man, to that place where he had seen the woman. Of a truth, when he came in sight of the place, he beheld the woman. As he went, he kept looking, and still there she sat. Then to where she was he went; when he drew nigh to her, a very (pleasant) smile he received from her, and he too smiled back at her. Again up she raised her arm, then into the air she ascended; after she had gone out of sight, then in truth very bitterly did he weep. After he was sated with crying, then back home he went. When he was come at where they lived, all the while did he weep.*

Jibwaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid (before he had finished eating) aazha mii iw gii-maajaanid (already he set out). Mii sa miinawaa iwidi eni-izhaad a’aw inini (again the man went over there), iwidi gii-waabamaad iniw ikwewan (where he had seen the woman). Geget debaabandang imaa (truly sighting the place) gii-waabamaad iniw ikwewan (he saw the woman) ani-inaabid (as he looked on), geyaabi namadabiwan (still she sat). Eni-izhi-naazikawaad (he approached her); zhigwa ezhi-beshwaabamaad (drawing near) aapiji obaapi’igoon (a smile from her) gaye wiin obaapi’aan (and he smiled back). Miinawaa ishpiming izhini’geniwan (again she raised her arm), ezhi-oombishkaanid (rose into the air); gaa-bishkonaagozinid (after she vanished), mii dash geget ji-enigok ezhi-mawid (then truly hard he wept). Aapiji gaa-demawid ezhi-giiwed (sated, he went home). Gaa-dagwishing, endaawaad moonzhag mawi (home, he wept all the while).

(7) Wänāgusig tạgwicin aᵉa‘ᵘ Kā‘u‘miskwāciganit; unōntawān mạdwämawinit wī‘tāwisạn. Änipīndigät kīckōwäwạn. Pitcīnạg äjimạnisät; kā∙i∙ckwāmạnisät, äjikīsisä‘kwät. Cigwa ōkạnōnān wī‘tāwisạn: “Nī‘tāwis, ạmbä wīsinin\!”

**Wenaagozig dagwishin a’aw Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid; onoondawaan madwe-mawinid wiitaawisan. Eni-biindiged giishkowewan. Bijiinag ezhi-manised; gaa-ishkwaa-manised, ezhi-giizisekwed. Zhigwa ogaanoonaan wiitaawisan: “Niitaawis, ambe wiisinin\!”**

*In the evening home came Red-Stocking; he heard the sound of his cousin weeping. When in he went, then (his cousin) ceased crying. Presently then went he to get some fire-wood; after he had gathered the fire-wood, he then prepared a meal. In a while he spoke to his cousin, saying: “My cousin, come, eat some food\!”*

Wenaagozig dagwishin a’aw Gaa-ozhi-miskwaachiganid (in the evening Red-Stocking came home); onoondawaan madwe-mawinid wiitaawisan (he heard his cousin weeping). Eni-biindiged giishkowewan (when he entered, the other ceased). Bijiinag ezhi-manised (then he went for firewood); gaa-ishkwaa-manised (when he had gathered it), ezhi-giizisekwed (he cooked a meal). Zhigwa ogaanoonaan wiitaawisan (in a while he spoke to his cousin): “Niitaawis, ambe wiisinin\!” (“my cousin, come, eat\!”)

(8) Kägä‘t ānumādci‘tāwạn wīwisininit; kāwäsa ugạckitōsinini tciwīsininit.

**Geget aanu-maaji’taawan wii-wiisininid; gaawesa ogashkitoosinini ji-wiisininid.**

*In truth, (his cousin) tried to eat; but it was no use, for he was not able to eat.*

Geget aanu-maaji’taawan wii-wiisininid (truly he tried to begin to eat); gaawesa ogashkitoosinini ji-wiisininid (but in vain — he could not eat).

*In truth, up from bed rose Red-Stocking.
