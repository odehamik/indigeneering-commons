PŌ‘KIDI.

**BOOKIDI.**

BOBTAIL.

*(Narrator not credited on the page — left for M.; no series header sighted in this range.)*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ninguding kīwäⁿ tāwạg anicinābäᵉg. Äniwä‘kigu ōdä‘tōⁿsiwạg. Kāwin gägōn pīwābi‘k udayāsīnāwa, kāwin wâgā‘kwạt,¹ kāwin gayä mō‘kumān;¹ kāwin gayä kägō ano‘kātcigan; mīyä‘ta kījiginigä‘kwạn wä‘kunāsiwāt, kayä awäsīyäⁿsạwayānạn udō‘kunāsināwān; kayä pīwānạgōn ugī∙u∙mō‘kumāniwạg; asînīn ogīwâgā‘kwạdōwạg.

**Ningoding giiwenh daawag anishinaabeg. Eniwekigo odenadoonsiwag. Gaawiin gegoon biiwaabik odayaasiinaawaa, gaawiin waagaakwad, gaawiin gaye mookomaan; gaawiin gaye gegoo anokaajigan; miiyeta giizhiginigekwan gaa-gonaasiwaad, gaye awesiiyensawayaanan odookonaasinaawaan; gaye biiwaanagoon ogii-omookomaaniwag; asiniin ogii-waagaakwadowag.**

*Now, once on a time, they say, there lived some people. Hardly even a town did they have. Nothing of metal did they use, no axe, and no knife; and nothing in the way of goods; of cedar-bark only did they make blankets to wear, and the skins of the small animal-folk did they also use for robes; and of flint were their knives; stones did they use for axes.*

Ningoding giiwenh (once, they say) daawag anishinaabeg (there lived some people). Eniwekigo odenadoonsiwag (hardly even a little town did they have). Gaawiin gegoon biiwaabik odayaasiinaawaa (nothing of metal did they use), gaawiin waagaakwad (no axe), gaawiin gaye mookomaan (and no knife); gaawiin gaye gegoo anokaajigan (and nothing in the way of goods); miiyeta giizhiginigekwan (only of cedar-bark) gaa-gonaasiwaad (did they make blankets to wear), gaye awesiiyensawayaanan (and the skins of the small animals) odookonaasinaawaan (they used for robes); gaye biiwaanagoon (and flint) ogii-omookomaaniwag (they made into their knives); asiniin ogii-waagaakwadowag (stones they used for axes).

*¹ Of metal. — Jones’s footnote, marking the axe (waagaakwad) and knife (mookomaan); prints at the foot of the Ojibwe p. 262. odenadoonsiwag — “hardly a town did they have,” the oodena-diminutive verb, drafted; stem flagged. ogii-omookomaaniwag (§1) — “they made (flint) into their knives,” drafted, flagged.*

(2) Ningudingidạc päᵉjik uckinawä pō‘kidi kā∙i∙nint ugīkạnōnān wīdcickinawän: “Ạmbäsạnōnā, mādcādā\! Kägu kayä gīn awiya windạmawā‘kän\!”

**Ningodingidash bezhig oshkinawe Bookidi gaa-inind ogii-ganoonaan wiijiishkinawen: “Ambezhanoonaa, maajaadaa\! Gego gaye giin awiya wiindamawaaken\!”**

*Now, once a certain youth, who was known by the name of Bobtail, spoke to his youthful comrade, saying: “Pray, let us go away\! And tell it not to any one\!”*

Ningodingidash bezhig oshkinawe (now once a certain youth) Bookidi gaa-inind (who was called Bobtail — \*name flagged\*) ogii-ganoonaan wiijiishkinawen (spoke to his youthful comrade): “Ambezhanoonaa, maajaadaa\! (“pray, let us go away\!) Gego gaye giin awiya wiindamawaaken\!” (and you, tell it to no one\!”)

*Bookidi — the protagonist’s name (English “Bobtail”); pō‘kidi converted by lenis rule, but the name itself is FLAGGED for M. (its formation is uncertain; the close prints pō‘kidi‘k, drafted Bookidig). Ambezhanoonaa — “pray, let us…,” the urging particle, drafted.*

(3) Mīdac kä‘gä‘t kā∙i∙jitcigäwād. Kāwin ōmā ayāsīgwābạn. Wâbanung kitci∙a∙gāming mī∙i∙widi ändạna‘kīwạgubạnän. Mī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙jimādcāwād kwaya‘k äpạngicimut kī∙i∙jāwạg. Kīnwäᵉj kīpîmosāwạg. Nāningudinū kāwin kägō kämidciwād ugī∙a∙yāsīnāwa; nāningudinū pînäwạn unîsāwān; nāningudinū kayä ạnōtc kägōn nä‘tāwigink a‘kīng² umī‘kānāwa mādciwād. Kāwin ā‘pidci kīkîjī‘kāsiwạg, wäwänigu kīpîmusāwạg; pînic kīmạdāpīwād kistciki‘tcigamīng. Mīdec imā kīnwäᵉj kīpạbāmusāwād; ạnōtc kägō owâbạndānāwa kistcigamīng inābiwād, kistcikiⁿgōⁿyạn sāgibisänit owâbamāwān. Pä‘kicigu ma‘kadä‘kägwâbạn igi‘ᵘ uckinawäg. Mī∙i∙dạc nāningudinū onäⁿgucing ạnwāting, ā‘pidci minunāguzi kizis anipạngicimut. Mēdạc ä‘kidōwāt: “A‘pägic ijāⁿyạnk iwidi\! Nạmạntc äjināgwatogwän\!”

**Mii dash gaget gaa-izhichigewaad. Gaawiin omaa ayaasiigwaaban. Waabanong gichi-agaaming mii iwidi endanakiiwagobanen. Mii dash gaa-izhi-maajaawaad gwayak epangizimod gii-izhaawag. Giinwezh gii-bimosaawag. Naaningodinoo gaawiin gegoo gemijiwaad ogii-ayaasiinaawaa; naaningodinoo binewan onisaawaan; naaningodinoo gaye anooj gegoon netaawiging akiing omiikaanaawaa majiiwaad. Gaawiin aapiji gii-gizhiikaasiwag, wewenigo gii-bimosaawag; biinish gii-madaabiiwaad gichi-gichigamiing. Mii dash imaa giinwezh gii-babaamosaawaad; anooj gegoo owaabandaanaawaa gichigamiing inaabiwaad, gichi-giigoonyan zaagibizenid owaabamaawaan. Bekizhigo makadekegwaaban igiw oshkinaweg. Mii dash naaningodinoo onaagoshing anwaating, aapiji minonaagozi giizis ani-bangizimod. Mii dash ekidowaad: “Apegizh izhaayaank iwidi\! Namanj ezhinaagwatogwen\!”**

*Thereupon truly such was what they did. Not at this place did they belong. Eastward, on the farther great shore, was their native place. And so, when they started off, straight toward where the sun sets was the way they went. A long while were they travelling. Sometimes they had nothing to eat; sometimes a ruffed grouse they killed; and sometimes the various things that grow upon the ground they found to eat. They did not walk very fast, comfortably they travelled along; at last they came out upon the great sea. And so roundabout the place for a long while they wandered; all sorts of things they saw in the sea while they were looking, a great fish that leaped up out of the water they saw. And at the same time did the youths blacken (their faces and fast). And then frequently in the evening-time, when it was calm on the water, very beautiful was the sight of the sun when it was setting. And so they said: “Would that we might go over there\! Wonder what it be like\!”*

Mii dash gaget gaa-izhichigewaad (and so truly that was what they did). Gaawiin omaa ayaasiigwaaban (not at this place did they belong). Waabanong gichi-agaaming (eastward, on the farther great shore) mii iwidi endanakiiwagobanen (was where their home once was). Mii dash gaa-izhi-maajaawaad (and so when they set off) gwayak epangizimod (straight toward where the sun sets) gii-izhaawag (they went). Giinwezh gii-bimosaawag (a long while they walked). Naaningodinoo gaawiin gegoo gemijiwaad (sometimes nothing did they have to eat); naaningodinoo binewan onisaawaan (sometimes a ruffed grouse they killed); naaningodinoo gaye anooj gegoon netaawiging akiing (and sometimes the various things that grow on the ground) omiikaanaawaa majiiwaad (they found to eat). Gaawiin aapiji gii-gizhiikaasiwag (not very fast did they go), wewenigo gii-bimosaawag (comfortably they travelled along); biinish gii-madaabiiwaad gichi-gichigamiing (at last they came out on the great sea). Mii dash imaa giinwezh gii-babaamosaawaad (and there a long while they wandered about); anooj gegoo owaabandaanaawaa gichigamiing inaabiwaad (all sorts of things they saw looking into the sea), gichi-giigoonyan zaagibizenid owaabamaawaan (a great fish leaping out of the water they saw). Bekizhigo makadekegwaaban igiw oshkinaweg (and at the same time the youths blackened/fasted; \*preterite\*). Mii dash naaningodinoo onaagoshing anwaating (and so sometimes in the evening when it was calm), aapiji minonaagozi giizis ani-bangizimod (very beautiful was the sun as it set). Mii dash ekidowaad (and so they said): “Apegizh izhaayaank iwidi\! (“would that we might go over there\!) Namanj ezhinaagwatogwen\!” (wonder what it is like\!”)

*¹ Berries. — Jones’s footnote on “the various things that grow upon the ground” (netaawiging akiing); prints at the foot of the English p. 263 (Jones numbers footnotes per page here — p. 262 carries “Of metal,” p. 263 carries “Berries”). endanakiiwagobanen — “where they once had their home,” the -goban defunct/preterite frame, drafted. makadekegwaaban — “they blackened/fasted,” the ma‘kadeke- fasting verb in a preterite-dubitative print, drafted.*

(4) Ninguding päcu‘ owâbạmāwān kīⁿgōⁿyạn mō‘kibīsänit, kī‘pimipimisä aᵘ kīⁿgu. Mēdạc kā∙i∙jikạnōnāwāt ini‘ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyạn päcu‘ tcîbījānit. Mēdạc änāt aᵘ pō‘kidi ini‘ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyạn: “Kāwīnina kîdā∙i∙ji∙u∙wijisīnām kwaya‘k kīsis ä‘pạngicimut?”

**Ningoding besho owaabamaawaan giigoonyan mookibiisenid, gii-bimi-bimise aw giigoonh. Mii dash gaa-izhi-ganoonaawaad iniw giigoonyan besho ji-biizhaanid. Mii dash enaad aw Bookidi iniw giigoonyan: “Gaawiinina gidaa-izhi-wiijishinaam gwayak giizis epangizimod?”**

*Once near by they saw a fish come quickly up to the surface of the water, up in the air and back into the water leaped the fish. Accordingly they spoke to the fish, telling it to come nigh. And then Bobtail said to the Fish: “Would you not bear us straight to the place where the sun sets?”*

Ningoding besho owaabamaawaan giigoonyan mookibiisenid (once near by they saw a fish come to the surface), gii-bimi-bimise aw giigoonh (up and back leaped the fish). Mii dash gaa-izhi-ganoonaawaad iniw giigoonyan besho ji-biizhaanid (and so they spoke to the fish, telling it to come near). Mii dash enaad aw Bookidi iniw giigoonyan (and then Bobtail said to the fish): “Gaawiinina gidaa-izhi-wiijishinaam (“could you not carry us) gwayak giizis epangizimod?” (straight to where the sun sets?”)

*mookibiisenid — “coming to the surface,” drafted (mookibii- + -se); kii-bimi-bimise — “leaped up and along,” reduplicated, drafted.*

(5) Awidac kīⁿgōⁿ kī∙i∙‘kido: “Kāwin nîndākạckitōsīn nīⁿj tcîmādcīninagu‘k. Päᵉjik ninganāzi‘kawā käwīdcīwạg.” Mīdạc kīmādcād ‘aᵉa’ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿ pämisät; ugīnāzi‘kawān päᵉjik kīⁿgōⁿyạn. Mī∙i∙dạc nīⁿj kī‘pījāwād igi‘ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyạg.

**Awidash giigoonh gii-ikido: “Gaawiin nindaa-gashkitoosiin niizh ji-maajiininagok. Bezhig ninga-naazikawaa ge-wiijiiwag.” Mii dash gii-maajiid a’aw giigoonh bemised; ogii-naazikawaan bezhig giigoonyan. Mii dash niizh gii-biizhaawaad igiw giigoonyag.**

*And the Fish said: “I would not be able to carry both of you together. I will go fetch one with whom I may go.” Thereupon away departed the Fish, flying through the air; he went to fetch a certain fish. Accordingly two were the fishes that came.*

Awidash giigoonh gii-ikido (and the fish said): “Gaawiin nindaa-gashkitoosiin (“I would not be able) niizh ji-maajiininagok (to carry both of you together). Bezhig ninga-naazikawaa ge-wiijiiwag.” (I will go fetch one to go with me.”) Mii dash gii-maajiid a’aw giigoonh bemised (thereupon away went the fish, flying); ogii-naazikawaan bezhig giigoonyan (he went to fetch a certain fish). Mii dash niizh gii-biizhaawaad igiw giigoonyag (and so two were the fishes that came).

(6) Mē∙i∙dạc kä‘gä‘t kīmādcīnigōwād, päpäᵉjik ini‘ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyan. “Wäwäni mindcimīyu‘k,” udigūwān, “sạnagạt mạmāngāskāt kitcigạmi. Nāningudinū kayä mi‘kwạm ayā kitcigạmīng kayä; nāningudinū kayä a‘kipīg ayāmạgạt. Kīcpîn mi‘kwạm ayāt ạnāmindîm ningatijāmîn,” i‘kidōwạg igi‘ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyạg. “Kīcpîn gayä a‘kibīk ayāmạgạ‘k ningapîmisämin. Wäwäni mindcemīyu‘k ninindciganāng\!”

**Mii dash gaget gii-maajiinigoowaad, bebezhig iniw giigoonyan. “Wewena minjimiiyok,” odigowaan, “zanagad memaangaaskaad gichigami. Naaningodinoo gaye mikwam ayaa gichigamiing gaye; naaningodinoo gaye akwabiig ayaamagad. Giishpin mikwam ayaad anaamindim ningad-izhaamin,” ikidowag igiw giigoonyag. “Giishpin gaye akwabiig ayaamagak ninga-bimisemin. Wewena minjimiiyok nininjiganaang\!”**

*Thereupon, to be sure, were they borne away, each by a fish. “Carefully do you hold on,” they were told, “for it is difficult when the sea is rolling high. And frequently there is ice in the sea, too; and sometimes there is a small floating island. When there is ice, then underneath will we go,” said the fishes. “When there is a small floating island, then into the air will we fly. Carefully hold you on to our fins\!”*

Mii dash gaget gii-maajiinigoowaad (thereupon truly were they borne away), bebezhig iniw giigoonyan (each by a fish). “Wewena minjimiiyok,” odigowaan (“carefully hold on,” they were told), “zanagad memaangaaskaad gichigami (“it is hard when the sea rolls high). Naaningodinoo gaye mikwam ayaa gichigamiing (sometimes there is ice in the sea too); naaningodinoo gaye akwabiig ayaamagad (and sometimes there is open water). Giishpin mikwam ayaad anaamindim ningad-izhaamin,” (if there is ice, underneath we will go,”) ikidowag igiw giigoonyag (said the fishes). “Giishpin gaye akwabiig ayaamagak ninga-bimisemin (“and if there is open water, we will fly). Wewena minjimiiyok nininjiganaang\!” (carefully hold on to our fins\!”)

*memaangaaskaad — “(the sea) rolls high,” drafted; akwabiig — “open water,” drafted (cf. aki-bii- / agwabii-), flagged. ningad-izhaamin / ninga-bimisemin — liaison futures (ningad-/ninga-), hyphenated per precedent. nininjiganaang “on our fins” — drafted.*

(7) Mīsa kä‘gä‘t mādcīniguwād kwaya‘k ä‘pạngicimut kīsis. Änicwäsugunạgạtinik kī‘tagwicinōg igi‘ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyạg i∙i∙māⁿ, a‘ki ki∙u∙di‘tạmuwād sī‘bi ayāmạgạtinig. Mī∙u∙wä Sagi‘tawābi‘kāng äjini‘kätäg ozîbi. Äniwä‘k wä‘saᵘ ugīdādciwạn ugī∙i∙jiwinigōyan ini‘ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyan. “Mī∙o∙mā tcîbạgidinigōyäg,” ugī∙i∙gōwān. Mī∙i∙māⁿ kī‘kạbāwād; kīⁿgōⁿyạg dec kī∙a∙nikīwäwạg näyāp kitcigamīng.

**Mii sa gaget maajiinigowaad gwayak epangizimod giizis. Eshwaaso-gonagadinig gii-dagwishinoog igiw giigoonyag i’imaa, aki gii-odi’tamowaad ziibi ayaamagadinig. Mii iwe Zaagitawaabikaang ezhinikaadeg oziibi. Eniwek wezhao ogii-daadagiiwan ogii-izhiwinigooyan iniw giigoonyan. “Mii omaa ji-bagidinigooyeg,” ogii-igoowaan. Mii imaa gii-gabaawaad; giigoonyag dash gii-ani-giiwewag neyaab gichigamiing.**

*Thereupon truly by them were they conveyed straight to where the sun sets. On the eighth day arrived the fishes at the place, to a land they came where there was a river. Now, this was Projection-out-over-the-Water, which was the name of this river. At some distance up the stream were they conveyed by the fish. “Here is where you shall be left,” they were told. Accordingly they stepped ashore there; while the fishes went their homeward way, back over the sea.*

Mii sa gaget maajiinigowaad gwayak epangizimod giizis (so truly were they carried straight toward where the sun sets). Eshwaaso-gonagadinig gii-dagwishinoog igiw giigoonyag i’imaa (on the eighth day the fishes arrived there), aki gii-odi’tamowaad ziibi ayaamagadinig (coming to a land where there was a river). Mii iwe Zaagitawaabikaang ezhinikaadeg oziibi (this was the river called Projection-out-over-the-Water). Eniwek wezhao ogii-daadagiiwan (some distance up the stream) ogii-izhiwinigooyan iniw giigoonyan (were they conveyed by the fish). “Mii omaa ji-bagidinigooyeg,” ogii-igoowaan (“here is where you will be set free,” they were told). Mii imaa gii-gabaawaad (so there they went ashore); giigoonyag dash gii-ani-giiwewag neyaab gichigamiing (and the fishes went back home over the sea).

*Zaagitawaabikaang — place name, Jones’s English “Projection-out-over-the-Water”; drafted, flagged. eshwaaso-gonagadinig — “on the eighth day,” the day-count final (-gonagad), drafted. daadagiiwan — “up the stream,” drafted, flagged.*

Kīsạnagiziwạg kämidciwād; mīnawā kägō ugîmī‘kānāwa a‘king nä‘tāwiginig, minạn kāmidciwād. Mī∙i∙dạc kīmādcāwād kwaya‘k nîngābi∙a∙nunk; nāningutinū sāga∙i∙gạn ogīmạdābīnāwa; nāningudinō kayä sībing kīwī‘kwadci∙o∙wạg agāming ki∙i∙jāwad. Mīnawā ogīwâbạmāwān nāningutinū pînäwaᵉ mizisaᵉ gayä, mī ini‘ᵘ kā∙a∙mwāwāt. Kī‘pîskạnäpōtcigäwạg skudä uji‘tōwād; kīnwäᵉj ninguding anicinābän ugī∙o∙disāwān; kāwin ogīnisitotawasiwāwān änwänit; ogīcamigūwān mizisä wīyās. ‘Aᵉa’ᵘ gayä anicinābä kāwin kägōn odayāsīn pīwâbi‘k ābạdcîtcigạn. Awäsīwayānạn udō‘kunāsîwāwān. Ogītā‘tānga∙a∙mawāwān uzitāwān pīguzitäcinuwāt, mīdạc kīmīniguwād ma‘kizinạn.

**Miinawaa dash Bookidi gaawiin ogii-gikendaziinaawaa dibi iʼi akiing ayaawaagwen. Anooj gii-ikidowag: “Amanj ezhinaagwatogwen o’ow aki wedi’tamank\!” Mii dash maanoogo apane ezhaawaad gii-izhaawag. Gii-zanagiziwag gemijiwaad; miinawaa gegoo ogii-miikaanaawaa akiing netaawiginig, miinan gaa-mijiwaad. Mii dash gii-maajaawaad gwayak ningaabaya’anong; naaningodinoo zaaga’igan ogii-madaabiinaawaa; naaningodinoo gaye ziibing gii-wiikwajiiwag agaaming gii-izhaawaad. Miinawaa ogii-waabamaawaan naaningodinoo binewa’ mizisa’ gaye, mii iniw gaa-amwaawaad. Gii-biskanebootchigewag ishkode ozhitoowaad; giinwezh ningoding anishinaaben ogii-odisaawaan; gaawiin ogii-nisidotawaasiwaawaan enwenid; ogii-ashamigoowaan mizise-wiiyaas. A’aw gaye anishinaabe gaawiin gegoon odayaasiin biiwaabik abajiichigan. Awesii-wayaanan odookonaasiiwaawaan. Ogii-daadaangaamawaawaan oziidaawaan biigozideshinowaad, mii dash gii-miinigowaad makizinan.**

*Now, Bobtail and his companion did not know in what part of the land they were. All manner of things they said: “Wonder what sort of country this is to which we have come\!” However, without tarrying they continued toward the place whither they were bound. They had a hard time obtaining food to eat; something else they found that grew upon the ground, blueberries was what they ate. Thereupon they continued straight on toward the west; frequently out upon a lake they came; sometimes they tried crossing a river to get over to the other shore. Furthermore, they sometimes saw ruffed grouse and turkeys, and them they ate for food. By boring with a drill they obtained fire; a long while were they travelling. Once to where some people were they came, but they did not understand the language they spoke; by them were they fed upon turkey-meat. And those people, too, possessed nothing in the way of metal tools. With the skins of game-animals were they clad. (Bobtail and his comrade) touched their feet (to show) that they were foot-sore, whereupon they were given moccasins.*

Miinawaa dash Bookidi (now Bobtail) gaawiin ogii-gikendaziinaawaa (did not know) dibi iʼi akiing ayaawaagwen (in what part of the land they were — \*dubitative\*). Anooj gii-ikidowag (all sorts of things they said): “Amanj ezhinaagwatogwen o’ow aki wedi’tamank\!” (“wonder what this land is like that we have reached\!”) Mii dash maanoogo apane ezhaawaad gii-izhaawag (but anyway they kept on going). Gii-zanagiziwag gemijiwaad (they had a hard time finding food); miinawaa gegoo ogii-miikaanaawaa akiing netaawiginig (again they found something growing on the ground), miinan gaa-mijiwaad (blueberries they ate). Mii dash gii-maajaawaad gwayak ningaabaya’anong (and so they went straight toward the west); naaningodinoo zaaga’igan ogii-madaabiinaawaa (sometimes upon a lake they came out); naaningodinoo gaye ziibing gii-wiikwajiiwag agaaming gii-izhaawaad (sometimes they tried to cross a river to the other shore). Miinawaa ogii-waabamaawaan naaningodinoo binewa’ mizisa’ gaye (again they saw sometimes ruffed grouse and turkeys), mii iniw gaa-amwaawaad (and those they ate). Gii-biskanebootchigewag ishkode ozhitoowaad (by drilling they made fire); giinwezh ningoding anishinaaben ogii-odisaawaan (a long while; once they came upon some people); gaawiin ogii-nisidotawaasiwaawaan enwenid (they did not understand their language); ogii-ashamigoowaan mizise-wiiyaas (they were fed turkey-meat). A’aw gaye anishinaabe gaawiin gegoon odayaasiin biiwaabik abajiichigan (these people too had nothing of metal for tools).
