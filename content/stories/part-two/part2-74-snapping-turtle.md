MI‘KINĀ‘K.

**MIKINAAK.**

SNAPPING-TURTLE.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ānīc, i‘kitō anicinābā mi‘kinā‘k ā‘tisō‘kān.

**Aaniish, ikido anishinaabe mikinaak aadizookaan.**

*Well, the people say (that this is) a story of Snapping-Turtle.*

Aaniish (well), ikido (say) anishinaabe (the people), mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) aadizookaan (a story).

(2) Iᵍi witạc ạnicinābäg ōdänawi∙i∙tiwạg. Päjik ugimāwi, aᵍaᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzī. Midā‘tciwaᵍ udānisaᵍ, ga‘kina uckinīgiwaᵍ. Ninguting māminunäntạm aᵍa’ᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzī. “Ambäsạnō ga‘kina kawīdigäwạg ōgo’ᵘ nintānîsạg\! mī∙i∙’ᵘ kä∙i∙ciminwātạmān.”

**I’iw idash anishinaabeg oodenawi’idiwag. Bezhig ogimaawi, a’aw akiwenzii. Midaachiwag odaanisag, gakina oshkiniigiwag. Ningoding maaminonendam a’aw akiwenzii. “Ambesanoo gakina ga-wiidigewag ogow nindaanisag\! mii iw ge-izhi-minwaadamaan.”**

*Now, in a (certain) place the people were living together in a town. A certain one was chief, an old man. Ten in number were his daughters, all maidens. Once the old man got to revolving matters over in his mind. “Would that all these daughters of mine might marry\! and then I should be happy.”*

I’iw idash (now then) anishinaabeg (the people) oodenawi’idiwag (lived together as a town). Bezhig ogimaawi (one was chief), a’aw akiwenzii (that old man). Midaachiwag (ten in number were) odaanisag (his daughters), gakina oshkiniigiwag (all were young — maidens). Ningoding (once) maaminonendam (he pondered over things) a’aw akiwenzii (that old man). “Ambesanoo (would that) gakina (all) ga-wiidigewag (they will marry) ogow nindaanisag (these daughters of mine)\! mii iw (that is) ge-izhi-minwaadamaan (how I would be glad of it).”

(3) Ānīc mīdạc aᵍa’ᵘ mi‘kinā‘k päci‘k wâwīwit ini‘kwāwạn; kayä aᵍa’ᵘ wâbōs mī∙a∙ päji‘k wâwīwit īni‘kwäwạn; kayä aᵍa’ᵘ wâguc; kayä aᵍa’ᵘ ma∙ī∙ngạn, kayä picyᵘ, kayä aᵍa’ᵘ cāⁿgwäci, kayä wīn aᵍa’ᵘ nigi‘k; mīnawā utcig, kayä aᵍa’ᵘ kōkō‘ku‘u, kayä wīn pōtawatūmā; mī iᵘ midāswi.

**Aaniish mii dash a’aw mikinaak bezhig waa-wiiwid iniw ikwewan; gaye a’aw waabooz mii a∙ bezhig waa-wiiwid iniw ikwewan; gaye a’aw waagosh; gaye a’aw ma’iingan, gaye bizhiw, gaye a’aw zhaangweshi, gaye wiin a’aw nigig; miinawaa ojiig, gaye a’aw gookooko’oo, gaye wiin boodawadoomaa; mii iw midaaswi.**

*Now, there was Snapping-Turtle who desired to take one of the women to wife; so too Hare wished one of the women for a wife, likewise Fox, and Wolf, and Lynx, and Mink, and Otter; also Fisher, and Horned Owl, and also Owl; so that was ten.*

Aaniish mii dash (now then) a’aw mikinaak (that Snapping-Turtle) bezhig waa-wiiwid (wanted one as wife) iniw ikwewan (of those women); gaye a’aw waabooz (and that Hare) bezhig waa-wiiwid iniw ikwewan (likewise wanted one of the women as wife); gaye a’aw waagosh (and Fox); gaye a’aw ma’iingan (and Wolf), gaye bizhiw (and Lynx), gaye a’aw zhaangweshi (and Mink), gaye wiin a’aw nigig (and Otter too); miinawaa ojiig (also Fisher), gaye a’aw gookooko’oo (and Horned Owl), gaye wiin boodawadoomaa (and Owl as well); mii iw midaaswi (so that was ten).

*pōtawatūmā — Jones translates “Owl”; the word is otherwise unattested in the corpus and the conversion (boodawadoomaa) is drafted by character rules only — flagged for review. mī∙a∙ (after wâbōs) carried as printed, function unclear — flagged.*

(4) Mīsa iᵍi’ᵘ ki‘tciwigiwām ạnō‘kit aᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzī. Kā‘kīcitcigātäg iᵍi’ᵘ wīgiwām, mī∙i∙māⁿ ka‘kina äcipīndigäwāt. “Mīsa’ iᵘ äciki‘tciminwäntạmān cigwa ka‘kina kīwītigäwāt ōgo’ᵘ nintānisạg,” i‘kito aᵘ a‘kiwäⁿzī.

**Mii sa i’iw gichi-wiigiwaam anookid aw akiwenzii. Gaa-giizhichigaadeg i’iw wiigiwaam, mii imaa gakina ezhi-biindigewaad. “Mii sa iw ezhi-gichi-minwendamaan zhigwa gakina gii-wiidigewaad ogow nindaanisag,” ikido aw akiwenzii.**

*Thereupon a large wigwam did the old man have made. When the wigwam was finished, then all went inside. “This is what I greatly like, that now all these daughters of mine are married,” said the old man.*

Mii sa i’iw (thereupon) gichi-wiigiwaam (a great wigwam) anookid (he ordered made) aw akiwenzii (that old man). Gaa-giizhichigaadeg (when it was finished) i’iw wiigiwaam (that wigwam), mii imaa (then there) gakina (all) ezhi-biindigewaad (they went inside). “Mii sa iw (this is) ezhi-gichi-minwendamaan (how greatly I am pleased) zhigwa (now) gakina gii-wiidigewaad (that they all are married) ogow nindaanisag (these daughters of mine),” ikido (said) aw akiwenzii (the old man).

(5) Äjigīgitut päjik inini: “Mīsa cigwa tcimādci‘tāyạnk tci∙ạ∙ntawäntcigäyạnk.”

**Ezhi-giigidod bezhig inini: “Mii sa zhigwa ji-maajiitaayang ji-andawenjigeyang.”**

*Then spoke a certain man, saying: “It is now time that we start on a hunt to seek for game.”*

Ezhi-giigidod (then spoke) bezhig inini (a certain man): “Mii sa zhigwa (it is now time) ji-maajiitaayang (for us to begin — \*inclusive we\*) ji-andawenjigeyang (for us to go seeking game).”

(6) Mīnōtc na kayä wīn nạnā‘kwä‘tạng mi‘kinā‘k. Kägä‘t utānawänimigō iᵍi’ᵘ wītcikiwäyaᵍ. “Kạnạbạtc mī aᵘ nīgān kämạtäntāgusit mini‘k wātigäⁿyạnk,” inā’.

**Minooj na gaye wiin nanaakwetang mikinaak. Geget odaanawenimigoo i’iw wiijikiwenya’. “Ganabaj mii aw niigaan ge-majendaagozid minik wedigeyang,” inaa’.**

*(Despite his displeasure with all those words,) yet Snapping-Turtle gave assenting response.¹ Verily, he was despised by his companions. “Perhaps he will take the lead in the matter of being good for nothing among the number of us here who are married,” was said of him.*

Minooj na gaye wiin (despite it all, he too) nanaakwetang (gave answering assent) mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle). Geget (verily) odaanawenimigoo (he was held in low regard — \*aano- ‘in vain’ inside the inverse\*) i’iw wiijikiwenya’ (by his companions). “Ganabaj (perhaps) mii aw niigaan (he will be the one to lead) ge-majendaagozid (in being thought good for nothing) minik wedigeyang (among as many of us as are married),” inaa’ (was said of him).

*Jones’s footnote (marker at “assenting response,” prints across the feet of pp. 736–737): During the course of a speech it is common for individuals, singly or together, to give utterances with the voice. As a rule, it indicates agreement to the sentiments expressed; often it is given out of etiquette, as in this case by Snapping-Turtle. — W.J. kämạtäntāgusit — print mạt- where mạtc- (maji-) is expected (cf. mạtcänimā §23); suspected dropped c, converted majendaagozid, flagged.*

(7) Cigwa pipōnini, cigwa mādci‘tāwạg kīyusäwāt. Kāwäsa mi‘kinā‘k ugạckitōsīn tcigīyusät. Ānīc ā‘pidci ạtcina ānint untäntōwạg mī āca kīni‘tāgäwāt. Kägä‘t uwânạtci‘tōnāwa kämīdciwāt. Mīsa ä‘ta mi‘kinā‘k kāwīn wī‘kā kägō wạni‘tōsig.

**Zhigwa biboonini, zhigwa maajiitaawag giiyosewaad. Gaawesa mikinaak ogashkitoosiin ji-giiyosed. Aaniish aapiji ajina aanind ondendowag mii azha gii-nitaagewaad. Geget owaanaji’toonaawaa ge-miijiwaad. Mii sa eta mikinaak gaawiin wiikaa gegoo waniitoosig.**

*Soon it began to be winter, in a while they began to hunt for game. Not at all was Snapping-Turtle able to walk. Now, but a very little while were some of them gone, when already they had made a killing. In truth, they had a store of food to eat. It was Snapping-Turtle only that never killed a thing.*

Zhigwa biboonini (soon it was coming on winter), zhigwa maajiitaawag (then they began) giiyosewaad (their hunting). Gaawesa (not at all) mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) ogashkitoosiin (was able) ji-giiyosed (to go hunting). Aaniish aapiji ajina (only a very little while) aanind (some of them) ondendowag (were gone) mii azha (when already) gii-nitaagewaad (they had made kills). Geget (truly) owaanaji’toonaawaa (they stored away) ge-miijiwaad (what they would eat). Mii sa eta (it was only) mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) gaawiin wiikaa gegoo (never anything) waniitoosig (who got nothing at all — \*negative conjunct\*).

*Jones’s English “able to walk” for tcigīyusät (to hunt) is his free rendering; the Ojibwe says hunt — noted, not corrected.*

(8) Ningutingigu awänibạn wâbōs kätagwicink, mīsa iᵘ kā∙i∙citibi‘kändit. Sägisiwạg wäwītcikiwäyiwāt. “Māgicā kīnibu,” inäntạmōg. Wäyābạninig skwạtc mīnawā änōnāgucininig, kīgitōwạg īniniwạg: “Wâbạnk intawā nạntunäwā‘kank.” Änitibi‘kạtinig tạgwicinōg; aᵘ wâbōs tạgwicin kayä wīn, mīsa cigwa kīminwäntạmuwāt. Äjikạnōnint: “Ānīn, wâbōs\! kā∙i∙cictcigäyạn kītibi‘käntiyạn?”

**Ningoding igo aweniban waabooz ge-dagwishing, mii sa iw gaa-izhi-dibikendid. Zegiziwag wewiijikiweyiwaad. “Maagizhaa gii-nibo,” inendamoog. Weyaabaninig ishkwaaj miinawaa enoonaagoshininig, giigidowag ininiwag: “Waabang indawaa nandonewaakang.” Eni-tibikadinig dagwishinoog; aw waabooz dagwishin gaye wiin, mii sa zhigwa gii-minwendamowaad. Ezhi-ganoonind: “Aaniin, waabooz\! gaa-izhichigeyan gii-dibikendiyan?”**

*And once Hare was the only one not to return, for he had gone to be away over night. Alarmed became those that were his companions. “Perhaps he may have died,” they thought. On the morrow, when the evening was drawing on, up spoke the men, saying: “To-morrow, therefore, we should look for him.” When the night was drawing on, they came home; Hare came back also, whereupon then they were glad. Then was he addressed: “What, O Hare\! were you doing, that you should stay out over night?”*

Ningoding igo (and once) aweniban (gone was — \*preterite -ban on the absent one\*) waabooz (Hare) ge-dagwishing (who should have arrived), mii sa iw (for it was that) gaa-izhi-dibikendid (he stayed out over night). Zegiziwag (alarmed became) wewiijikiweyiwaad (they who were his companions). “Maagizhaa gii-nibo (perhaps he has died),” inendamoog (they thought). Weyaabaninig (on the morrow) ishkwaaj miinawaa enoonaagoshininig (when at last evening was again drawing on), giigidowag ininiwag (up spoke the men): “Waabang indawaa (to-morrow, therefore) nandonewaakang (let us look for him).” Eni-tibikadinig (as night drew on) dagwishinoog (they came home); aw waabooz (that Hare) dagwishin gaye wiin (arrived also), mii sa zhigwa (and then) gii-minwendamowaad (they were glad). Ezhi-ganoonind (then was he addressed): “Aaniin, waabooz\! (what, O Hare\!) gaa-izhichigeyan (what were you doing) gii-dibikendiyan (that you stayed out over night)?”

*awänibạn — drafted aweniban (‘the absent one,’ preterite -ban; cf. Skwạtcībạn \#53, pawānạgipạn \#45); the parse is flagged for review. nạntunäwā‘kank — morphology unclear (expected nandone’wang); converted by character rules and flagged.*

(9) “Kā, wāsa’ ningī∙i∙cā, mī∙i∙’ᵘ kīyō‘kawi∙ạ∙gwa īgi’ᵘ mōⁿsōg. Mīdāswi tasu‘kawäwạg.”

**“Gaa, waasa’ ningii-izhaa, mii iw gii-okawi’agwaa igiw moonzoog. Midaaswi dasookawewag.”**

*“Why, a long way off was I gone, for I was on trail of the moose. Ten was their number by the trail they made.”*

“Gaa (why), waasa’ (a long way off) ningii-izhaa (I went), mii iw (for it was that) gii-okawi’agwaa (I found the trail of them) igiw moonzoog (those moose). Midaaswi (ten) dasookawewag (was their number by their tracks).”

(10) Kägä‘t minwäntạmōg.

**Geget minwendamoog.**

*Verily, they were pleased.*

Geget (verily) minwendamoog (they were pleased).

(11) “Wâbạnk kamādcāmin, mīsạgạ iᵘ kä∙i∙cimidātcīyank.”

**“Waabang ga-maajaamin, mii sa go iw ge-izhi-midaachiiyang.”**

*“To-morrow we will set out, for there are just ten of us.”*

“Waabang (to-morrow) ga-maajaamin (we will set out), mii sa go iw (for it is just that) ge-izhi-midaachiiyang (we number ten — \*inclusive we\*).”

*mīsạgạ — suspected printer’s ạ for u (mīsạgu = mii sa go is attested at \#73); preserved as printed in the Jones line, converted mii sa go, flagged.*

(12) Kägä‘t mi‘kinā‘k cīngi‘tạm; ānīc minōtc kayä wīn mi‘kinā‘k nạnā‘kwä‘tạm. Ā‘pitci kigicäp mādcāwạg, ạgāwā ạnitibi‘twa; mōⁿcạg nạgạnā mi‘kinā‘k; ạnicāgu‘ku ạnibī∙ā∙. Ningutingigu sāgạtciwäwạg, mīdạc imāⁿ ạnwäpiwāt. Äjikīgitut wâbōs: “Mī∙i∙witi kīpimigitcigạtināg kīyō‘kawi∙ạ∙gwa īgi’ᵘ mōⁿsōg.”

**Geget mikinaak zhiingitam; aaniish minooj gaye wiin mikinaak nanaakwetam. Aapiji gigizheb maajaawag, agaawaa ani-dibi’twaa; moonzhag naganaa mikinaak; anishaa go iko ani-bii’aa. Ningoding igo zaagajiwewag, mii dash imaa anwebiwaad. Ezhi-giigidod waabooz: “Mii iwidi gii-bimi-gichigadinaag gii-okawi’agwaa igiw moonzoog.”**

*To be sure, Snapping-Turtle was not pleased with it; yet, in spite of (his dislike), Snapping-Turtle responded assent. Very early in the morning they set out, hardly could he keep up; all the while he was left behind; now and then he was waited for. And once they came out upon the summit of a hill, and so there they rested. Then spoke Hare, saying: “It was yonder where the hills rise one upon another that I got on trail of those moose.”*

Geget (to be sure) mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) zhiingitam (disliked the sound of it); aaniish minooj (yet despite that) gaye wiin (he too) mikinaak nanaakwetam (gave answering assent). Aapiji gigizheb (very early in the morning) maajaawag (they set out), agaawaa (hardly) ani-dibi’twaa (could he be kept up with — \*flagged\*); moonzhag (all the while) naganaa (he was left behind); anishaa go iko (just now and then) ani-bii’aa (he was waited for along the way). Ningoding igo (and once) zaagajiwewag (they came out on a hilltop), mii dash imaa (and so there) anwebiwaad (they rested). Ezhi-giigidod waabooz (then spoke Hare): “Mii iwidi (it was yonder) gii-bimi-gichigadinaag (where big hills run one upon another) gii-okawi’agwaa (that I got on the trail of them) igiw moonzoog (those moose).”

*ạnitibi‘twa — Jones “hardly could he keep up”; morphology unparsed (debi’- ‘catch up to’ suspected) — converted by character rules, flagged.*

(13) Aᵘ mi‘kinā‘k mīnạngwạnagu iᵘ nickimint. Aᵘ mi‘kinā‘k wīnigu mägwā‘tạm iᵍi’ᵘ kībimigitcigatināg i‘kitōwint. “Nīn kāwī∙i∙gōyān,” ināndạm aᵍa’ᵘ mi‘kinā‘k; mīdạc iᵍi’ᵘ kitcigisit imāⁿ upi‘kwanānk. Äjikīgitut aᵘ mī‘kinā‘k: “Īⁱ∙ⁱ∙ⁱ∙, mīsa’ iwitci kạnạbạtc kipimipạsi‘kutunäwạtināg.”

**Aw mikinaak mii nangwana go iw nishkimind. Aw mikinaak wiin igo megwaatam i’iw gii-bimi-gichigadinaag ikidowind. “Niin gaa-wii’igooyaan,” inendam a’aw mikinaak; mii dash i’iw gichigizid imaa obikwanaang. Ezhi-giigidod aw mikinaak: “Īⁱ∙ⁱ∙ⁱ∙, mii sa’ iwidi ganabaj gi-bimi-basikodoonewadinaag.”**

*Snapping-Turtle then, by reason of these words, was made angry. Snapping-Turtle disliked to hear it said that it was rough and hilly. “I am the one that is meant,” thought Snapping-Turtle; for he was rough there on his back.¹ Then spoke Snapping-Turtle, saying: “Well, it was over there perhaps on the cracked-mouth mountain.”²*

Aw mikinaak (that Snapping-Turtle) mii nangwana go iw (so it turned out by that) nishkimind (he was angered). Aw mikinaak wiin igo (Snapping-Turtle for his part) megwaatam (disliked hearing — \*flagged\*) i’iw gii-bimi-gichigadinaag ikidowind (the saying of ‘where big hills run along’). “Niin gaa-wii’igooyaan (it is I who am meant),” inendam (thought) a’aw mikinaak; mii dash i’iw (for it was that) gichigizid (he was rough — \*flagged\*) imaa obikwanaang (there on his back). Ezhi-giigidod aw mikinaak (then spoke Snapping-Turtle): “Īⁱ∙ⁱ∙ⁱ∙ (— \*cry, carried as printed\*), mii sa’ iwidi ganabaj (well, it was over there perhaps) gi-bimi-basikodoonewadinaag (where you tracked along the cracked-mouth mountain).”

*Jones’s footnotes: ¹ To select a physical characteristic and play upon it with insinuation, is common. ² Referring to the perpendicular fissure on the upper lip of the hare. — W.J. Printer’s error: mī‘kinā‘k (long ī) where the page otherwise prints mi‘kinā‘k — preserved as printed. iwitci — iwidi with tc liaison before k, carried; mägwā‘tạm and kitcigisit converted by character rules and flagged.*

(14) Mīnawā kīgito wâbōs: “Iwäti ningī∙ō∙‘kawi∙ā∙g īgi’ᵘ mōⁿsōg kī∙o∙zīgickickincigwäwạtināg.”

**Miinawaa giigido waabooz: “Iwedi ningii-okawi’aag igiw moonzoog gii-oziigishkishkiinzhigwewadinaag.”**

*Again spoke Hare, saying: “Over there I trailed the moose, where the mountain is wrinkle-eyed.”*

Miinawaa giigido waabooz (again spoke Hare): “Iwedi (over there) ningii-okawi’aag (I trailed them) igiw moonzoog (those moose) gii-oziigishkishkiinzhigwewadinaag (where the mountain is wrinkle-eyed).”

*oziigishkishkiinzhigwewadinaa = oziigi- ‘wrinkled’ + shkiinzhigw- ‘eye’ (reduplicated in print: -ckick-) + -waadinaa ‘mountain’ — insult-mountain \#3, returning Snapping-Turtle’s jab; conversion flagged with the set.*

(15) Mīdạc iᵍi’ᵘ änīcimādcāwāt, cigwa udōdi‘tānāwa kägä‘t midāsu‘kawäwaᵍ iᵍi’ᵘ mōsōᵍ. Mādcāwạg māta∙ạ∙nāwāt. Päcu’ tägwicinōwāt cigwa ucimōwaᵍ iᵍi’ᵘ mōsōᵍ, nībitäyābạnigusiwaᵍ iᵍi’ᵘ mōsōᵍ.

**Mii dash i’iw eni-izhi-maajaawaad, zhigwa ododitaanaawaa geget midaasookawewag i’iw moozoog. Maajaawag mada’anaawaad. Beshoo degwishinowaad zhigwa ozhimowag i’iw moozoog, niibideyaabanigoziwag i’iw moozoog.**

*Thereupon away they went, soon they came to where there were truly ten trails of the moose. They started off following in their trails. When close they were come, then away fled the moose, abreast in line moved the moose.*

Mii dash i’iw (thereupon) eni-izhi-maajaawaad (away they went), zhigwa ododitaanaawaa (soon they came to it) geget (truly) midaasookawewag (ten were they by their trails) i’iw moozoog (those moose). Maajaawag (off they started) mada’anaawaad (following their trail). Beshoo degwishinowaad (when they were come close) zhigwa ozhimowag (then fled) i’iw moozoog (the moose), niibideyaabanigoziwag (abreast in a line they moved) i’iw moozoog (the moose).

*iᵍi’ᵘ printed before the animate plural mōsōᵍ throughout this section — converted i’iw per the locked \#41 reading; the demonstrative-number mismatch is flagged (same pattern at \#73 §21).*

(16) Aᵘ mi‘kinā‘k ā‘pidci mämāngickạminit uwipiminicawān. Ānīc ānint ki‘tcipäcu utạcinisāwān īgi’ᵘ ininiwạg. Tayā, mīnōtc na’ kayä wīn mi‘kinā‘k piminica∙i∙gä. Cigwa mi‘tigōn pimā‘kwicinōn. Ānīc mīsa’ äci∙ā∙tā‘kucink; intawā äcipōtawät a‘kawä tawā‘kizwāt īni’ᵘ mi‘tigōn. Mī∙i∙māⁿ pitcīnạg kātawā‘kisunit äni∙i∙cāt. Mīsā‘panä äcictcigät ningutci mi‘tigōn pimā‘kwicininit. **Ningoding maajaa mikinaak, bakobiid i’iw zaaga’igan; geget niibiwa wanisaa’ i’iw giigoonya’. Geget minwendam aw akiwenzii gaye mindimooyenh minwendam. Giigido mikinaak: “Mii ya’ apane moozhag giigoonyag niwii-nooji’aag.” Mii sa apane moozhag giigoonyan onisaan. Mikinaak aapiji nishkinawaa’, mii wa i’iw wiijikiwenya’ neshkinaagod. Gegapii ningoding onanaakonaan a’aw mikinaak wii-nisind. Geget ningoding noojigiigoonyiwed akamawaa. Gaa-bi-agwaataad mikinaak mii dash i’imaa wii-niiwana’ond. Gaawesa gashki’aasii ji-nisind, mii go i’iw anaakwiid. Gakina gaa-izhi-nisaad, mii sa binewi-jiid gii-agoode.**

*Once on a time away went Snapping-Turtle, into the water of a lake he went; truly many fish he killed. Truly pleased was the old man, and the old woman was pleased. Said Snapping-Turtle: “Now, all the time do I intend to keep on killing fish.” And so continually all the while he killed fish. Snapping-Turtle was very much disliked by the others, it was by his companions that he was disliked. At last Snapping-Turtle suddenly decided to fight them for wanting to kill him. Of a truth, when once he was getting fish, they lay in wait for him. After Snapping-Turtle was come out of the water, then was the time that they intended to smite him with a club. But they did not succeed in killing him, for he also fought. After he had slain them all, accordingly then the buttocks of the ruffed grouse were hanging aloft.*

Ningoding maajaa mikinaak (once away went Snapping-Turtle), bakobiid i’iw zaaga’igan (into the water of a lake he went); geget niibiwa (truly many) wanisaa’ (he killed of them — \*flagged\*) i’iw giigoonya’ (those fish). Geget minwendam aw akiwenzii (truly pleased was the old man) gaye mindimooyenh minwendam (and the old woman was pleased). Giigido mikinaak (said Snapping-Turtle): “Mii ya’ apane (now, all the time) moozhag giigoonyag (continually fish) niwii-nooji’aag (do I intend to keep catching).” Mii sa apane (and so continually) moozhag giigoonyan onisaan (all the while he killed fish). Mikinaak aapiji nishkinawaa’ (Snapping-Turtle greatly displeased them), mii wa i’iw wiijikiwenya’ (it was his companions) neshkinaagod (by whom he was disliked). Gegapii ningoding (at last on a time) onanaakonaan (he resolved upon it) a’aw mikinaak (Snapping-Turtle) wii-nisind (— that he was to be killed). Geget ningoding (of a truth, once) noojigiigoonyiwed (while he was getting fish) akamawaa (he was lain in wait for). Gaa-bi-agwaataad mikinaak (after Snapping-Turtle had come out of the water) mii dash i’imaa (then was the time there) wii-niiwana’ond (that he was to be smitten with a club). Gaawesa gashki’aasii ji-nisind (but they could not kill him), mii go i’iw anaakwiid (for he fought back — \*flagged\*). Gakina gaa-izhi-nisaad (after he had slain them all), mii sa binewi-jiid (and so the ruffed grouse’s rump) gii-agoode (hung aloft) (*the closing that leaves the story to the listener’s wondering*).
