WÂBANŌWIWIN.

**WAABANOOWIWIN.**

THE WIZARD RITE.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Minawā bäᵉjik ījitcigäwin ningadibādodān kā∙i∙jinōndawạgwa īgi’ᵘ wâbạnōg. Pä∙e∙jik ạnicinābä ni‘tạm kā∙u∙ji‘tōt wâbạnōwiwin, mī∙i∙’ᵘ kayä wīn kā∙i∙nādcimut. “Wâbạnōmạnidō ningīmini‘k īᵍi’ᵘ tcī∙i∙jitcigäyān tciwâbạnōwiyān.” Mạmindagä wâbạnung ina‘kakä kī∙i∙jikāgīgitut, kayä miziwä a‘kīng äyāwāt īgi’ᵘ wâbanūmạnidōg, miziwä gayä gīwi‘tākīji‘k ändạsing wändānima‘k, gayä miziwä icpîmīng kījigunk. Kayä wīn anạngōᵍ ogītạcimān, äjiwäbạtogwän iwidi anạngōg äyawād; kīcisōn kayä ogītạcimān.

**Minawaa bezhig izhichigewin ninga-dibaadodaan gaa-izhi-noondawagwaa igi’w waabanoog. Bezhig anishinaabe nitam gaa-ozhitood waabanoowiwin, mii i’w gaye wiin gaa-inaajimod. “Waabanoo-manidoo ningii-minig i’iw ji-izhichigeyaan ji-waabanoowiyaan.” Memindage waabanong inakake gii-izhi-gaagiigidod, gaye miziwe akiing eyaawaad igi’w waabanoo-manidoog, miziwe gaye giiwitaa-giizhig endasing wendaanimak, gaye miziwe ishpiming giizhigong. Gaye wiin anangoog ogii-dazhimaan, ezhiwebadogwen iwidi anangoog eyaawaad; giizisoon gaye ogii-dazhimaan.**

*About a certain other practice will I now tell, according as I have heard it from the wizards. A certain man was the first to found the wizard rite, and this was what he told: “By a wizard manitou was I given power to become a wizard.” Especially of things toward the east did he discourse, and of things everywhere upon the earth where the wizard manitous were, and of things everywhere in the circle of the sky, of things everywhere from whence blew the winds, and of things everywhere in the sky above. And he also told of the stars, what takes place yonder where the stars were; of the sun too he spoke.*

Minawaa bezhig izhichigewin (about another practice/way), ninga-dibaadodaan (I will tell of it), gaa-izhi-noondawagwaa (as I heard them) igi’w waabanoog (those waabanoo people). Bezhig anishinaabe (one person) nitam gaa-ozhitood (who first made it) waabanoowiwin (the waabanoo rite), mii i’w gaye wiin gaa-inaajimod (this is what he himself told): “Waabanoo-manidoo (a waabanoo manidoo) ningii-minig (gave it to me; \*inverse\*) i’iw ji-izhichigeyaan (that I should do this) ji-waabanoowiyaan (that I should be a waabanoo).” Memindage waabanong inakake (especially toward the east) gii-izhi-gaagiigidod (he spoke at length), gaye miziwe akiing eyaawaad (and of everywhere on the earth where they are) igi’w waabanoo-manidoog (those waabanoo manidoog), miziwe gaye giiwitaa-giizhig (and everywhere around the circle of the sky) endasing wendaanimak (every place from which the wind blows), gaye miziwe ishpiming giizhigong (and everywhere above in the sky). Gaye wiin anangoog ogii-dazhimaan (also of the stars he told), ezhiwebadogwen (whatever may take place there; \*dubitative\*) iwidi anangoog eyaawaad (yonder where the stars are); giizisoon gaye ogii-dazhimaan (of the sun too he spoke).

(2) Mī∙i∙dac ī∙i∙’ᵘ kayä wīnawa kā∙i∙jitcigäwād, wâbạnūwīgiwâm kī∙u∙ji‘tōwād wâ‘tajiwâbạnōwiwād. Kīkistci∙i∙na‘kạmigiziwạg, anōdcikägō ogī∙ā∙bạdci‘tōnāwa mīdcim kīwī‘kundiwād. Mämindagä asämān ogī∙ā∙bạdci∙ā∙wān; miziwä kī∙i∙nā‘kunāwād pwāgạnạn kīwâwīnāwād īni’ᵘ wäbạnūmạnidōn. Mämindagä undinūᵍ ugīpīndā‘kōnāwān. Misiwä kayä icpimīng ayäyānit ugīwâwīndamawâwān kīcisōn, kayä dạc icpimīng mạnidōn äyānt ugīwâwīnāwān.

**Mii dash i’iw gaye wiinawaa gaa-izhichigewaad, waabanoo-wiigiwaam gii-ozhitoowaad waa-dazhi-waabanoowiwaad. Gii-gichi-inakamigiziwag, anooj gegoo ogii-aabaji’toonaawaa miijim gii-wiikondiwaad. Memindage asemaan ogii-aabaji’aawaan; miziwe gii-inaakonaawaad bwaaganan gii-waawiinaawaad ini’w waabanoo-manidoon. Memindage ondinoog ogii-biindaakoonaawaan. Misiwe gaye ishpiming ayaayaanid ogii-waawiindamawaawaan giizisoon, gaye dash ishpiming manidoon eyaanid ogii-waawiinaawaan.**

*And now this was what they also did, a wizard lodge they put up at the place where they were to perform the wizard rite. They were exceedingly active about it, of every sort of thing in the way of food they made use when they celebrated a feast together. Tobacco in particular they used; in every direction they pointed with their pipes when they spoke of the wizard manitou by name. To (the sources of) the winds in particular they made offerings with the pipe. And of every place above where the suns were they told, and of the manitou on high they spoke by name.*

Mii dash i’iw gaye wiinawaa gaa-izhichigewaad (and this is what they also did), waabanoo-wiigiwaam gii-ozhitoowaad (a waabanoo lodge they made) waa-dazhi-waabanoowiwaad (where they would perform the waabanoo rite). Gii-gichi-inakamigiziwag (they were exceedingly active), anooj gegoo ogii-aabaji’toonaawaa miijim (every sort of food they used) gii-wiikondiwaad (when they feasted together). Memindage asemaan ogii-aabaji’aawaan (especially tobacco they used; \*animate\*); miziwe gii-inaakonaawaad bwaaganan (in every direction they pointed the pipes) gii-waawiinaawaad ini’w waabanoo-manidoon (when they named the waabanoo manidoo). Memindage ondinoog (especially the winds; \*animate\*) ogii-biindaakoonaawaan (to them they made the tobacco offering). Misiwe gaye ishpiming ayaayaanid (and of everywhere above where he is) ogii-waawiindamawaawaan giizisoon (they told of the sun; \*obviative\*), gaye dash ishpiming manidoon eyaanid (and of the manidoo being on high) ogii-waawiinaawaan (they spoke of him by name).

*Printed: ogī∙ā∙bạdci‘tōnāwa (§2, no final -n) beside ugī∙ā∙bạdci‘tōnāwān (§3, with -n) — both grammatical (singular vs plural inanimate object), not printer’s errors; noted to keep the final-n file clean.*

(3) Mī∙i∙dec ī∙i∙’wa‘pī kīwunạbiwāt kīwâbạnūwiwāt. Kayä wīnawā madōdosạnạn ugī∙ā∙bạdci‘tōnāwān, anōdc kayä kägō wänicicink ạno‘kātcigạn kīmīnidīwạg. Kīwī‘kwạdci‘tōwāt wī‘pimādisiwāt kīkistcinạgạmōwạg. Nîswitäwä∙i∙gạnạn ugī∙a∙wâwān. Kī‘kistcinīmiwạg ī∙i∙’māⁿ wīgiwâming, ugīgījibāckānāwa ī∙i∙’ᵘ wīgiwām imāⁿ pīndik wīgiwâming. Ayā‘pī kī∙u∙nabiwāt kī∙ạ∙ni∙ā∙yāndinamātīwạg. Kīcpîn awiya ayā‘kuzitcin mī∙i∙’māⁿ ābi‘tawind kīnạmadabi∙ā∙wād. Ugīgījibāckawāwān pä‘kic nīmiwāt. Mīgū∙i∙’ᵘ nānīngutinō awiya pābigä kīminupîmādizit.

**Mii dash i’iw apii gii-onabiwaad gii-waabanoowiwaad. Gaye wiinawaa madoodoosanan ogii-aabaji’toonaawaan, anooj gaye gegoo wenizhishing anokaajigan gii-miinidiwag. Gii-wiikwaji’toowaad wii-bimaadiziwaad gii-gichi-nagamowag. Niswi dewe’iganan ogii-ayaawaawaan. Gii-gichi-niimiwag i’imaa wiigiwaaming, ogii-giizhibaashkaanaawaa i’iw wiigiwaam imaa biindig wiigiwaaming. Ayaapii gii-onabiwaad gii-ani-ayaandinamaadiwag. Giishpin awiya ayaakozijin mii i’imaa aabitawind gii-namadabi’aawaad. Ogii-giizhibaashkawaawaan bekish niimiwaad. Mii go i’iw naaningodinong awiya baabige gii-mino-bimaadizid.**

*Now, that was the time when they sat down to perform sorcery. And they also made use of sweat-lodges, and every sort of wearing-apparel that was pleasing they gave one to another. When conjuring for the purpose of gaining power to live long, they sang with great solemnity. Three hand-drums did they use. Earnestly did they dance there in the wigwam, they moved about in a circle there inside of the wigwam. As they took their seats, they handed (the drums) over to others. If any one was sick, then over at the middle of the lodge they seated (the person). They moved around (the person) in a circle, while at the same time they danced. Whereupon many a time did one immediately get well.*

Mii dash i’iw apii (and that was the time) gii-onabiwaad (they sat down) gii-waabanoowiwaad (they performed the waabanoo rite). Gaye wiinawaa madoodoosanan ogii-aabaji’toonaawaan (sweat-lodges too they used), anooj gaye gegoo wenizhishing anokaajigan (and every sort of fine handiwork/apparel) gii-miinidiwag (they gave to one another; \*reciprocal\*). Gii-wiikwaji’toowaad (when they strove) wii-bimaadiziwaad (to live (long)) gii-gichi-nagamowag (greatly did they sing). Niswi dewe’iganan ogii-ayaawaawaan (three drums they had; \*animate\*). Gii-gichi-niimiwag i’imaa wiigiwaaming (earnestly they danced there in the wigwam), ogii-giizhibaashkaanaawaa i’iw wiigiwaam (around the inside of the wigwam they circled) imaa biindig wiigiwaaming (there within the wigwam). Ayaapii gii-onabiwaad (as they took their seats) gii-ani-ayaandinamaadiwag (they handed (the drums) along to one another; \*reciprocal\*). Giishpin awiya ayaakozijin (if anyone was sick; \*iterative\*) mii i’imaa aabitawind (then there at the middle) gii-namadabi’aawaad (they seated that one). Ogii-giizhibaashkawaawaan (around that one they circled) bekish niimiwaad (while at the same time dancing). Mii go i’iw naaningodinong (and thus many a time) awiya baabige gii-mino-bimaadizid (someone at once became well).

(4) Nānīngutinū gayä kī‘kudcīwạg kīwâbanda∙i∙wäwād äjikacki∙ä∙wiziwād; nānīngutinū pinäsiwayānạn ugīpāckizwāwān, kāwīn dac kī‘pigōskususī ‘aᵍa’ᵘ käkä‘kwayānic. Nānīngutinō kayä macki‘ki ogīcācāgwạndānawa, mī∙i∙’dec kīzazīswamāwāt īni’ᵘ käkä‘kwayānicạn; pä‘kic pimājạgāmäwāt pīndik wīgiwāming. ‘Aᵍa’widạc pämita‘kunāt īni’ᵘ käkä‘kwayānicạn käga‘pīgu pîmādisiwạn ‘iᵍi’ᵘ pämijipạgidināt, mī∙i∙’ᵘ äjipimusät ‘aᵍa’ᵘ käkä‘k pä‘kic piminōndāguzit, “Käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ\!”

**Naaningodinong gaye gii-gojiiwag gii-waabanda’iwewaad ezhi-gashki’ewiziwaad; naaningodinong binesiwayaanan ogii-baashkizwaawaan, gaawiin dash gii-bigoshkozosii a’aw gekekwayaanish. Naaningodinong gaye mashkiki ogii-zhaazhaagwandaanaawaa, mii dash gii-zaziiswamaawaad ini’w gekekwayaanishan; bekish bimaazhagaamewaad biindig wiigiwaaming. A’aw idash bemi-dakonaad ini’w gekekwayaanishan gegapii go bimaadiziwan i’iw bemi-izhi-bagidinaad, mii i’w ezhi-bimosed a’aw gekek bekish bimi-noondaagozid, “Käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ\!”**

*And they sometimes exhibited their skill by showing what they had power to do; at times they would fire guns at bird-skins,¹ but unpenetrated would remain the old bird-hawk skin. And frequently some medicine they chewed, whereupon they spit it out in a shower upon the old bird-hawk skin; at the same time they moved round in a circle within the lodge. And the (person) passing (round) with the old bird-hawk skin in his hands would finally let it go when it showed life, whereupon off it would walk, and at the same time it would be heard making the sound, “Käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ\!”²*

Naaningodinong gaye gii-gojiiwag (and sometimes they made trial) gii-waabanda’iwewaad (showing to people) ezhi-gashki’ewiziwaad (what they had the power to do); naaningodinong binesiwayaanan ogii-baashkizwaawaan (at times they shot at bird-skins), gaawiin dash gii-bigoshkozosii (but it was not shattered/penetrated) a’aw gekekwayaanish (that old bird-hawk skin). Naaningodinong gaye mashkiki ogii-zhaazhaagwandaanaawaa (and often medicine they chewed), mii dash gii-zaziiswamaawaad (whereupon they spit it in a shower upon them) ini’w gekekwayaanishan (those old bird-hawk skins); bekish bimaazhagaamewaad (while moving along around) biindig wiigiwaaming (inside the lodge). A’aw idash bemi-dakonaad (and the one carrying it along in the hand) ini’w gekekwayaanishan (that old bird-hawk skin) gegapii go bimaadiziwan (at last it showed life; \*obviative\*) i’iw bemi-izhi-bagidinaad (whereupon he let it go), mii i’w ezhi-bimosed a’aw gekek (then off walked the bird-hawk) bekish bimi-noondaagozid (at the same time heard sounding as it went), “Käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ, käⁿ\!” (*the bird-hawk’s cry, carried as printed*).

*¹ Magic pouches. ² The cry of the bird-hawk. (Footnotes in the 1919 printing; both markers print on the English page of the 316/317 spread, the texts at the foot of the Ojibwe page.)*

(5) Kayä dạc cigu ickudä udōdā‘pinānāwa; a‘kawä kīzazīsundamowāt unindciwān, kāwīn dac tcāgizusīwag. Mägwā kistciwâbạnūwiwāt mīgu iᵘ awiya äjinōndawâwāt nōndāguzinit, “Yahōō weᵍhi, weᵍhi, weᵍhi,” inwäwạn.

**Gaye dash zhigwa ishkode odoodaapinaanaawaa; akawe gii-zaziisondamowaad oninjiiwaan, gaawiin dash jaagizosiiwag. Megwaa gichi-waabanoowiwaad mii go iw awiya ezhi-noondawaawaad noondaagozinid, “Yahōō weᵍhi, weᵍhi, weᵍhi,” inwewan.**

*And then some fire they would also take up in their hands; but before doing so, they would spit a shower (of medicine) upon their hands, but they would not be burned. While they were at the height of the wizard rite, then would they hear the sound of some one say, “Yahōō, weᵍhi, weᵍhi, weᵍhi\!” such was the sound that one made.*

Gaye dash zhigwa ishkode odoodaapinaanaawaa (and then fire they took up); akawe gii-zaziisondamowaad oninjiiwaan (first spitting a shower upon their own hands), gaawiin dash jaagizosiiwag (and they were not burned). Megwaa gichi-waabanoowiwaad (while at the height of the waabanoo rite) mii go iw awiya ezhi-noondawaawaad (then they would hear someone) noondaagozinid (making a sound), “Yahōō weᵍhi, weᵍhi, weᵍhi,” (*the heard cry, carried as printed*) inwewan (such was the sound of that one’s voice).

(6) Mạzininīᵍ kayä udōji∙ā∙wān. Nīmiwạg īgi’ᵘ mạzinimī∙i∙sag. Päᵉjig wâbạnunk ina‘ka‘kä unība∙i∙ā∙wān, mī īni’ᵘ mäskwâwigījik äjini‘kānāwāt. Minawa päᵉjik ābi‘tawind nībawiwạn, mī īni’ᵘ nạbạnägījik änāwāt. Mīnawā päᵉjik ningābī∙a∙nunk ina‘ka‘kä unībawi∙ā∙wān, mī īni’ᵘ Mänugījiwäckank änāwāt. Mīnawā paᵉjik ābi‘tawind nābawit, kīwädin udināwān. Nībiwa kayä mạzininī∙i∙saᵍ miziwä pīndik wīgiwāming udạsāwān, ka‘kina īgi’ᵘ mạzininīg nīmiwạg. Nānīngutinū kayä nōndāguziwạg. Mī∙i∙’ᵘ äjictcigäwāt. Nāningutinū kayä awiya unōndawâwān icpîmīng ina‘ka‘kä tạnwäwidamōn.

**Mazininiig gaye odoozhi’aawaan. Niimiwag igi’w mazininiinsag. Bezhig waabanong inakake oniibawi’aawaan, mii ini’w Meskwaawi-giizhig ezhinikaanaawaad. Minawaa bezhig aabitawind niibawiwan, mii ini’w Nabane-giizhig enaawaad. Minawaa bezhig ningaabii’anong inakake oniibawi’aawaan, mii ini’w Menogiizhiweshkang enaawaad. Minawaa bezhig aabitawind naabawid, Giiwedin odinaawaan. Niibiwa gaye mazininiinsag miziwe biindig wiigiwaaming odasaawaan, gakina igi’w mazininiig niimiwag. Naaningodinong gaye noondaagoziwag. Mii i’w ezhichigewaad. Naaningodinong gaye awiya onoondawaawaan ishpiming inakake danwewidamoon.**

*And some images did they make. The images danced. One over towards the east they placed in standing position, and the name of that one they called Red-Sky. Another at the middle of the lodge stood, and that one they called One-Face-of-the-Sky.³ Another over towards the west they placed in standing position, and that one they called The-Sound-of-whose-Footstep-is-heard-with-Pleasure-in-the-Sky. Another stood at the centre of the lodge, North-Wind they called it. And they put many images everywhere about inside the lodge, and all the images danced. And frequently could the sound of their voices be heard. That was the way they did. And they sometimes heard the sound of some one’s voice coming from above.*

Mazininiig gaye odoozhi’aawaan (and images they made; \*animate\*). Niimiwag igi’w mazininiinsag (the little images danced). Bezhig waabanong inakake oniibawi’aawaan (one toward the east they stood up), mii ini’w Meskwaawi-giizhig ezhinikaanaawaad (that one they named Red-Sky). Minawaa bezhig aabitawind niibawiwan (another stood at the middle; \*obviative\*), mii ini’w Nabane-giizhig enaawaad (that one they called One-Face-of-the-Sky). Minawaa bezhig ningaabii’anong inakake oniibawi’aawaan (another toward the west they stood up), mii ini’w Menogiizhiweshkang enaawaad (that one they called The-Sound-of-whose-Footstep-is-heard-with-Pleasure-in-the-Sky). Minawaa bezhig aabitawind naabawid (another standing at the middle; \*changed conjunct\*), Giiwedin odinaawaan (North-Wind they called it). Niibiwa gaye mazininiinsag (and many little images) miziwe biindig wiigiwaaming odasaawaan (everywhere inside the lodge they placed), gakina igi’w mazininiig niimiwag (all the images danced). Naaningodinong gaye noondaagoziwag (and often they could be heard). Mii i’w ezhichigewaad (that is what they did). Naaningodinong gaye awiya onoondawaawaan (and sometimes they heard someone) ishpiming inakake danwewidamoon (the sound of a voice from up above).

(7) Kayä mīnawā kā∙u∙ndci∙i∙jictcigäwāt kînwänj wī‘pimādisiwāt, kunimā gayä kägōn tcîni‘tōwāt kä∙u∙ndcipîmādisiwāt ōmāⁿ a‘kīng, kunimā gayä kistcigạmīng kägō tci∙u∙ndinạmowād kämīdciwād, kunimā gayä ā‘pidci tcini‘tāwigink anōdcikägō wiyạgiminạn, gayä tci∙a∙wäsī∙i∙‘kāg, kayä tcîminī∙i∙jiwäba‘k nībink pîbōnk kayä.

**Gaye minawaa gaa-onji-izhichigewaad ginwenh wii-bimaadiziwaad, gonimaa gaye gegoon ji-nitoowaad ge-onji-bimaadiziwaad o’omaa akiing, gonimaa gaye gichigamiing gegoo ji-ondinamowaad ge-miijiwaad, gonimaa gaye aapiji ji-nitaawiging anooj gegoo wiyagiminan, gaye ji-awesii’kaag, gaye ji-mino-izhiwebak niibing biboong gaye.**

*And another reason why they did (these things) was that they might live a long time, or else that they might kill something whereby they might live here upon earth, or that they might obtain something to eat from across the sea, or that in great abundance might grow every kind of thing, such as berries, or that game might be plentiful, and that things might go well in summer and winter.*

Gaye minawaa gaa-onji-izhichigewaad (and another reason why they did it): ginwenh wii-bimaadiziwaad (that they might live long), gonimaa gaye gegoon ji-nitoowaad (or that they might kill something) ge-onji-bimaadiziwaad o’omaa akiing (whereby they might live here on earth), gonimaa gaye gichigamiing (or from the great sea) gegoo ji-ondinamowaad ge-miijiwaad (that they obtain something to eat), gonimaa gaye aapiji ji-nitaawiging (or that there grow in great abundance) anooj gegoo wiyagiminan (every kind of berry), gaye ji-awesii’kaag (and that game be plentiful), gaye ji-mino-izhiwebak (and that things go well) niibing biboong gaye (in summer and in winter).

(8) A‘pī∙i∙dạc ickwā‘tāwāt, mī∙i∙’ᵘ ä‘kidōwāt: “Ānīc wīnisa’ kōsinān, kistcimạnidō, ka‘kina mīnu∙i∙jiwäbisiwin äyāt. Ka‘kina kägōn täbā‘kunạnk wīn ōga∙o∙na∙u∙ji‘tōn mini‘k äjictcigäyạnk, ka‘kina äjipạgusändạmank. **Apii idash ishkwaataawaad, mii i’w ekidowaad: “Aaniish wiin isa goosinaan, gichi-manidoo, gakina mino-izhiwebiziwin eyaad. *And when they were closing, this was what they said: “Now, yonder father of ours, the great manitou, possesses every kind of uprightness. *And this, furthermore, was what they also said: “Whenever any one dies, to the land of ghosts will one go.” With good words did they speak to the one that had died. “Do not ask any one (to go with you).” They pointed straight the way along which (the dead) should go, straight towards the south; some (went) to where it was never winter, that was where the ghosts went to dwell. And some were called skeletons.² It was the skeletons that sometimes told the people how it was at the place where the ghosts went. Often one heard the sound of a skeleton, always directly southward went the sound of it. Some became unconscious when they heard the sound of such a creature. It was not good for one that became unconscious to hear a skeleton. But the one that did not become unconscious on hearing the sound of a skeleton was one that would live long. Now, all these things were what the wizards told about.*
