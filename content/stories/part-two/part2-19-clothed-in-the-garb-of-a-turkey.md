WÄMISISÄ‘KUNÄ.

**WEMIZISAAKONAYE.**

CLOTHED-IN-THE-GARB-OF-A-TURKEY.

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ninguding kī∙i∙we päjïk uckinïgi‘kwe kī∙i∙dä; ucimäⁿyạn udayāwān, kwīwizänsạn ạgacī∙i∙wạn; uni‘tāwigi∙ā∙n. Kägạ‘pī∙i∙dạc nawạtc kī∙ạ∙niminditōwạn; a‘pī∙i∙dạc käcki‘tōd tcita‘kunād mī‘tigwābīnsạn, aᵘ uskinïgi‘kwe ugī∙u∙ji‘tawān mī‘tigwabīnsạn pigwakūns gayä. Mī∙i∙dạc ‘aᵉaᵘ kwīwizäns kī∙u∙dạminut ạgwadcīng. Ningudingidạc aᵘ kwīwizäns ugīnisạn pînäcīyạn, gitcigänäⁿcīyạn, mī∙i∙dạc äji∙a∙pạ‘tōd ändāwād. “Nīmîsäⁿ\! nạckä pînäcīⁿ\!”

**Ningoding giiwenh bezhig oshkiniigikwe gii-daa; oshiimenyan odayaawaan, gwiiwizensan agaashiiwan; oni’taawigi’aan. Gegapii dash nawaj gii-ani-mindidowan; apii dash gashki’tood ji-dakonaad mitigwaabiinsan, a’aw oshkiniigikwe ogii-ozhi’taawaan mitigwaabiinsan bigwakoons gaye. Mii dash a’aw gwiiwizens gii-odaminod agwajiing. Ningoding idash a’aw gwiiwizens ogii-nisaan bineshiinyan, gijigaaneshiinyan, mii dash ezhi-aba’tood endaawaad. “Nimisenh\! nashke bineshiinh\!”**

*Once on a time, they say, there was a maiden living there; a younger brother she had, a boy that was small; she was bringing him up. As time went on, he continued growing in size; and when he was able to handle a small bow and arrow, the maiden made for him a little bow and arrow. And now the boy played out of doors. Now, once the boy killed a bird, a chickadee, whereupon he ran home. “O my elder sister\! see this bird\!”*

Ningoding giiwenh (once, they say) bezhig oshkiniigikwe (a certain maiden) gii-daa (dwelt); oshiimenyan odayaawaan (a younger sibling she had), gwiiwizensan agaashiiwan (a little boy, small was he); oni’taawigi’aan (she was raising him). Gegapii dash (and by and by) nawaj gii-ani-mindidowan (more and more he grew in size); apii dash gashki’tood (and when he was able) ji-dakonaad (to handle) mitigwaabiinsan (a little bow), a’aw oshkiniigikwe ogii-ozhi’taawaan (the maiden made for him) mitigwaabiinsan bigwakoons gaye (a little bow and an arrow too). Mii dash a’aw gwiiwizens gii-odaminod agwajiing (and now the boy played out of doors). Ningoding idash (and once) a’aw gwiiwizens ogii-nisaan bineshiinyan (the boy killed a bird), gijigaaneshiinyan (a chickadee), mii dash ezhi-aba’tood endaawaad (whereupon he ran to where they dwelt). “Nimisenh\! (O my elder sister\!) nashke bineshiinh\! (look, a bird\!)”

*\*kī∙i∙dä read gii-daa “she dwelt” (the ∙i∙ a weak glide); flagged-light. The opening “Ningoding giiwenh … oshkiniigikwe” parallels the formula of the introduction Jones cross-references at §3.\**

(2) Mī∙i∙dạc uskinīgi‘kwä∙ä∙‘kidot: “Niyā\! nicīmäⁿ pînäcī∙a∙n unîsān\!” Mī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙jipa‘kunād pînäcīwayānạn ugīpāswān. Mī∙i∙dạc änād ucīmäyạn: “Mīnawā wīnicⁱ pînäcīⁿ\! Kīcpîn täpicäwād ninga∙u∙ci‘tōn kibapīsi‘kawāgạn.”

**Mii dash oshkiniigikwe ekidod: “Niyaa\! nishiimenh bineshiinyan onisaan\!” Mii dash gaa-izhi-bakonaad bineshiinh-wayaanan ogii-baaswaan. Mii dash enaad oshiimeyan: “Miinawaa wii-nizh bineshiinh\! Giishpin debishewaad ninga-ozhi’toon gibabiizikawaagan.”**

*Whereupon the maiden said: “Oh, dear me\! my little brother has killed a bird\!” Thereupon she flayed the bird of its skin, (and) she dried it. And then she said to her little brother: “Another bird do try and kill\! When there is enough of them, then I will make you a coat.”*

Mii dash oshkiniigikwe ekidod (and then the maiden said): “Niyaa\! (oh dear me\!) nishiimenh bineshiinyan onisaan\! (my little brother has killed a bird\!)” Mii dash gaa-izhi-bakonaad (and so she flayed) bineshiinh-wayaanan (the bird’s skin) ogii-baaswaan (she dried it). Mii dash enaad oshiimeyan (and then she said to her little brother): “Miinawaa wii-nizh bineshiinh\! (another bird, do kill\!) Giishpin debishewaad (when there are enough of them) ninga-ozhi’toon gibabiizikawaagan (I will make you a coat).”

*\*kā∙i∙jipa‘kunād = gaa-izhi-bakonaad “she skinned/flayed it”. wīnicⁱ (“do kill another”) read wii-nizh (desiderative + “again/two”); flagged-light. kibapīsi‘kawāgạn = gi-babiizikawaagan “your coat/garment” — the garment the title names.\**

(3) Kägä‘t idạc mīnawā ugīnisān pînäcī∙a∙n. A‘pī∙i∙dạc tayäbisä∙i∙nït pînäcīwayānäᵉ mī∙i∙we kī∙u∙ci‘tōd pạbisigawägāns aᵘ uskinïgi‘kwe, mī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙jibīsi‘kūnād ucimä∙a∙n.

**Geget idash miinawaa ogii-nisaan bineshiinyan. Apii dash debisenid bineshiinh-wayaanan mii iwe gii-ozhi’tood babiizikawaagaans a’aw oshkiniigikwe, mii dash gaa-izhi-biizikonaad oshiimeyan.**

*So truly another bird he killed. And when the number of bird-skins was enough, then a small coat the maiden made for him, whereupon she fitted it upon her little brother.*

Geget idash (so truly) miinawaa ogii-nisaan bineshiinyan (another bird he killed). Apii dash debisenid bineshiinh-wayaanan (and when the bird-skins were enough) mii iwe gii-ozhi’tood babiizikawaagaans (then a small coat she made) a’aw oshkiniigikwe (the maiden), mii dash gaa-izhi-biizikonaad oshiimeyan (whereupon she clothed her little brother in it).

*Jones’s footnote here: “The introduction up to this point is much like that in the story of the Gnome.” \*tayäbisä∙i∙nït = debisenid “(when) they were enough” (obviative). pạbisigawägāns / babiizikawaagaans “a little coat”, dim. of the garment-word at §2; flagged-light for the exact stem.\**

(4) Kägä‘pī∙i∙dạc kī∙a∙nimĩnditu ‘aᵉaᵘ uskinawä, anōdcitạc kägō ugīni‘tōn, — pînäwaᵉ, misisaⁿᵉ, awäsī∙a∙n gayä, wâwâckäciwaᵉ, ạdi‘kwạn gayä, ạnōdcigo awäsīyaᵉ. Nībiwa mīdcĩm udayānāwa, wīyās pimidä gayä. A‘pī∙i∙dạc kạni‘tā∙a∙wigit aᵘ uskinawä kīcigạdändạm īmäⁿ ayāwād. Mī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙jigạgwädcimād umîsä∙ạ∙n: “Kāwīnina nîngudci ayāsīwạg ạnicinābēg?”

**Gegapii dash gii-ani-mindidod a’aw oshkinawe, anooj idash gegoo ogii-nita’oon, — binewan, misisenan, awesiinyan gaye, waawaashkeshiwan, adikwan gaye, anooj igo awesiinyan. Niibiwa miijim odayaanaawaa, wiiyaas bimide gaye. Apii dash gani-da’aawigid a’aw oshkinawe gii-gizhigadendam imaa ayaawaad. Mii dash gaa-izhi-gagwejimaad omisenyan: “Gaawiin ina ningoji ayaasiiwag anishinaabeg?”**

*And in the fulness of time he grew to be a youth; and then all kinds of game he killed, — ruffed grouse, turkeys, and game-folk, deer, and caribou, and the various kinds of (big) game. Much food they had, meat, and grease. And when to manhood had grown the youth, he became tired of the place where they were. Accordingly he asked his elder sister: “Are there not some people anywhere?”*

Gegapii dash gii-ani-mindidod a’aw oshkinawe (and by and by the youth grew in size), anooj idash gegoo ogii-nita’oon (and all sorts of things he was able to kill), — binewan (ruffed grouse), misisenan (turkeys), awesiinyan gaye (and game-beasts), waawaashkeshiwan (deer), adikwan gaye (and caribou), anooj igo awesiinyan (all kinds of game). Niibiwa miijim odayaanaawaa (much food they had), wiiyaas bimide gaye (meat and grease). Apii dash gani-da’aawigid a’aw oshkinawe (and when the youth had grown to manhood) gii-gizhigadendam (he grew weary) imaa ayaawaad (of where they were). Mii dash gaa-izhi-gagwejimaad omisenyan (so he asked his elder sister): “Gaawiin ina ningoji ayaasiiwag anishinaabeg? (Are there not people somewhere?)”

*\*kạni‘tā∙a∙wigit = gani-da’aawigid “(when) he had grown up” ( taawigi- “grow up”); flagged-light. kīcigạdändạm = gii-gizhigadendam “he became weary/tired of it”. ugīni‘tōn read ogii-nita’oon “he was able to kill (them)”; flagged-light.\**

(5) Mī∙i∙dạc änād ucīmäyạn: “Kägä‘t ayāwạg ạnicinābēg uwîdiningābī∙ạ∙nung.”

**Mii dash enaad oshiimeyan: “Geget ayaawag anishinaabeg owidi ningaabii’anong.”**

*Whereupon she said to her little brother: “Truly, there are some people at yonder place toward the west.”*

Mii dash enaad oshiimeyan (and then she said to her younger brother): “Geget ayaawag anishinaabeg (truly there are people) owidi ningaabii’anong (over yonder toward the west).”

(6) Mī∙i∙dạc ä‘kidot uskînawä: “Tạga nîngadija.”

**Mii dash ekidod oshkinawe: “Daga ningad-izhaa.”**

*Accordingly said the youth: “I think I will go (there).”*

Mii dash ekidod oshkinawe (and then said the youth): “Daga ningad-izhaa. (well, I will go.)” \*ningad-izhaa = liaison-future “I will go,” written with hyphen per scholar precedent.\*

(7) Mīdạc ä‘kidot uskînīgi‘kwe: “Ānīc pōskägīn.”

**Mii dash ekidod oshkiniigikwe: “Aaniish, booshke giin.”**

*And then said the maiden: “Do as you like.”*

Mii dash ekidod oshkiniigikwe (and then said the maiden): “Aaniish, booshke giin. (well, it is up to you.)” \*pōskägīn read booshke giin “as you please, up to you”; flagged-light.\*

(8) Mēdạc ä‘kidot uskînawä: “Wâbạng nîngamādcā. Uci‘tōn nîma‘kizinạn gayä nîndāsạn gayä.”

**Mii dash ekidod oshkinawe: “Waabang ninga-maajaa. Ozhi’toon nimakizinan gaye nindaasan gaye.”**

*At which said the youth: “To-morrow will I go. Make for me some moccasins and stockings.”*

Mii dash ekidod oshkinawe (and then said the youth): “Waabang ninga-maajaa (tomorrow I will set out). Ozhi’toon nimakizinan gaye (make my moccasins) nindaasan gaye (and my leggings/stockings).”

(9) Mēdạc kägä‘t kī∙u∙ji‘tawād käbīsî‘kạminit. Kīgijäbitạc kāwīsînît uskinawä, ugī‘kạnonigōn umisäyạn: “Kwaya‘k nīngābī∙ạ∙nung ijān. Kō‘komis kīgāudisā unāgucig.”

**Mii dash geget gii-ozhi’taawaad ge-biizikaminid. Gigizheb idash gaa-wiisinid oshkinawe, ogii-ganoonigoon omisenyan: “Gwayak ningaabii’anong izhaan. Gookomis giga-odisaa onaagoshig.”**

*Accordingly, in truth, she made for him what he was to wear. And in the morning, after the youth had eaten, he was addressed by his elder sister saying: “Straight towards the west shall you go. To your grandmother will you come in the evening.”*

Mii dash geget gii-ozhi’taawaad ge-biizikaminid (and so truly she made for him what he would wear). Gigizheb idash gaa-wiisinid oshkinawe (and in the morning, the youth having eaten), ogii-ganoonigoon omisenyan (he was addressed by his elder sister): “Gwayak ningaabii’anong izhaan (straight toward the west go). Gookomis giga-odisaa onaagoshig (your grandmother you will reach by evening).”

*\*kāwīsînît here = gaa-wiisinid “the one who had eaten” (Jones: “after the youth had eaten”); not the negative “without eating”. giga-odisaa = liaison-future “you will reach (her)”.\**

(10) Mī∙i∙dạc kīmādcād uskînawä, kạbägījîg gī‘pîmōsä; wänāgucigidạc ugīwâbạndān wīgiwâmäns. Mī∙i∙dạc ta‘pābît skwāndäng owâbạmān mîndimoyä∙ạ∙n. Kāwīngạnạgä pīnābîsīwạn. “Nōjis pîndigän\!” udigūn. Mīdạc gī‘pîndigät. “Nāmạdạbîn, nōjis\!” udigūn. Mīdạc gīnāmạdạbît ugī∙a∙camigūn īdạc u‘kumisạn pā‘tāmīnạn pîmidä gayä tạgunigādäni ma‘kudpîmidä. Mī∙i∙dec kīwīsinît. Nibî gayä ugīmīnigūn kämini‘kwät.

**Mii dash gii-maajaad oshkinawe, gabe-giizhig gii-bimose; wenaagoshig idash ogii-waabandaan wiigiwaamens. Mii dash daa-baabid ishkwaandeng owaabamaan mindimooyenyan. Gaawiin ganage biinaabiisiiwan. “Noozhis, biindigen\!” odigoon. Mii dash gii-biindiged. “Namadabin, noozhis\!” odigoon. Mii dash gii-namadabid ogii-ashamigoon idash ookomisan baate-miinan bimide gaye dagonigaadeni makade-bimide. Mii dash gii-wiisinid. Nibi gaye ogii-miinigoon ge-minikwed.**

*Thereupon away started the youth, all day long he walked; and in the evening he saw a small wigwam. And so, when he peeped in at the doorway, he beheld an old woman. She did not even look up. “My grandson, come in\!” he was told. Whereupon he went in. “Be seated, my grandson\!” he was told. And so, when he sat down, he was then fed by his grandmother on dried blueberries and grease mixed with bear-grease. Thereupon he ate. And water too he was given to drink.*

Mii dash gii-maajaad oshkinawe (and so the youth set out), gabe-giizhig gii-bimose (all day he walked); wenaagoshig idash ogii-waabandaan wiigiwaamens (and toward evening he saw a little wigwam). Mii dash daa-baabid ishkwaandeng (and when he peeped in at the doorway) owaabamaan mindimooyenyan (he beheld an old woman). Gaawiin ganage biinaabiisiiwan (she did not even look up). “Noozhis, biindigen\! (my grandson, come in\!)” odigoon (he was told). Mii dash gii-biindiged (whereupon he entered). “Namadabin, noozhis\! (sit down, my grandson\!)” odigoon. Mii dash gii-namadabid (and so when he sat) ogii-ashamigoon idash ookomisan (he was fed by his grandmother) baate-miinan bimide gaye (dried blueberries and grease) dagonigaadeni makade-bimide (mixed with bear-grease). Mii dash gii-wiisinid (and then he ate). Nibi gaye ogii-miinigoon ge-minikwed (and water too he was given to drink).

*\*ta‘pābît = daa-baabid “when he peeped in”; flagged-light. kämini‘kwät = ge-minikwed “(that) he would drink”. makade-bimide “bear-grease/black-grease”; dagonigaade- “it is mixed in”.\**

(11) Kā∙i∙ckwâbîsînît dạc ugī‘kạnōnigōn ō‘komisạn: “Wâbạng mīnawā unāgucîg kîga∙u∙disā kīmicōmîs. Mī∙i∙dạc ‘aᵉaᵘ wäwäni käwîndạmō‘k äjiwäba‘k äjāyạn.”

**Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid dash ogii-ganoonigoon ookomisan: “Waabang miinawaa onaagoshig giga-odisaa gimishoomis. Mii dash a’aw weweni ge-wiindamook ezhiwebak ezhaayan.”**

*And after he had eaten, he was addressed by his grandmother saying: “To-morrow again, in the evening, you will come to where your grandfather is. And so rightly will he relate to you how things are where you are going.”*

Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid dash (and after he had eaten) ogii-ganoonigoon ookomisan (he was addressed by his grandmother): “Waabang miinawaa onaagoshig (tomorrow again, in the evening) giga-odisaa gimishoomis (you will reach your grandfather). Mii dash a’aw weweni ge-wiindamook (and he will rightly tell you) ezhiwebak ezhaayan (how things are where you are going).”

(12) Wayābạndigạc mīnawā ugi∙a∙camigūn ō‘komisạn mạnōmin pîmitä‘kātäni.

**Waabandig idash miinawaa ogii-ashamigoon ookomisan manoomin bimide-gaadeni.**

*So on the morrow again was he fed by his grandmother on rice prepared with grease.*

Waabandig idash (and the next morning) miinawaa ogii-ashamigoon ookomisan (again she fed him, the grandmother) manoomin bimide-gaadeni (wild rice prepared with grease). \*pîmitä‘kātäni = bimide-gaadeni “prepared/cooked with grease” (obviative); flagged-light.\*

(13) Kā∙i∙ckwāwīsinît dạc uskinawä kīmādcä; kạbägījîk kī‘pimōsä; wänāgucididạc mīnawā owâbạmān wīgiwâmäns. Mīdạc mīnawā äjita‘pābît skwāndäng, owâbạmān a‘kiwäⁿzī∙a∙n nạmạtạbinît. Kāwīngạnạgä pīnābîsīwạn. “Nōjis, pîndigän\!” udigōn. “Nạmạdạbin nōjis\!”

**Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid dash oshkinawe gii-maajaa; gabe-giizhik gii-bimose; wenaagoshig idash miinawaa owaabamaan wiigiwaamens. Mii dash miinawaa ezhi-daa-baabid ishkwaandeng, owaabamaan akiwenziiyan namadabinid. Gaawiin ganage biinaabiisiiwan. “Noozhis, biindigen\!” odigoon. “Namadabin, noozhis\!”**

*So, after the youth had finished eating, he started away; all day long he walked along; and in the evening he saw another small wigwam. And so, when again he peeped in at the doorway, he beheld an old man sitting down. Not at all did he look up. “My grandson, come in\!” he was told. “Be seated, my grandson\!”*

Gaa-ishkwaa-wiisinid dash oshkinawe gii-maajaa (and after the youth had eaten he set out); gabe-giizhik gii-bimose (all day he walked); wenaagoshig idash miinawaa owaabamaan wiigiwaamens (and toward evening again he saw a little wigwam). Mii dash miinawaa ezhi-daa-baabid ishkwaandeng (and so again, when he peeped in at the doorway), owaabamaan akiwenziiyan namadabinid (he beheld an old man sitting). Gaawiin ganage biinaabiisiiwan (not at all did he look up). “Noozhis, biindigen\! (my grandson, come in\!)” odigoon. “Namadabin, noozhis\! (sit down, my grandson\!)”

(14) A‘ki‘konsạnidạc owâbạmān ạbînît tcīgạskudä. Mī∙i∙dạc kā∙i∙jiminigut umîcōmisạn īni‘ᵘ a‘ki‘kōnsạn. “Nōjis, wīsinîn.” A‘pī∙i∙dạc wayābạndạng tcībā‘kwän mạndāmīnaᵉ, mī∙i∙dạc änāndạng aᵘ uskinawä: “Kāwīn nindātäpiwîsînisi.” A‘pī∙i∙dạc wāsinit migwa‘pạnä ägōskinänît a‘ki‘kōnsạn pī∙i∙nîc kītāwīsinît.

**Akikoonsan idash owaabamaan abinid jiigaskode. Mii dash gaa-izhi-miinigod omishoomisan iniw akikoonsan. “Noozhis, wiisinin.” Apii dash waabandang jiibaakwen mandaaminan, mii dash enendang a’aw oshkinawe: “Gaawiin nindaa-debiwisinisii.” Apii dash waasinid megwaa go apane egooshkinenid akikoonsan biinish gii-debwiisinid.**

*And a tiny kettle he saw placed beside the fire. Thereupon by his grandfather was he given the little kettle. “My grandson, eat.” And when he saw the cooked food was corn, thereupon thought the youth: “I shall not have enough to eat.” And when he began eating, there was always the same amount left in the little kettle, (which continued to replenish itself) until he had eaten enough.*

Akikoonsan idash owaabamaan abinid jiigaskode (and a little kettle he saw set beside the fire). Mii dash gaa-izhi-miinigod omishoomisan iniw akikoonsan (and then he was given by his grandfather that little kettle). “Noozhis, wiisinin. (my grandson, eat.)” Apii dash waabandang jiibaakwen mandaaminan (and when he saw the cooked food was corn), mii dash enendang a’aw oshkinawe (the youth thought): “Gaawiin nindaa-debiwisinisii. (I shall not eat enough.)” Apii dash waasinid (and when he ate), megwaa go apane egooshkinenid akikoonsan (always still full was the little kettle) biinish gii-debwiisinid (until he had eaten his fill).

*\*nindātäpiwîsînisi = nindaa-debiwiisinisii “I shall not eat to satiety”; ägōskinänît = e-gooshkinenid “(it) staying full” (the self-replenishing kettle); both flagged-light. mandaamin = corn; jiibaakwe- = to cook.\**

(15) Mēdạc ägut omîcōmisạn: “Ayāmgwânisîn, nōjis, sạnạgạt äjayạn. Wâbạng nāwa‘kwäg kigāwudi‘tān micawạckutayäg; ạpạnägu gwaya‘k äjayạn inābin. Päjïk īmäⁿ ayä mạdcänicinäbä, ä‘pitcimạnādisī. Kīgasāsä‘kwänîk, ‘A‘kawä∙i∙bi∙i∙cîn,’ gîgạtîg. Kägu gạnạgä pîzîndawā‘kän. Gī‘kạnōpinạnîg, ạnōj ta∙i∙gito tciwiwayäjîmî‘k. Kägu gạnạgä täbwä‘tawā‘kän. Mīyä‘tạgu a‘panä tciwâwâbạmạd, kägu mīnawā ābạnābi‘kän.” Mī∙i∙‘ᵘ gā∙i∙gud omîcōmîsạn. Mī∙i∙dạc kīgijäb mīnawā ogī∙a∙camigōn pîmîdä wiyās gayä.

(33) Mī∙i∙dạc mägwā uskinawäwît, pạpigwạnäns udayānābạn ōmäⁿ ugudāgạning. Mī∙i∙dạc kīwigwạtcidōt; mī∙i∙dec äjipōdādạng, “to-no-no-no\!” nībiwa misisäg pījāwạg. Nī’biwa ogīpō‘kuwgäbinān. Ā, mīdạc mạdci‘ki‘kwäwîsạg äji∙u∙ci∙ā∙wād\!

Ningoding idash a’aw mindimooyenh wedaanisid ikido (now once the old woman who had daughters said): “Ningad-izhaa nindaanisag (I will go to my daughters), ningatawi-binaakwe’waag (I will go comb their hair).” Jibwaa-odisaad idash odaanisan (and before she reached her daughters) niibiwa miigwanan owaabamaan (many feathers she saw). *Now, another time out of doors went the man, whereupon once more he blew upon his little flute. Thereupon truly many turkeys he slew. And so, when they carried the turkeys home, very big were their packs. So, many people they fed upon what their son-in-law had killed.*
