MŌWIS TAYĀBAWĀ.

**MOOWIS DAYAABAWAA.**

DUNG-WARM WEATHER.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ạnicinābäg ōdänawi∙i∙‘tiwag; nībiwa änicinābäg ayāwag; zībink tāwag. Päcigidạc a‘kiwäⁿzī ugimāwi, ininiwạn udayāwān, ki‘tci∙u∙ckinawäwiwạn; kayä dạc udānisạn mī gäyābi uckīnīgi‘kwäwinit. Kägä‘t nībiwa ānuwiwītigämā awi‘kwä. Mīsa wạnawi∙ā∙wāt igi‘ᵘ ininiwạg ānuwiwītigämāwāt, käga‘pigu nickādisiwag igi‘ᵘ ininiwạg.

**Anishinaabeg odoodenawi’idiwag; niibiwa anishinaabeg ayaawag; ziibiing daawag. Bezhig idash akiwenzii ogimaawi, ininiwan odayaawaan, gichi-oshkinawewiwan; gaye dash odaanisan mii geyaabi oshkiniigikwewinid. Geget niibiwa aano-wii-wiidigemaa a’aw ikwe. Mii sa wenawi-ayaawaad igiw ininiwag aano-wii-wiidigemaawaad, gegapii go nishkaadiziwag igiw ininiwag.**

*Some people were living together in a town; many people they were; by a river they dwelt. And a certain man was chief, and he had a son, a full-grown youth; and also a daughter who was yet a maiden. In truth, many sought to marry the (young) woman, but to no purpose. Thereupon the men who had tried in vain to marry her agreed upon a plan concerning her, for at last angry became the men.*

Anishinaabeg odoodenawi’idiwag (people were living together in a town); niibiwa anishinaabeg ayaawag (many people there were); ziibiing daawag (by a river they dwelt). Bezhig idash akiwenzii ogimaawi (and a certain old-man/elder was chief), ininiwan odayaawaan (he had a son), gichi-oshkinawewiwan (who was a full-grown youth); gaye dash odaanisan (and also his daughter) mii geyaabi oshkiniigikwewinid (who was still a maiden). Geget niibiwa aano-wii-wiidigemaa a’aw ikwe (truly many tried in vain to marry that woman). Mii sa wenawi-ayaawaad igiw ininiwag (and so the men who had failed) aano-wii-wiidigemaawaad (in trying to marry her), gegapii go nishkaadiziwag igiw ininiwag (at last the men became angry).

(2) Ānic pa‘kān wīgiwāmäns ạbi awi‘kwä. Mīdạc iᵉi‘ᵘ äcipīndigawāt ‘aᵉa‘ᵘ wäcīmäyit awinini. “Ạmbäsanō, täbwä‘tawicin, nicimä\! Kạnagä gīn wītigän.” Ajikanōnigut: “Aⁱt, kāwäsā, nisayäⁿ\! Ānawi kīsāgi∙i∙n, awacimä niminwändān tciwītigäsiwān.”

**Aaniish bakaan wiigiwaamens abi a’aw ikwe. Mii dash i’iw ezhi-biindigawaad a’aw weshiimeyid awinini. “Ambesanoo, debwetawishin, nishiime\! Ganage giin wiidigen.” Ezhi-ganoonigod: “Ayid, gaawesaa, nisayenh\! Aanawi gii-zaagi’in, awashime niminwendaan ji-wiidigesiwaan.”**

*Now, apart in a small wigwam was the woman. Accordingly then into where she was went the man that was elder brother to her. “Pray, give heed to what I say, my little sister\! Do go and be married.” Then he was addressed by her saying: “Why, impossible, my elder brother\! In spite of my love for you, I would much rather not marry.”*

Aaniish bakaan wiigiwaamens abi a’aw ikwe (now, in a separate little wigwam the woman dwelt). Mii dash i’iw ezhi-biindigawaad a’aw weshiimeyid awinini (and so the man who was elder-brother to her went in to her — \*the brother term flagged\*). “Ambesanoo, debwetawishin, nishiime\! (“pray, heed me, my little sister\!) Ganage giin wiidigen.” (“do go and marry.”) Ezhi-ganoonigod (then she addressed him): “Ayid, gaawesaa, nisayenh\! (“why, impossible, my elder brother\!) Aanawi gii-zaagi’in (in spite of my love for you), awashime niminwendaan ji-wiidigesiwaan.” (I would rather not marry.”)

*wäcīmäyit — “the one who is younger-sibling to her” / her elder brother is the speaker; the kin-term direction is flagged (Jones renders the speaker as her elder brother; the verb wäcīmäyit reads as a -shiim- ‘younger sibling’ stem). nisayäⁿ — ‘my elder brother,’ printed with final superscript ⁿ (nisayenh).*

(3) “Ānic, kinicki∙ạ∙g igi‘ᵘ ininiwạg.” Mīsa iᵉi‘ᵘ äci∙ā∙‘pitcibwāmāt. Cigwa dạc unā‘kunigäwag igi‘ᵘ cāngänimiguwāt ini‘ᵘ i‘kwäwạn. “Ạmbäsanō, wīmänici∙ā∙dā\! Ningutci kigawāni‘kämin, mīdạc imāⁿ käi∙cimīsīyank.”

**“Aaniish, ginishki’aag igiw ininiwag.” Mii sa i’iw ezhi-aapiji-bwaamaad. Zhigwa dash onaakonigewag igiw zhaangenimigowaad iniw ikwewan. “Ambesanoo, wii-manizhi-ayaadaa\! Ningoji giga-waanikemin, mii dash imaa ge-izhi-miisiiyang.”**

*“Well, you are angering the men.” But it was so that he failed to persuade her (to marry). And then to an agreement came those by whom the woman was disliked. “Come, let us shame her\! In a certain place we will dig a hole, and then into that place we will ease ourselves.”*

“Aaniish, ginishki’aag igiw ininiwag.” (“well, you are angering the men.”) Mii sa i’iw ezhi-aapiji-bwaamaad (but it was so that he quite failed to persuade her — \*stem flagged\*). Zhigwa dash onaakonigewag igiw zhaangenimigowaad iniw ikwewan (and then those by whom the woman was disliked made a plan — \*zhaang-enim- ‘disdain/dislike’ flagged\*). “Ambesanoo, wii-manizhi-ayaadaa\! (“come, let us shame her\! — \*shame-verb flagged\*) Ningoji giga-waanikemin (in a certain place we will dig a hole), mii dash imaa ge-izhi-miisiiyang.” (and then there we will ease ourselves — \*miisii- ‘defecate’\*.)

*äci∙ā∙‘pitcibwāmāt — ‘he quite failed to persuade her,’ the bwaa-/bwaam- ‘fail to / be unable to do to’ stem, parse flagged. cāngänimiguwāt — ‘by whom she was disdained/disliked,’ zhaang-enim- flagged. käi∙cimīsīyank — ‘where we will ease ourselves,’ miisii- defecate.*

(4) Kägä‘t mī∙i∙mā äcimisiwāt. Kāmōckinänik mīdạc iᵉi‘ᵘ mādci‘tāwāt mạzinitcīskiwạginạmowāt, ạnicinābänk udici‘tōnāwa iᵉi‘ᵘ mōwⁱ. Kā‘kīci‘tōwan, zazägā papagiwayān äcipisi‘kōtōwād; käyä ā‘pitci wänicicininig midasan äcipisi‘kōtōwāt, kayä ma‘kisinạn unicicininiwạn; äjipisi‘kōtōwāt kayä iᵉi‘ᵘ upīsi‘kwāgạn wänicicininik; äcipisi‘kōtōwāt kayä iᵉi‘ᵘ wīwa‘kwān; mīdạc aᵉa‘ᵘ ma‘kadā∙ą∙ mi‘kwayānan äcinīgucwāwāt, mīdạc iᵉi‘ᵘ kạckạckitāsäbināwāt, miziwä ägwa‘pitcigāsōwan; kā‘kīci∙i∙nt äcipasigunctisa∙ā∙wāt. Päcig aᵉawinini kāgīgitu: “Ạmbäsa tawi∙u∙ndci∙ạ∙nicinābäwi.” Ānīc ānind äjizītunāwāt, mīdạc iᵉi‘ᵘ äcigīgitut ‘aᵉa‘ᵘ inini: “Mōwis\! kibimātisina?”

**Geget mii imaa ezhi-miisiwaad. Gaa-mooshkinenig mii dash i’iw maajitaawaad mazinichiskiwaginamowaad, anishinaabenk odizhitoonaawaa i’iw moowi. Gaa-giizhitoowan, zazege babagiwayaan ezhi-bizikodoowaad; gaye aapiji wenizhishinig midaasan ezhi-bizikodoowaad, gaye makizinan wenizhishininiwan; ezhi-bizikodoowaad gaye i’iw obiizikwaagan wenizhishininig; ezhi-bizikodoowaad gaye i’iw wiiwakwaan; mii dash a’aw makade-aa-amikwayaanan ezhi-niigozhwaawaad, mii dash i’iw gashkashkidaasebinaawaad, miziwe agwaabichigaasowan; gaa-giizhi’ind ezhi-bazigwanjitiza’aawaad. Bezhig a’aw awinini gaagiigido: “Ambe sa daa-wonji-anishinaabewi.” Aaniish anind ezhi-zhiidoonaawaad, mii dash i’iw ezhi-gaagiigidod a’aw inini: “Moowis\! gibimaadis ina?”**

*Truly then into that place they eased themselves. After the place was filled, they then set to work patting it into the form of an image, into the form of a human being they fashioned the dung. When they had finished it, then a fine shirt they put on it; and exceedingly handsome leggings they fitted it into, likewise moccasins that were nice; and then they clothed it with a coat that was handsome; and then they fixed upon it a hat; and then the skin of a black beaver they cut into strips, whereupon they tied them about the leggings for garters; all over was it tied (with the strips). When (the clothing of) it was finished, they then stood it upon its feet. A certain man spoke at length, saying: “(I) will that (this thing) become a human being.” Now, while some kept it from falling, then accordingly spoke a man, saying: “O Dung-Being\! are you alive?”*

Geget mii imaa ezhi-miisiwaad (truly there they eased themselves). Gaa-mooshkinenig (after the place was filled) mii dash i’iw maajitaawaad mazinichiskiwaginamowaad (they set to work patting it into an image — \*long compound stem flagged\*), anishinaabenk odizhitoonaawaa i’iw moowi (into a human-being form they fashioned the dung). Gaa-giizhitoowan (when they had finished it), zazege babagiwayaan ezhi-bizikodoowaad (a fine shirt they put on it); gaye aapiji wenizhishinig midaasan ezhi-bizikodoowaad (and very handsome leggings they put on it), gaye makizinan wenizhishininiwan (and moccasins that were nice); ezhi-bizikodoowaad gaye i’iw obiizikwaagan wenizhishininig (and a handsome coat they clothed it with); ezhi-bizikodoowaad gaye i’iw wiiwakwaan (and a hat they put on it); mii dash a’aw makade-amikwayaanan ezhi-niigozhwaawaad (and the skin of a black beaver they cut into strips), mii dash i’iw gashkashkidaasebinaawaad (which they tied about the leggings for garters — \*stem flagged\*), miziwe agwaabichigaasowan (all over it was tied). Gaa-giizhi’ind (when it was finished) ezhi-bazigwanjitiza’aawaad (they stood it on its feet — \*stem flagged\*). Bezhig a’aw awinini gaagiigido (a certain man spoke at length): “Ambe sa daa-wonji-anishinaabewi.” (“come now, let this become a human being.”) Aaniish anind ezhi-zhiidoonaawaad (now while some held it up), mii dash i’iw ezhi-gaagiigidod a’aw inini (then a man spoke): “Moowis\! gibimaadis ina?” (“O Dung-Being\! are you alive?”)

*Page foot (p.414): ¹ “Because she had refused them.” — Jones’s note, attaching to “Moowis\! gibimaadis ina?” (the men address the dung-image because the woman had refused them). mōwⁱ / mōⁱ — the noun ‘moo’ (dung) prints with a final superscript (wi / i) throughout this story; read moo (moowi when possessed/obviative). mạzinitcīskiwạginạmowāt, kạckạckitāsäbināwāt, äcipasigunctisa∙ā∙wāt — three long fused verbs (patting-into-an-image / garter-tying / standing-it-up), parses flagged. The proper name Mōwis = Moowis ‘Dung-Being’ (moo ‘dung’ + personifying).*

(6) “Wunāgucig ki∙a∙nimatābi.”

**“Onaagoshig giga-ani-madaabii.”**

*“When evening comes, then are you to go down toward the water.”*

“Onaagoshig (“when evening comes) giga-ani-madaabii.” (you are to go down toward the water.”)

(7) “Wägunäc käsa‘ka∙u∙‘yān?” i‘kito aᵉa‘ᵘ mōwis.

**“Wegonesh ge-zaka’oyaan?” ikido a’aw moowis.**

*“What shall I use for a cane?” said Dung-Being.*

“Wegonesh ge-zaka’oyaan?” (“what shall I use for a cane?”) ikido a’aw moowis (said Dung-Being).

(8) “Kägä‘t kigawaci‘tōmin iᵉi‘ᵘ käsa‘ka∙u∙t.” Kägä‘t mādci‘tāwag, ā‘pidcisa wäwäni uzazägā‘tōnāwa iᵉi‘ᵘ sa‘ka‘u∙n. Mīsa iᵉi‘ᵘ kā‘kīci‘tōwāt, ạmbä, ā‘pidci minwändam aᵉa‘ᵘ mōwis; picigänimu. Mādcāwag äjāwāt ä‘kupīgạtinig mī∙i∙ⁿ sībi. Mīdạc imāⁿ ānint ininiwạg kanawänimāwāt, kayä änint acawa∙ō∙‘wag ändāwāt icāwāt. Cigwasa ani∙u∙nāgucinini, mīsa iᵉi‘ᵘ cigwa mādcād aᵉa‘ᵘ mōwis, ä‘kubīgạtinig kā∙i∙cāt. Madwäpīpāgi päciginini: “Ä‘eᵉe, piwidä kidōdisigunān\!”

**“Geget giga-wazhitoomin i’iw ge-zaka’ot.” Geget maajitaawag, aapiji sa weweni ozazegaatoonaawaa i’iw zaka’on. Mii sa i’iw gaa-giizhitoowaad, ambe, aapiji minwendam a’aw moowis; bizhigenimo. Maajaawag ezhaawaad ekobiigadinig mii iw ziibi. Mii dash imaa anind ininiwag ganawenimaawaad, gaye anind aazhawa’oowag endaawaad izhaawaad. Zhigwa sa ani-onaagoshinini, mii sa i’iw zhigwa maajaad a’aw moowis, ekobiigadinig gaa-izhaad. Madwe-biibaagi bezhig inini: “Aa-ee-e, biwide gidoodisigonaan\!”**

*“To be sure, we will make what he shall use for a cane.” Truly set they to work, in an exceedingly beautiful form they fashioned the cane for him. Accordingly, when they had finished it, ah, very pleased was Dung-Being; he felt proud. They started forth, going as far as the water of the river. And it was there that some men were watching for him, and some were crossing over in their canoes on their way home. In a while the evening was drawing on, and that was when Dung-Being started forth, to the edge of the water was where he went. The voice of a man was heard calling out: “Halloo\! by a stranger are we visited\!”*

“Geget giga-wazhitoomin i’iw ge-zaka’ot.” (“to be sure, we will make what he shall use for a cane.”) Geget maajitaawag (truly they set to work), aapiji sa weweni ozazegaatoonaawaa i’iw zaka’on (very beautifully they fashioned the cane for him). Mii sa i’iw gaa-giizhitoowaad (when they had finished it), ambe, aapiji minwendam a’aw moowis (ah, very pleased was Dung-Being); bizhigenimo (he felt proud). Maajaawag ezhaawaad ekobiigadinig mii iw ziibi (they started off, going to the river’s edge). Mii dash imaa anind ininiwag ganawenimaawaad (and there some men were watching for him), gaye anind aazhawa’oowag endaawaad izhaawaad (and some were crossing over to their homes). Zhigwa sa ani-onaagoshinini (in a while evening was drawing on), mii sa i’iw zhigwa maajaad a’aw moowis (and that was when Dung-Being set out), ekobiigadinig gaa-izhaad (to the water’s edge he went). Madwe-biibaagi bezhig inini (a man’s voice was heard calling): “Aa-ee-e, biwide gidoodisigonaan\!” (“Halloo\! by a stranger are we visited\!”)

(9) Ka‘kina sāga∙a∙‘mōg, kayä wīn aᵉawi‘kwä cāngänimāt iᵉi‘ᵘ niniwạs. Ā‘pitci päcu‘ kī∙a∙‘pa‘tō kinībawiwạn ininiwạn, ini‘ᵘ mōwisạn. Kägä‘t omisawīnạmān. “Ạmbägic aᵉa‘ᵘ wītigämạg\!” inändạm.

**Gakina zaaga’amoog, gaye wiin a’aw ikwe zhaangenimaad iniw ininiwan. Aapiji besho gii-aba’too ge-niibawiwan ininiwan, iniw moowisan. Geget omisawiinamaan. “Ambegizh a’aw wiidigemag\!” inendam.**

*All came out of doors, likewise the woman who disliked the men. Very close did she run to where the man was standing, to him (that was) Dung-Being. In truth, she felt desire for him. “Would that I might marry him\!” she thought.*

Gakina zaaga’amoog (all came out of doors), gaye wiin a’aw ikwe zhaangenimaad iniw ininiwan (likewise the woman who disliked the men). Aapiji besho gii-aba’too ge-niibawiwan ininiwan, iniw moowisan (very close she ran to where the man stood, to Dung-Being). Geget omisawiinamaan (truly she felt desire for him). “Ambegizh a’aw wiidigemag\!” (“would that I might marry him\!”) inendam (she thought).

(14) Kägä‘t a‘kiwäⁿzī äjimādcāt nātawāt īni‘ᵘ mōwisạn. Cigwa änitcä‘kisät, kīgitō aᵘ mōwis: “Nawạtc niminäwätciguntcitōn iᵉi‘ᵘ kitcīmān.” Tcigwa äcipōsikwāckwaninit. “Ä‘nᵉyōⁿ, ä‘nᵉyōⁿ\!” inwäwạn äci∙a∙cawa∙ō∙‘nāt. **Geget akiwenzii ezhi-maajaad naadawaad iniw moowisan. Zhigwa eni-jiigised, giigido a’aw moowis: “Nawaj nimine-wejigonjitoon i’iw gichi-jiimaan.” Zhigwa ezhi-boozi-gwaashkwaninid. “Ä‘nᵉyōⁿ, ä‘nᵉyōⁿ\!” inwewan ezhi-aazhawa’oonaad. Bichiinag igo zezhik enayaawaad ezhi-gwaashkwaninid. Mii sa geyaabi enwenid: “Äⁿyōⁿ\!” Mii sa ezhi-gabaad akiwenzii.**

*Truly, then the old man started forth to fetch Dung-Being. When his canoe glided up into the shore, up spoke Dung-Being, saying: “Just a little farther out do you push your canoe.” Presently then into the canoe he leaped. “Heyo, heyo\!” he exclaimed. Then (the chief) took him across the water. As soon as they drew up close to the shore, then (Dung-Being) leaped out (upon the land). And still was he exclaiming: “Heyo\!” And then ashore stepped the old man.*

Geget akiwenzii ezhi-maajaad naadawaad iniw moowisan (truly the old man set out to fetch Dung-Being). Zhigwa eni-jiigised (when his canoe glided up to shore), giigido a’aw moowis (up spoke Dung-Being): “Nawaj nimine-wejigonjitoon i’iw gichi-jiimaan.” (“just push your canoe a little farther out” — \*stem flagged\*).
