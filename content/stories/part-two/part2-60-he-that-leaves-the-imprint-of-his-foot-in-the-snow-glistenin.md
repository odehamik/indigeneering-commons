WÂSĀGUNÄCKANK UTATIBĀTCIMUWIN.

**WAASAAGONESHKANG ODADIBAAJIMOWIN.**

THE NARRATIVE OF HE-THAT-LEAVES-THE-IMPRINT-OF-HIS-FOOT-IN-THE-SNOW-GLISTENING.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ānint i‘kitō ạnicinābä — kä‘tä ạnicinābä, kīcitäbwäyäntạm — mīdac ‘i‘i‘ᵘ ōguwäti tibickō‘kamig kā∙i∙nā‘kunigäwāt ki‘tcimanitōg. A‘pisa‘ kā‘kici‘tōt Nänabucu a‘ki‘ mī cigwa kītibä‘kunigäwāt īgi‘ᵘ ki‘tcimanitōg. “Ạmbäsa, uci∙ā∙tānig īgi‘ᵘ kä∙ạ∙nicinābäwiwāt,” i‘kitōwaᵍ ‘i‘i‘ᵘ manitōᵍ.

**Aaniind ikido anishinaabe — gete-anishinaabe, giizhi-debweyendam — mii dash i’iw ogowedi dibishkoo-akamig gaa-izhi-naakonigewaad gichi-manidoog. Apii sa gaa-giizhitood Nenabozho aki, mii zhigwa gii-dibaakonigewaad igiw gichi-manidoog. “Ambe sa, ozhi-aataanig igiw ge-anishinaabewiwaad,” ikidowag i’iw manidoog.**

*Some people say that is, the old-time people, (and they) believed in it that it was these great manitous, that dwell directly opposite from us on the other side of the world, who decreed what should be. After Nänabushu had created the earth, then it was that the great manitous decreed what should be. “Behold, let us create them that are to be human beings,” (thus) said the manitous.*

Aaniind ikido anishinaabe (some of the people say), gete-anishinaabe (the old-time people), giizhi-debweyendam (and they fully believed it); mii dash i’iw (and so it is) ogowedi dibishkoo-akamig (these ones directly opposite, on the other side of the world — \*dibishkoo-akamig flagged\*) gaa-izhi-naakonigewaad gichi-manidoog (the way the great manitous decreed it). Apii sa gaa-giizhitood Nenabozho aki (after Nenabozho had finished making the earth), mii zhigwa gii-dibaakonigewaad igiw gichi-manidoog (then the great manitous laid down the law). “Ambe sa, ozhi-aataanig (“come now, let us make them — \*ozhi-aataanig: oshi- ‘make’ + imperative; flagged\*) igiw ge-anishinaabewiwaad,” (those that are to be human beings,”) ikidowag i’iw manidoog (say the manitous).

(2) Pājigidac äjikīgigitut ‘a‘a‘ᵘ ki‘tcimạnidō: “O‘ōwisa ta∙i∙ciwäbạt, tagagwatạgi‘tō aᵘ ạnicinābä. Māmwä‘tcigu uskabinōtciwiwit mī aᵘ käcawänimäk. Ta udci∙tcāgudạc aᵘ abinōtcī. Kīnawādạc, manitōtug\! ninguting kīgakawạtisăwa a‘a‘ᵘ ạnicinābä. A‘pī wimawatisäg i‘kwädạc kapindigäckawāwa; mīdạc a‘a‘ᵘ kä‘ugiyäg. ‘I‘i‘widạc kä‘a‘kwānig ạnicinābä äciwindamawik mini‘k käbimādisit; wawingä kiwâpi‘kwät tcipikiwät.”

**Bezhig idash ezhi-giigidod a’aw gichi-manidoo: “O’oo sa da-izhiwebad, da-gagwaadagi’ido aw anishinaabe. Maamwe go go oshki-abinoojiiwiwid mii aw ge-zhawenimeg. Da-ojichaago dash aw abinoojii. Giinawaa dash, manidootog\! ningoding giga-gawaadizaawaa a’aw anishinaabe. Apii wii-mawadiseg ikwe dash ga-biindigeshkawaawaa; mii dash a’aw ge-oogiyeg. I’iwi dash ge-aakwaanig anishinaabe ezhi-windamaw’eg minik ge-bimaadizid; waawiinge gii-waabikwed ji-bigiwed.”**

*Then up spoke one of the great manitous, saying: “This is the way it shall be, let human beings undergo trials. And in particular, while they are yet in early infancy, that is when you shall bless them. And souls shall the infants have. And you, O manitous\! at times shall you visit the people. When you desire to go visit the people, then into woman shall you enter; and of that one shall you make a mother. And knowledge shall you impart to the people of the length of time that they shall live; that when their hair is entirely white, then shall they return home.”*

Bezhig idash ezhi-giigidod a’aw gichi-manidoo (then one of the great manitous spoke): “O’oo sa da-izhiwebad (“this is how it shall be), da-gagwaadagi’ido aw anishinaabe (the human being shall undergo hardship). Maamwe go go oshki-abinoojiiwiwid (especially while still a new infant — \*oshki-abinoojiiwiwid flagged\*) mii aw ge-zhawenimeg (that is the one you shall bless). Da-ojichaago dash aw abinoojii (and the infant shall have a soul). Giinawaa dash, manidootog\! (and you, O manitous\! — \*vocative; flagged\*) ningoding giga-gawaadizaawaa a’aw anishinaabe (at times you shall visit the human being — \*gawaadizaawaa: ‘visit’, stem flagged\*). Apii wii-mawadiseg (when you wish to go visit) ikwe dash ga-biindigeshkawaawaa (you shall enter into a woman); mii dash a’aw ge-oogiyeg (and of that one you shall make a mother). I’iwi dash ge-aakwaanig anishinaabe (and that — the length the person will live —) ezhi-windamaw’eg (you shall make known to him) minik ge-bimaadizid (how long he is to live); waawiinge gii-waabikwed (when his hair is fully white) ji-bigiwed.” (then he shall return home.”)

(3) Mī‘sa cigwa manitō omawadisān anicinābän; a‘pidcidạc manitōwi o∙o∙mā a‘king. Mini‘kidạc kayä mänitōwisik anicinābä, kämāgayä atcina tapimadisi. Mī win i∙i∙‘ᵘ äjitäbwäyäntank a‘a‘ᵘ anicinābä.

**Mii sa zhigwa manidoo omawadisaan anishinaaben; aapiji dash manidoowi o’omaa aking. Minik idash gaye manidoowisig anishinaabe, gemaa gaye achina da-bimaadizi. Mii wiin i’iw ezhi-debweyendang a’aw anishinaabe.**

*And so now the manitous visit the people; and with very much supernatural power are they endued (while) here on earth. And of the number of people that are not endued with a sufficient amount of supernatural power, only a short while will they live. This is what the people believe.*

Mii sa zhigwa manidoo omawadisaan anishinaaben (and so now the manidoo visits the person); aapiji dash manidoowi o’omaa aking (and very full of spirit-power is he here on earth). Minik idash gaye manidoowisig anishinaabe (and the person who is not endowed with enough spirit-power), gemaa gaye achina da-bimaadizi (perhaps will live but a short while). Mii wiin i’iw ezhi-debweyendang a’aw anishinaabe (this is what the person believes).

(4) Mīdạc ōwiti äcănit u‘tci∙tcāgwan. Pōtc dạc kayä winawā manitōn ugwä‘kā‘kunigowan, tibickō manitōng āwiwag. Mī win i∙i∙‘ᵘ äcitäbwäyäntank a‘a‘ᵘ anicinābä.

**Mii dash oowidi ezhaanid ojichaagwan. Booch idash gaye wiinawaa manidoon ogwekaakonigowaan, dibishkoo manidoong aawiwag. Mii wiin i’iw ezhi-debweyendang a’aw anishinaabe.**

*And it is off in this direction that the soul goes. And according as they desire, so are they transformed by the manitous, like manitous do they become. This, therefore, is what the people believe.*

Mii dash oowidi ezhaanid ojichaagwan (and it is off in this direction that the soul goes — ojichaagwan ‘his/her soul’, \*obviative\*). Booch idash gaye wiinawaa manidoon ogwekaakonigowaan (and of necessity, too, by the manidoo they are turned/transformed — \*gwekaakonige- ‘turn, decree-over’, stem flagged\*), dibishkoo manidoong aawiwag (just like manidoos they become). Mii wiin i’iw ezhi-debweyendang a’aw anishinaabe (this is what the person believes).

(5) Naskä dac o∙o∙‘ᵘ nanīsānạt,—nibōwin, i‘i‘ᵘ a‘pitcinibuwin. Awägwän ä‘ta a‘pidci käminopimādisigwän mī yä‘ta aᵘ kä‘udi‘tank iti‘ᵘ tanakiwin. Naskäkuta ändasingin nanīsānisiwinan\! A‘pidci yä‘ta mänupimādisit ayā iwiti tanakiwining. Kāwindạc wi‘kā nibusi. Mī win i∙i∙‘ᵘ äcitäbwäyäntank a‘a‘ᵘ anicinābä, mī a‘panä gāginik ayānit utci∙tcāgwạn.

**Naske dash o’ow naniizaanad,—nibowin, i’iw aapiji-nibowin. Awegwen eta aapiji ge-mino-bimaadizigwen mii yeta aw ge-odidang i’iw danakiiwin. Naske gida endasingin naniizaanisiwinan\! Aapiji yeta mino-bimaadizid ayaa iwidi danakiiwining. Gaawiin idash wiikaa niboosii. Mii wiin i’iw ezhi-debweyendang a’aw anishinaabe, mii apane gaaginiig ayaanid ojichaagwan.**

*Behold, this is the thing to be feared, death, the death (that is) everlasting. Only they that live an upright life will come to that abiding-place. Why, behold the number of things to be feared\! Only they that have lived an exceedingly upright life are at yonder habitation. And never will they die. This therefore is what the people believe, that forever do their souls continue there.*

Naske dash o’ow naniizaanad (now behold, this is the fearsome thing),—nibowin, i’iw aapiji-nibowin (death, the everlasting death). Awegwen eta aapiji ge-mino-bimaadizigwen (whoever lives an exceedingly upright life — \*dubitative participle\*) mii yeta aw ge-odidang i’iw danakiiwin (that is the only one to reach that dwelling-place). Naske gida endasingin naniizaanisiwinan\! (behold how many are the things to be feared\! — \*endasingin flagged\*) Aapiji yeta mino-bimaadizid (only the one who lives exceedingly well) ayaa iwidi danakiiwining (is at yonder habitation). Gaawiin idash wiikaa niboosii (and never does he die). Mii wiin i’iw ezhi-debweyendang a’aw anishinaabe (this is what the person believes), mii apane gaaginiig ayaanid ojichaagwan (that forever, eternally, his soul continues there).

(6) Mạtcimạnitō ayā, pīg icini‘kāsuwitug, kīyōn kayä kānō kayä, tạnäs kayä. Ningī∙i∙gidạc nimicōmisiban: “Nōjis, kägu‘ wi‘kā taci‘kawa‘kän. Pitcīnạg kīgawâbạmāg, a‘tātiwinạg ta∙i∙cini‘kāsuwag; mī aᵘ wayābickiwät kätaci‘kawāt, kayä ‘isi‘ tci∙o∙ci∙a∙t.

**Maji-manidoo ayaa, biig izhinikaazowidog, giyoon gaye gaano gaye, danes gaye. Ningii-igo idash nimishoomisiban: “Noozhis, gego wiikaa dazhi-gawa’aaken. Bijiinag giga-waabamaag, adaadiwinan da-izhinikaazowag; mii aw wayaabishkiwed ge-dazhi-gawaad, gaye izhi ji-oozhi’aad.**

*An evil manitou exists; a spade must his name be, and a diamond, and a heart, and a club. Now, I was told by my grandfather who is no more: “My grandson, never have anything to do with them. After a while you shall see them, objects for gambling is what they shall be called; for that is what the white (people) shall occupy themselves with, and they shall make them.*

Maji-manidoo ayaa (there is an evil manidoo), biig izhinikaazowidog (he must be named ‘Spade’ — \*biig = Eng. ‘spade/pique’ loan; dubitative\*), giyoon gaye (and ‘Diamond’ — \*Eng. ‘queen’? carried as named in body; flagged\*) gaano gaye (and ‘Heart’ — \*Eng. ‘knave’? carried; flagged\*), danes gaye (and ‘Club’ — \*Eng. ‘deuce’? carried; flagged\*). Ningii-igo idash nimishoomisiban (and I was told by my late grandfather): “Noozhis, gego wiikaa dazhi-gawa’aaken (“my grandson, never busy yourself with them — \*dazhi-gawa’- ‘occupy oneself / strike at’, stem flagged\*). Bijiinag giga-waabamaag (by and by you will see them), adaadiwinan da-izhinikaazowag (‘gambling-objects’ they will be called); mii aw wayaabishkiwed ge-dazhi-gawaad (it is the white one who will occupy himself with them), gaye izhi ji-oozhi’aad (and so he will make them).

*The four suit-words biig / giyoon / gaano / danes are English card-suit loans the narrator names as manitou-marked gambling objects (English page: Spade / Diamond / Heart / Club); they are carried as printed and EXCLUDED from rule-mining. They reappear as the four labels of the p.516 diagram (§9). The exact source English word behind each (the t/d, the ‘pig/spade’ pairing) is flagged for M.*

(7) Mīdac ini‘ᵘ käki‘kino∙a∙māgut mạtcimạnitōn. Kīcpīn täbwä‘tawisiwạn äninān, kabäya∙i∙ tacī‘kawạtwā, kīgawâbạmā ‘a‘a‘ᵘ mạtcimạnitō.”

**Mii dash iniw ge-gikino’amaagod maji-manidoon. Giishpin debwetawisiwan eninaan, gabe-ayi’ii dazhi-gawaadwaa, giga-waabamaa a’aw maji-manidoo.”**

*Thereupon by the evil manitou shall one be taught. If you do not heed what I tell you then, if for a long time you occupy yourself with them, you shall see that evil manitou.”*

Mii dash iniw ge-gikino’amaagod maji-manidoon (and then he will be taught by the evil manidoo). Giishpin debwetawisiwan eninaan (if you do not believe what I tell you), gabe-ayi’ii dazhi-gawaadwaa (if for a long time you busy yourself with them), giga-waabamaa a’aw maji-manidoo.” (you will see that evil manidoo.”)

(8) Nīnidạc Wâsāgunäckạnk ägōyān. Kägabäya∙i∙ nintacī‘kawāg īgi‘ᵘ a‘tātiwinạg. Nīcugun tacī‘kawagwā mī∙i∙‘ᵘ äjikīwanātisiyān.

**Niin idash Waasaagoneshkang egooyaan. Ge-gabe-ayi’ii nindazhi-gawaag igiw adaadiwinag. Niizho-gon dazhi-gawagwaa mii i’iw ezhi-giiwanaadiziyaan.**

*Now, I myself am named He-that-leaves-the-Imprint-of-his-Foot-in-the-Snow-glistening. For long periods at a time do I occupy myself with those gambling-objects. If for two days I am busied with them, then I am out of my wits.*

Niin idash Waasaagoneshkang egooyaan (now I myself am the one called Waasaagoneshkang). Ge-gabe-ayi’ii nindazhi-gawaag igiw adaadiwinag (for long stretches I busy myself with those gambling-objects). Niizho-gon dazhi-gawagwaa (when I occupy myself with them for two days) mii i’iw ezhi-giiwanaadiziyaan (then it is that I go out of my wits — giiwanaadizi ‘be deranged/crazed’).

(11) Kayä nīn wäntcinibwā‘kāyān ‘a‘a‘ᵘ nōs mī∙o∙wä kāmāmawinigānisit. ‘A‘a‘ᵘ nōs Wämbwäyāc, mī∙i∙‘ᵘ kā∙i∙cini‘kāsut. Mīnawā kī∙ō∙sit nōsiban, Zäzōngini‘k kī∙i∙jini‘kāsu nimicōmisiban. **Gaye niin wenji-nibwaakaayaan a’aw noos mii iwe gaa-maamawinigaanizid. A’aw noos Wembweyaazh, mii i’iw gaa-izhinikaazod. Miinawaa gii-oozisid noosiban, Zhezhoonginik gii-izhinikaazo nimishoomisiban. Miinawaa gii-oozisid Zhezhoonginik, Awaasi’giwenzhi; gii-gichi-midewi. Awaasi’giwenzhi Esiban gii-izhinimaawan oosan. Mii dash igiw gaa-bi-gidowaad: “Gego wiikaa nisaakegon weyaabishkiiwed. Gaye go iw wiikaa ningoding gichi-miigaading ji-wii-miigaaneg. Giishpin miigaanind mii go i’iw ji-ishkwaa-anishinaabekaag.”**

*Now, the reason why I am wise is that my father was the leader of all at this place. This father of mine (was) Snow-blown-by-the-Wind, that was what he was called. Furthermore, my father, who is no more, had a father; Big-Strong-Arm was the name of my grandfather who is no more. Again, Big-Strong-Arm had a father, (he was) Old-Bull-Head; he was versed in the mystic rite. Old-Bull-Head had a father by the name of Raccoon. And this is what they have handed down (one to another): “Never slay the white (people). And if ever in the future there is a big war, don’t fight them. If they are warred against, then that shall be the end of the people.”*

Gaye niin wenji-nibwaakaayaan a’aw noos (and the reason I am wise is my father) mii iwe gaa-maamawinigaanizid (he was the one who led all here — \*maamawinigaanizi flagged\*). A’aw noos Wembweyaazh (this father of mine was Snow-blown-by-the-Wind), mii i’iw gaa-izhinikaazod (that is what he was called). Miinawaa gii-oozisid noosiban (again, my late father had a father), Zhezhoonginik gii-izhinikaazo nimishoomisiban (Big-Strong-Arm my late grandfather was named). Miinawaa gii-oozisid Zhezhoonginik (again, Big-Strong-Arm had a father), Awaasi’giwenzhi (Old-Bull-Head); gii-gichi-midewi (he was deeply versed in the Midewiwin — \*gichi-midewi: the mystic rite\*). Awaasi’giwenzhi Esiban gii-izhinimaawan oosan (Old-Bull-Head’s father was named Raccoon). Mii dash igiw gaa-bi-gidowaad (and this is what they handed down): “Gego wiikaa nisaakegon weyaabishkiiwed (“never slay the white man). Gaye go iw wiikaa ningoding gichi-miigaading ji-wii-miigaaneg (and never, if some day there is a great war, are you to fight them). Giishpin miigaanind mii go i’iw ji-ishkwaa-anishinaabekaag.” (if they are fought, then that is the end of the people’s being.”)

**Gitimaagi’aa anishinaabe nindinendam; Waasaagoneshkang niin egooyaan. Indigo niin daanimagad i’iw nindakiim. Gaawiin wiikaa nimbi-danisisii i’iw gii-adaanting o’ow nindakiiminaan. *Ill-used are the people, is what I think; He-that-leaves-the-Imprint-of-his-Foot-in-the-Snow-glistening is what I am called. It seems to me that my land is resourceful. Gitimaagi’aa anishinaabe nindinendam (the people are ill-used, I think); Waasaagoneshkang niin egooyaan (I am the one called Waasaagoneshkang). Indigo niin daanimagad i’iw nindakiim (it seems to me my land is rich/resourceful — \*daanimagad ‘it is wealthy’, stem flagged\*). Gaawiin wiikaa nimbi-danisisii i’iw gii-adaanting o’ow nindakiiminaan (never was I present when this our land was being sold — \*adaanting ‘it being sold/traded’, flagged\*). Ambegizh wiidookawid geshawaadizid (would that a kindly one might help me), gaye go a’aw gichi-manidoo wiidookawid (and that the great manidoo might help me) i’iw bangii ji-mino-ayaapan a’aw abinoojii gaye a’aw geget. (so it might be a little better for the child and for the aged — \*kä‘kāt ‘the one who is old’, flagged\*.)

(14) Mīgu i‘ᵘ ninguting käcikawatcit. Manu‘, taga, täbwä‘tawicig mini‘k käwâbandamäg o∙o∙‘ᵘ ucibi‘i∙gan. Anica nintigōg anint anicinābäg. Igi‘ᵘ näbwā‘kāwāt, “Mīyä‘ta aᵘ kägō kägacki‘tō‘pan,” ijiwāt. Mīdāswi taciwạg wâtō‘kawiwāt, kāwīn kayä ogimāwisīwạg; kayä nīn kāwin nintōgimāwisi omā∙a∙yāyān.

**Mii go iw ningoding ge-zhi-gawaadizid. Mano, daga, debwetawishig minik ge-waabandameg o’ow ozhibii’igan. Aanizha nindigoog aniind anishinaabeg. Igiw nebwaakaawaad, “Mii eta aw gego ge-gashkitoopan,” izhiwaad. Midaaswi dazhiwag waa-wiidookawiwaad, gaawiin gaye ogimaawisiiwag; gaye niin gaawiin nindogimaawisii omaa ayaayaan.**

*In fact, there are times when they shall freeze to death. But nevertheless, (I) pray, believe me, as many of you as shall see what is here written. In jest am I spoken of by some of the people. Those that are wise, “He is the only one who is capable of accomplishing things,” they say of me. Ten is the number of them who are of help to me, and they are not chiefs; and I too am not chief here where I am.*

Mii go iw ningoding ge-zhi-gawaadizid (it is so that at times they will freeze — \*gawaadizi ‘freeze to death’; cf. §2 the homophonous ‘visit/afflict’ stem; flagged\*). Mano, daga, debwetawishig (but please, do, believe me) minik ge-waabandameg o’ow ozhibii’igan (as many of you as will see this writing). Aanizha nindigoog aniind anishinaabeg (in jest I am spoken of by some people). Igiw nebwaakaawaad (those who are wise), “Mii eta aw gego ge-gashkitoopan,” izhiwaad (“he is the only one who could accomplish anything,” they say). Midaaswi dazhiwag waa-wiidookawiwaad (ten in number are those who would help me), gaawiin gaye ogimaawisiiwag (and they are not chiefs); gaye niin gaawiin nindogimaawisii omaa ayaayaan (and I too am no chief here where I am).

**Maajiwewenizhkang izhinikaazo waa-wiidookawid. Waasigoneshkang, Anakamiginang, Nibidegiizhigokwe, Medweyaandagaazh, Ogabegiizhig, Bekidachiweshkang, Mitigoowimakakiins, Niizhode’, Eniweweyaazh, Mishaakwanibinesh, Nabane-giizhigwep, Baashitaanakwad, — mii sa endashiwaad waa-wiidookawiwaad.**
