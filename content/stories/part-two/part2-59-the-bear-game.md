59\. THE BEAR-GAME (MA‘KWA‘TĀTIWIN).

**MAKWA-ATAADIIWIN.**

THE BEAR-GAME.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk (Series III; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (not credited on the story page itself).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Kāwin Nänabucu āwisī kā∙u∙ci‘tōd ‘iᵉi’ᵘ atātiwin, mīgu aᵘ anicinābä; ‘iᵉi’ᵘ a‘pī ka‘kina kägō kā‘kici‘tōd ‘aᵉa’ᵘ Nänabucu. Mī a‘pī päcig kwīwisäns äcinibāt kayä iyi kī∙i∙gwi∙i∙cimut; a‘pī∙i∙dạc midāsugun wâsinisig mī∙a∙‘pī kīnāsi‘kāgut ini’ᵘ ma‘kwan ugī‘kạnōnigōn: “Ambäsa’, nōjis, kīwindạmōn kä∙i∙cictcigät anicinābä.”

**Gaawiin Nenabozho aawizii gaa-oozhi’tood i’iw ataadiiwin, mii go aw anishinaabe; i’iw apii gakina gegoo gaa-giizhi’tood a’aw Nenabozho. Mii apii bezhig gwiiwizens ezhi-nibaad gaye iyi gii-igwii-izhimod; apii idash midaaso-giizhig waa-asinizig mii apii gii-naazikaagod iniw makwan ogii-ganoonigoon: “Ambegizh, noozhis, gii-wiindamoon ge-izhichiged anishinaabe.”**

*It was not Nänabushu who created this gambling-game, it was (one of) the people; it was after the time that Nänabushu had created everything. It was when a certain boy, while in a fast, was asleep; it was when for ten days he had gone without food, that he was visited by a Bear, who spoke to him, saying: “Behold, my grandson, I now impart to you what the people shall do.”*

Gaawiin Nenabozho aawizii (it was not Nänabushu) gaa-oozhi’tood i’iw ataadiiwin (who made this gambling-game), mii go aw anishinaabe (it was the person/people); i’iw apii (it was the time) gakina gegoo gaa-giizhi’tood a’aw Nenabozho (after Nänabushu had finished making everything). Mii apii bezhig gwiiwizens (it was when a certain boy) ezhi-nibaad (was asleep) gaye iyi gii-igwii-izhimod (while he was fasting; \*gii-iigwiishimod, the fast-sleep verb — flagged\*); apii idash midaaso-giizhig waa-asinizig (and when for ten days he had not eaten — \*waa-/wii-asinizig flagged\*) mii apii gii-naazikaagod iniw makwan (then he was visited by that bear) ogii-ganoonigoon (he was spoken to by him): “Ambegizh, noozhis (“behold, my grandson), gii-wiindamoon ge-izhichiged anishinaabe.” (I tell you what the people shall do.”)

*kā∙u∙ci‘tōd / kā‘kici‘tōd — the make/finish-making verb pair (oozhi’too- / giizhi’too-), kept distinct as printed. wâsinisig “he went without food” (the faster does not eat) — stem flagged. kī∙i∙gwi∙i∙cimut — the fasting-sleep verb; segmentation flagged.*

(2) Mīdạc ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kīwindạmāgut a‘tātiwin kä∙a∙yāg ō∙o∙mā a‘kīng. Cigwadạc ugīmāsumigōn mini‘k kä∙a∙‘kīwạnk tcī∙a∙yāg ‘iᵉi’ᵘ a‘tātiwin. “Ambäsa’\! pisindạwicin kä∙i∙ninān.” Ānīc ‘aᵉa’ᵘ uskinawä ā‘pidci kīnibwā‘kā inini.

**Mii dash i’iw gii-wiindamaagod ataadiiwin ge-ayaag o’omaa akiing. Zhigwa dash ogii-maazomigoon minik ge-akiiwang ji-ayaag i’iw ataadiiwin. “Ambegizh\! bizindawishin ge-ininaan.” Aaniish a’aw oshkinawe aapiji gii-nibwaakaa inini.**

*Thereupon was (the boy) given instruction in the game that was to be here upon earth. So then did he begin to receive knowledge about the game that was to continue as long as the world would last. “Hark\! do you give ear to what I shall tell you.” Now, the youth was a very bright fellow.*

Mii dash i’iw gii-wiindamaagod ataadiiwin (and so he was instructed in the game) ge-ayaag o’omaa akiing (that was to be here on earth). Zhigwa dash ogii-maazomigoon (then he was urged/blessed by him) minik ge-akiiwang (for as long as the world would last) ji-ayaag i’iw ataadiiwin (that the game should endure). “Ambegizh\! bizindawishin (“come, listen to me) ge-ininaan.” (what I shall tell you.”) Aaniish a’aw oshkinawe (now, that youth) aapiji gii-nibwaakaa inini (was a very wise man).

(3) “Mīsa cigwa tciki‘kina∙ạ∙mōnān ‘iᵉi’ᵘ a‘tātiwin. Mīsa iᵘ kä∙i∙cini‘kādäg ma‘kwa‘tātiwin. Kāwīn win kīciga‘k tā‘tadisī ‘aᵉa’ᵘ anicinābä, tibi‘kak mämwätc tā∙a∙tāti ‘aᵉa’ᵘ anicinābä. Kīnawā, anicinābätug\! kīgakanawändānāwa ‘iᵉi’ᵘ a‘tātiwin. Īgiwidạc mindci‘kāwạnaᵉ nīwin tāyābạtisiwạg.” O∙ō∙widạc ogī∙i∙gōn: “Mīwạnōnu ninindcin käyäwätcigät ‘aᵉa’ᵘ anicinābä a‘pīdạc wi∙a∙tātit. Aᵘ anicinābä wäwäni ta∙i∙cictciga udaya∙i∙miwan tibickōgu wīpạgitcāsut; mī∙i∙‘ᵘ kä∙i∙cictcigät ‘aᵉa’ᵘ anicinābä. Nōjis, wäwäni tibātcimun nōngum äninän. Kayä dạc ‘aᵉa’ᵘ mi‘tigwap kīwindạmōn, pābigägu kīga∙u∙ci∙ā. Kaya ini’ pi‘kwa‘kōn kīga∙u∙citōnan. A‘pīdạc ki‘kīci‘tōyan mī∙a∙‘pī käwunā‘kunigäyan, nīwin idac kwīwisänsag kīgamīnāg īni’ᵘ mi‘tigwābīn. Mīdạc ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kä∙i∙citibātcimoyan nōngum äninān īgiwä kwīwisänsạg kīmīnatwā mi‘tigwābin, nīwạg igiu kägapäpimātisiwạt. Kāwīn āwīya tanōntänibusīwạg. Mīdạc ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kä∙u∙ndcimanitōwiwạt īgi’ᵘ anicinābäg. Nōjis, mīgu i’ᵘ mini‘k käkanōninān. Kayä kīn kigakabäpimātis. Mīsạgu iᵘ mini‘k äcicawäniminān. Pạnädạc kīgayābatci‘tōn. Mīsagu iᵘ mini‘k käganōninān. Mīgu iᵘ ici∙a∙naci‘tan ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kī∙i∙gwicimoyạn.”

**“Mii sa zhigwa ji-gikina’amoonaan i’iw ataadiiwin. Mii iw ge-izhi-nikaadeg makwa-ataadiiwin. Gaawiin wiin giizhigak daa-ataadisii a’aw anishinaabe, dibikak memwech daa-ataadi a’aw anishinaabe. Giinawaa, anishinaabedog\! gigad-ganawendaanaawaa i’iw ataadiiwin. Igiw idash minjikaawanan niiwin daa-aabadiziwag.” Onow idash ogii-igoon: “Miiwanoono nininjin ge-yewejiged a’aw anishinaabe apii dash wii-ataadid. Aw anishinaabe weweni daa-izhichige odaya’iiminan dibishkoo go wii-bagijaazod; mii iw ge-izhichiged a’aw anishinaabe. Noozhis, weweni dibaajimon noongom enenaan. Gaye dash a’aw mitigwaab gii-wiindamoon, baabige go giga-oozhi’aa. Gaye iniw bikwakoon giga-oozhitoonan. Apii dash gii-giizhi’tooyan mii apii ge-onaakonigeyan, niiwin idash gwiiwizensag gigad-miinaag iniw mitigwaabiin. Mii dash i’iw ge-izhi-dibaajimoyan noongom enenaan igiwe gwiiwizensag gii-miinadwaa mitigwaabiin, niiwag igiw ge-gabe-bimaadiziwaad. Gaawiin awiya da-noondenibosiiwag. Mii dash i’iw ge-onji-manidoowiwaad igiw anishinaabeg. Noozhis, mii go iw minik ge-ganooninaan. Gaye giin giga-gabe-bimaadiz. Mii sa go iw minik ezhi-zhaweniminaan. Banaa dash giga-aabaji’ton. Mii sa go iw minik ge-ganooninaan. Mii go iw izhi-anaji’tan i’iw gii-iigwiishimoyan.”**

*“The time has now come for me to teach you the game. And so it shall be called a bear-game. Not in the day-time shall the people engage in the play, at night only shall they play together. You, O people\! you shall keep watch over the game. And these mittens, four in number, shall be used.” And this (the youth) was told: “These paws of mine shall the people represent when they wish to play the game. The people shall be careful, when conducting (the game), to put up their possessions over against each other as a wager; thus shall the people do. My grandson, plainly do you relate the story of what I am now saying to you. And I also make known to you a bow, at once shall you make it. And the arrows too shall you make. And when you have finished doing that, then shall you make known a formal announcement, and to four boys shall you give the bow (and arrows). Thereupon shall you relate what I now tell you, how that after you have given the bow (and arrows) to the boys, the four then shall live out the full span of their life. None of them will die before their time. Therefore for this reason will those boys become endowed with manitou power. My grandson, that is as much as I shall tell you. You too shall live for a long while. Such is the extent of the blessing that I bestow upon you. And always shall you put it to (good) use. Now, that is as much as I shall speak to you. Accordingly do you now cease from your fasting.”*

“Mii sa zhigwa ji-gikina’amoonaan i’iw ataadiiwin (“now it is time for me to teach you the game). Mii iw ge-izhi-nikaadeg makwa-ataadiiwin (it shall be called the bear-game). Gaawiin wiin giizhigak daa-ataadisii a’aw anishinaabe (the people shall not play in the daytime), dibikak memwech daa-ataadi a’aw anishinaabe (at night especially shall the people play). Giinawaa, anishinaabedog\! (you, O people\!) gigad-ganawendaanaawaa i’iw ataadiiwin (you shall keep/guard the game). Igiw idash minjikaawanan niiwin daa-aabadiziwag.” (and these mittens, four, shall be used.”) Onow idash ogii-igoon (and this he was told): “Miiwanoono nininjin (“these paws of mine — \*flagged\*) ge-yewejiged a’aw anishinaabe (the people shall act out/represent) apii dash wii-ataadid (when they wish to play). Aw anishinaabe weweni daa-izhichige (the people shall do it carefully) odaya’iiminan dibishkoo go wii-bagijaazod (setting their goods one against another as a wager — \*bagijaazo- / wager-idiom flagged, H3\*); mii iw ge-izhichiged a’aw anishinaabe (thus shall the people do). Noozhis, weweni dibaajimon noongom enenaan (my grandson, tell plainly what I now say to you). Gaye dash a’aw mitigwaab gii-wiindamoon (and a bow I make known to you), baabige go giga-oozhi’aa (at once you shall make it). Gaye iniw bikwakoon giga-oozhitoonan (and the arrows too you shall make). Apii dash gii-giizhi’tooyan (and when you have finished) mii apii ge-onaakonigeyan (then you shall make a formal announcement), niiwin idash gwiiwizensag gigad-miinaag iniw mitigwaabiin (and to four boys you shall give the bow). Mii dash i’iw ge-izhi-dibaajimoyan noongom enenaan (and you shall relate what I now tell you), igiwe gwiiwizensag gii-miinadwaa mitigwaabiin (those boys to whom the bow is given), niiwag igiw ge-gabe-bimaadiziwaad (the four shall live out their full lives). Gaawiin awiya da-noondenibosiiwag (none of them will die before their time). Mii dash i’iw ge-onji-manidoowiwaad igiw anishinaabeg (and for this reason those people shall be endowed with manitou power). Noozhis, mii go iw minik ge-ganooninaan (my grandson, that is as much as I shall tell you). Gaye giin giga-gabe-bimaadiz (you too shall live long). Mii sa go iw minik ezhi-zhaweniminaan (such is the extent of the blessing I give you). Banaa dash giga-aabaji’ton (and always you shall use it). Mii sa go iw minik ge-ganooninaan (that is as much as I shall say to you). Mii go iw izhi-anaji’tan i’iw gii-iigwiishimoyan.” (now then, cease from your fasting.”)

*Miiwanoono ninindcin “these paws of mine” — drafted; the paws-of-the-bear that the mittens (minjikaawanan) represent; stem flagged. käyäwätcigät “acts willfully / represents” — drafted yeweji-, flagged. tibickōgu wīpạgitcāsut — Jones: “to put up their possessions… as a wager”; literal wii-bagijaazod “as he wishes to lay (himself/it) down,” the wager-idiom flagged (H3). The four boys = the four arrows/players who live out a full life span (the blessing’s core).*

(4) Mīsa gägä‘t aᵘ inini äcīckwā‘tāt ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kī∙i∙gwicimut. Cigwadạc tägwicink äntäwāt, —ānīc kāwīn anōtcigu āwisiwan ini’ᵘ ōsan, —äci wīntạmawāt: “Mīsa’, nōs, kīwintạmōn a‘tātiwin. Ningīcawänimi‘k ‘aᵉa’ᵘ ma‘kwa; kayä dạc mi‘tigwābīn ningīmini‘k nīwinidạc uskinawänsag tcimīnạgwa ini’ᵘ mi‘tigwābīn. Mīdạc igi’ᵘ kägi‘kāwāt; mī∙i∙mā kä∙u∙ndcisāgi‘tōt anicinābä kicpîn tābita tāwāt kayä ‘iᵉi’ᵘ a‘tātiwin.”

**Mii sa geget aw inini ezhi-ishkwaa’taad i’iw gii-iigwiishimod. Zhigwa dash dagwishing endewaad, —aaniish gaawiin anooji go aawiziiwan iniw oosan, —ezhi-wiindamawaad: “Mii sa, noos, gii-wiindamoon ataadiiwin. Ningii-zhawenimig a’aw makwa; gaye dash mitigwaabiin ningii-miinig niiwin idash oshkinawensag ji-miinagwaa iniw mitigwaabiin. Mii dash igiw ge-gikaawaad; mii imaa ge-onji-zaagi’tood anishinaabe gishpin daabita daawaad gaye i’iw ataadiiwin.”**

*Thereupon truly did the man make an end of his fasting. And when he arrived at home, for no ordinary sort of man was his father, he then made known to him, saying: “Therefore now, my father, do I impart to you the knowledge of a game. I have been blessed by a Bear; and a bow (and some arrows) was I also given, so that to four lads I might give the bow (and arrows). Therefore shall they live to old age; and on that account shall the people cherish it when they live together, and (they shall also cherish) the game.”*

Mii sa geget aw inini ezhi-ishkwaa’taad i’iw gii-iigwiishimod (so truly the man ended his fasting). Zhigwa dash dagwishing endewaad (and when he arrived home), —aaniish gaawiin anooji go aawiziiwan iniw oosan (for his father was no ordinary being), —ezhi-wiindamawaad (he told him): “Mii sa, noos, gii-wiindamoon ataadiiwin (“now, my father, I make known to you a game). Ningii-zhawenimig a’aw makwa (I was blessed by the bear); gaye dash mitigwaabiin ningii-miinig (and a bow I was given) niiwin idash oshkinawensag ji-miinagwaa iniw mitigwaabiin (so that I might give the bow to four lads). Mii dash igiw ge-gikaawaad (and so they shall live to old age); mii imaa ge-onji-zaagi’tood anishinaabe (and for that the people shall cherish it) gishpin daabita daawaad gaye i’iw ataadiiwin.” (when they live together, and the game too.”)

(5) Cigwadạc ōsan kīkīkitōwan: “Ningwisis, ambäsa’, kägu’ āgunwä‘tạngän nōngum änātcimuyạn. Mānōgu tawī∙i∙ci∙ā∙wạn ạmạntc ā‘pidci kwätc ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kä∙i∙näntạmōwạnän, mānōgu tawī∙i∙ci∙ā∙wạn ạmạntc.”

**Zhigwa dash oosan gii-gigitowan: “Ningwizis, ambegizh, gego aagonwetangen noongom enaajimoyan. Maano go da-wii-izhi-aawan amanj aapiji gwech i’iw ge-inendamowanen, maano go da-wii-izhi-aawan amanj.”**

*And then his father spoke, saying: “My dear son, behold, do not leave undone what you are now relating. For readily shall you comply, in spite of what you might have in mind; for readily should you comply, despite of (everything).”*

Zhigwa dash oosan gii-gigitowan (and then his father spoke): “Ningwizis, ambegizh (“my son, behold), gego aagonwetangen noongom enaajimoyan (do not gainsay/leave undone what you now relate). Maano go da-wii-izhi-aawan amanj (let it just be so, however it may be) aapiji gwech i’iw ge-inendamowanen (whatever you might think of it), maano go da-wii-izhi-aawan amanj.” (let it just be so, however.”)

*āgunwä‘tạngän “gainsay / leave undone” — drafted aagonwetangen, flagged. mānōgu… ạmạntc “let it just be so, however / despite” — Jones renders “readily shall you comply, in spite of…”; the maano…amanj framing flagged.*

(6) Ugī‘kạnōnān idạc ini’ᵘ ōsan: “Nōs\! kä∙i∙citinä kuca tci∙i∙cictcigäwinäk, mī∙i∙’ᵘ kä∙i∙cictcigäyank. Intawādạc kä∙i∙citinä ninga∙o∙ji‘tōn ‘iᵉi’ᵘ a‘tātiwin.” Cayīgwadạc kīmādci‘tā ‘aᵉa’ᵘ inini wīgiwāmidạc ugī∙u∙ci‘tōn, kiwīdci∙i∙gut ini’ᵘ ōsan kayä ‘iᵉi’ᵘ uskinawänsaᵉ. Abā‘pic kā‘pīni‘tōwat ‘iᵉi’ᵘ wīgiwām, māmwätcidạc uda‘kawā‘tōnāwa ‘iᵉi’ᵘ tcī∙a∙nitibi‘kạdinik. Abā‘pic ạnitibi‘kạdinik, mīsa cigwa sạgaswä∙i∙wäwāt. Abā‘pic pīndigäwāt kāsạgaswä∙i∙ntwā, owâbandānāwā kägō ābi‘tawint a‘tänig. Cigwadạc ‘aᵉa’ᵘ uskinawä kīgitu: “Mīsa cigwa tci∙a∙‘tātiyäg, kabābā‘tạnīnum.”

**Ogii-ganoonaan idash iniw oosan: “Noos\! ge-izhitinaa gosha ji-izhichigewinaag, mii iw ge-izhichigeyang. Indawaa dash ge-izhitinaa ninga-oozhi’toon i’iw ataadiiwin.” Zhayiigwa dash gii-maaji’taa a’aw inini wiigiwaam idash ogii-oozhi’toon, gii-wiidoo’igod iniw oosan gaye i’iw oshkinawensag. Abaapizh gaa-biini’toowaad i’iw wiigiwaam, memwech idash oda’akawaa’toonaawaa i’iw ji-ani-dibikadinig. Abaapizh ani-dibikadinig, mii sa zhigwa zagaswe’iwewaad. Abaapizh biindigewaad gaa-zagaswe’indwaa, owaabandaanaawaa gegoo abita-wiind atenig. Zhigwa dash a’aw oshkinawe gii-gigid: “Mii sa zhigwa ji-ataadiyeg, gabaa-baataniinom.”**

*And he spoke to his father, saying: “O my father\! forthwith must it surely be accomplished, this is what we shall do. So therefore shall I proceed at once to create the game.” And presently the man began building a wigwam, he was helped by his father and the lads. In course of time, when they had set the wigwam in order, they then became exceedingly anxious for the coming-on of the night. While the night was drawing on, it was then that they sent forth invitations to come and smoke. While in were coming the guests who had been invited to smoke, they beheld something that was at the rear of the fire. And in a while the youth spoke, saying: “The time has now come for you to play a game together, you shall be many on a side.”*

Ogii-ganoonaan idash iniw oosan (and he spoke to his father): “Noos\! ge-izhitinaa gosha ji-izhichigewinaag (“my father\! it surely must be accomplished), mii iw ge-izhichigeyang (this is what we shall do). Indawaa dash ge-izhitinaa ninga-oozhi’toon i’iw ataadiiwin.” (and so at once I shall make the game.”) Zhayiigwa dash gii-maaji’taa a’aw inini (presently the man began) wiigiwaam idash ogii-oozhi’toon (and he made a wigwam), gii-wiidoo’igod iniw oosan gaye i’iw oshkinawensag (helped by his father and the lads). Abaapizh gaa-biini’toowaad i’iw wiigiwaam (when they had set the wigwam in order), memwech idash oda’akawaa’toonaawaa i’iw ji-ani-dibikadinig (they grew very anxious for night to come). Abaapizh ani-dibikadinig (as night came on), mii sa zhigwa zagaswe’iwewaad (then they sent out smoke-invitations). Abaapizh biindigewaad gaa-zagaswe’indwaa (as the invited smokers came in), owaabandaanaawaa gegoo abita-wiind atenig (they saw something placed at the rear of the fire). Zhigwa dash a’aw oshkinawe gii-gigid (then the youth spoke): “Mii sa zhigwa ji-ataadiyeg (“now it is time for you to play), gabaa-baataniinom.” (you shall be many on a side.” \*H2\*)

*kabābā‘tạnīnum — “you (pl.) shall be many (on a side)”; drafted gabaa-baataniinom, segmentation/preverb flagged (H2). ābi‘tawint “at the rear of the fire / behind” — the something placed behind the fire (the Bear-token); drafted abita-wiind, flagged.*

(7) Mīdạc iᵘ ki‘kino∙ạ∙māgät kä∙a∙i∙cīying. Kuniginīn, āgwitōcinō ‘iᵉi’ᵘ mindci‘kāwạnaᵉ. Mämwätcidạc ubīwaiᵉ tcigātcigātäg waᵉawidạc cigwa wi‘kātōn, igi’wäti kayātawintwā patagwingwäcinōk. A‘kawä kīnạgạmōwạg mī∙i∙’ᵘ kā∙i∙na∙ạ∙māgut kācawänimigut ini’ᵘ ma‘kwan. Cigwadạc mādci∙a∙mōg:—

**Mii dash iw gii-gikina’amaaged ge-izhi-izhiing. Goniginiin, agwidooshino i’iw minjikaawanan. Memwech idash obiiway jiigaajigaadeg wa’aw idash zhigwa wii-gaadoon, igiwedi gayaatawindwaa badagwingweshinog. Akawe gii-nagamowag mii iw gaa-inana’amaagod gaa-zhawenimigod iniw makwan. Zhigwa dash maaji-anamog:—**

*Accordingly he taught how it should be done. Lo, one over the other were placed the moccasins. And a very (light ball of) fur to be concealed did he now begin hiding, while they from whom it was to be concealed covered their faces (in a blanket).
