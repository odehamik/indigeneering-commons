SUN AND MOON.

*Narrated by Wāsāgunäckạnk and/or Midāsugaⁿj (Series IV; Bois Fort). — narrator per Jones, Ojibwa Texts Part II, Introduction p.ix (“either … or both”; not credited on the story page).*

***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1919).***

(1) Ānīc tāwạg ạnicinābäg. Mīsa’ kigicäp mādca ‘aᵍa’ᵘ inini; ạnitibi‘katinig tạgwicin; obītōn ạno‘kātcigan kayä wīsiniwin mānigut ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ạnicinābä⁸, ạsämān kayä. Mīsa’ cigwa kā‘tagwicing mādcāwan wīwạn, mīsa’ kabätibi‘k undändinit; cigwa weyābạninik tạgwicinōn ạnicinābä cägwasunit. “Kägä‘tsa kitinigā∙ā‘g mīgi’ᵘ ạnicinābäg kitōtawạtwā tasing kīpīnạtwā.”

**Aaniish daawag anishinaabeg. Mii sa giizhep maajaa a’aw inini; ani-dibi’kadinig dagwishin; obidoon anokaajigan gaye wiisiniwin maanigod i’iw anishinaabe’, asemaan gaye. Mii sa zhigwa gaa-dagwishing maajaawan wiiwan, mii sa gabe-dibi’k ondendinid; zhigwa weyaabaninig dagwishinoon anishinaabe zhaagwazonid. “Geget sa gidinigaa’aag i’iw anishinaabeg gidoodawadwaa dasing gii-biinadwaa.”**

*Now, some people were abiding (there). And so in the morning away went the man; when night was drawing on, he came back home; he fetched home some goods and some food that had been given him by the people, likewise some tobacco. And when he was come, then away went his wife, whereupon all night long she was gone; then on the morrow back she came with a human being hanging from her belt. “In truth, you do harm to the people by the way you treat them every time that you come home with one.”*

Aaniish daawag anishinaabeg (now, some people were dwelling). Mii sa giizhep maajaa a’aw inini (and so in the morning that man set off); ani-dibi’kadinig dagwishin (when night was coming on he arrived home); obidoon anokaajigan gaye wiisiniwin (he brought home goods and food) maanigod i’iw anishinaabe’ (given him by the people), asemaan gaye (and tobacco too). Mii sa zhigwa gaa-dagwishing (and so when he had arrived) maajaawan wiiwan (off went his wife — \*obviative\*), mii sa gabe-dibi’k ondendinid (and all night long she was gone); zhigwa weyaabaninig dagwishinoon anishinaabe zhaagwazonid (then at daybreak she came back with a person hanging from her belt — \*carrying a human at her sash; stem zhaagwazo- flagged\*). “Geget sa gidinigaa’aag (“truly you do harm to) i’iw anishinaabeg (the people) gidoodawadwaa dasing gii-biinadwaa.” (by what you do to them every time you fetch one.”)

*¹ The Sun. (Jones’s page-foot note, p. 608/609; attaches to inini, ‘the man’ — the husband is the Sun.)*

(2) “Kāwīn kuca nimāminonända\[ⁿ\]zī, ạnicāgu‘ku nindōdā‘pināg nintinändạm. Mīnawä‘ku nimpạgidînāg nintinändạm. Mīdạc kāwīn ninga∙a∙dāpīnāsīg mīnawä,” udinān īni’ᵘ uwītigämāgạnan.

**“Gaawiin gosha nimaaminonendanzii, anishaa go ko nindoodaapinaag nindinendam. Miinawaa ko nimbagidinaag nindinendam. Mii dash gaawiin ninga-adaapinaasiig miinawaa,” odinaan iniw owiidigemaaganan.**

*“I am really not serious about it, for with no definite purpose do I seize them, I think. Again I will let them go, I think. Therefore I will not seize them again,” she said to her husband.*

“Gaawiin gosha nimaaminonendanzii (“I really do not mean it / take it seriously), anishaa go ko nindoodaapinaag nindinendam (just idly I pick them up, I think), miinawaa ko nimbagidinaag nindinendam (and again I will let them go, I think). Mii dash gaawiin ninga-adaapinaasiig miinawaa,” (so I will not take them up again,”) odinaan iniw owiidigemaaganan (she said to her husband).

*² The Moon. (Jones’s page-foot note, p. 609; attaches to wīwan / ‘his wife’ in §1 — the wife is the Moon.)*

(3) Ānīc, mī cigwa mīnawā kä∙ạ∙nimādcāt ‘aᵍa’ᵘ inini weyābạninig; mīsa’ mīnawā wänāgucininig tạgwicin; mīgäyābi pạgamiwanät ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ạno‘kātcigan. Mīsa’ änāt īni’ᵘ wīwan: “Ambäsinō, aiyāngwāmisin\! kägo\!” udinān īni’ᵘ wīwan. “Kägä‘tsa kidiniga∙ā‘g,” udinān. “Ambäsinō, ‘iᵍi’ᵘ änātisiyān inātisin,” udinān īni’ᵘ wīwan.

**Aaniish, mii zhigwa miinawaa gaa-ani-maajaad a’aw inini weyaabaninig; mii sa miinawaa wenaagoshininig dagwishin; mii geyaabi bagamiwanad i’iw anokaajigan. Mii sa enaad iniw wiiwan: “Ambesino, ayaangwaamizin\! gego\!” odinaan iniw wiiwan. “Geget sa gidinigaa’aag,” odinaan. “Ambesino, i’iw enaadiziyaan inaadizin,” odinaan iniw wiiwan.**

*Well, it was so that the man set forth on the next day; and again, when it was evening, he came home; just as before, he came home bringing some goods. Whereupon he said to his wife: “Please have a care\! don’t do so\!” he said to his wife. “In truth, you do them harm,” he said to her. “Behold, as I behave, so do you,” he said to his wife.*

Aaniish, mii zhigwa miinawaa gaa-ani-maajaad a’aw inini weyaabaninig (well, again that man set off the next day); mii sa miinawaa wenaagoshininig dagwishin (and again when evening came he arrived); mii geyaabi bagamiwanad i’iw anokaajigan (he came carrying goods on his back as before). Mii sa enaad iniw wiiwan (and so he said to his wife): “Ambesino, ayaangwaamizin\! gego\!” (“please, be careful\! don’t\!”) odinaan iniw wiiwan (he said to his wife). “Geget sa gidinigaa’aag,” odinaan (“truly you harm them,” he said). “Ambesino, i’iw enaadiziyaan inaadizin,” (“behold, as I live, so you live,”) odinaan iniw wiiwan (he said to his wife).

(4) Mīdạc kägä‘t cigwa wīmādcānit ạnitibu‘kạdînik īni’ᵘ wīwan. Cigwa mīnawā sāgatciwät ‘aᵍa’ᵘ i‘kwä, wâwâbamān sāga∙ạ∙minit i‘kwäwạn; ubiganawâbamigōn nījōni‘k tci‘tcīgīwan; a‘pidci owâsa opada‘kitōni īni’ᵘ u‘kādini kīcīginit; kanawâbamigut kägä‘tsa ạnicki∙i∙gōn. Äniji∙o∙dā‘pināt, äjipạgidînāt mīnawā; äjitānginạnk ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ugi‘tcipisun. Ānīc mīgu’ cingantạm kanawâbamigut īni’ᵘ ạnicinābän, mīdạc mīnawā äji∙o∙dā‘nināt. “Mīgu’ mīnawā nimpạgidînā,” inwāsut. Mīnawā äjitānginạng ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ugi‘tcipisun. Mīsa’ pīnic nībiwa udōtawä⁸ ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ạnicinābä. Cigwa tạgwicin iwiti ändāwāt, mīsa’ pīnābiwạn īni’ᵘ unābämạn. “Wänäniwinän idạc wīn kā∙i∙ndit? Kägä‘tsa kāwīn kīni‘tānōndạ\[ⁿ\]zī kägō ānägōyạn.”

**Mii dash geget zhigwa wii-maajaanid ani-dibi’kadinig iniw wiiwan. Zhigwa miinawaa zaagajiwed a’aw ikwe, waawaabamaan zaaga’aminid ikwewan; obi-ganawaabamigoon niizhoonik ji-jiigiiwan; aapiji owaasa obadakidooni iniw okaadini giizhiiginid; ganawaabamigod geget sa anishki’igoon. Eni-izhi-odaapinaad, ezhi-bagidinaad miinawaa; ezhi-daanginang i’iw ogichi-bizon. Aaniish mii go zhiingendam ganawaabamigod iniw anishinaaben, mii dash miinawaa ezhi-odaapinaad. “Mii go miinawaa nimbagidinaa,” inwaazod. Miinawaa ezhi-daanginang i’iw ogichi-bizon. Mii sa biinish niibiwa odoodawaa’ i’iw anishinaabe. Zhigwa dagwishin iwidi endaawaad, mii sa biinaabiwan iniw onaabeman. “Weneniiwinen idash wiin gaa-indid? Geget sa gaawiin gizhitaanoondanzii gego enagoyan.”**

*And now, of a truth, it was growing dark when his wife was about to start. When again she was come out on the top of a hill, she fixed her look upon a woman that came forth (from her dwelling), she was watched by her who was scratching her head with both hands; exceedingly far apart were her legs as she stood making water; when watched by her (thus), truly was she angered by her. Then she went and seized her, (but) she let her go again; then she touched her belt. Now, of course she was angered to be watched by that person, whereupon she again seized her. “Indeed, again (shall) I let her go,” was what she pretended. Again she touched her belt. And that was what she kept on doing to the people. In time she came back to the place where she lived, whereupon at her gazed her husband. “What has that fool been doing? Of a truth, are you in the habit of not heeding what you are told.”*

Mii dash geget zhigwa wii-maajaanid ani-dibi’kadinig iniw wiiwan (and now truly his wife was about to set off as dark came on). Zhigwa miinawaa zaagajiwed a’aw ikwe (when again that woman came up over the hill), waawaabamaan zaaga’aminid ikwewan (she fixed her gaze on a woman coming out); obi-ganawaabamigoon niizhoonik ji-jiigiiwan (she was watched by one scratching her head with both hands — \*segmentation flagged\*); aapiji owaasa obadakidooni iniw okaadini giizhiiginid (very far apart she set her legs as she made water); ganawaabamigod geget sa anishki’igoon (being watched thus, truly she was angered by her). Eni-izhi-odaapinaad (then she seized her), ezhi-bagidinaad miinawaa (and let her go again); ezhi-daanginang i’iw ogichi-bizon (then she touched her great belt). Aaniish mii go zhiingendam ganawaabamigod iniw anishinaaben (now of course she resented being watched by that person), mii dash miinawaa ezhi-odaapinaad (so she seized her again). “Mii go miinawaa nimbagidinaa,” inwaazod (“again I will let her go,” she pretended). Miinawaa ezhi-daanginang i’iw ogichi-bizon (again she touched her great belt). Mii sa biinish niibiwa odoodawaa’ i’iw anishinaabe (and so she kept on doing this to many of the people). Zhigwa dagwishin iwidi endaawaad (in time she got back to where they lived), mii sa biinaabiwan iniw onaabeman (whereupon her husband gazed in at her). “Weneniiwinen idash wiin gaa-indid? (“what has that fool been doing? — \*wenaniiwinen flagged\*) Geget sa gaawiin gizhitaanoondanzii gego enagoyan.” (truly you do not heed at all what you are told.”)

*ogichi-bizon ‘her great belt/sash’ — Jones ugi‘tcipisun; the woman’s gichi-bizon (sash) is the thing she ‘touches’ each time she releases a captive. zhitaanoondanzii / kīni‘tānōndạzī — the ‘not heeding’ verb, drafted, flagged. obi-ganawaabamigoon niizhoonik ji-jiigiiwan ‘watched … scratching her head with both hands’ — the niizhoonik (‘with both hands’) + head-scratch clause, drafted; segmentation flagged.*

(5) Inābit ‘aᵍa’ᵘ i‘kwä, ugīcängwasuna⁸ ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ạnicinābä⁸.

**Inaabid a’aw ikwe, ogii-zhaagwazonaa’ i’iw anishinaabe’.**

*As the woman looked, (she saw) the people she had hanging to her belt.*

Inaabid a’aw ikwe (when that woman looked), ogii-zhaagwazonaa’ i’iw anishinaabe’ (she had the people hanging from her belt — \*the carrying/hanging-at-the-sash stem zhaagwazo- flagged; cf. cägwasunit §1\*).

*ugīcängwasuna⁸ — the ‘hanging-at-the-belt’ verb again (cf. cägwasunit, §1); the captives dangle from her sash. Stem flagged.*

(6) Mīsa’ a‘panä äcictcigät, mādcāwạn unābämạn kigicäp; unāgucininik tạgwicinōn. Kägä‘t nībiwa ạno‘kātcigan ubītōni, kayä wīsiniwin.

**Mii sa apane ezhichiged, maajaawan onaabeman giizhep; onaagoshininig dagwishinoon. Geget niibiwa anokaajigan obidooni, gaye wiisiniwin.**

*And this was what he always did, away would go her husband in the morning; when it was evening, back home would he come. In truth, much goods he fetched, and food.*

Mii sa apane ezhichiged (and this is what he always did), maajaawan onaabeman giizhep (her husband would go off in the morning); onaagoshininig dagwishinoon (when evening came he would arrive). Geget niibiwa anokaajigan obidooni, gaye wiisiniwin (truly much goods he brought, and food too).

(7) Mīsa’ mīnawā mādcāt ‘aᵍa’ᵘ i‘kwä. Ānīc mīgu tạsing tängwicingin pīnāt ‘iᵍi’ᵘ ạnicinābä. Ānīc udinān: “Kägä‘tsa kimājitōtawāg kayä kīn. Niwi‘pami∙ā∙g inwä∙ạ∙soyạn,” udinān īni’ᵘ wīwan.

**Mii sa miinawaa maajaad a’aw ikwe. Aaniish mii go dasing degwishingin biinaad i’iw anishinaabe. Aaniish odinaan: “Geget sa gimaaji-doodawaag gaye giin. Niwii-bami’aag inwe’aazoyan,” odinaan iniw wiiwan.**

*Whereupon again departed the woman. Now, every time that she returned, she came fetching some people. So he said to her: “Truly, but you do mistreat them. ‘I intend to give them sustenance’, is what you pretend,” he said to his wife.*

Mii sa miinawaa maajaad a’aw ikwe (and so again that woman set off). Aaniish mii go dasing degwishingin biinaad i’iw anishinaabe (now every time she came back she fetched people). Aaniish odinaan (so he said to her): “Geget sa gimaaji-doodawaag gaye giin (“truly you mistreat them, you too). Niwii-bami’aag inwe’aazoyan,” (‘I mean to sustain them,’ is what you pretend,” — \*inwe’aazo- ‘pretend / say of oneself’; drafted, flagged\*) odinaan iniw wiiwan (he said to his wife).

(8) Mīnawā ạnimādcā waᵍa’ᵘ inini. Ningutingiku pạpimusät kägä‘tsa ugagwātạgänimān īni’ᵘ i‘kwäwạn. Mīdạc änäntank: “Ạmbäsinō, ningawidciwa,” inändạm ‘aᵍa’ᵘ inini. Unābämini ubiminicimāni, mīdạc kā∙i∙gut: “Kägä‘tsa ningagwātạgändam,” udigōn īni’ᵘ i‘kwäwạn.

**Miinawaa ani-maajaa wa’aw inini. Ningoding igo babimosed geget sa ogagwaadagenimaan iniw ikwewan. Mii dash enendang: “Ambesino, ningawiijiwaa,” inendam a’aw inini. Onaabemini obiminizhimaani, mii dash gaa-igod: “Geget sa ningagwaadagendam,” odigoon iniw ikwewan.**

*Again upon his way went the man. And once, while walking along, truly did he feel pity for a woman (that he saw in trouble). And this he thought: “Behold, I will join her,” thought the man. From her husband was she fleeing, and this by her he was told: “Verily, I am in awful trouble,” he was told by the woman.*

Miinawaa ani-maajaa wa’aw inini (again that man went on his way). Ningoding igo babimosed (and once, while walking along) geget sa ogagwaadagenimaan iniw ikwewan (truly he pitied a woman in distress). Mii dash enendang (and this he thought): “Ambesino, ningawiijiwaa,” inendam a’aw inini (“come, I will go with her,” thought the man). Onaabemini obiminizhimaani (from her husband she was fleeing — \*obviative; bimi-zhimo- ‘flee from’ flagged\*), mii dash gaa-igod (and this she told him): “Geget sa ningagwaadagendam,” odigoon iniw ikwewan (“truly I am in dreadful trouble,” he was told by the woman).

(9) ‘Aᵍa’ᵘ kīsis pimusät, mīdạc kā∙i∙nāt: “Awawa, kaniwitciwin,” ugī∙i∙nān. Mīsa’ gägä‘t cigwa kī∙ạ∙niwīdcīwāt īni’ᵘ i‘kwäwạn. Tägwicink āndāwāt, “Kāwīnigu pō‘tc kitāwanu‘kīsī,” udigōn. Ānīc mīsa gägä‘t cigwa kitạgwicinowāt i∙i∙mā ändāwāt. Namạdạpiwạn u‘tāngwäyạn.

**A’aw giizis bimosed, mii dash gaa-inaad: “Awawa, ganii-wiijiwin,” ogii-inaan. Mii sa geget zhigwa gii-ani-wiijiiwaad iniw ikwewan. Dagwishing endaawaad, “Gaawiin igo boozh gidaa-anokiisii,” odigoon. Aaniish mii sa geget zhigwa gii-dagwishinowaad i’imaa endaawaad. Namadabiwan otaangweyan.**

*While the Sun went walking along, this is what he said to her: “Very well, I will go along with you,” he said to her. Whereupon truly he then went along with the woman. When they got home, “It is not necessary for you to work,” she was told. So accordingly, when they were now truly come at the place of their home, there was seated her companion.*

A’aw giizis bimosed (as the Sun walked along), mii dash gaa-inaad (this is what he said to her): “Awawa, ganii-wiijiwin,” ogii-inaan (“very well, I will go with you,” he said to her). Mii sa geget zhigwa gii-ani-wiijiiwaad iniw ikwewan (and so truly he went along with the woman). Dagwishing endaawaad (when they reached home), “Gaawiin igo boozh gidaa-anokiisii,” odigoon (“you need not work at all,” she was told). Aaniish mii sa geget zhigwa gii-dagwishinowaad i’imaa endaawaad (so when they had truly arrived there at their home), namadabiwan otaangweyan (there sat her companion — \*otaangwe- ‘her female companion/co-wife’; obviative\*).

*u‘tāngwäyạn / otaangweyan — ‘her (female) companion’ runs through the swing-episode; the relationship term is preserved as printed (taangwe-).*

(10) Cigwa änitibi‘kạdînik ki‘tcipisōwan a‘pan mādcānit; mīsa’ kabätibi‘k undändinit.

**Zhigwa ani-dibi’kadinig gichi-bizoowan apan maajaanid; mii sa gabe-dibi’k ondendinid.**

*When it was growing dark, (the old woman) girdled on her belt, and then was off on her way; whereupon throughout the whole night was she gone.*

Zhigwa ani-dibi’kadinig (when it grew dark) gichi-bizoowan (she girt on her great belt — \*obviative\*) apan maajaanid (and was off and gone); mii sa gabe-dibi’k ondendinid (and all night long she was away).

(11) “Naskä kä∙i∙cinawạt tagwicing\!” udigōn īni’ᵘ unābämạn. “Naskä mī cigwa tcitạgwicing. Ambädạc, āyāngwāmisin\!” udinān.

**“Naske ge-izhinawad dagwishing\!” odigoon iniw onaabeman. “Naske mii zhigwa ji-dagwishing. Ambe dash, ayaangwaamizin\!” odinaan.**

*“Just you watch and see how she behaves when she comes back\!” (the woman) was told by her husband. “In truth, it is now time for her to return. Now, come, and be on the watch\!” he said to her.*

“Naske ge-izhinawad dagwishing\!” (“just watch how she acts when she gets back\!”) odigoon iniw onaabeman (she was told by her husband). “Naske mii zhigwa ji-dagwishing (“look, it is now time for her to arrive). Ambe dash, ayaangwaamizin\!” odinaan (now then, be on your guard\!” he said to her).

(12) Cigwa gägä‘t tạgwicinōn ạnicinābä⁸ cängwasunit.

**Zhigwa geget dagwishinoon anishinaabe’ zhaagwazonid.**

*In a while she truly came back home with some people hanging from her belt.*

Zhigwa geget dagwishinoon (in a while truly she arrived) anishinaabe’ zhaagwazonid (with people hanging from her belt — \*the same sash-carrying stem; flagged\*).

(13) “Nạskä, kīwâbạmāna änātisit?”

**“Naske, gii-waabamaa na enaadizid?”**

*“Look\! do you see what her nature is?”*

“Naske, gii-waabamaa na enaadizid?” (“look\! do you see how she behaves / what her nature is?”)

(14) Cigwa mādcāwạn cigwa gägä‘t uwīcāmigōn u‘tāngwäyạn. “Ambätạganā\! mạnisätā\!” udigōn īni’ᵘ u‘tāngwäyạn. Ānīc mīsa’ cigwa kī‘kīci‘tāwāt cigwa mīnawā uwīcāmigōn: “Ambätạganā\! papātaci‘kätā\! O∙o∙mā gwaya‘k gagwānisạgiminwändāgwạt, mīnīni‘ku ändaci∙o∙tạminoyān,” udigōn.

**Zhigwa maajaawan zhigwa geget owiizhaamigoon otaangweyan. “Ambe daga naa\! manisedaa\!” odigoon iniw otaangweyan. Aaniish mii sa zhigwa gii-giizhi’taawaad zhigwa miinawaa owiizhaamigoon: “Ambe daga naa\! babaa-odamikedaa\! O’omaa gwayak gagwaanisagi-minwendaagwad, mii nini go ko endazhi-odaminoyaan,” odigoon.**

*When (the man) departed, (the maiden) was invited by her friend saying: “Please, come hither\! let us go gather fire-wood\!” she was told by her friend. So accordingly, when they were done with their work, then again was the woman asked: “Please, come hither\! let us go wander about in play\! Straight off in this direction is an exceedingly pleasant place, it is there that I usually amuse myself in play,” she was told.*

Zhigwa maajaawan zhigwa geget owiizhaamigoon otaangweyan (when he had gone, truly she was invited by her companion). “Ambe daga naa\! manisedaa\!” (“please come\! let us gather firewood\!”) odigoon iniw otaangweyan (she was told by her companion). Aaniish mii sa zhigwa gii-giizhi’taawaad (so when they had finished their work) zhigwa miinawaa owiizhaamigoon (again she was invited): “Ambe daga naa\! babaa-odamikedaa\! (“please come\! let us go about playing\! — \*babaa-odamike- ‘wander in play’ flagged\*) O’omaa gwayak gagwaanisagi-minwendaagwad (right over this way is a wonderfully pleasant place), mii nini go ko endazhi-odaminoyaan,” odigoon (it is there that I always amuse myself,” she was told).

(15) Mīdạc kägä‘t cigwa kuniginīn wäwäbisun agōtänig. “ ‘Auwisạnā\! o∙o∙mā kädạcōtạminoyạnk tciwäwäbisoyạnk.” Mīsa’ kägä‘t, “ ‘Auwisạnā, kīn ni‘tam\!” udigōn. Mīsa’ änāt: “Kāwīn, nīn ni‘tamina ima wäwäbisoyān?
