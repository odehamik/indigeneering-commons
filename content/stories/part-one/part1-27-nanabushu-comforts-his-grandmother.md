27\. NĀNABUSHU COMFORTS HIS GRANDMOTHER.

<span class="l-fiero">**Nenabozh comforts his grandmother**</span>

Nanabushu Comforts His Grandmother

<span class="l-eng">*Narrated by Midaasoganzh (probable) — Part I Series 2 (Nos. 19–32), Bois Fort, per Corbiere roster; not printed on the page — verify.*</span>

<span class="l-fiero">***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1917).***</span>

<span class="l-orig">(1) Ningutingsa anipapimosät cigwasa udäbābạndān ö‘kumisạn kāpi∙u∙ntcikạnā‘pạn. Kägä‘t käyābi kī∙a∙‘täni utcimānini. Kägä‘tsa minwäntạm Nänabuc tayōc ‘iⁱi‘ᵘ pimādisinit. Ạnigu∙pītä∙i∙tāg mạdwämawiwạn: “Nōjis\!” mạdwä∙i∙nạtämowạn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Ningoding sa ani-babimosed gziwa sa odaababandaan ookomisan gaa-bi-onji-\*ginaaban\*. Geget gayaabi gii-ate-ni odjiimaanini. Geget sa minwendam Nenabozh \*dayoozh\* i’iw bimaadizinid. Anigo bi-de-i-taag madwe-mawiwan: “Noojis\!” madwe-inadamowan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Once, while travelling along, he came in sight of where he had left his grandmother. To be sure, there still was her canoe. Truly pleased was Nänabushu that she was still alive. When he went up from the shore, at that moment he caught the sound of her voice crying: “O my dear grandchild\!” was the sound of her voice crying.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Ningoding sa (once) ∙ ani-babimosed (as he went walking along) ∙ gziwa sa odaababandaan (then he came in sight of it) ∙ ookomisan (his grandmother) ∙ gaa-bi-onji-ginaaban (the place she had come from / where he had left her) ∙ geget gayaabi (truly still) ∙ gii-ate-ni odjiimaanini (her canoe was there, hers) ∙ geget sa minwendam Nenabozh (truly glad was Nenabozh) ∙ dayoozh i’iw bimaadizinid (that she was still living) ∙ anigo bi-de-i-taag (at that moment he caught the sound of it coming) ∙ madwe-mawiwan (the sound of her crying) ∙ “Noojis\!” (“My grandchild\!”) ∙ madwe-inadamowan (the sound of her saying it).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(2) ∙i∙nạtämowạn. Ạnida‘pābit ‘iⁱi‘ᵘ ändạnsiwit, kuniginin, kicingicinōn, ämi‘kwān kigicinōn. Nänabucu äjikạnōnāt: “No‘kō, nintạgwicin.” Kā‘kwābatawạngā∙i∙gänit ‘iⁱi‘ᵘ utämi‘kwānini upi∙a∙pạgitōni. “Cicī, mạtci∙a∙nimōg\! Ningīgagwänisạgänimäg igi‘ᵘ mạtciwâbicäcīwicạg wīnanāpagänsumiwāt.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**inadamowan. Ani-dabaabid i’iw endaansiwid, goniginin, gizhingishinoon, ami’kwaan gizhingishinoon. Nenabozh ezhi-ganoonaad: “Nookoo, nindagwishin.” Gaa-gwaabadawangaa-i-genid i’iw odami’kwaanini obi-a-bagidoonii. “Zhizhii, maji-aaninim\! Ningii-gagwaanisaganimaag igi’iw maji-waabizheshiiwizhig wiinanaabaagenizomiwaad.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*When he went up (and) peeped into her little dwelling, lo, there she lay, by a spoon she lay. Nänabushu then addressed her, saying: “O my grandmother\! I am come.” Dipping up some ashes with her spoon, she threw them toward (the speaker). “Begone, vile creature\! I thoroughly loathe those wicked martins that wish to sadden me.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">inadamowan (the sound of her saying it) ∙ ani-dabaabid (as he peeped in) ∙ i’iw endaansiwid (into her little dwelling) ∙ goniginin (lo, behold) ∙ gizhingishinoon (there she lay) ∙ ami’kwaan gizhingishinoon (by a spoon she lay) ∙ Nenabozh ezhi-ganoonaad (Nenabozh then spoke to her) ∙ “Nookoo, nindagwishin” (“My grandmother, I have arrived”) ∙ gaa-gwaabadawangaa-i-genid (when she scooped up ashes) ∙ i’iw odami’kwaanini (with her spoon) ∙ obi-a-bagidoonii (she threw it toward him) ∙ “Zhizhii, maji-aaninim\!” (“Begone, bad creature\!”) ∙ ningii-gagwaanisaganimaag (I greatly detest them) ∙ igi’iw maji-waabizheshiiwizhig (those bad martins) ∙ wiinanaabaagenizomiwaad (who would sadden me).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(3) Äjikạnōnāt mīnawā: “Nō‘kō, kägä‘t kuca nintạgwicin.” Nänabucu ōmā äni∙i∙jipīndigät; udani∙u∙di‘tinān. “Nō‘kō, kägä‘t kucagu nintạgwicin.” Wägunänīwinän wäwänigu udābi‘tōtcīnān, ā‘pidcigu wīnisiwạn, kayä imā uski‘cigunink mi‘tāwangōwiwạn. Mīdạc äjisāgisi∙ā∙t Nänabucu ö‘kumisạn, ạgạmīng ijiwināt. “Nō‘kō, intawā misawä kabingwäcägitōbinin.” Kägä‘t wawīngä ugi‘tcikunayä∙ā∙n; äcikisīyābāwạnāt, ā‘pidcigu upini∙ā∙n. Kā‘pīni∙ā∙t äji∙i∙jät äntāt. Ka‘kina ucki∙a∙yi∙i∙n ubīsi‘kōnān. “Nō‘kō, mī∙i∙‘ᵘ icikiwätā imā äntänsiyạng.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Ezhi-ganoonaad miinawaa: “Nookoo, geget gosha nindagwishin.” Nenabozh oomaa ani-i-zhi-biindiged; odani-o-di’tinaan. “Nookoo, geget gosha go nindagwishin.” Wegonen iiwinen wewenigo odaabi’toojiinaan, aabidig go wiinizhiwan, gaye imaa oshki-zhigonink mitaawangoowiwan. Mii dash ezhi-saagizi-aad Nenabozh ookomisan, agamiing izhiwinaad. “Nookoo, indawaa misawaa ga-bingweshegitoobinin.” Geget wawiinge ogii-jiigonayaa’an; ezhi-gizii-zaabaawanaad, aabidig go obini-aan. Gaa-bini-aad ezhi-i-zhaad endaad. Gakina oshki-a-ya’iin obiskonaan. “Nookoo, mii-i-iw izhi-giiwetaa imaa endaansiyang.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Then he spoke to her again, saying: “My grandmother, in reality have I come.” Nänabushu at that moment went on in; he went over and took hold of her. “My grandmother, in reality have I come.” At last he put his arms tenderly around her waist, and she was very unclean, and there in her eyes was sand. Thereupon out of doors Nänabushu took his grandmother, to the shore was where he took her. “My grandmother, therefore entirely of all your clothes will I strip you.” Really of every single piece of clothing he stripped her; then by washing he made her clean, and very clean he made her. After he had made her clean, then he went home. With clothes all new he dressed her. “My grandmother, now let us return to the little place where we dwell.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Ezhi-ganoonaad miinawaa (he spoke to her again) ∙ “Nookoo, geget gosha nindagwishin” (“Grandmother, truly indeed I have come”) ∙ Nenabozh oomaa ani-i-zhi-biindiged (Nenabozh then went on inside here) ∙ odani-o-di’tinaan (he went over and took hold of her) ∙ “Nookoo, geget gosha go nindagwishin” (“Grandmother, truly indeed I have come”) ∙ wegonen iiwinen (whatever it was) ∙ wewenigo odaabi’toojiinaan (carefully he put his arms about her waist) ∙ aabidig go wiinizhiwan (and she was very dirty) ∙ gaye imaa oshki-zhigonink (and there in her eyes) ∙ mitaawangoowiwan (was sand) ∙ mii dash ezhi-saagizi-aad (and so then he took her out) ∙ Nenabozh ookomisan (Nenabozh, his grandmother) ∙ agamiing izhiwinaad (to the shore he took her) ∙ “Nookoo, indawaa misawaa ga-bingweshegitoobinin” (“Grandmother, therefore I will strip all your clothes off you”) ∙ geget wawiinge ogii-jiigonayaa’an (truly entirely he undressed her) ∙ ezhi-gizii-zaabaawanaad (then by washing he cleaned her) ∙ aabidig go obini-aan (and very clean he made her) ∙ gaa-bini-aad (after he had cleaned her) ∙ ezhi-i-zhaad endaad (then he went home) ∙ gakina oshki-a-ya’iin obiskonaan (with all new things he dressed her) ∙ “Nookoo, mii-i-iw izhi-giiwetaa imaa endaansiyang” (“Grandmother, now let us go back there to the little place where we dwell”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(4) Misa‘ kätagwicinuwat ändawat uganōnigōn ō‘kumisạn: “Nōcis, kāwin win nimpa‘kạdäsi. Maga‘kuckwämạg päjik nạnta wâbạntcikän, mī∙i∙māⁿ tcimi‘kạmạn wänicicing wīsiniwin.” Nänabucu ugä‘kikipitōn ‘iⁱi‘ᵘ mạga‘kuckwämạg. Ä‘tiwä, änīn kä∙i∙cinạnk picicig ‘iⁱi‘ᵘ pimitä kayä wīyās\! “Nōko, kägä‘tigu kīgaminowīsinimin.” Nänabucu mīsạnā i‘ᵘ pitcinạg minowīsinit.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii sa’ gaa-dagwishinowaad endawaad oganoonigoon ookomisan: “Noozhis, gaawiin wiin nimbakadesii. Makakoshkwemag bezhig nanda-waabanjigen, mii-i-imaa ji-mikaman wenizhishing wiisiniwin.” Nenabozh ogii-gizhi-gibidoon i’iw makakoshkwemag. Aatiwe, aaniin gaa-i-zhinang bizhizhig i’iw bimide gaye wiiyaas\! “Nookoo, geget igo gii-ga-mino-wiisinimin.” Nenabozh mii sa naa iw biijinaag mino-wiisinid.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And so when they were come at their home, he was addressed by his grandmother, saying: “My grandchild, I am not hungry myself. In one of the birch-bark boxes do you search, for there will you find some food that is nice.” Nänabushu ripped open the birch-bark box. My\! what should he behold but a vast store of grease and meat\! “My grandmother, in truth, shall we be well supplied with food.” Nänabushu then for the first time in a long while ate a hearty meal.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Mii sa’ gaa-dagwishinowaad endawaad (and so when they came to where they dwell) ∙ oganoonigoon ookomisan (he was spoken to by his grandmother) ∙ “Noozhis, gaawiin wiin nimbakadesii” (“My grandchild, I am not hungry myself”) ∙ makakoshkwemag bezhig (in one of the birch-bark boxes) ∙ nanda-waabanjigen (do you search) ∙ mii-i-imaa ji-mikaman (for there you will find) ∙ wenizhishing wiisiniwin (food that is good) ∙ Nenabozh ogii-gizhi-gibidoon (Nenabozh ripped it open) ∙ i’iw makakoshkwemag (that birch-bark box) ∙ Aatiwe\! (My\!) ∙ aaniin gaa-i-zhinang (what should he behold) ∙ bizhizhig i’iw bimide gaye wiiyaas (a great heap of that grease and meat) ∙ “Nookoo, geget igo gii-ga-mino-wiisinimin” (“Grandmother, truly indeed we shall eat well”) ∙ Nenabozh mii sa naa iw biijinaag mino-wiisinid (Nenabozh then for the first time in a long while ate a good meal).</span>
