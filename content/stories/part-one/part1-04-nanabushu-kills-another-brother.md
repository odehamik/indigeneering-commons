<span class="l-fiero">**4. Naanabozho Kills Another Brother.**</span>

4\. NANABUSHU KILLS ANOTHER BROTHER.

<span class="l-eng">*Narrated by Waasaagoneshkang (Part I Series 1, Bois Fort — per Corbiere roster; not printed on these pages, verify)*</span>

<span class="l-fiero">***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1917).***</span>

<span class="l-orig">(1) Na, mīsa kā·i·jictcigät ‘aᵋa′ᵘ Nänabucu. Na, mīdạc cigwa kī·a·ndawâbamāt īni′ᵘ o‘kumisạn. Mīdạc mīnawa kī·a·wītci·a·yāwāt. Nigutīngigu mīnawā kā·i·ci·i·‘kitut ‘iᵋi′ᵘ wīnạndunäwāt īni′ᵘ päji‘k wītcikiwäyạn, ‘oᵋō′dạc ugī·i·gōn īni′ᵘ ō‘kumisạn: “Kāwīn kidānisāsī,” ugī·i·gōn īni′ᵘ ō‘kumisạn. “Pā‘tanīnōwan ‘iᵋi′ᵘ känawänimigut. Mīdạc kä·u·ndcikaskitōsiwạmbạn. Nạckäguta kīgawīndạmōn kä·i·cinạmạmbạbạn misawā icāyạn. Ānīc minising ayā ‘aᵋa′ᵘ uka‘kwạn ‘aᵋa′ᵘ tcāgīga·ạ·nk. Nackädạc, ō·o·māⁿ nāwiki‘tcigạmi pīgi′ᵘ ta‘kạmakuntcin. Kāwīn kitākạckitōsin tcikapi‘kawạtipan misawā tcīmān ānu·a·yōyạn; mīgō·i·māⁿ ka‘kina käjāku‘kät kitcīmāning.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Na, miisa gaa-izhichiged ’a’aw Naanabozho. Na, miidash zhigwa gii-andawaabamaad iniw ookomisan. Miidash miinawaa gii-awiijiiayaawaad. Ningodingigo miinawaa gaa-izhi-ikidod ’i’iw wii-nandonewaad iniw bezhig wiijikiwenyan, ’o’oodash ogii-igoon iniw ookomisan: “Gaawiin gidaa-nisaasii,” ogii-igoon iniw ookomisan. “Baataniinoowan ’i’iw genawenimigod. Miidash gaa-ondazhikaskitoosiwaambaan. Nashkeguda giga-wiindamoon ge-izhi-namaambaaban miizhewaa izhaayan. Aaniish minising ayaa ’a’aw oka’kwan ’a’aw zhaagiigaa’ank. Nashkedash, oomaa naawi-gichigami biigiw da-akamagondin. Gaawiin gidaa-gashkitoosiin ji-gabi’kawadiban miizhewaa jiimaan anawi-aayooyan; miigo-iimaa gakina gezhaagoo’ked gijiimaaning.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Such, therefore, was what Nänabushu did. So accordingly he went to seek his grandmother. Thereupon again he went and staid with her. On another occasion when he said that he intended to go and look for another of his brothers, then this was he told by his grandmother: “You would not be able to kill him,” he was told by his grandmother. “Many are they under whose watch he is kept. That is the reason why it will be impossible for you to succeed. Give ear, and I will declare to you what you are likely to see should you by chance happen to go. Now, upon an island abides he that hews upon his shin. And listen\! Out across the middle of this sea some pitch extends, floating upon the water. Not will you succeed in passing (by that place), even though you should try to make use of a canoe; there on every part of your canoe will (the pitch) stick. So that is one of the things which you will see.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">miisa gaa-izhichiged Naanabozho (that was what Nanabushu did); miidash zhigwa gii-andawaabamaad iniw ookomisan (then he went to look for his grandmother); miinawaa gii-awiijiiayaawaad (again he went and stayed with her); ningodingigo miinawaa gaa-izhi-ikidod wii-nandonewaad iniw bezhig wiijikiwenyan (one time he said he wanted to look for one of his brothers); ogii-igoon iniw ookomisan (his grandmother told him): “gaawiin gidaa-nisaasii” (you could not kill him); “baataniinoowan genawenimigod” (many are those who watch over him); gaa-ondazhikaskitoosiwaambaan (that is why you could not do it); nashkeguda giga-wiindamoon ge-izhi-namaambaaban miizhewaa izhaayan (listen, I will tell you what you would see if you go); aaniish minising ayaa oka’kwan zhaagiigaa’ank (on an island lives the one who hews on his shin); nashkedash oomaa naawi-gichigami biigiw da-akamagondin (out in the middle of the sea pitch lies floating on the water); gaawiin gidaa-gashkitoosiin ji-gabi’kawadiban anawi jiimaan aayooyan (you could not get past it even with a canoe); miigo-iimaa gakina gezhaagoo’ked gijiimaaning (it would stick all over your canoe).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(2) Nā, mī′·i·ᵘ ābitink kä·i·jinạmạn. Mīnawā pạpīmickaiyạn misawā ‘iᵋimāⁿ anigạcki·o·yạn, mīnawā kägō kīgawâbandān, cingwā‘k kīga·i·nändạm. Mīdạc ‘aᵋa′ᵘ kiˢtci·ō·gā ōmi ˢtcigā käwābạmimạt. Kāwīn dạc pō‘tc kitākạckitōsin misawā ānawīwīmāckawạt. Na, mīsa i·i·māⁿ ābitink ‘iᵋi′ᵘ sạnạgusiwin ändaguk. Misawā mīnawa i·i·mā ạnikạckioyạn, kumā′ tacigu a‘pī pạpimickaiyạn kīgạtäbābandān i·i·mā ayāt. Mīdạcigu päcuwābạndạmạn, kīgawābamāg wäᵋwäg tcibā‘ta·ī·nowāt.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Na, mii’iw aabiding ge-izhi-namaan. Miinawaa babiimishkaayan miizhewaa ’i’imaa anigashki’ooyan, miinawaa gegoo giga-waabandaan, zhingwaak giga-inendam. Miidash ’a’aw gichi-ogaa oomi-zhichigaa ge-waabamimad. Gaawiindash boozh gidaa-gashkitoosiin miizhewaa anawi-wii-maashkawad. Na, miisa i’imaa aabiding ’i’iw zanagisiwin endagwak. Miizhewaa miinawaa i’imaa anigashki’ooyan, gomaa dashigo apii babiimishkaayan giga-debaabandaan i’imaa ayaad. Miidashigo besho-waabandaman, giga-waabamaag wewe ji-baataa’iinowaad.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And as you go paddling along, should you by chance succeed in passing in your canoe, then something else will you behold, some pines you will think them to be. That is the Great Pike whose big fins upon the back you will see. Not at all will you succeed if you wish to pass around. Therefore that is one of the difficult places lying in (your way). Should you again succeed in passing the place with your canoe, and then go paddling on a certain distance, you will come in sight of the place where he is. And as you approach the place, you will see some swans that will be in a swarm.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">mii’iw aabiding ge-izhi-namaan (that is one thing you will see); miinawaa babiimishkaayan miizhewaa anigashki’ooyan (and as you paddle along, if you get past); miinawaa gegoo giga-waabandaan, zhingwaak giga-inendam (you will see something, you will think it is pines); miidash ’a’aw gichi-ogaa (that is the great pike); ge-waabamimad (whose big back-fins you will see); gaawiindash gidaa-gashkitoosiin anawi-wii-maashkawad (you could not pass around it); miisa i’imaa zanagisiwin endagwak (that is a hard place there); gomaa apii babiimishkaayan giga-debaabandaan i’imaa ayaad (when you paddle a certain way you will sight where he is); besho-waabandaman, giga-waabamaag wewe ji-baataa’iinowaad (as you come near you will see swans crowded together).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(3) Mīdạc kāwīn kītākackitōsīn mīnawā ānawi wīmāckawạtwā. Mī·i′·ᵘ känawänimigut ‘aᵋa′ᵘ wânantunäwạt. Wo·ō·widạc kīgạtinā waᵋa′ᵘ wäᵋwä awägwän ni‘tạm känōntagusigwän: “Cä\!” kigạtinā. “Nạckä aᵘ mäckwạt wâbisī‘pin,” kiga·i·nā. Mīdạc kä·i·jinīminamawạt o·ō·dac ta·i·‘kitō waᵋa′ᵘ wäᵋwä: “Nima‘kamig īni′ᵘ wâbisipînīn,” ‘aᵋa′ᵘ wäᵋwä tā·i·‘kitō. Mîsawādạc mīnawā i·i·mā anikạcki·o·yạn i·i·māⁿ mīnawā ābiting käyābi. Mīdạc cigwa tcigī‘kabaiyạn i·i·māⁿ ayāt. Mīdạc ạnigu‘pīyạn tīndīsiwạn nōndāgusinit. Tạbītciba‘tō, o·ō·dạc kīgạtcictcigä; mi‘tigumin kīganīminamawā. Mīdạc kä·i·kitut: “Nima‘kạmig ‘i′iᵘ mi‘tigumin ‘aᵋa′ᵘ tīndīsi tai‘kito.” Mīdạc mīnawā i·i·mā tcīpitagwicing ‘aᵋa′wisa i·i·mā äyāt minising.” O·ō·widac ogī·i·gōn īni′ᵘ ō‘kumisạn: “Wäntcita nāwiminis tā ‘aᵋa′ᵘ nändawâbạmạt. Wä‘kwāgān ‘iᵋi′ᵘ ändāt.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash gaawiin gidaa-gashkitoosiin miinawaa anawi-wii-maashkawadwaa. Mii’iw genawenimigod ’a’aw waa-nandonewad. Wa’oowidash giga-dinaa wa’aw wewe awegwen niitam ge-noondaagozigwen: “Zhe\!” giga-dinaa. “Nashke aw mashkwad waabizii’pin,” giga-inaa. Miidash ge-izhi-niiminamawad o’oodash da-ikido wa’aw wewe: “Nimakamig iniw waabizibiniin,” ’a’aw wewe daa-ikido. Miisawaadash miinawaa i’imaa anigashki’ooyan i’imaa miinawaa aabiding geyaabi. Miidash zhigwa ji-giiwgabaayan i’imaa ayaad. Miidash anigopiiyan diindiisiwan noondaagozinid. Dabiijibatoo, o’oodash giga-jichigaa; mitigomin giga-niiminamawaa. Miidash ge-ikidod: “Nimakamig ’i’iw mitigomin ’a’aw diindiisi,” da-ikido. Miidash miinawaa i’imaa ji-bidagwishing ’a’awisa i’imaa eyaad minising.” Wa’oowidash ogii-igoon iniw ookomisan: “Wenjida naawi-minis daa ’a’aw nandawaabamad. Wekwaagaan ’i’iw endaad.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And now you will not succeed again, even though it be your wish to pass round them. So in the keeping of these is he whom you wish to seek. Now, this is what you will say to the Swan, whichsoever shall be the first to make itself heard: ‘Hist\!’ you shall say to it. ‘Look, here is in payment a white potato,’ you shall say to it. Thereupon as you offer it (the potato), this is what the Swan will say: ‘He is taking from me the white potato,’ the Swan will say. And if by chance again through that place you succeed in passing with your canoe, there will still remain one other (difficulty), and it will be where you go ashore there where he is. And when you go up from the shore, a Bluejay will be heard calling. He will come running hitherward, and this is what you shall do: an acorn shall you offer to it. And this is what it will say: ‘He is taking the acorn from me,’ the Bluejay will say. So then next to the place will come he who abides there on the island.” And this was what he was told by his grandmother: “In the very centre of the island dwells the one whom you seek. At one end of it is his home.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">miidash gaawiin gidaa-gashkitoosiin anawi-wii-maashkawadwaa (you will not be able to go around them); mii’iw genawenimigod waa-nandonewad (in their keeping is the one you seek); wa’aw wewe awegwen niitam ge-noondaagozigwen, giga-dinaa: “zhe\!” (to whichever swan is heard first you will say: hist\!); “nashke aw mashkwad waabizii’pin” (look, here in payment is a white potato); ge-izhi-niiminamawad, da-ikido wewe: “nimakamig iniw waabizibiniin” (as you offer it the swan will say: he is taking the white potato from me); miisawaa miinawaa anigashki’ooyan, geyaabi aabiding (if you get past again, there is still one more); zhigwa ji-giiwgabaayan i’imaa ayaad (when you go ashore where he is); anigopiiyan diindiisiwan noondaagozinid (as you go up a bluejay will be heard); dabiijibatoo (it will come running); mitigomin giga-niiminamawaa (you will offer it an acorn); da-ikido: “nimakamig mitigomin” diindiisi (the bluejay will say: he is taking the acorn from me); miidash ji-bidagwishing ’a’awisa eyaad minising (then the one who lives on the island will come near); ogii-igoon ookomisan: “wenjida naawi-minis daa nandawaabamad” (his grandmother told him: right in the middle of the island lives the one you seek); “wekwaagaan endaad” (at one end is his home).</span>

Kā·i·ji·u·ci‘tot ‘iᵋi′wisa pimitä ‘iᵋi′wisa pimitä mīgu iᵘ ka‘kina kā·i·cina·i·nạng ‘iᵋi′ᵘ wāy·ā·bạtci‘tōt. Mīnawa ugi·a·ndawâbamān wâbisīpinīn. A‘pī mīnawa kāmamāt īni′ᵘ wâbisīpinin. Mīnawā ogī·ạ·ndawâbandānạn īni′ᵘ mi‘tiguminạn. Ānīc mīi′ᵘ cayīgwā kī‘kījī‘tāt. Midạc a‘pī kīmādci‘tāt īni′ᵘ ūpi‘twa‘kōn kī·u·ji‘tōt. Midạc a‘pī kākīci‘tōt īni′ᵘ ūpi‘twa‘kōn, “Ānīc mīsa cigwa wīmādcāyān,” ogī·i·nān īni′ᵘ ō‘kumisạn. Wo·ō·widạc ugī·i·nān: “Ānīc misa wīnạntopạniyān,” ogī·i·nān īni′ᵘ ō‘kumisạn. <span class="l-fiero">**Na’, miidash apii gaa-maadazhi’taad ’i’iw gii-ozhi’tood ’i’iw ojiimaan. Apiidash gaa-giizhitood miinawaa gaa-izhi-nandawaabamaad niigaanigo waaninonid iniw awesiiyan. Gaa-izhi-ozhi’tood ’i’iwisa bimide miigo iw gakina gaa-izhinaaynang ’i’iw waa-aabajitood. Miinawaa ogii-andawaabamaan waabizibiniin. Apii miinawaa gaa-mamaad iniw waabizibinin. Miinawaa ogii-andawaabandaanan iniw mitigominan. Aaniish mii’iw zhayiigwa gii-giizhii’taad. Miidash apii gii-maadazhi’taad iniw obitwaakoon gii-ozhi’tood. Miidash apii gaa-giizhitood iniw obitwaakoon, “Aaniish miisa zhigwa wii-maajaayaan,” ogii-inaan iniw ookomisan. Wa’oowidash ogii-inaan: “Aaniish miisa wii-nandobaniyaan,” ogii-inaan iniw ookomisan. Wa’oowidash gii-ikido: “Niiyo-gon ningad-azhi’taa wa’oo ji-aazhawa’ooyaan wa’oo gichigami. Aaniish miigo-iw minik ge-anwaa’ting ’i’iw niiyo-gon,” gii-ikido ’a’aw Naanabozho.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*So thereupon he began upon the work of making his canoe. After he had finished it, he then sought for a game-being that was unsurpassingly fat. After he had made the oil, he then put away all that he expected to use. Next he sought for a white potato. After he had obtained the white potato, he next looked for the acorns. So therefore was he now fully prepared. And then was when he started on the work of making his arrows. Now, when he had finished making his arrows, “Now, therefore, I want to be off (to war),” he said to his grandmother. And this was what he said to her: “Four days I shall spend crossing this sea, for the length of time that it will be (calm will be) four days,” (so) said Nänabushu.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">miidash apii gaa-maadazhi’taad gii-ozhi’tood ojiimaan (then he began making his canoe); apiidash gaa-giizhitood miinawaa gaa-izhi-nandawaabamaad niigaanigo waaninonid awesiiyan (after finishing it he sought a game-animal that was the fattest); gaa-izhi-ozhi’tood bimide miigo gakina gaa-izhinaaynang waa-aabajitood (after making the oil he put away all he meant to use); ogii-andawaabamaan waabizibiniin (he looked for a white potato); apii gaa-mamaad iniw waabizibinin (after he got the white potato); ogii-andawaabandaanan mitigominan (he looked for acorns); mii’iw zhayiigwa gii-giizhii’taad (now he was ready); apii gii-maadazhi’taad obitwaakoon gii-ozhi’tood (then he began making his arrows); apii gaa-giizhitood obitwaakoon (when he finished his arrows): “miisa zhigwa wii-maajaayaan” (now I want to go), ogii-inaan ookomisan (he told his grandmother); “miisa wii-nandobaniyaan” (I want to go to war); gii-ikido: “niiyo-gon ningad-azhi’taa ji-aazhawa’ooyaan gichigami” (four days it will take me to cross this sea); “miigo minik ge-anwaa’ting niiyo-gon” (that long it will be calm, four days), gii-ikido Naanabozho.</span>

<span class="l-orig">(5) Na′, mīdạc cigwa kīmādcāt, kī‘pōzit ‘iᵋi′ᵘ utcīmān. Ningutingigu käᵉgä‘t pāpimickāt i·i·wisa kiˢtcigạmi cigwa gägä‘t ogīwâbạndān kägō ta‘kamakundänik. “Ānīc,” ogī·i·gōn īni′ᵘ ō‘kumisạn ‘oᵋō′ᵘ; “kāwīn pōtc kitākackitōsīn tciwīmāckạmạn” ogī·i·gōn īni′ᵘ ō‘kumisạn. A‘pī·i·dạc wädi‘tawāt īni′ᵘ pigiwạn, mīdạc kā·i·jipōsinank ‘iᵋi′ᵘ utcīmān kayä ‘iᵋi′ᵘ utạbwi; mīdạc kā·i·citcä‘kisät, mīsa kākạnạgä pạngī kī·a·gukäsīwạn i·i·mā utcīmāning kayä utạbwīng. Na′, mīdạc käᵉgä‘t wīwayība kīpōsinạnk ‘iᵋi′ᵘ utcīmān kayä ‘iᵋi′ᵘ utạbwi. Mīdạc äjikạckitōt kīgabi‘kawāt īni′ᵘ pigiwan. Mīdạc kā·i·jimādcī·u·t. Ningutingigu mīnawā pāpimickāt mī cigwa gägä‘t ogiwābamān cingwā‘kwan kī·i·nändạm. Ajigwa ubäcwâbạndān, kuninginīn mīwa′nini′ᵘ ki‘tci·ō·gāwan. O·ō·widạc ogī·i·nān a‘pī wädi‘tawāt: “Ambäsạnō kawā‘kun īgi′ᵘ kimistcigu‘t,” ugī·i·nān īni′ᵘ tci·ō·gāwạn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Na’, miidash zhigwa gii-maajaad, gii-boozid ’i’iw ojiimaan. Ningodingigo geget baabimishkaad i’iwisa gichigami zhigwa geget ogii-waabandaan gegoo da-akamagondenik. “Aaniish,” ogii-igoon iniw ookomisan ’o’oo; “gaawiin booch gidaa-gashkitoosiin ji-wii-maashkaman,” ogii-igoon iniw ookomisan. Apiidash wedi’tawaad iniw biigiwan, miidash gaa-izhi-boozinang ’i’iw ojiimaan gaye ’i’iw odabwi; miidash gaa-izhi-jaakizaad, miisa gaa-ganage bangii gii-agokesiiwan i’imaa ojiimaaning gaye odabwiing. Na’, miidash geget wii-wayiiba gii-boozinang ’i’iw ojiimaan gaye ’i’iw odabwi. Miidash ezhi-gashkitood gii-gabi’kawaad iniw biigiwan. Miidash gaa-izhi-maajii’ood. Ningodingigo miinawaa baabimishkaad mii zhigwa geget ogii-waabamaan zhingwaakwan gii-inendam. Azhigwa obeshwaabandaan, gonaginiin miiwa’ iniw gichi-ogaawan. Wa’oowidash ogii-inaan apii wedi’tawaad: “Ambeszanoo gawaakon igiw gimisjigot,” ogii-inaan iniw ogaawan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*So therefore he started away, he was in his canoe. Now, truly, once as he went journeying by canoe over that sea, he now indeed beheld something lying lengthwise upon the water across his way. “To be sure,” he had been told by his grandmother about this; “in no way will you succeed in passing round it,” he had been told by his grandmother. Now, when he was approaching the pitch, he then oiled his canoe and his paddle; and when he drove his canoe into (the pitch), not even a speck (of the pitch) was stuck to his canoe or his paddle. So it was true that every now and then he kept oiling his canoe and his paddle. So thus he succeeded in passing through the pitch. Thereupon he continued his way. And another time as he was journeying along in his canoe, he now truly saw some pines, he thought. Soon he got a near view, and, sure enough, it was the Great Pike.
