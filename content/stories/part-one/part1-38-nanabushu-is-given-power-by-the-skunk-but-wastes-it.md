38\. NÄNABUSHU IS GIVEN POWER BY THE SKUNK, BUT WASTES IT.

<span class="l-fiero">**NENABOZHO GII-MIINAA MASHKAWIZIIWIN ZHIGAAGAN, MIIDASH GAA-IZHI-BANAAJITOOD.**</span>

NÄNABUSHU IS GIVEN POWER BY THE SKUNK, BUT WASTES IT.

<span class="l-eng">*Narrated by Midaasoganzh (probable) — Part I Series 3 (Nos. 33–38), Bois Fort, per Corbiere roster; not printed on the page — verify.*</span>

<span class="l-fiero">***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1917).***</span>

<span class="l-orig">(1) Cigwaⁿ minawā mādcā bạbāmusät. Ningutingiku sāga∙i∙gạn umạdā‘kạn, owâbạmā ki‘kināntawānaᵉ¹ pata‘kisunit. Mīdạc änäntạnk: “Ạnicinābäg inangwạna ayāwạg,” inäntam. Animādcāt. Cigwa kägä‘t owâbandān twā∙i∙gạn wänta∙i∙pinit; mōsotcīt wätwā∙i∙bāninit, kägätsa miᵇtcä‘kwạtini.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Zhigwanh miinawaa maajaa babaamosed. Ningodingiko zaaga’igan omadaakan, owaabamaa gikinaandawaan’ batakizonid. Miidash enendang: “Anishinaabeg inange ayaawag,” inendam. Ani-maajaad. Zhigwa geget owaabandaan dwaa’igan wendi-binid; moozojiid\* wedwaa-baaninid, gegetsa mizhi-de’akwadini\*.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Soon again he was off travelling afoot. When once out upon the ice of a lake he came, he saw a balsam¹ standing. And this he thought: “Some people, no doubt, are living there,” he thought. On his way he continued. Presently he truly saw a hole (in the ice) from which they drew water; with the anal gut of a moose had they made the hole, exceedingly large was the vesicle.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Zhigwanh miinawaa (now once again), maajaa babaamosed (off he set out walking about). Ningodingiko (once) zaaga’igan omadaakan (he came out upon the ice of a lake), owaabamaa (he saw) gikinaandawaan’ (a balsam; \*“the one that spots the place,” see footnote\*) batakizonid (standing there). Miidash enendang (and so he thought): “Anishinaabeg inange ayaawag (people, it seems, are living here),” inendam (he thought). Ani-maajaad (on he went). Zhigwa geget (then truly) owaabandaan dwaa’igan (he saw a water-hole) wendi-binid (from which they were drawing water); moozojiid (the anal gut of a moose; \*moozo- + -jiid, gut\*) wedwaa-baaninid (was what they had used to make it), gegetsa (and truly) mizhi-de’akwadini (exceedingly large was it; \*reading uncertain\*).</span>

<span class="l-eng">*¹ Ki‘kināntawān, “balsam.” This is an old word seldom used in conversation; it means “the one that spots the place” (where water is). The more usual word is cingup, which, however, refers to any kind of fir-tree.*</span>

<span class="l-orig">(2) Kägätsa umisawinān. Äji∙o∙dā‘pinạng, mạdwäkạnōnigōn: “Ic, Nänabucu\! pōni‘tōn ’iᵉi’ᵘ. Kīgasạnagi∙ā∙,”¹ udigōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Gegetsa omizawinaan. Ezhi-odaapinang, madwe-ganoonigon: “Ish, Nenabozho\! booni’toon i’iw. Gigazanagi’aa\*,” odigoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Great was his desire for it. When he laid hands on it, he heard the voice of some one speaking to him: “Hold, Nänabushu\! do you let that alone. You will put us in want for another,”¹ he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Gegetsa omizawinaan (truly great was his desire for it). Ezhi-odaapinang (as he took hold of it), madwe-ganoonigon (he was spoken to — a voice was heard addressing him): “Ish, Nenabozho\! (Hold, Nenabozho\!) booni’toon i’iw (let that be). Gigazanagi’aa (you will make it hard for us / put us in want for another; \*see footnote\*),” odigoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-eng">*¹ Kīgasạnagi·ā·, “you will put us in want for another;” literally, “you will render it difficult” (to replace).*</span>

<span class="l-orig">(3) Mīsa’ gägä‘t intawā äjipagitînạnk. “Omä pijän\!” udigōn. Mīsa’ gägä‘t kāniciku‘pīt, cigwasa’ ugīgä∙i∙gōn, mīsa’ wīsinit. Ānawiwīskuntcigä Nänabucu. “Mīgu’ ga‘kina icimitcîn mi äji∙a∙caminān,” udigōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii sa geget indawaa ezhi-bagidinang. “Omaa biijaan\!” odigoon. Mii sa geget gaa-nizhigobiid\*, zhigwasa’ ogiigeyigoon\*, mii sa wiisinid. Aanawi-wiiskonjige Nenabozho. “Mii go gakina izhi-miijin mii ezhi-ashaminaan,” odigoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Thereupon truly he chose to leave it be. “Come hither\!” he was told. It was true that when he went up from the lake, then was he given food, (some of) the food. “Just you eat all that I have set before you,” he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Mii sa geget indawaa (and so truly, at last) ezhi-bagidinang (he let go of it). “Omaa biijaan\! (Come here\!)” odigoon (he was told). Mii sa geget (and so truly) gaa-nizhigobiid (when he came up from the lake; \*form uncertain\*), zhigwasa’ ogiigeyigoon (then he was given of the food\*), mii sa wiisinid (and so he ate). Aanawi-wiiskonjige Nenabozho (Nenabozho could hardly finish eating it all). “Mii go gakina izhi-miijin (just eat all of it) mii ezhi-ashaminaan (as I have set it before you),” odigoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(4) Mīsa’ gägä‘t ga‘kina äcimidcit. Uwâbạmān gägä‘t minditōwạn cigwa uganōnigōn. “Nänabucu, intigu kuca kipa‘kạdä.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii sa geget gakina ezhi-miijid. Owaabamaan geget minidoowan zhigwa oganoonigoon. “Nenabozho, indigo gosha gibakade.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And so actually the whole of it he ate. He saw really big was the one who now was speaking to him. “Nänabushu, you must surely be hungry.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Mii sa geget gakina (and so truly all of it) ezhi-miijid (he ate it up). Owaabamaan geget (he saw indeed) minidoowan (the big one) zhigwa oganoonigoon (and then he was addressed by it). “Nenabozho, indigo gosha gibakade (Nenabozho, surely you must be hungry).”</span>

<span class="l-orig">(5) “Kāwīn,” udinān.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Gaawiin,” odinaan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“No,” he said to him.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Gaawiin (No),” odinaan (he said to him).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(6) “Kāwīn, Nänabucu, kipa‘kadäsạgu. Kigi‘känimin pa‘kạdäyạn. Pạngīns kiwīcawänimin, wändci∙i∙ninān,” udigōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Gaawiin, Nenabozho, gibakadezago. Gigikenimin bakadeyan. Bangiins giwii-zhawenimin, wenji-ininaan,” odigoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Nay, Nänabushu, but you are really hungry. I know you are hungry. That a little mercy I may bestow on you, is my reason for speaking thus to you,” he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Gaawiin, Nenabozho, gibakadezago (Nay, Nenabozho, you surely are hungry). Gigikenimin bakadeyan (I know you, that you are hungry). Bangiins giwii-zhawenimin (a little I wish to bless/take pity on you), wenji-ininaan (that is why I say it to you),” odigoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(7) “Äyeᵉ, nistcīmistä, gägä‘t nimba‘kạdä,” udinān.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Aye’, nishiimishe, geget nimbakade,” odinaan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Yes, my younger brother, truly hungry am I,” he said to him.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Aye’ (Yes), nishiimishe (my dear younger sibling), geget nimbakade (truly I am hungry),” odinaan (he said to him).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(8) “Ānīc, mīsa’ cigwa’ tciki‘kino∙ạ∙mōnān kädicictcigäyạn,” udigōn. Ominigōn bibigwāns. “Mīsa’ ō kägābatci‘tōyạn,” udigōn. “Mīgu’ ō tci∙ạ∙nikīwäyạn tci∙ō∙ci‘tōt kimindimō∙i∙mic wä‘kwāgān; mānōgu taginōndä. Mīdạc kīkici‘tōt, oᵇō‘dac kiwiminin kä∙u∙ndcinanatwa igi’ᵘ käpindigäwät imā kiwä‘kwägäning. Mīgu’ iᵘ äciki‘kino∙ạ∙mōnān, oᵇō‘dac kīga∙i∙cictcigä,” udigōn. Mīnangwạna ini’ micicigạgwạn käganōnigut. “Nīcing tci∙a∙batci‘tōyạn kiwiminin iᵘ kä∙u∙ndcinanatwa,” udigōn. “Tcigwasa’ taga tcängitiyä∙ä∙cinān,” inäntạm ’aᵉa’ᵘ Nänabucu.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Aaniish, mii sa zhigwa ji-gikino’amoonaan ge-izhichigeyan,” odigoon. Ominigoon bibigwaans. “Mii sa o’o ge-aabaji’toyan,” odigoon. “Mii go o’o ji-ani-giiweyan ji-oozhi’tood gimindimooyimish\* waakwaagan\*; maanoo go da-ginoonde. Miidash gii-giizhi’tood, o’o idash giwiimin\* ge-ondinanatwaa igiw ge-biindigewaad imaa giwaakwaagaaning. Mii go iw ezhi-gikino’amoonaan, o’o idash giga-izhichige,” odigoon. Miinange iniw mizhi-zhigaagwan\* ge-ganoonigod. “Niizhing ji-aabaji’toyan giwiimin iw ge-ondinanatwaa,” odigoon. “Zhigwasa’ daga ji-jaangidiyeshinaan\*,” inendam a’aw Nenabozho.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Well, therefore then will I teach you what you shall do,” he was told. He was given a small flute. “Now, this is what you shall use,” he was told. “Now, this is what when you go back home you shall make a long lodge of yours; let it be, oh, a long one. And when she has finished it, then this do I wish to give you, so that with it you may kill them that come into your long lodge. So accordingly as I instruct you, thus shall you do,” he was told. It happened to be the Big Skunk that was addressing him. “I intend to give you the means of using twice what you are to use in killing them,” he was told. “Then go you down upon your hands and knees,” Nänabushu was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Aaniish (Well now), mii sa zhigwa (it is now time) ji-gikino’amoonaan (that I teach you) ge-izhichigeyan (what you are to do),” odigoon (he was told). Ominigoon bibigwaans (he is given a little flute). “Mii sa o’o ge-aabaji’toyan (this is what you shall use),” odigoon (he was told). “Mii go o’o ji-ani-giiweyan (so when you go back home) ji-oozhi’tood gimindimooyimish (your old woman is to build; \*form uncertain\*) waakwaagan (a long lodge; \*form uncertain\*); maanoo go da-ginoonde (no matter how long it be). Miidash gii-giizhi’tood (and when she has finished it), o’o idash giwiimin (this then I give you) ge-ondinanatwaa (the means to slay them with) igiw ge-biindigewaad (those that will come inside) imaa giwaakwaagaaning (there in your long lodge). Mii go iw ezhi-gikino’amoonaan (this is what I am teaching you), o’o idash giga-izhichige (and this you shall do),” odigoon (he was told). Miinange iniw mizhi-zhigaagwan (it was that very Skunk) ge-ganoonigod (that was speaking to him). “Niizhing ji-aabaji’toyan (twice you shall use it) giwiimin iw (this I give you) ge-ondinanatwaa (the means to slay them with),” odigoon (he was told). “Zhigwasa’ daga ji-jaangidiyeshinaan (now then, let me turn-the-rear/discharge at you; \*form uncertain — Layer 1 reads inendam ‘he thought,’ where Jones renders ‘Nänabushu was told’\*),” inendam a’aw Nenabozho (thought Nenabozho).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(9) Ō∙ᵉo∙dac ugī∙i∙gōn: “Ạmbäsinō, iyangwāmisin, Nänabucu,” ugī∙i∙gōn; “kīga∙i∙nigā∙ā∙g kinitcānisạg,” kī∙i∙nā. “Naskä-guta kä∙i∙cictcigäyạn ki∙ạ∙nitạgwicinạn äntāyạn; käjinōndāgwamo‘tōyạn oᵇō’ᵘ kibabigwạn, mīdạcigu käcipīndigäwät mōsōg ’iᵉi’ᵘ kiwä‘kwāgān. Nībawạdạcigu pīndigäwät, ’oᵇō’ ta∙i∙cictcigäwạg, kiwi‘tācagāmäwạg i∙i∙mā tciwä‘kwāgāning. Mīsa’ pisāga∙ạ∙nk aᵘ nāgānīt, mī∙ạ∙‘pī käpōgitiyạn; tcipīndcipōgitiyạn ’iᵉi’ᵘ tciwä‘kwāgān. Mīsa’ iⁿ äjiki‘kino∙ạ∙mōnān,” udigōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**O’o idash ogii’igoon: “Ambesino, ayaangwaamizin, Nenabozho,” ogii’igoon; “giga-inigaa’aag ginijaanisag,” gii’inaa. “Naske go da izhichigeyan gii-ani-dagwishinan endaayan; ge-zhi-noondaagomo’toyan o’ow gibabigwan, miidash igo ge-zhi-biindigewaad moozoog i’iw giwaakwaagan. Niibewad idash igo biindigewaad, o’ow da-izhichigewag, giwii-daazhagaamewag imaa ji-waakwaagaaning. Mii sa bisaaga’ang aw niigaaniid, mii apii ge-boogidiyan; ji-biinjiboogidiyan i’iw ji-waakwaagan. Miidash gakina ge-izhi-niboowaad i’iwidi biinjik eyaawaad. Mii sa ji-waawiisiniyan. Miinawaa giga-aatamatwaa\*, miinawaa giga-noondaagamatawaag\*. Mii sa ji-waabaniziyan, gaawiin geyaabi gigabakadezii. Mii sa iw ezhi-gikino’amoonaan,” odigoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And this was what he was told: “Please be careful, Nänabushu,” he was told; “(else) you will do your children a hurt,” he was told. “Now, precisely this shall you do when you have come at the place where you live: you shall blow a tune upon this flute of yours, whereupon into your long lodge will come the moose. And after many have entered in, this they will do: they will walk round about inside of your long lodge. So when out comes the one in the lead, then is the time you shall break wind; (do it so) that you break wind into your long lodge. Thereupon shall die all that are there within. So then will you have plenty to eat. Then again shall you blow upon it for them, and again will you produce the sound for them. Consequently you will live through till morning (= the season), no more will you be hungry. That is what I teach you,” he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">O’o idash ogii’igoon (and this was what he was told): “Ambesino, ayaangwaamizin, Nenabozho (Come now, be careful, Nenabozho),” ogii’igoon (he was told); “giga-inigaa’aag ginijaanisag (you will bring harm on your children),” gii’inaa (he was told). “Naske go da izhichigeyan (now look, this you shall do) gii-ani-dagwishinan endaayan (when you have reached the place you live); ge-zhi-noondaagomo’toyan o’ow gibabigwan (you shall sound this flute of yours), miidash igo ge-zhi-biindigewaad moozoog i’iw giwaakwaagan (and then the moose will enter your long lodge). Niibewad idash igo biindigewaad (and when many of them have come in), o’ow da-izhichigewag (this they will do), giwii-daazhagaamewag imaa ji-waakwaagaaning (they will walk about there inside your long lodge). Mii sa bisaaga’ang aw niigaaniid (so when the one in the lead comes out), mii apii ge-boogidiyan (that is when you shall break wind); ji-biinjiboogidiyan i’iw ji-waakwaagan (that you break wind into the long lodge). Miidash gakina ge-izhi-niboowaad (and so all of them will die) i’iwidi biinjik eyaawaad (those there inside). Mii sa ji-waawiisiniyan (so then you will have plenty to eat). Miinawaa giga-aatamatwaa (again you will blow for them\*), miinawaa giga-noondaagamatawaag (again you will make the sound for them\*). Mii sa ji-waabaniziyan (so you will last till morning/through the season), gaawiin geyaabi gigabakadezii (no longer will you be hungry). Mii sa iw ezhi-gikino’amoonaan (that is what I teach you),” odigoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(10) Cigwasa animādcā Nänabucu, gägätsa’ wâwicäntam. Ningutingiku, anipapimusät, gägätsa’ ki‘tcimi‘tigōn owâbamān. “Kuniga indäbimigutuk nîcīmisa’ kāgī∙i∙cit\!” inändạm. “Taga, ningapōgitcinā,” inäntam ’aᵉa’ᵘ Nänabucu. <span class="l-fiero">**Zhigwasa ani-maajaa Nenabozho, gegetsa waawiizhendam. Ningodingiko, ani-babaamosed, gegetsa gichi-mitigoon owaabamaan. “Goonige indaabimigodog niizhiimisha gaa-gii-i’id\!” inendam. “Daga, ningaboogijinaa\*,” inendam a’aw Nenabozho. Mii sa geget ezhi-boogijinaad iniw gichi-mitigoon, mii go iw ezhi-biigishkizenid. “Mh, miinange geget debimid a’aw nishiimisha gaa-gii-i’id,” inendam.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Then upon his way started Nänabushu, truly very proud was he. By and by, while walking along, he saw an exceedingly large tree. “Wonder if my younger brother could be telling me the truth in what he said to me\!” he thought. “I say, I am going to break wind at it,” thought Nänabushu. Thereupon truly he broke wind at the big tree, accordingly he wrecked it completely. “Why, there really is no doubt but that my younger brother is telling me the truth in what he said to me,” he thought.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Zhigwasa ani-maajaa Nenabozho (then off started Nenabozho), gegetsa waawiizhendam (truly proud was he). Ningodingiko (once), ani-babaamosed (as he walked along), gegetsa gichi-mitigoon owaabamaan (he saw a really big tree). “Goonige indaabimigodog (wonder if he is telling me the truth) niizhiimisha gaa-gii-i’id (my younger brother, in what he said to me)\!” inendam (he thought). “Daga, ningaboogijinaa (now then, I will break wind at it\*),” inendam a’aw Nenabozho (thought Nenabozho). Mii sa geget ezhi-boogijinaad iniw gichi-mitigoon (and so truly he broke wind at that big tree), mii go iw ezhi-biigishkizenid (and so it was shattered to pieces). “Mh, miinange geget debimid (why, it is true he tells me true) a’aw nishiimisha gaa-gii-i’id (my younger brother, in what he said to me),” inendam (he thought).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(11) Mīsa’ papimusät ningutingiku, mīnawā owâbamān agāmatci’ ki‘tci∙a∙sinin abinit. “Taga’, kunigänata gägä‘t indäbimigut\!” inändam. “Taga, mīnawā, ningagutciā ’aᵉa’ᵘ ki‘tci∙a∙sin,” inäntam. <span class="l-fiero">**Mii sa babaamosed ningodingiko, miinawaa owaabamaan agaamaji\* gichi-asinin abinid. “Daga, goonige naa geget indaabimigod\!” inendam. “Daga, miinawaa, ningagojiaa a’aw gichi-asin,” inendam. Mii sa geget zhigwa miinawaa ezhi-boogijinaad; inaabid awaniban iniw gichi-asiniin.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*While walking about on another occasion, he saw a large rock over beyond a hill. “Now, wonder if really he told me the truth\!” he thought. “I say, once more I will make a test out on that great rock,” he thought. Thereupon truly did he break wind at it; when he looked, there was nothing left of the big rock.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Mii sa babaamosed ningodingiko (so as he walked about once), miinawaa owaabamaan (again he saw) agaamaji gichi-asinin abinid (a big rock lying over beyond a rise\*). “Daga, goonige naa geget indaabimigod (now, wonder if really he tells me true)\!” inendam (he thought). “Daga, miinawaa, ningagojiaa a’aw gichi-asin (now, again, I will put that big rock to the test),” inendam (he thought). Mii sa geget zhigwa miinawaa ezhi-boogijinaad (and so truly then again he broke wind at it); inaabid awaniban iniw gichi-asiniin (when he looked, that big rock was gone).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(12) Ānic, udạmạnisu‘tāgōn ini’ kācawänimigut. “Wägunä-niwinän Nänabucu wäni‘tānōndasi‘k inigā∙ā∙t unītcānisaᵉ\!”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Aaniish, odamanizo’taagoon iniw gaa-zhawenimigod. “Wegonen-iniwinen\* Nenabozho waani’taanoondazi’k inigaa’aad oniijaanisa’\!”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Now, the sound of (Nänabushu) doing this was heard by him who had taken pity on him. “How stupid of Nänabushu to bring disaster upon his children by not paying heed\!”*</span>
