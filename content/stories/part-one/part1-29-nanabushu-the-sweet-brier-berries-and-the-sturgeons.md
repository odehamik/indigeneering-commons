Nänabushu, the Sweet-Brier Berries, and the Sturgeons.

<span class="l-eng">*Narrated by Midaasoganzh (probable) — Part I Series 2 (Nos. 19–32), Bois Fort, per Corbiere roster; not printed on the page — verify.*</span>

<span class="l-fiero">***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1917).***</span>

<span class="l-orig">(1) Weyābạninik äjimādcāt Nänabushu, sāga∙i∙gạning odōdisā ‘iᵉi’ᵘ anicinābäᵉ; kuniginin, ininiwạn kayä wīwini; nīciwäᵉ kwīwisänsaᵉ, unidcānisini. Pājikidạc pa∙kān i‘kwäwạn ningutci imā tāwạn, ä∙pidcisa usāᵍi∙gōn ini’ᵘ ininiwạn. “Kägä∙tsa, Nänabushu, ambäsạ, wīwin imā aⁿ i‘kwä.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Weyaabaninig e-zhi-maajaad Nenabozho, zaaga’iganing odoodisaa’ i’iw anishinaabe’; gonowaa, ininiwan gaye wiiwini; niizhiwe’ gwiiwizhensa’, oniijaanisini. Bezhigidash ba’kaan i’kwewan ningoji imaa daawan, e-bizhishig osaagi’igoon iniw ininiwan. “Gegetsa, Nenabozho, ambe, wiiwin imaa a’aw i’kwe.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*When the morrow came, then off started Nänabushu; at a lake he came upon some people, behold, a man and his wife; there were two boys, their children. And there was one other woman who lived in another place, very much was she loved by the man. “To be sure, Nänabushu, come, do you take to wife the woman yonder.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Weyaabaninig e-zhi-maajaad Nenabozho (when morning came, off Nenabozho went) ∙ zaaga’iganing odoodisaa’ i’iw anishinaabe’ (at a lake he reached the people) ∙ gonowaa, ininiwan gaye wiiwini (behold, a man and his wife) ∙ niizhiwe’ gwiiwizhensa’, oniijaanisini (two boys, their children) ∙ bezhigidash ba’kaan i’kwewan ningoji imaa daawan (and one other woman dwelt somewhere there) ∙ e-bizhishig osaagi’igoon iniw ininiwan (greatly was she loved by the man) ∙ “Gegetsa, Nenabozho, ambe, wiiwin imaa a’aw i’kwe” (“indeed, Nenabozho, come, take that woman to wife”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(2) “Nicīmisā, kāwäsa nintā∙i∙cictcigāsī. Kāwīn pō∙tc kabạya∙i∙ nintāwiwisinạn kīcpin wīwiyān.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Nishiimishe, gaawesa ninda-izhichigesii. Gaawiin booch gabeya’ii ninda-wiiwisinaan giishpin wiiwiyaan.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“My little brother, I could not possibly do it. Not even for a brief period of time could I have her for a wife if I should marry her.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Nishiimishe, gaawesa ninda-izhichigesii (“my little brother, I could not do it) ∙ gaawiin booch gabeya’ii ninda-wiiwisinaan (not even for a while could I keep her) ∙ giishpin wiiwiyaan” (if I married her”).</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Nenabozho, maanoogo, debwetawishin.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Nänabushu, never you mind, but do as I tell you.”*</span>

“Nenabozho, maanoogo, debwetawishin” (“Nenabozho, never mind, do as I tell you”).

<span class="l-orig">(4) “∙Aⁿ, misa iⁿ täbwä∙tōnān.” Misa∙ kägä∙t widigämāt ini’ᵘ i‘kwäwạn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“A’aw, misa iw debwetoonaan.” Misa geget wiidigemaad iniw i’kwewan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Very well, then will I do as you say.” Thereupon truly he married the woman.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“A’aw, misa iw debwetoonaan” (“very well, that I will do as told”) ∙ misa geget wiidigemaad iniw i’kwewan (so truly he married the woman).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(5) Ānic, mī∙i‘∙ⁿ cigwa ạnitạgwāgininig; kayädạc päpa∙kān tāwạg, ānic mamawadisitiwạg ini’ᵘ ininiwạn. “Misa cigwa tcimādci∙i∙taiyank ‘iᵉiᵘwa tcinōtcigⁱⁿgōⁿ∙i∙wäyank.” Ānic kägä∙t ạjinōtcigⁱⁿgōⁿ∙i∙wäwāt, adi∙kạmägwäᵉ ki∙tcinībiwa unisāwäᵉ. Ānic ạdcidagōnāᵉku∙kāwạg. Ä∙pidcisa nībiwa unisāwäᵉ.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Aaniish, mii’iw zhigwa ani-dagwaagininig; gayedash ba’kaan daawag, aaniish mamawi-daawag iniw ininiwan. “Misa zhigwa ji-maajii-itaayang i’iwa ji-noojigiigoonyiwewang.” Aaniish geget e-zhi-noojigiigoonyiwewaad, adikamegwe’ gichi-niibiwa onisaawe’. Aaniish ajidagonaa’ko-gaawag. E-bizhishig niibiwa onisaawe’.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Now, by this time the autumn was come; and they dwelt apart from (the other family), but they visited back and forth with the man. “It is now time for us to set to work getting fish.” Now, to be sure, when they went to get fish, whitefish in great plenty they killed. Now they made a rack to hang them with head down. Ever so many they killed.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Aaniish, mii’iw zhigwa ani-dagwaagininig (now, by then the fall was coming on) ∙ gayedash ba’kaan daawag (and apart they dwelt) ∙ aaniish mamawi-daawag iniw ininiwan (yet they kept company with the man) ∙ “Misa zhigwa ji-maajii-itaayang i’iwa ji-noojigiigoonyiwewang” (“now it is time we set to fishing”) ∙ aaniish geget e-zhi-noojigiigoonyiwewaad (so truly when they fished) ∙ adikamegwe’ gichi-niibiwa onisaawe’ (whitefish in great plenty they took) ∙ ajidagonaa’ko-gaawag (they made a rack to hang them head-down) ∙ e-bizhishig niibiwa onisaawe’ (very many they killed).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(6) Ningutingigu äcikackạtininig ‘iᵉi‘mā ändạcikiⁿgōⁿ∙i∙kāwāt; misäᵉpạn kikackạtininig ‘iᵉi’ᵘ sāga∙i∙gạn. Abābic kākackạtininig, “Ambäsạnō,” i∙kito Nänabucu: “intawānā kīni∙tạmawā kīga∙a∙mwānānig īgiwa kīdạtcitcagōtākaniminānig.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Ningoding igo e-zhi-gashkadininig i’iw endazhi-giigoonyi-gaawaad; misa’pan gashkadininig i’iw zaaga’igan. A’pii gaa-gashkadininig, “Ambesanoo,” ikido Nenabozho: “indawaa giin’tamawaa giga-amwaanaanig igiw gidaji-jaagootaakaniminaanig.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And once after it had frozen over there where they were catching the fish; for the lake was now frozen. When it was frozen over, “Now then,” said Nänabushu: “let us first eat up these (fishes) of yours which we have hung up with head down.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Ningoding igo e-zhi-gashkadininig (and once when it froze over) ∙ i’iw endazhi-giigoonyi-gaawaad (where they had been taking fish) ∙ misa’pan gashkadininig i’iw zaaga’igan (so frozen was the lake) ∙ “Ambesanoo,” ikido Nenabozho (“now then,” said Nenabozho) ∙ “indawaa giin’tamawaa giga-amwaanaanig igiw (“let us first eat up those) ∙ gidaji-jaagootaakaniminaanig” (we have hung up to dry”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(7) Ānic, mīgu iⁿ gägä∙t äcictcigäwāt. Kumāgu ä∙pidcibibōninig äjigitạmāwāt, mīdạc ä∙ta wīnawā ugiⁿgōⁿ∙i∙miwa. Cigwasa mādcā ‘aᵉa’ᵘ inini; änitạgwicing wänāgucininig; ä∙tawā, kuniginin kā∙u∙ndcikusinigwän. Mīdạc awinini äcigạnōnāt ini’ᵘ wīwạn: “Mīmāwīni iⁿ kạnabạtc tcikitcinōntāyābạniciyạng. Intawāsa mīnōtc ningakīyusä.” Ānic kägä∙t äjikiyusät, kāwīnsa kägō uni∙tōsīn. Kägä∙t sạnạgatini; wī∙kā∙ku päjik pinawān upīnān. Ningutingigu kāwīn kạnạgä ōpīnāsin ini’ᵘ pinawạn. Misa’ kägä∙t äjipa∙kadāwāt. Intawādạc ugini∙ unạntunäwāwān. Mīsạna ‘iᵉi’ᵘ ugini∙ äjiạmwāwāt, wänkitci cigu kawạnāntạmōg.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Aaniish, mii go iw geget e-zhichigewaad. Gomaago e-bizhishigi-biibooninig e-zhi-gidamaawaad, miidash eta wiinawaa ogiigoonyimiwaa. Zhigwasa maajaa a’aw inini; ani-dagwishing wenaagoshininig; e-tawaa, gonowaa gaa-oonji-goozinigwen. Miidash a’aw inini e-zhi-ganoonaad iniw wiiwan: “Miimaawiini iw ganabaj ji-gichi-noondaayaabanizhiyang. Indawaasa miinooch ninga-giiyose.” Aaniish geget e-zhi-giiyosed, gaawiinsa gegoo oni’toosiin. Geget zanagadini; wii’kaa go bezhig binewan obiinaan. Ningoding igo gaawiin ganage obiinaasiin iniw binewan. Misa’ geget e-zhi-ba’kadewaad. Indawaadash ogini’ onandawewaawaan. Miisana i’iw ogini’ e-zhi-amwaawaad, wenji-go gawanaandamoog.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Well, that was what they truly did. And later in the winter when they ate them all up, there still remained the fish (of Nänabushu and his wife). Then off went the man; he arrived at where he went in the evening; alas\! he found that they must have moved camp. Thereupon the man spoke to his wife, saying: “There is a doubtful chance if we shall be able to live through the winter. Therefore on that account I will hunt for game.” So truly when he hunted, he could not kill anything. To be sure, it was a trying time; once in a long while he fetched home only a single ruffed grouse. And at last not even a single ruffed grouse did he bring home. Thereupon they were truly hungry. So on that account for sweet-brier berries they went to seek. Although they ate the sweet-brier berries, yet by degrees were they starving.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Aaniish, mii go iw geget e-zhichigewaad (well, that truly they did) ∙ gomaago e-bizhishigi-biibooninig e-zhi-gidamaawaad (later in deep winter when they ate them up) ∙ miidash eta wiinawaa ogiigoonyimiwaa (then only theirs, their fish, remained) ∙ zhigwasa maajaa a’aw inini (then off went the man) ∙ ani-dagwishing wenaagoshininig (he arrived as evening came) ∙ gonowaa gaa-oonji-goozinigwen (behold, they must have moved camp) ∙ e-zhi-ganoonaad iniw wiiwan (he spoke to his wife) ∙ “ganabaj ji-gichi-noondaayaabanizhiyang” (“a doubtful chance we live through the winter”) ∙ “Indawaasa miinooch ninga-giiyose” (“so even so I will hunt”) ∙ gaawiinsa gegoo oni’toosiin (yet nothing did he kill) ∙ wii’kaa go bezhig binewan obiinaan (once in a long while one grouse he brought) ∙ gaawiin ganage obiinaasiin (not even one did he bring) ∙ misa’ geget e-zhi-ba’kadewaad (so truly they hungered) ∙ ogini’ onandawewaawaan (sweet-brier berries they sought) ∙ wenji-go gawanaandamoog (yet they were starving).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(8) Ningutingidạc minawā äjimādcāt papạnạndawīginīwāt ä∙pidcigu kisināni kayädạcigu gī∙kạtci. Sāga∙i∙gạning äjimạdābīt. Mīdạc imā änāsạmā∙tāwạning äni∙i∙jāt ä∙pidcigu umi∙kawäᵉ ‘iᵉi’ᵘ ugineᵉ. Ānic papạgiwạyānäckimutācing udạnibinawāⁿᵉ. Ningutingigu ạnipimācagāmät, pāmāgu kägō nwäntạnk mạdwäsininig imā mi‘kwạming. Äji∙i∙nābit kägō k∙i∙a’täni. Wägunänīwinän niminā∙ku nä∙zi’kank; payäcwâbạndạnk, kuniginin pikwa‘k ma‘ku∙tawạgạn ạsawäwink\! Äji∙o∙dōdä‘pinạnk wīwâwânbạndạnk, pāmāgu awiya wändcikanōnigut: “Tawa∙t, tawa∙t\! Kīnina kīpi‘kwa‘k wätä‘pinạmạn?”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Ningoding idash miinawaa e-zhi-maajaad babaa-nandawiiginiiwaad e-bizhishig-igo gisinaani gayedash-igo gii’kaj. Zaaga’iganing e-zhi-madaabiid. Miidash imaa enaasamaa-daawaning ani-izhi-ayaad e-bizhishig-igo omii’kawe’ i’iw ogini’. Aaniish babagiwayaane-ashkimodaazhing odanibinaawan. Ningoding igo ani-bimaajagaamed, baamaa-go gegoo noondang madwesininig imaa mi’kwaming. E-zhi-inaabid gegoo gii-a’tani. Wegonen-iiwinen niminaa’ko-nazi’kang; bayeshwaabandang, gonowaa bikwa’k ma’ko-dawagan asawewing\! E-zhi-odaa’pinang wiiwaawaanaabandang, baamaa-go awiya wenji-ganoonigod: “Daawa’t, daawa’t\! Giinina gii-bikwa’k wedaa’pinaman?”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And another time when he set out to seek for sweet-brier berries, the weather was very cold and he was shivering. Out upon a lake he came. And so when he went along yonder sunny side, in abundance he found the sweet-brier berries. Now, into a miserable cloth bag he put them. And by and by, while going along the shore, he suddenly heard the sound of something fall yonder on the ice. When he looked, something was there. Thereupon he went out upon the ice, going up to where it was; when he got a near view of it, lo, (it was) an arrow feathered with the ear of a bear\! As he started to pick (the arrow) up to examine it, all at once by some one was he addressed: “Hold, hold\! Is the arrow yours that you are picking up?”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Ningoding idash miinawaa e-zhi-maajaad babaa-nandawiiginiiwaad (another time off he went seeking sweet-brier berries) ∙ e-bizhishig-igo gisinaani gayedash gii’kaj (the weather very cold and he shivering) ∙ zaaga’iganing e-zhi-madaabiid (out upon a lake he came) ∙ enaasamaa-daawaning ani-izhi-ayaad (going along the sunny side) ∙ e-bizhishig omii’kawe’ i’iw ogini’ (in plenty he found the berries) ∙ babagiwayaane-ashkimodaazhing odanibinaawan (into a poor cloth bag he put them) ∙ ani-bimaajagaamed (going along the shore) ∙ gegoo noondang madwesininig imaa mi’kwaming (he heard something fall on the ice) ∙ gonowaa bikwa’k ma’ko-dawagan asawewing (behold, an arrow feathered with a bear’s ear) ∙ e-zhi-odaa’pinang wiiwaawaanaabandang (as he took it up to look at it) ∙ baamaa-go awiya wenji-ganoonigod (suddenly someone spoke to him) ∙ “Daawa’t, daawa’t\! Giinina gii-bikwa’k wedaa’pinaman?” (“Hold, hold\! Is the arrow yours you are picking up?”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(9) Äjikạnōnāt: “Kāwīn, ạnicāgu niwīwâbạndān.” Papa‘kiwis äjikanōnigut: “Kigī‘kạtc māwīn.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**E-zhi-ganoonaad: “Gaawiin, anishaa-go niwii-waabandaan.” Papa’kiiwis e-zhi-ganoonigod: “Gigii’kaj maawiin.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Then he spoke to the being: “No, I only wanted to look at it.” Pilferer then was addressed: “You are cold, no doubt?”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">E-zhi-ganoonaad (then he spoke) ∙ “Gaawiin, anishaa-go niwii-waabandaan” (“no, I only wished to look at it”) ∙ Papa’kiiwis e-zhi-ganoonigod (Pilferer was then addressed) ∙ “Gigii’kaj maawiin” (“you are cold, no doubt?”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(10) “Kägä∙t ningī‘kạtc.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Geget ningii’kaj.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Indeed, I am cold.”*</span>

“Geget ningii’kaj” (“truly I am cold”).

<span class="l-orig">(11) “Tạga, kạbōtawạnin, intigu kigī‘kạtc.” Kägä∙t nādagā‘kōwạn pōdawäwạn. Kāki‘tcibōtawänit, a∙i∙ta ickutäng cingobīn uda‘paginäni; kayä dạc gagī∙tciwạn mạdwägīgitōwạn: “Ba‘kiwis, kāwīna kitamīdcisīnạn ini’ᵘ nintạcigạnạn?”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Daga, gaboodawanin, indigo gigii’kaj.” Geget naadagaa’koowan boodawewan. Gaa-gii’chiboodawenid, a’ita ishkodeng zhingobiin oda’baginaani; gaye dash gagii’chiwan madwe-giigidowan: “Ba’kiiwis, gaawiina gidamiijisiinan iniw nindashiganan?”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Well, I will build you a fire, for it seems that you are cold.” Truly, then on the ice went the other towards the shore to build the fire. When he had a great fire going, then right into it he flung some balsams; and as he was taking off his moccasins, he was heard to say: “Pilferer, would you not eat those stockings of mine?”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Daga, gaboodawanin, indigo gigii’kaj” (“come, I will build you a fire, for you are cold”) ∙ geget naadagaa’koowan boodawewan (truly the other went out on the ice to make a fire) ∙ gaa-gii’chiboodawenid (when he had built a great fire) ∙ a’ita ishkodeng zhingobiin oda’baginaani (into the fire he flung balsam boughs) ∙ gagii’chiwan madwe-giigidowan (and the great one was heard speaking) ∙ “Ba’kiiwis, gaawiina gidamiijisiinan iniw nindashiganan?” (“Pilferer, would you not eat these stockings of mine?”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(12) “∙Aⁿ, ningamīdcinạn.” Opi∙ā∙cawagāmäwäpina māgōn. Äjiwâbandānk, kuniginin, kāskạmi‘kwānowạn\! Ānic ä∙pidci pa∙kadā, mīdạc ‘iᵉi’ᵘ äcimīdcit. Udanuwi∙ckwạntānạn. “Kägu’\!” udigōn; “mānōgu ga‘kina mīdcin.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“A’aw, ningamiijinan.” Obii-aazhawagaamewebinaa’ migoon. E-zhi-waabandang, gonowaa, gaa-zkami’kwaanowan\! Aaniish e-bizhishig ba’kade, miidash i’iw e-zhi-miijid. Odaanawi-ishkwantaanan. “Gego’\!” odigoon; “maanoogo ga’kina miijin.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Very well, I will eat them.” He had them flung over to him from across the fire. As he looked at them, lo, there was the dried tail of a beaver\! Naturally he was exceedingly hungry, and therefore he ate (it). He tried in vain not to eat it all up. “Don’t\!” he was told; “you must eat it all.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“A’aw, ningamiijinan” (“very well, I will eat them”) ∙ obii-aazhawagaamewebinaa’ migoon (across the fire they were flung to him) ∙ e-zhi-waabandang, gonowaa, gaa-zkami’kwaanowan (when he looked, behold, a dried beaver-tail) ∙ aaniish e-bizhishig ba’kade (now, he was exceedingly hungry) ∙ miidash i’iw e-zhi-miijid (so he ate it) ∙ odaanawi-ishkwantaanan (in vain he tried to leave some) ∙ “Gego’\!” odigoon (“don’t\!” he was told) ∙ “maanoogo ga’kina miijin” (“you must eat it all”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(13) Mī cigwa kīcī‘tānit, kīmäckwạtcipīta‘kisinänit. Cigwa pasigwīwạn, “Pa‘kiwis, wägunän ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kāpinōndạmạn?”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii zhigwa giizhii’taanid, gii-meshkwaji-biita’kizinenid. Zhigwa basigwiiwan, “Ba’kiiwis, wegonen i’iw gaa-bi-noondaman?”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Now, when he was ready, he put on the moccasins of the other. When he rose to his feet, “Pilferer, what is that you are carrying on your back?”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Mii zhigwa giizhii’taanid (now when the other was ready) ∙ gii-meshkwaji-biita’kizinenid (he put on the other’s moccasins) ∙ zhigwa basigwiiwan (when he rose to his feet) ∙ “Ba’kiiwis, wegonen i’iw gaa-bi-noondaman?” (“Pilferer, what is that you carry on your back?”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(14) “Kā, uginīg. Kägätusagu nimpa‘kadämin. Mīnā igi‘ wâamwạngitwā.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Gaa, oginiig. Gegedosa-go nimba’kademin. Miinaa igiw wii-amwangidwaa.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Oh, sweet-brier berries. Really we are in want of food. Those are what we are going to eat.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Gaa, oginiig” (“oh, sweet-brier berries”) ∙ gegedosa-go nimba’kademin (truly we are in want of food) ∙ miinaa igiw wii-amwangidwaa (those are what we mean to eat).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(15) Kuniginin, upimi∙u∙tā‘pinạmini ‘iᵉi’ᵘ umackimut, pitawaiya∙ī∙ kā∙tạninạminit; äcisīgwäbinänit, a‘panä tcätcatccipän äcipạngicininit udōginīmaᵉ. “∙A∙tawā, ugī∙i∙nigä∙aᵉ ‘iᵉi’ᵘ unidcānisaᵉ\!” inäntạm. Aniniminā∙kowạn ạnita‘kunaminit ‘iᵉi’ᵘ umackimut; kīmạdwäsigwa∙i∙gäwạn.</span>
