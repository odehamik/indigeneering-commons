Nanabushu is Miraculously Fed Bear-Grease.

<span class="l-eng">*Narrated by Waasaagoneshkang (probable) — Part I Series 5 (Nos. 40–42), Bois Fort, per Corbiere roster; not printed on the page — verify.*</span>

<span class="l-orig">(1) Nänabucu a∙ī∙ndä wīwạn kayä mīnawä kayä ’i⁸i’ᵘ unī∙tcānisaᵉ nīciwaᵉ. Mīsa’ mī∙i’∙ᵘ wī⁺pibōnicit Nänabucu, kāwin kägō udayäsīn kämīdcit. <span class="l-fiero">**Nanabosho a’iinde wiiwan gaye miinawaa gaye i’iw oniijaanisa’ niijiwa’. Miisa’ mii’iw wii-biiboonijid Nanabosho, gaawiin gego odayaasiin gaa-miijid. Miisa’ mii gizhwa biibooninig maaji-’taad andawenjiged. Ee, gaawesa gego oni’toosiin\! Moonzhag ogii-gii’igoon iniw awesiyan, gaawiin ogii-wanishaasiin. Ningoding oganoonigoon wiiwan: "Aaniin ge-izhi-bimaadiziyang? Wii’kaa gego \*ji-ni’toosiwan\*."**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Nānabushu was living with his wife and two children. It was there that Nānabushu intended passing the winter, but he had nothing to eat. It was now becoming winter when he undertook to seek for game. Alas\! not a thing could he kill. Often was he given the slip by the game-kind, none did he kill. Once he was addressed by his wife saying: "How are we going to live? Never a thing do you kill."*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Nanabosho a’iinde wiiwan (Nanabosho was dwelling-there with his wife) ∙ gaye miinawaa gaye i’iw oniijaanisa’ niijiwa’ (and also his two children) ∙ Miisa’ mii’iw wii-biiboonijid Nanabosho (and so it was there that Nanabosho was going to spend the winter) ∙ gaawiin gego odayaasiin gaa-miijid (he had nothing to eat) ∙ Miisa’ mii gizhwa biiboon (and so now it was winter) ∙ maaji-’taad andawenjiged (he began to hunt for game) ∙ Ee, gaawesa gego oni’toosiin\! (Alas, nothing did he kill\!) ∙ Moonzhag ogii-gii’igoon iniw awesiyan (often the game-animals gave him the slip) ∙ gaawiin ogii-wanishaasiin (he did not kill any) ∙ Ningoding oganoonigoon wiiwan (once his wife spoke to him) ∙ "Aaniin ge-izhi-bimaadiziyang?" (How are we going to live?) ∙ "Wii’kaa gego ji-ni’toosiwan." (Never do you kill a thing.)</span>

<span class="l-orig">(2) "Nintawä mōcạg ningakīyōtä,"² i’kitu Nänabucu. Wayäbạnk äjimādcāt. Ningutingidạc, pimusät, ạnicinābän utō-’kawi∙ā∙n māda∙ạ∙nāt; sāga∙i∙ganing tāwạn. Anīnābit utōn-ta∙i∙bānining, mīdạc imāⁿ wâbạndạnk ma’kūtiskạ’k;¹ i∙i’∙wisa twa∙i∙bāgänit i∙i∙māⁿ wänta∙i∙bīnit. Änīci’kupīt, wīgiwām kīpata’kitänig. Äjipīndigät ininiwạn nạmạdạpiwạn, kayä i’kwäwạn, kayä ’i⁸i’ᵘ unītcānisini nīciwaᵉ. Inābit Nänabucu ma’kōwīninōn päpa’kwänit.³ Kägä’t umisawīnawān īni’ᵘ ma’kuwīninōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**"Nindawaa moonzhag ningad-giiwoode," ikido Nanabosho. Waabaning ezhi-maajaad. Ningodingidash, bimosed, anishinaaben oto-’kawiin maada’aanaad; zaaga’iganing dawan. Aaniinaabid otoonda’ibaaning, miidash imaan waabandang ma’kotiskaa’k; i’iwisa dwa’ibaagenid i’imaan wenda’ibiinid. Aaniizhi-gobiid, wiigiwaam gibada’kidenig. Aazhi-biindiged ininiwan namadabiwan, gaye ikwewan, gaye i’iw oniijaanisini niijiwa’. Inaabid Nanabosho makoowiininan \*baapa’kwenid\*. Geged omisawinawaan iniw makoowiininan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*"Therefore always will I go a-visiting,"² said Nānabushu. On the morrow then he departed. Now once, while walking along, he chanced upon the footprints of some people, in whose path he now followed; by a lake they lived. As he looked about the place from which they drew water, he saw there the anal gut¹ of a bear; now, that was what they used in making a hole from which to obtain water. On going up from the shore, (he saw) a wigwam standing. On entering, (he saw) a man who was seated, also a woman, and their children, numbering two. While looking about, Nānabushu saw (chunks of) bear-tallow, which they used for a lodge-covering.³ Truly did he covet the bear-tallow.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">"Nindawaa moonzhag ningad-giiwoode," ikido Nanabosho ("So then I will always go visiting," said Nanabosho) ∙ Waabaning ezhi-maajaad (on the next day he set out) ∙ Ningodingidash, bimosed (now once, as he was walking) ∙ anishinaaben oto-’kawiin maada’aanaad (he followed the trail of some people) ∙ zaaga’iganing dawan (by a lake they dwelt) ∙ Aaniinaabid otoonda’ibaaning (as he looked at their water-hole) ∙ miidash imaan waabandang ma’kotiskaa’k (and then there he saw a bear’s anal gut) ∙ i’iwisa dwa’ibaagenid i’imaan wenda’ibiinid (that was what they used to make the hole where they drew water) ∙ Aaniizhi-gobiid (going up from the shore) ∙ wiigiwaam gibada’kidenig (a wigwam stood there) ∙ Aazhi-biindiged ininiwan namadabiwan (on entering, a man sat there) ∙ gaye ikwewan, gaye i’iw oniijaanisini niijiwa’ (and a woman, and their two children) ∙ Inaabid Nanabosho makoowiininan baapa’kwenid (Nanabosho looked, bear-tallow was used as a covering) ∙ Geged omisawinawaan iniw makoowiininan (truly he coveted that bear-tallow).</span>

<span class="l-eng">*\[Jones fn 2, p.341\] Visiting among friends, generally with the sense that the visitor goes to "sponge" off his guests. — \[Jones fn 1, p.342\] The anal gut was used to start the hole in the ice by one end of it being held down on the ice, and some one sucking from the other. — \[Jones fn 3, p.342\] The true idea conveyed here is that the strangers had so much food that they could even use it for making a dwelling.*</span>

<span class="l-orig">(3) Mīdạc ’i⁸i’ᵘ kīgitōwạn ininiwạn: "Wägunäcina kägīgä∙ạ∙nk³ aᵘ bīwitä?" Mīdạc iᵘ äjikīgitunit īni’ᵘ i’kwäwạn: "Ānīcînā äntōtạmạn wāwīsiniyạngiban, mī∙i’∙ᵘ kätōtamạmbạn tcigīgä∙i∙wäyạn."</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash i’iw giigidowan ininiwan: "Wegonezhinaa ge-giigi-aank a’aw biiwide?" Miidash iw aazhi-giigidonid iniw ikwewan: "Aaniizhinaa endodamaan waa-wiisiniyaangiban, mii’iw ge-doodamambaan ji-giigi-iiweyan."**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And then up spoke the man: "What shall we give the guest (to eat)?" Whereat up spoke the woman: "Why, the same as you generally do when we want to eat, is what you should do when providing your gift."*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Miidash i’iw giigidowan ininiwan (and then the man spoke) ∙ "Wegonezhinaa ge-giigi-aank a’aw biiwide?" ("What shall we feed that visitor?") ∙ Miidash iw aazhi-giigidonid iniw ikwewan (and then the woman spoke) ∙ "Aaniizhinaa endodamaan waa-wiisiniyaangiban" ("In whatever way I usually do when we want to eat") ∙ "mii’iw ge-doodamambaan ji-giigi-iiweyan." ("that is what you should do when you make your gift.")</span>

<span class="l-eng">*\[Jones fn 3, p.342\] Ugicōta·u·waⁿ, "smitten;" the usual word for smitten is mintcikitwanag. The word used here is for protection against cold, and it may refer to mittens or ear-protectors; it also refers to the string of rabbit-fur that is put through the hole of the ear in order, so it is said, to keep the ear warm. The sense of the word would often seem to imply that it meant ear-warmers or ear-protectors; but it is given as mittens, because the Ojibwa themselves regard that as the sense of the word.*</span>

<span class="l-orig">(4) Äjikīgitut ’a⁸a’ᵘ inini: "Tạga, pīdōn ’i⁸i’ᵘ utcictcini’k."⁴ Kägä’t äcimīnint ’a⁸a’ᵘ inini. Kāijimīnint, "’Ā’aᵘ, unāgạn kāsīyāpi’kinạn."</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Aazhi-giigidod a’aw inini: "Daga, biidoon i’iw oji’jini’k." Geged ezhi-miinind a’aw inini. Gaa-izhi-miinind, "’Aaw, onaagan gaaziiyaabi’kinan."**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Then up spoke the man: "Well, fetch hither the awl."⁴ Truly was it then given to the man. After it had been given to him, "Now, then, a vessel do you wipe."*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Aazhi-giigidod a’aw inini (then the man spoke) ∙ "Daga, biidoon i’iw oji’jini’k." ("Now, bring the awl.") ∙ Geged ezhi-miinind a’aw inini (truly it was given to the man) ∙ Gaa-izhi-miinind (when it had been given to him) ∙ "’Aaw, onaagan gaaziiyaabi’kinan." ("Now, wipe out a vessel.")</span>

<span class="l-eng">*\[Jones fn 4, p.342\] Utcictcini’k, "awl;" that is, the awl made from the ulna, usually of a moose, deer, or caribou.*</span>

Kā’kīci’tōt, mägwāgu nạmạdạpinit īni’ᵘ ininiwạn, pānimāgu pasingutcisäwạn nawạtinạminit ’i⁸i’ᵘ ạbạⁿj "Saⁿ, saⁿ, saⁿ, saⁿ\!" inwäwạn. Mīdạc imāⁿ pimitā’kupitcigạning kuniginīn uniciciwāni owācänāni, pä’kic nōndāgusiwan, "Sạnk, sạnk\!" inwänit. Mīdạc ’i⁸i’ utcictcini’k änīma°wānit ’i⁸i’ᵘ uniciciwāni kāgīcîm pacipa°wānit ’i⁸i’ᵘ uniciciwāni. Ningutingiku, äcipạcipa∙°∙wänit ’i⁸i’ᵘ uniciciwāni, pạnägu ma’kupimitä untcitciwạnini. <span class="l-fiero">**Geged a’a’ikwe aazhi-gaaziiyaabi’kinaank, wawiinge gaa-biini’tood iw onaagan; miinawaa i’iw oji’jini’k aazhi-gaaziiyaabi’kinaang. Aazhi-giigidod a’aw aji-dam: "Miinooj agonan iw onaagan."**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Truly the woman wiped it, thoroughly clean did she make the vessel; next the awl was what she wiped. After she was done with her work, and while the man was seated, of a sudden up he sprang, seizing hold of the lodge-pole. "Saⁿ, saⁿ, saⁿ\!" (such) was the sound of his voice. Thereupon yonder upon the cross-pole (he was surprised to see him) exposing his testes, while at the same time he could be heard making the sound, "Sạnk, sạnk\!" (such) was the sound he made. And it was with the awl that he aimed at his testes and almost piercing his testes when he did it. And then of a sudden, when he pierced his testes, immediately some bear-grease came flowing out. Then up spoke the Squirrel: "Hold the vessel close up against it\!"*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Geged a’a’ikwe aazhi-gaaziiyaabi’kinaank (truly the woman wiped it) ∙ wawiinge gaa-biini’tood iw onaagan (thoroughly she cleaned the vessel) ∙ miinawaa i’iw oji’jini’k aazhi-gaaziiyaabi’kinaang (and again the awl she wiped) ∙ Gaa-gizhi’tood (when she had finished) ∙ megwaagu namadabinid iniw ininiwan (while the man was sitting) ∙ baanimaagu basingojisewan (suddenly he sprang up) ∙ nawadinaminid i’iw abanj (he seized the lodge-pole) ∙ "Zan, zan, zan, zan\!" inwewan ("Zan, zan, zan, zan\!" was his cry) ∙ Miidash imaan bimida’kobijiganing (and then there on the cross-pole) ∙ goniginiin onizhizhiwaani owaajenaani (to his surprise he was exposing his testes) ∙ baa’kij noondaagosiwan, "Zank, zank\!" inwenid (at the same time he was heard, "Zank, zank\!" was his sound) ∙ Miidash i’iw oji’jini’k aanimaa’wanid i’iw onizhizhiwaani (and then with the awl he aimed at his testes) ∙ gaa-gizhiim bazhiba’wanid i’iw onizhizhiwaani (almost piercing his testes) ∙ Ningodingigo, aazhi-bazhiba’wanid i’iw onizhizhiwaani (suddenly, when he pierced his testes) ∙ banaagu makobimide ondijijiwanini (right away bear-grease came flowing out) ∙ Aazhi-giigidod a’aw aji-dam (then the Squirrel spoke) ∙ "Miinooj agonan iw onaagan." <span class="l-orig">(6) Kägä’t a∙a∙i∙kwä äjiada∙tōt imāⁿ kä∙i∙cipangigänik ’i⁸i’ᵘ pimita; mistci wība mōckinäbīnik. Kā∙i∙jimōckinäbīni’k i wunāgạn, pinīsāntawäwạn. "Mīsạgunā∙i∙ᵘ ä∙i∙cīyān kayänīn wāwīsiniyānin."</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Geged a’a’ikwe aazhi-ada’tood imaan gaa-izhi-bangigenik i’iw bimide; mishchi wiiba mooshkinebiinik. Gaa-izhi-mooshkinebiini’k i onaagan, biniisaandawewan. "Miisagunaaw ezhi-iiyaan gaye niin waa-wiisiniyaanin."**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Truly, the woman then placed it there, where the grease might drip into it; and very soon it was full. When the vessel was full, then down came (the man) climbing (the lodge-pole). "This is just a way I have whenever I too am hungry to eat."*</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Naturally, Nānabushu was very hungry. "Would that I might presently be given something to eat\!" thought Nānabushu.
