(55) The Origin of Likeness of Nenabosho

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash imaa (and so there) gchi-gbeyiing (for a very long while) gii-yaad (he stayed) wa Nenabosho (that Nenabosho).</span>

1)  > **Mii dash imaa gchi-gbeyiing gii-yaad wa Nenabosho.**

> And so there for a long while continued Nanabushu.

<span class="l-gloss">Ngoding dash (now once) gii-aanjiiwag (they moved camp) Nenaboozhoo wiidgemaagnan (Nenabosho and his wife), gookmisan dash (and his grandmother) ogii-wiijii’aagoowaan (went along with them).</span>

2)  > **Ngoding dash gii-aanjiiwag Nenaboozhoo wiidgemaagnan, gookmisan dash ogii-wiijii’aagoowaan.**

> Now, once on a time to another place moved Nanabushu and his wife and by his grandmother were they accompanied.

Pane gii-ndawenjige (he was always out hunting for food).

3)  > **Pane gii-ndawenjige.**

> Ever was Nanabushu in quest of game,

<span class="l-gloss">Nenaboozhoo, amikwan gaye gii-nooj’aan (Nenabosho hunted beavers too); naab’gan gii-zhitoon Nenaboozhoo (Nenabosho made a harpoon) mii wi gaa-aabajtood (that was what he used) mikwan gii-bjibwaad (when he speared the beaver); michigiw zhinkaade (michigiw it is called) wi okan gaa-zhitood (that bone point he made).</span>

4)  > **Nenaboozhoo, amikwan gaye gii-nooj’aan; naab’gan gii-zhitoon Nenaboozhoo mii wi gaa-aabajtood mikwan gii-bjibwaad; michigiw([<span class="underline">1</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)) zhinkaade wi okan gaa-zhitood.**

> For beavers too he hunted; a harpoon Nanabushu made, and that was what he used when he speared the beaver; spine-of-a-pickerel-fin is the name of the bone (point) which he made.

<span class="l-gloss">Ngoding dash gii-mkawaan (now once he found) gchi-amikwan daanid (where the great beavers lived) mii owe gchi-gaming (over here in the great water), aapji maangmingizwag (they were very huge).</span>

5)  > **Ngoding dash gii-mkawaan gchi-amikwan daanid mii owe gchi-gaming, aapji maangmingizwag.**

> Now, once he found a place over here in the sea where the giant beavers dwelt, they were very huge.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash e-naad wiidgenmaagnan (and so he said to his wife): “Nga-nooj’aag giwi amikwag.” (“I am going after those beavers.”)</span>

6)  > **Mii dash e-naad wiidgenmaagnan: “Nga-nooj’aag giwi amikwag.”**

> Thereupon he said to his wife: “I am going after these beavers.”

<span class="l-gloss">Niizhinoon niwi amikwag endaawaad (two were the places where those beavers lived), mii iwe bezhig Minong (one was over there, Minong) miinwaa dash Mishibiigwadoo-mnising (and the other at Mishibiigwadoo Island).</span>

7)  > **Niizhinoon niwi amikwag endaawaad, mii iwe bezhig Minong([<span class="underline">2</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)) miinwaa dash Mishibiigwadoo-mnising.([<span class="underline">3</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html))**

> Two were the places where those beavers dwelt, one was over here at Isle Royal, the other was at Michipicoten Island.

Mii iwe gaye amikwag gaa-daawagbanen (those too were where the beavers used to live).

8)  > **Mii iwe gaye amikwag gaa-daawagbanen.**

> Now, those were the places where the beavers dwelt.

Ogii-biig’aanan niwi amik-gamgoon (he broke apart those beaver lodges).

9)  > **Ogii-biig’aanan niwi amik-gamgoon.**

> He destroyed the beaver dwellings.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash gii-bbaamaazhgaamed (and so he went along the shore) owe gchi-gami (of this great water); aanind gii-nsaan amikoonsan (some of the little beavers he killed) bezhig gaye gchi-amikwan (and one big beaver too); bezhig dash gchi-amikwan (but one other big beaver) gaawiin mkawaasiin (he did not find).</span>

10) > **Mii dash gii-bbaamaazhgaamed owe gchi-gami; aanind gii-nsaan amikoonsan bezhig gaye gchi-amikwan; bezhig dash gchi-amikwan gaawiin mkawaasiin.**

> Thereupon he wandered away, following the shore of this sea; some of the small beavers he killed, one large beaver too; but one other large beaver he did not find.

<span class="l-gloss">Gegpii dash gii-nendam (so at last he thought): “Indawaa nga-biig’aan wi gbaakwignan (“so then I will break the dam), maanoo dash da-skate maanda gchi-gami (let this great water go dry), mii wi ji-mkawag wa amik.” (for then I shall find that beaver.”)</span>

11) > **Gegpii dash gii-nendam: “Indawaa nga-biig’aan wi gbaakwignan,([<span class="underline">4</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)) maanoo dash da-skate maanda gchi-gami, mii wi ji-mkawag wa amik.”**

> So at last he thought: “Therefore I will destroy the (beaver) dam, no matter if this sea should go dry, for then I shall find the beaver.”

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash gaa-zhi-wiijiiwaad gookmisan (and so he had his grandmother go with him) widi gbaakwigning (over there to the dam).</span>

12) > **Mii dash gaa-zhi-wiijiiwaad gookmisan widi gbaakwigning.**

> Thereupon he had his grandmother go with him to yonder (beaver) dam.

<span class="l-gloss">Pii dash gaa-biig’ang gbaakwigan (and when he broke the dam), “Mii maa yaan (“you stay right here), ga-kwaabmaa wa amik (watch for that beaver) jibwaa-bmaaboozod wa amik,” (so the beaver does not float past with the current,”) dinaan (he said to her).</span>

13) > **Pii dash gaa-biig’ang gbaakwigan, “Mii maa yaan, ga-kwaabmaa wa amik jibwaa-bmaaboozod wa amik,” dinaan.**

> And when he demolished the dam, “In this place do you remain, do you watch that the beaver does not float by with the current,” he said to her.

Aa\! Mii dash gii-zaagjiwang wi nbi (ah\! and then the water poured out).

14) > **Aa\! Mii dash gii-zaagjiwang wi nbi.**

> Ah\! And then out the water flowed.

<span class="l-gloss">Miinwaa dash Nenaboozhoo gii-maajiiyaazhgaame (and once more Nenabosho set off along the shore) naagdood maanda gchi-gami (following this great water).</span>

15) > **Miinwaa dash Nenaboozhoo gii-maajiiyaazhgaame naagdood maanda gchi-gami.**

> So once more Nanabushu set out, following the shore of this sea.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash maa Mishibigwadoo-mnising gaa-aazhigwaashkonid (and so he leaped across to Mishibiigwadoo Island); megwaa dash imaa naaniibwid (and while he stood there) waabmaan ngigwan (he saw an otter) eni-skatenig (where the water was going dry).</span>

16) > **Mii dash maa Mishibigwadoo-mnising gaa-aazhigwaashkonid; megwaa dash imaa naaniibwid waabmaan ngigwan eni-skatenig.**

> And then across to Michipicoten Island he leaped; and while he was standing over there, he saw an otter where the water was running low.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash gaa-ni-bskaabigwaashkonid (and so he leaped back across), mii dash imaa gii-zhaazh’shing zhashkiikaang (and there he slid in the mud), gii-zhaashkjiishkweshing (he slipped and fell).</span>

17) > **Mii dash gaa-ni-bskaabigwaashkonid, mii dash imaa gii-zhaazh’shing zhashkiikaang, gii-zhaashkjiishkweshing.**

> Accordingly back across he leaped, whereat he slipped and fell in the mud.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash gaa-bzigwiid (and so when he stood up), gii-baaptoon imaa gaa-mzinaakdiyeshing (he laughed at the spot where his backside left its imprint); myaa go e-zhinaagzid zhinaagwadni (just as he himself looks was how it appeared).</span>

18) > **Mii dash gaa-bzigwiid, gii-baaptoon imaa gaa-mzinaakdiyeshing; myaa go e-zhinaagzid zhinaagwadni.**

> And so, when he rose to his feet, he laughed at the spot where he had left an imprint of his bottom; precisely like the form on him was the way it looked.

<span class="l-gloss">“Maanoo, noozhshenyag (“no matter, my grandchildren) bjiinag ge-ni-bmaadziwaad (who will live by and by) oga-baaptoonaawaa.” (will have it to laugh at.”)</span>

19) > **“Maanoo, noozhshenyag<span class="underline">[(](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-line-by-line.html#sdfootnote1sym)[5](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)</span>) bjiinag ge-ni-bmaadziwaad oga-baaptoonaawaa.”**

> “No matter, let my grandchildren that shall live hereafter have it to laugh at.”

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash gaa-zhi-noopnanaad niwi ngigwan (and so when he chased that otter) gii-bjibwaan wi mchigiwanit (he speared it with that fin-spine point).</span>

20) > **Mii dash gaa-zhi-noopnanaad niwi ngigwan gii-bjibwaan wi mchigiwanit.**

> And when he pursued the otter, he pierced it with the fin spine of his harpoon.

Wii-wiisni giiwenh (he wanted to eat, they say).

21) > **Wii-wiisni giiwenh.**

> He was eager to eat, they say.

<span class="l-gloss">“Indawaa kawe nga-mwaa wa ngig,” (“so then first I will eat that otter,”) gii-nendam (he thought).</span>

22) > **“Indawaa kawe nga-mwaa wa ngig([<span class="underline">6</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)),” gii-nendam.**

> “Accordingly before (proceeding further) I will eat the otter.” He thought.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash gaa-zhi-bkonaad (and so when he had skinned it), gii-boodwe (he made a fire); mii dash ezhi-bwed (and then he roasted it on a spit).</span>

23) > **Mii dash gaa-zhi-bkonaad, gii-boodwe; mii dash ezhi-bwed.**

> And so, when he had flayed it, he built a fire; thereupon he roasted it on the spit.

<span class="l-gloss">Pii dash gaa-giizhizwaad (and when he had finished cooking it), gii-bdakinaan (he stuck it upright in the ground).</span>

24) > **Pii dash gaa-giizhizwaad, gii-bdakinaan.**

> And when he had finished cooking it, he stuck (the spit into the ground) with (the otter still) on it.

Mii wi ezhi-wawenabid (then he sat down comfortably).

25) > **Mii wi ezhi-wawenabid.**

> Then he sat down.

<span class="l-gloss">Zhigwa dash gaa-giizhkizhang (and when he sliced off) bezhig wi ngig-otawag (one of the otter’s ears), mii wi noondwaad gookmisan (he then heard his grandmother) mdwe-gwiishk’zhinid (whistling) widi Baawting (off yonder at the Sault).</span>

26) > **Zhigwa dash gaa-giizhkizhang bezhig wi ngig-otawag,([<span class="underline">7</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)) mii wi noondwaad gookmisan mdwe-gwiishk’zhinid widi Baawting.**

> And when with a knife he sliced off one of the otter’s ears, he then heard the sound of his grandmother whistling off yonder at the Sault.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash gaa-zhi-bzingonjised (and so springing up), gii-maajiibtood (he started running). Mii dash gaa-zhi-bzingonjised (springing up), gii-maajiibtood (he started running).</span>

27) > **Mii dash gaa-zhi-bzingonjised, gii-maajiibtood. Mii dash gaa-zhi-bzingonjised, gii-maajiibtood.**

> Thereupon leaping to his feet, he started off a-running.

<span class="l-gloss">Pii dash begmabtood widi Baawting (and when he came running up to the Sault), “Aaniin?” (“what is it?”) dinaan gookmisan (he said to his grandmother).</span>

28) > **Pii dash begmabtood widi Baawting, “Aaniin?” dinaan gookmisan.**

> And when he came running up to yonder Sault, “What (is it)?” he said to his grandmother.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash ekidod wa magkii-mdimooye (and so said the old frog-woman): “Zhaazhi go gii-bmaaboozo wa amik.” (“already that beaver floated by with the current.”)</span>

29) > **Mii dash ekidod wa magkii-mdimooye;([<span class="underline">8</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)) “Zhaazhi go gii-bmaaboozo wa amik.”**

> Thereupon said the old Frog-Woman: “It is gone, floating with the current went the beaver.”

<span class="l-gloss">Epiichi-nshkaadzid dash wa Nenaboozhoo (and so angry was Nenabosho) mii wi gaa-zhi-naaniimadwaad niwi gookmisan (that he struck his grandmother again and again).</span>

30) > **Epiichi-nshkaadzid dash wa Nenaboozhoo mii wi gaa-zhi-naaniimadwaad niwi gookmisan.**

> And so angry was Nanabushu, that he then smote his grandmother (till she was dead).

Mziwe dash gii-mskwiiwaabkaa wi wajiw (and everywhere the mountain was reddened with blood).

31) > **Mziwe dash gii-mskwiiwaabkaa wi wajiw.**

> And everywhere was the mountain reddened with blood.

<span class="l-gloss">“Magkiiwaabkong da-zhinkaade,” (“Frog-Rock it shall be called,”) gii-kido Nenaboozhoo (said Nenabosho).</span>

32) > **“Magkiiwaabkong da-zhinkaade,” gii-kido Nenaboozhoo.**

> “Frog Mountain shall it be called,” said Nanabushu.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash widi aano-gii-bbaa-ndawaabmaad niwi amikwan (and so off yonder he went searching in vain for that beaver), gaawiin dash ogii-mkawaasiin (but he did not find it).</span>

33) > **Mii dash widi aano-gii-bbaa-ndawaabmaad niwi amikwan, gaawiin dash ogii-mkawaasiin.**

> Thereupon off yonder he wandered, looking in vain for the beaver, but he did not find it.

<span class="l-gloss">Miinwaa dash gii-bi-giiwe (and so again he came homeward), geyaabi gii-bbaa-ndawaabanjiged (still searching about) geyaabi ji-yaanid amikwan (whether there were yet any beavers); gaawiin dash wiya gii-mkawaasiin (but he found none).</span>

34) > **Miinwaa dash gii-bi-giiwe, geyaabi gii-bbaa-ndawaabanjiged geyaabi ji-yaanid amikwan; gaawiin dash wiya gii-mkawaasiin.**

> So again he turned his way homeward, still yet was he roaming from place to place to find if yet there were any beavers; but he found none.

<span class="l-gloss">Mziwe aano-gii-bbaa-zhaa (everywhere he wandered in vain); imaa amik-gamgoon gaa-nji-biig’ang (there where he had broken the beaver lodges), mii eta mtigoon gaa-biingdamwaad amikwag (only the logs the beavers had gnawed to pieces) gaa-waabandangin (were what he saw).</span>

35) > **Mziwe aano-gii-bbaa-zhaa; imaa amik-gamgoon gaa-nji-biig’ang, mii eta mtigoon gaa-biingdamwaad amikwag gaa-waabandangin.**

> Everywhere he went wandering, but without success; there where he had broken up the beaver dwellings, all that he saw were the logs which the beavers had gnawed to pieces.

Miinwaa dash gii-ni-giiwe’aazhgaame (and so again he headed back home along the shore).

36) > **Miinwaa dash gii-ni-giiwe’aazhgaame.**

> So again he turned back home, going by way of the shore.

<span class="l-gloss">Pane dash go enso-waamdang (and every time he saw, along the way) eni-piiskaad (as he passed) oodi gaa-nji-mzinaakdiyeshing (yonder where his backside had left its imprint), gchi-baaptoon (he laughed heartily at them).</span>

37) > **Pane dash go enso-waamdang eni-piiskaad oodi gaa-nji-mzinaakdiyeshing, gchi-baaptoon.**

> As often as he beheld the places on the way where he had slipped and fell, heartily he laughed at them.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash imaa Mishibiigwadoo-wiikwedong (and so there at Mishibiigwadoo Bay) mii maa gaa-zhi-wenabid (was where he sat down).</span>

38) > **Mii dash imaa Mishibiigwadoo-wiikwedong mii maa gaa-zhi-wenabid.**

> And so yonder at Michipicoten Bay was where he sat down.

<span class="l-gloss">“Giishpin anishnaabeg ge-bmi-waabmiwaad (“if the people see me as they pass by) giishpin bangii semaan miizhwaad (if they give a little tobacco) nendawendmowaad ji-naam’amwaad (when they wish for a fair wind), mii wi negaaj ji-boodaajgeyaan.” (then gently I would blow.”)</span>

39) > **“Giishpin anishnaabeg ge-bmi-waabmiwaad giishpin bangii semaan miizhwaad nendawendmowaad ji-naam’amwaad, mii wi negaaj ji-boodaajgeyaan.”**

> “If people behold me when passing by, if they should give me a little tobacco in their wish for a fair wind, then gently would I blow (with my breath).”

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash go imaa pane nmadbid (and so there he sits forever), anishnaabe gii-nmadbid (like a person sitting), mii wi ezhinaagwag wi asin (that is how the rock looks).</span>

40) > **Mii dash go imaa pane nmadbid, anishnaabe gii-nmadbid, mii wi ezhinaagwag wi asin([<span class="underline">9</span>](http://ojibweproject.weebly.com/the-origin-of-likeness-of-nenabosho-footnotes.html)).**

> And so there he still sits, like a person sitting is the way the rock looks.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii dash go geget e-zhwebag (and that, sure enough, is what happens); giishpin wiya bngii bgidnaad semaan (if anyone sets down a little tobacco), “Nenaboozhoo\! G-biindaakoonin (“Nenabosho\! I make you a tobacco offering) ga-bgosenmin ji-mnowaanmak,” (we ask of you for fair weather,”) mii go geget ezhi-mnowaanmak (then sure enough the weather turns fair).</span>

41) > **Mii dash go geget e-zhwebag; giishpin wiya bngii bgidnaad semaan, “Nenaboozhoo\! G-biindaakoonin ga-bgosenmin ji-mnowaanmak,” mii go geget ezhi-mnowaanmak.**

> And that, sure enough, is what happens; if any one offers a little tobacco (with) “O Nanabushu\! I come with an offering to you, we wish for a fair wind,” verily there comes up a fair wind.

<span class="l-gloss">Mii sa ekoozid (that is as far as it goes), binewidis gii-agoode (and so the gizzard of the ruffed grouse hangs aloft).</span>

42) > **Mii sa ekoozid, binewidis gii-agoode.**

> That is as far as the story goes, the gizzard of the ruffed grouse now hangs aloft.

**Footnotes** :

<span class="l-gloss">(1) Pointed with a single barb. The shaft is longer than the barb, and has a hole at one end through which to fasten the cord (Jones 1917, p.428).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(2) West and not far from Fort William (Jones 1917, p. 429).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(3) North of Sualt Ste. Marie (Jones 1917, p. 429).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(4) At the head of Sault Ste. Marie. By destroying it, the rapids were made (Jones 1917, p.430).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(5) The usual expression is “my nephews,” which implied also “my aunts,” meaning the people (Jones 1917, p.430).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(6) This is the first instance where the transcription uses nigig as opposed to niigig for otter (a.c.).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(7) The otter on the spit can be seen, so it is said, as a shaft of rock on the Wisconsin shore of Lake Superior (Jones 1917, p. 431).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(8) Another name for Mother Earth, or the grandmother of Nenabosho (Jones 1917, p. 431).</span>

<span class="l-gloss">(9) In various places in the Ojibwa country may be observed a rock, island, or high land looking like a human being either reclining or seated, when seen from the distance, and it is generally called Nenabosho (Jones 1917, p.432).</span>
