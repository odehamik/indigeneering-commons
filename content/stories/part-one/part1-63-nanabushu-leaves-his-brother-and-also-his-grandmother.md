<span class="l-orig">(1) Mīdạc Nänabucō anicinā ātisō‘kän tibātcimint, kī∙a∙nimi∙tcätōgwän i∙i∙we minis, kā‘pītcipā‘tîninit pämādisinit imāⁿ minising. Mīdạc kayä win tibādcimint imā kīwunänigwändạnk. A‘pīdạc Nänabucu ga‘kina kā∙i∙cictcigät, mîgī∙i∙nāt kīwâwindạmawāt wägunän kā∙u∙ndcipimādisinit. Mīdạc kayä iwä a‘pī kīwāwinät iᵘ kädicinikānänit awäsīyaᵉ kayä awäsīyänsaᵉ kayä pābāmōtänit a‘king. Mīdạc kayä tibātcimint iwä a‘pī kīwâwinät iᵘ kīⁿgōⁿyaᵉ kā∙u∙ci∙ā∙t undci inī‘ᵘ micinạmägwän kābigickicwät, kayä wänänạn kädạmwāsinig; mī∙i∙ änādcimint Nänabucu.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii dash Nenabozho anishinaa aadizookaan dibaajimind, gii-ani-mi-jietoogwen i’iwe minis, gaa-bichibaa’tininid bemaadizinid imaaⁿ minising. Mii dash gaye wiin dibaajimind imaa giiwenh enigwendang. A’pii dash Nenabozho gakina gaa-izhi-izhichiged, mii gii-inaad giiwaawindamawaad wegonen gaa-onji-bimaadizinid. Mii dash gaye iwe a’pii giiwaawinaad iw ged-izhinikaanenid awesiiyan\* gaye awesiiyensan\* gaye babaamoodenid akiing. Mii dash gaye dibaajimind iwe a’pii giiwaawinaad iw giigooyan\* gaa-onji-aad inii’iw mishi-namegwan\* gaabigishkishwed, gaye wenenan ged-amwaasinig; mii i’ enaadimind Nenabozho.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Thereupon Nȧnabushu, according to the story that is told of him, must have set to work to enlarge the size of the island, so great was the throng of them living there on the island. Now, it is also told of him that there was he very content. And after Nȧnabushu had finished everything, he then spoke to them, and told them upon what they should subsist. And that was also the time he named what (the people) should call the big animal-folk and the little animal-folk and them that crawl upon the ground. And the story is also told of him how at the time he named the fishes which he had cut up, and them that should not be eaten for food, such is what they tell of Nȧnabushu.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">mii dash — and so / and then; Nenabozho aadizookaan dibaajimind — Nenabozho (in) the sacred-story as told of him; gii-ani-mi-jietoogwen — he must have gone on to make it \[the island\] thus (dubitative, “must have…”); i’iwe minis — that island; gaa-bichibaa’tininid bemaadizinid imaaⁿ minising — where the living-ones there on the island were crowded thick; mii dash gaye wiin … giiwenh enigwendang — and so too, so they say, was he content / minded-so there; a’pii dash … gakina gaa-izhi-izhichiged — and when he had done everything thus; mii gii-inaad giiwaawindamawaad — then he told them, naming-over for them; wegonen gaa-onji-bimaadizinid — by what they were to live; iw a’pii giiwaawinaad — that time he named-over; iw ged-izhinikaanenid awesiiyan\* gaye awesiiyensan\* — what they were to call the big animal-folk and the small animal-folk; gaye babaamoodenid akiing — and the ones that crawl about on the earth; iw giigooyan\* gaa-onji-aad — the fishes he had made/cut up; inii’iw mishi-namegwan\* gaabigishkishwed — those great sturgeon-fish he had cut to pieces; gaye wenenan ged-amwaasinig — and which were not to be eaten; mii i’ enaadimind Nenabozho — that is what is told of Nenabozho. (\*flagged — see footnote/flag list.)</span>

<span class="l-orig">(2) Mīdạc kīwä äjikanōnāt ōsạn ugīn kayä: “Nōs,” udinān, “mīsa cigwa tcimādcāyān. Kīndạc, nisayāⁿ, Nänä‘pādạm, mī gīn omāⁿ ayāⁿ tcikanawänimạtwā ōmā äyädcig,” udinān; “tciugīma‘kandawạtwā,” udinān. “Nīndạc niwī∙i∙jā; niwī∙nandunäwā nō‘kumis,” udinān. “Ningīwâwindạmawā,” udinān. “A‘pō‘tcinạngä tabickō kigiyāwimin a‘panä kāwitcayāwindiyạng,” udinān. “Kīn win, nisayāⁿ, wäntci‘ta tcayāwindiyạng,” udinān.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii dash giiwenh ezhi-ganoonaad oosan ogiin gaye: “Noos,” odinaan, “mii-sa zhigwa ji-maajaayaan. Giin dash, nisayenh, Nenaabaadam, mii giin omaaⁿ ayaaⁿ ji-ganawenimadwaa omaa eyaajig,” odinaan; “ji-oogimaa-ganawendwaa,” odinaan. “Niin dash niwii-izhaa; niwii-nandawenawaa nookomis,” odinaan. “Ningiiwaawindamawaa,” odinaan. “Aano-tchinange dabashkoo gigiiyaawimin apane gaa-wiijiayaawindiyang,” odinaan. “Giin wiin, nisayenh, wenji’taa ji-ayaawindiyang,” odinaan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And then they say that he spoke to his father and his mother, saying: “My father,” he said to them, “the time is at hand for me to go away. — And you, my elder brother, Nȧnä-pȧdȧm, do you stay here to watch over them who are here,” he said to him, “to be ruler over them,” he said to him. “And I wish to go away; I wish to seek my grandmother,” he said to him. “And myself, I shall go away: I wish to seek for my grandmother,” he said to him. “Anyhow, we both have not had the same kind of birth, so that we should ever be together,” he said to him. “You yourself, my elder brother, like a real human being; and (as for) myself, …”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">mii dash giiwenh ezhi-ganoonaad oosan ogiin gaye — and so, they say, he spoke to his father and his mother; “Noos” odinaan — “My father,” he said to him; mii-sa zhigwa ji-maajaayaan — now is the time for me to set out; giin dash, nisayenh, Nenaabaadam — and you, my elder brother, Nenaabaadam; mii giin omaaⁿ ayaaⁿ ji-ganawenimadwaa omaa eyaajig — you stay here to watch over the ones who are here; ji-oogimaa-ganawendwaa — to keep them as chief/ruler; niin dash niwii-izhaa — and I, I will go; niwii-nandawenawaa nookomis — I will go seek my grandmother; ningiiwaawindamawaa — I have named/foretold (it) to him; aano-tchinange … apane gaa-wiijiayaawindiyang — even though we are not of one same being to be always together; giin wiin, nisayenh, wenji’taa ji-ayaawindiyang — you, my elder brother, are the reason we are (as we are) together (sentence trails into §3).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(3) anicinābäng kigiyaᵉ; niodạc wayābinigätāg mī∙i∙mā wändciyāⁿ,” udinān usayäⁿyạn. Ānawidạc Nänä‘pātạm kāwin minwändạnzī pābîgä tcipa‘kä∙ā∙t ucimäyạn, ānodạc pagusinimān tciwīdciwāt.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**anishinaabeng gigiiyaa\*; niin dash wayaabinigeteg mii imaa wenjiyaaⁿ,” odinaan osayenyan. Aano dash Nenaabaadam gaawiin minwendanzii baabiige ji-bake’aad oshiimeyan, aano dash bagosinimaan ji-wiijiwaad.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“… like a real human being; and (as for) myself, what was thrown away (at birth) was the source from which I sprang,” he said to his elder brother. And though Nȧnä-pȧdȧm was not pleased with the thought that so soon was to part from him his younger brother, yet it was useless for him to part from him to let him go along.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">anishinaabeng gigiiyaa\* — you are (made) like a human being; niin dash wayaabinigeteg mii imaa wenjiyaaⁿ — but I, it is from what was thrown away that I come; odinaan osayenyan — he said to his elder brother; aano dash Nenaabaadam gaawiin minwendanzii — and although Nenaabaadam was not pleased; baabiige ji-bake’aad oshiimeyan — that so soon he was parted from his younger brother; aano dash bagosinimaan ji-wiijiwaad — and in vain did he beg to go along with him. (\*flagged — kigiyaᵉ / gigiiyaa\* reading.)</span>

<span class="l-orig">(4) Nänabucu dạc ugi‘känimān, mīdạc änāt: “Nisayāⁿ\! nōngum kijiga‘k kīgawidciwin, niwī‘kicipā‘kän o∙o∙we minis nawạtc tcimisteäg,” udinān, “pä‘kic tcigi‘kändạmạn ānin änigu‘kwāg käganawändaman.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Nenabozho dash ogii-genimaan, mii dash enaad: “Nisayenh\! noongom giizhigak giga-wiijiiwin, niwii-gizhiibaakaan o’owe minis nawach ji-mishteyaag,” odinaan, “bekish ji-gikendaman aaniin enigokwaag ge-ganawendaman.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And Nȧnabushu knew it (in advance); and so this he said: “My elder brother\! to-day I wish you to go with me, for I wish to walk round this island, that I may see how large it may become,” he said to him, “that at the same time you may know how big is the region over which you should keep watch.”*</span>

Nenabozho dash ogii-genimaan — and Nenabozho knew it (of him); mii dash enaad — and so he said to him; “Nisayenh\! noongom giizhigak giga-wiijiiwin — “My elder brother\! today I will take you along; niwii-gizhiibaakaan o’owe minis nawach ji-mishteyaag — I wish to round out / finish this island that it be the larger; bekish ji-gikendaman aaniin enigokwaag ge-ganawendaman — at the same time that you may know how wide is the place you are to keep watch over.”

<span class="l-orig">(5) Mīdạc kägä‘t ajimādcäwāt, tcätcikākusäwāt a‘kubîgänig. Ạnibạbimusäwād dạc ningudingigu māminonändạm awi nini; äbanābit, a‘ki ä‘ta wayābandạnk\! Pä‘kic kayä anigāgigitōwạg, kāwīn dạc kägō i‘kitosī wäsayä∙i∙mint. “Kici‘kätā,” udigōn ucimäyạn Nänabucōn. “Wîba tawunāgucin, kāwīn kīgatagwicinzimin ändāyạng,” udinān usayäⁿyạn. Mīdạc kī∙a∙niwâwindamawāt usayäⁿyạn kädici∙u∙gimā‘kandawänit imā äyānitciᵉ.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii dash gaget ezhi-maajaawaad, je-jiikaakosewaad akobiigenig. Ani-babimosewaad dash ningoding igo maaminonendam awi inini; ebaanaabid, aki eta wayaabandang\! Bekish gaye anigaa-gaagiigidoowag, gaawiin dash gegoo ikidosii wesayei-mind. “Gizhiikaadaa,” odigoon oshiimeyan Nenabozhoon. “Wiiba dawunaagozhin, gaawiin giga-dagwishinzimin endaayang,” odinaan osayenyan. Mii dash gii-ani-giiwaawindamawaad osayenyan ged-izhi-oogimaa-ganawendamenid imaa eyaanidi\*.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And so truly they two then set out, walking along by the edge of the water. Now, as they thus walked along, of a sudden mindful was the man; as he looked behind, land only did he see. Now, as they went they talked, but nothing did he say who was the elder brother. “Let us quicken our pace\!” he was told by his younger brother Nȧnabushu. “Soon will it be evening, (and) we shall not return to where we live,” he said to his elder brother. And so he explained to his elder brother how he should rule over them who were there.*</span>

mii dash gaget ezhi-maajaawaad — and so indeed the two set out; je-jiikaakosewaad akobiigenig — walking along the edge of the water; ani-babimosewaad dash ningoding igo maaminonendam awi inini — and as they walked along, of a sudden the man was thoughtful; ebaanaabid, aki eta wayaabandang — looking back, only land did he see; bekish gaye anigaa-gaagiigidoowag — meanwhile they talked easily/much; gaawiin dash gegoo ikidosii wesayei-mind — but the elder one said nothing; “Gizhiikaadaa” odigoon oshiimeyan Nenabozhoon — “Let us hurry,” he was told by his younger brother Nenabozho; “Wiiba dawunaagozhin, gaawiin giga-dagwishinzimin endaayang” — “Soon it will be evening, we will not get home where we live”; odinaan osayenyan — he said to his elder brother; mii dash gii-ani-giiwaawindamawaad osayenyan — and so he went on naming/explaining for his elder brother; ged-izhi-oogimaa-ganawendamenid imaa eyaanidi\* — how he was to rule over and watch the ones there. (\*flagged — eyaanitciᵉ ending.)

<span class="l-orig">(6) Käga‘pīdạc udigōn usayäⁿyạn: “Nīcim\!” udigōn, “wägunändạc kīn wändciogimā‘kandawāsiwạtwā, kin kipimātci∙a∙‘twā īgi’ᵘ pämādisiwāt?” udinān.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Gega’pii dash odigoon osayenyan: “Niishim\!” odigoon, “wegonen dash giin wenji-oogimaa-ganawendamaasiwadwaa, giin gi-bimaaji-a’adwaa igi’iw bemaadiziwaad?” odinaan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*At last he was asked by his elder brother: “My younger brother\!” he was told, “why is it that you yourself will not rule over them, you who give life to them that are living?” he said to him.*</span>

gega’pii dash odigoon osayenyan — at last he was asked by his elder brother; “Niishim\!” odigoon — “My younger brother\!” he was told; wegonen dash giin wenji-oogimaa-ganawendamaasiwadwaa — why is it that you yourself do not rule over them; giin gi-bimaaji-a’adwaa igi’iw bemaadiziwaad — you who give life to those who are living; odinaan — he said to him.

<span class="l-orig">(7) Mīdạc Nänabucu äjina‘kwä‘tawāt usayäⁿyạn: “Nisayāⁿ\!” udinān, “kīn mā kīdinänimin tcikanawänimạtwā,” udinān.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii dash Nenabozho ezhi-nakwe’tawaad osayenyan: “Nisayenh\!” odinaan, “giin maa gidinenimin ji-ganawenimadwaa,” odinaan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And then Nȧnabushu, making answer to his elder brother: “My elder brother\!” he said to him, “it is you whom I wish to watch over them,” he said to him.*</span>

mii dash Nenabozho ezhi-nakwe’tawaad osayenyan — and so Nenabozho, answering his elder brother; “Nisayenh\!” odinaan — “My elder brother\!” he said to him; giin maa gidinenimin ji-ganawenimadwaa — it is you indeed I intend to watch over them; odinaan — he said to him.

<span class="l-eng">*“Oh\!” he was told.*</span>

“Oonh\!” odigoon — “Oh\!” he was told.

<span class="l-orig">(9) Mīdạc cigwa anitạgwicinowāt kā∙u∙ndcimādcāwāt; cayigwa imā äni∙a∙yāwāt päcuᶜ mī wâbandạnk wäsayä∙i∙mint ki‘tci cīmạga‘k owä zībi? Kāwīn kägō niwâbandanzīnāban,” udinān ucīmäyạn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii dash zhigwa ani-dagwishinowaad gaa-onji-maajaawaad; zhayiigwa imaa ani-ayaawaad beshoᶜ mii waabandang wesayei-mind gichi-shimaagak owe ziibi? Gaawiin gegoo niwaabandanziinaaban,” odinaan ozhiimeyan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And then in time they came back to the place from whence they had departed; (but) when they were nearly there, then was beheld by him who was the elder brother a great river flowing by. (“From) whence flows this river? Nothing (of a river) did I see before,” he said to his younger brother.*</span>

mii dash zhigwa ani-dagwishinowaad gaa-onji-maajaawaad — and now in time they arrived back where they had set out from; zhayiigwa imaa ani-ayaawaad beshoᶜ — already as they came near there; mii waabandang wesayei-mind gichi-shimaagak owe ziibi — the elder one saw this great river flowing by; “Gaawiin gegoo niwaabandanziinaaban” — “Nothing (such) had I seen before”; odinaan ozhiimeyan — he said to his younger brother.

<span class="l-orig">(10) Mīdạc Nänabucu änāt: “Kägä‘t,” udinān. “Kiwâbandān na agāming? Mī∙i∙mā kā∙u∙ndcimādcäyạng,” udinān.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii dash Nenabozho enaad: “Gaget,” odinaan. “Giwaabandaan na agaaming? Mii imaa gaa-onji-maajaayang,” odinaan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And then Nȧnabushu said to him: “Ay,” he said to him. “Do you see the other shore? It is there from whence we started,” he said to him.*</span>

mii dash Nenabozho enaad — and so Nenabozho said to him; “Gaget” odinaan — “Truly,” he said to him; Giwaabandaan na agaaming — Do you see the far shore?; mii imaa gaa-onji-maajaayang — it is there we set out from; odinaan — he said to him.

<span class="l-orig">(11) Māma‘kādändạm idạc Nänä‘pādạm. Mīdạc änändạnk: “Tibi kā∙u∙ndcikaski∙o∙wängän?” inändạm.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Maamakaadendam idash Nenaabaadam. Mii dash enendang: “Tibi gaa-onji-gashki-owengen?” inendam.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Astonished then was Nȧnä-pȧdȧm. And then he thought: “Wonder where we shall be able to get across?” thus he thought.*</span>

maamakaadendam idash Nenaabaadam — and Nenaabaadam was amazed; mii dash enendang — and so he thought; “Tibi gaa-onji-gashki-owengen?” — “Where might we manage to cross over?”; inendam — he thought.

<span class="l-orig">(12) Nänabucu dạc awạntcicigu pä‘bā‘pi; ạni∙i∙yinäbit pä‘kic ani∙a∙ⁿdawâbandạnk kägō mi‘tig owayacawä‘kuwäbinạnk sibink kä∙u∙ndcikaski∙o∙wāt. Mēdạc kägä‘t kī∙a∙nimi‘kạng, mīgu ni‘tạm a‘tōd uzit minawā dạc päjik umbinạng, mī äja agāming kita‘ku‘kit. Mīdạc tcipwāpigwa‘kitāt mī äja kayä win Nänabucu. Mīdạc inābit, mī wâbandạng anigu‘kuta‘tigwäyānig iᵘ zibi äbiding ä‘ta kā∙u∙da∙a∙mīt.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Nenabozho dash aano-jiigo bebaa’pi; ani-iiyinaabid bekish ani-andawaabandang gegoo mitig owayazhawe’kuwebinang ziibing ge-onji-gashki-owaad. Mii dash gaget gii-ani-mi’kang, mii go nitam a’tood ozid minawaa dash bezhig oombinang, mii azhi agaaming gita’kookid. Mii dash jiipwaa-bigwa’kitaad mii azhi gaye wiin Nenabozho. Mii dash inaabid, mii waabandang anigokwa-mitigweyaanig iw ziibi ebiding eta gaa-oda’amiid.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*But Nȧnabushu, on his part, only gave a laugh; as he went he observed, and as he went he was at the same time seeking for some kind of a log to lay across the stream so that they might be able to cross. It was true that he found one on the way: “Come on\!” he said to his elder brother. “You first cross over on the log.” “How shall we be able to get over?” he said to him. “Why, we shall not fail in the undertaking\!” he said to him. Thereupon truly (he) crossed (it) he over upon the log; the moment he put down one foot and then lifted the other, that very instant he stepped on the other shore. And so before he turned about (to see if the other was coming), then was Nȧnabushu also across. And as he looked, he then beheld how wide was the river which only at a single step he had passed across.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Nenabozho dash aano-jiigo bebaa’pi — but Nenabozho only laughed (to no purpose); ani-iiyinaabid bekish ani-andawaabandang gegoo mitig — going along he looked about, searching for some log; owayazhawe’kuwebinang ziibing ge-onji-gashki-owaad — to toss across the river so they could get over; mii dash gaget gii-ani-mi’kang — and indeed he found (one); mii go nitam a’tood ozid minawaa dash bezhig oombinang — he set down one foot first and then lifted the other; mii azhi agaaming gita’kookid — and already he stepped on the far shore; mii dash jiipwaa-bigwa’kitaad — and so before he turned about; mii azhi gaye wiin Nenabozho — Nenabozho too was already (across); mii dash inaabid, mii waabandang anigokwa-mitigweyaanig iw ziibi — and looking, he saw how wide the river ran; ebiding eta gaa-oda’amiid — that at a single step he had crossed it.</span>

<span class="l-orig">(13) Mīdạc imā cayigwa Nänabucu wîpa‘kä∙ā∙t usayäⁿyạn: “Mīsa iᵘ, nisayāⁿ,” udinān, “tcipa‘kä∙i∙nān. Mī iwiti kwaya‘k ijān. Mī iwiti tciwâbạmạtwā kānaganạngwä,” udinān. “Ōwitidạc kayä nīn nīngạtijā,” udinān.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Mii dash imaa zhayiigwa Nenabozho wii-bake’aad osayenyan: “Mii-sa iw, nisayenh,” odinaan, “ji-bake’inaan. Mii iwidi gwayak izhaan. Mii iwidi ji-waabamadwaa gaa-naganangwaa,” odinaan. “Owidi dash gaye niin ningad-izhaa,” odinaan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*It was there that Nȧnabushu now meant to part with his elder brother. “The time has come, my elder brother,” he said to him, “for me to part from you.
