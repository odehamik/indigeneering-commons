Nänabushu, the Sweet-Brier Berries, and the Sturgeons.

<span class="l-fiero">**Nenabozho, Oginiig, gaye Namewag.**</span>

Nänabushu, the Sweet-Brier Berries, and the Sturgeons.

<span class="l-eng">*Narrated by Waasaagoneshkang (Part I Series 1, Bois Fort — per Corbiere roster; verify if printed)*</span>

<span class="l-fiero">***from Ojibwa Texts collected by William Jones (1917).***</span>

<span class="l-orig">(1) Ānic, mīsa kāni·i·cimāmādcāt. Ningutingigu kā·i·ciwâbạmāt ‘iᵉi’ᵘ anicinābä witạgwāgicinit, mīdạc kā·i·ci·nāt: “Ambäsạnō, nicimisä,” ugī·i·nā ‘iᵉi’ᵘ anicinābäᵉ. Ugiwâbạmān kīgōⁿi‘känit, ānic mīdạc kayä wīn i·i·mā wi·a·yāt, ō·ō·widac ugī·i·nān: “Ambäsạnō kawītcitạgwāgicimin.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Aaniish, miisa gaa-ni-izhi-maamaajaad. Ningoding igo gaa-izhi-waabamaad i’iw anishinaabe wii-dagwaagishinid, miidash gaa-izhi-inaad: “Ambesanoo, nishiimishe,” ogii-inaa i’iw anishinaabe’. Ogii-waabamaan giigoonh-ikenid, aaniish miidash gaye wiin imaa wii-ayaad, oo’owidash ogii-inaan: “Ambesanoo gawii-dagwaagishimin.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Well, accordingly then went he slowly along his way. And once after he had seen where some people were intending to spend the autumn, he then said to them: “I beg of you, my younger brothers,” he said to the people. He saw them engaged in catching fish, so naturally desired to remain there too, and this he said to them: “I beg that you let me spend the autumn with you.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Aaniish, miisa gaa-ni-izhi-maamaajaad (well, so off he went slowly along) · Ningoding igo gaa-izhi-waabamaad i’iw anishinaabe (once when he saw the people) · wii-dagwaagishinid (who meant to spend the fall) · gaa-izhi-inaad (he said to them) · “Ambesanoo, nishiimishe” (“I beg of you, my younger brothers”) · ogii-inaa i’iw anishinaabe’ (he said to the people) · Ogii-waabamaan giigoonh-ikenid (he saw them catching fish) · gaye wiin imaa wii-ayaad (and he too wished to stay there) · “Ambesanoo gawii-dagwaagishimin” (“I beg to spend the fall with you”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(2) “Awawa,” ugī·i·gōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Awawa,” ogii-igoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“You may,” he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Awawa” (“you may”) · ogii-igoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(3) Mīdạc kā·i·ciwītigämāt. Ānic unisāwaᵉ kīⁿgōⁿya·ᵉ i·i·māⁿ tạgwāgiciwāt. Abā‘pic cigwa käckạtininig, ānic mīsa i·i·māⁿ pibōniciwāt. Ānic ayāwaᵉ unīdcānisiwaᵉ. Abā‘pic cigwa gätamwāwāt ‘iᵉi’ᵘ ugīgōⁿimiwā. Mīdạc ō·ō· kā·i·nāt īni’ᵘ uwīdigämāgạnạn: “Indawā sạnā, kīni‘tạmawā igi’ᵘ kigīⁿgōⁿimiwa kīgạgitamwānānig; pāmādạc nīnawint ickwätc kīgạtamwānānig.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash gaa-izhi-wiidigemaad. Aaniish onisaawe’ giigoonhya’e imaan dagwaagishiwaad. Ababii’pich zhigwa gashkadininig, aaniish miisa imaan biboonishiwaad. Aaniish ayaawe’ oniijaanisiwe’. Ababii’pich zhigwa gedamwaawaad i’iw ogiigoonhimiwaa. Miidash oo’o gaa-inaad iniw owiidigemaaganan: “Indawaa sanaa, giin’tamawaa igiw gigiigoonhimiwaa giga-gidamwaanaanig; baamaadash niinawind ishkwech giga-gidamwaanaanig.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Thereupon he abode with them. Now they killed fish there where they were spending the autumn. In the course of time (the lake) was frozen over, so thereupon there they spent the winter. Now they had some children. As time went on, they ate up (all) their fish. Thereupon this was what (Nänabushu) said to them with whom he lived: “Now, therefore, we will eat your fishes first; and then afterwards, when they are gone, then our fish will we eat.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">gaa-izhi-wiidigemaad (so he dwelt with them) · giigoonhya’e imaan dagwaagishiwaad (they killed fish there where they spent the fall) · zhigwa gashkadininig (when it froze over) · imaan biboonishiwaad (there they spent winter) · ayaawe’ oniijaanisiwe’ (they had their children) · gedamwaawaad i’iw ogiigoonhimiwaa (they ate up their fish) · gaa-inaad iniw owiidigemaaganan (he said to those he lived with) · “giin’tamawaa ... giga-gidamwaanaanig (“first your fish we’ll eat up) · baamaadash niinawind ishkwech giga-gidamwaanaanig” (afterward ours we’ll eat”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(4) Mīdạc gägäᵗ kā·i·cictcigäwāt. Ānic mīsa’ kägäᵗ kā·i·ji·a·mwāwāt ‘iᵉi’ᵘ ugīgōⁿ·i·mini. Abā‘pic kädạmwāwāt ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kīⁿgōⁿya·ᵉ. Mīdạc a‘pī kā‘kidạmwāwād īni’ᵘ uwīdigämāgạnạn ogīgōimini, iniwisa utạgāmidạ‘kawāgạnạn, ānic mīgạ‘kina ‘iᵉi’ᵘ ugīgōⁿimini; mīdạc kā·i·ciniskädisītawat, mīdạc i·i·witi iciwināt īni’ᵘ ugīⁿgōⁿ·i·mạn. Ānic mîsa pa‘kadänit īni’ᵘ kā‘kidāmawāt īni’ᵘ ugīⁿgōⁿimini. Ānic mīdạc ‘aᵉa’ᵘ inini ạnicā uginīn wändcipimādci·ā·t ‘iᵉi’ᵘ unīdcānisaᵉ.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash geget gaa-izhi-izhichigewaad. Aaniish miisa’ geget gaa-izhi-amwaawaad i’iw ogiigoonhimini. Ababii’pich gedamwaawaad i’iw giigoonhya’e. Miidash a’pii gaa-gidamwaawaad iniw owiidigemaaganan ogiigoo’imini, iniwisa odagaamida’kawaaganan, aaniish miigakina i’iw ogiigoonhimini; miidash gaa-izhi-nishkedizii’tawaad, miidash i’iwidi izhiwinaad iniw ogiigoonh-imani. Aaniish miisa bakadenid iniw gaa-gidamawaad iniw ogiigoonhimini. Aaniish miidash a’aw inini anishaa oginiin wenji-bimaaji-aad i’iw oniijaanise’.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And so truly that was what they did. Now, it was true that they ate the fish of the others. In course of time they ate up (all) the fish. And so after they had eaten up the fish of his companions, they that were on the opposite side of the (lodge) fire, then gone were all the fish of the other; thereupon he became angry at them, and so moved away. Not far away he made his camp, and so of course thither he took his own fish. So thereby hungry became the others whose fish he had eaten up. Now, as for the man (whose fish had been eaten up), he kept his children alive by means of sweet-brier berries.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">geget gaa-izhi-izhichigewaad (truly that they did) · geget gaa-izhi-amwaawaad i’iw ogiigoonhimini (truly they ate the others’ fish) · gaa-gidamwaawaad iniw owiidigemaaganan (when they ate up his companions’) · iniwisa odagaamida’kawaaganan (those across the fire) · gaa-izhi-nishkedizii’tawaad (he grew angry at them) · i’iwidi izhiwinaad iniw ogiigoonh-imani (over there he took his own fish) · miisa bakadenid (so they were hungry) · a’aw inini ... wenji-bimaaji-aad i’iw oniijaanise’ (the man kept his children alive by sweet-brier berries).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(5) Ningutingigu tạgwicing ‘aᵉa’ᵘ inini. “Mīmāwīntcigawanān damạng,” ugī·i·nān īni’ᵘ wīwạn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Ningoding igo dagwishing a’aw inini. “Miimaawiinjigawanaan damang,” ogii-inaan iniw wiiwan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*So once when home came the man, “Now, I fear that we shall starve,” he said to his wife.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Ningoding igo dagwishing a’aw inini (once the man came home) · “Miimaawiinjigawanaan damang” (“I fear we will starve”) · ogii-inaan iniw wiiwan (he said to his wife).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(6) “Mīmāwīni·i·ᵘ,” ugī·i·gōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Miimaawini’iw,” ogii-igoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“I fear so,” he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Miimaawini’iw” (“I fear so”) · ogii-igoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(7) Mīdạc cigwa mīnawā äciwâbininig mīnawā ạnimādcā nạndawâbạmāt īni’ᵘ uginīn. Ningutingigu ạnipimādạgā‘kut sāgạ·i·gạning, ạnitcātcīkäwāt, ningutingigu kägō ugīⁿgōⁿdām madwäsininig mīcīwā‘kung. Ugiwâbạndān a‘tänig, mīdạc kā·i·cināsi‘kạnk, kuniginīn pikwa‘k\! Mīdạc kạnawâbạndạnk wā·i·ci·u·dā‘pinạnk. Kumiginīn awiya ugīmạdwägạnōnigōn: “Tatata,” ugī·i·gōn; “kīnina ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kipigwa‘k,” ugī·i·gōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash zhigwa miinawaa e-zhi-waabininig miinawaa ani-maajaa nandawaabamaad iniw oginiin. Ningoding igo ani-bimadagaa’kod zaaga’iganing, ani-jaajiikewaad, ningoding igo gegoo ogiigoonhdam madwesininig miijiiwaa’kong. Ogii-waabandaan a’tenig, miidash gaa-izhi-naazikang, gonowininh bikwa’k\! Miidash ganawaabandang waa-izhi-odaa’pinang. Gomiginiin awiya ogii-madwe-ganoonigoon: “Tatata,” ogii-igoon; “giinina i’iw gibigwa’k,” ogii-igoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And so on the following day he started on his way again to seek for sweet-brier berries. And once as he was travelling over the ice of the lake, as he went walking along the shore-line, he suddenly heard the sound of something out on the ice. He saw that an object was there, and so went up to it, and lo, it was an arrow\! Accordingly he gazed upon it with a desire to pick it up. He was startled at the sound of somebody’s voice saying to him: “You fool,” he was told; “is that your arrow?” he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">zhigwa miinawaa e-zhi-waabininig (when day came again) · ani-maajaa nandawaabamaad iniw oginiin (he set out seeking the sweet-brier berries) · ani-bimadagaa’kod zaaga’iganing (going across on the lake ice) · ani-jaajiikewaad (walking along the shore) · gegoo madwesininig miijiiwaa’kong (something sounding out on the ice) · gaa-izhi-naazikang, gonowininh bikwa’k\! (going up to it, lo, an arrow\!) · waa-izhi-odaa’pinang (as he meant to take it up) · awiya ogii-madwe-ganoonigoon (a voice spoke to him) · “Tatata” (“you fool”) · “giinina i’iw gibigwa’k” (“is that your arrow?”) · ogii-igoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-eng">*Tatata, “you fool,” an adverb of imprecation.*</span>

<span class="l-orig">(8) Oᵉ·ō·widac ugī·i·nān: “Kāwīn,” ugī·i·nān. “Anicāgu nīwiwâbạndān.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Oo’owidash ogii-inaan: “Gaawiin,” ogii-inaan. “Anishaa go niwii-waabandaan.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And this he said to him: “Nay,” he said to him. “I desire only to look at it.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Oo’owidash ogii-inaan (this he said to him) · “Gaawiin” (“no”) · “Anishaa go niwii-waabandaan” (“I just want to look at it”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(9) “Tạga, pōdawän kundigu kigī‘kạtc,” ugī·i·gōn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**“Daga, boodawen gondigo gigii’kach,” ogii-igoon.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*“Come, kindle a fire. It seems as if you are cold,” he was told.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">“Daga, boodawen (“come, build a fire) · gondigo gigii’kach” (it seems you are cold”) · ogii-igoon (he was told).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(10) O·o·dạc ugī·i·nān: “Äyäᵉ, kägäᵗ nigī‘kạtc.” Mīdạc kägäᵗ ạcipōdawat, mīdạc i·i·māⁿ äci·ā·wasut.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Oo’odash ogii-inaan: “Aye’, geget nigii’kach.” Miidash geget izhi-boodawad, miidash imaan ezhi-aawasod.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And this he said to him: “Yes, truly, I am cold.” Accordingly, indeed, he kindled a fire, and so there he warmed himself.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">Oo’odash ogii-inaan (this he said to him) · “Aye’, geget nigii’kach” (“yes, truly I am cold”) · geget izhi-boodawad (truly he built a fire) · imaan ezhi-aawasod (there he warmed himself).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(11) Mīdạc i·i·māⁿ äjigạgiˢtcīnit, mīdạc, “Tạga, mīdcin īni’ᵘ indạcikạnạn,” ugī·i·gōn. Mīdạc kägäᵗ āci·ō·dā‘pinạnk īni’ᵘ wayānạn nigānigu mä‘tcīgisinit, mīwạniniᵘ wäma‘kisinit; iniwidạc mạskitibānạbīn mīwạniniᵘ pägwa·i·gātänig ‘iᵉi’ᵘ uma‘kisining. Mīdạc kā‘kījibabī‘tcīnit kā·i·cināsi‘kạminit ‘iᵉi’ᵘ umạckimut, ugīckạckimut. Mīdạc kā·i·cisīgwäbinānit ‘iᵉi’ᵘ udūginīmaᵉ, mīdạc kā·i·cimōckina·ā·nit ami‘kuminạn.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash imaan e-zhi-gagii’chiinid, miidash, “Daga, miijin iniw indashiganan,” ogii-igoon. Miidash geget aazhi-odaa’pinang iniw wayaanan niigaanigo me’chiigisinid, miiwaniniw wema’kisinid; iniwidash mashkidibaanaabiin miiwaniniw begwa’igaadenig i’iw oma’kisining. Miidash gaa-giizhibabii’chiinid gaa-izhi-naazikaminid i’iw omashkimod, ogishkashkimod. Miidash gaa-izhi-ziigwebinaanid i’iw odooginiime’, miidash gaa-izhi-mooshkina-aanid amikominan.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And then the other took off his moccasins, whereupon, “Pray, eat these moccasins of mine,” he was told. Accordingly, indeed, he took the other’s moccasins, and what was he to behold when he took the other’s moccasins but really the dried tails of beavers\! (continuing) When the other had emptied his cedar-bark bag of its sweet-brier berries, he filled the bag up with beaver berries.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">imaan e-zhi-gagii’chiinid (there he took off his footwear) · “Daga, miijin iniw indashiganan” (“come, eat these moccasins of mine”) · geget aazhi-odaa’pinang iniw wayaanan (truly he took up the skins) · me’chiigisinid, miiwaniniw wema’kisinid (the dried things, his moccasins) · begwa’igaadenig i’iw oma’kisining (patched onto his moccasin) · gaa-izhi-naazikaminid i’iw omashkimod (he went to his bag) · gaa-izhi-ziigwebinaanid i’iw odooginiime’ (he poured out the sweet-brier berries) · gaa-izhi-mooshkina-aanid amikominan (he filled it with beaver berries).</span>

<span class="l-eng">*The tail of a beaver dried by fire is a delicacy.*</span>

<span class="l-orig">(12) Mīdạc kā·i·ci·u·mbawạnä·i·gut. Mīdạc a‘pī kā·ō·mbawạnä·i·gut ō·ō·dạc ugī·i·gōn: “Anipäcwānbandạmạn iimā ändāyäg unābạndạn kiˢtciwānu‘kạmigāg, mīdạc i·i·māⁿ kā·i·cipạgạtciwäpa‘kāndaman ‘iᵉi’ᵘ kimackimut. Mīdạc kānicimādcāyạn, kägu’ wīn äbanābi‘kän. Pāmādạc kigicäp kīgapi·i·nāp. Ayāngwāmisin; kici‘kän o·o·māⁿ tcimādcāyạn; awiya kīganōndawāg ‘iᵉi’ᵘ tcisä‘kwānikwa, o·o·dạc kīga·i·gōg: ‘ ‘Āᵉa’ᵘ, kungwau‘k\!’ kīga·i·gōg. Gägu’tạc äbanābi‘kän; ayāngwāmisin. Mīgu·i·ᵘ icictcigän i·i·wisa äciki·kino·a·mōnān.”</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash gaa-izhi-ombawana’igod. Miidash a’pii gaa-ombawana’igod oo’odash ogii-igoon: “Ani-besh-waabandaman iimaa endaayeg onaabandan gizhichiwaano’kamigaag, miidash imaan gaa-izhi-bagajiwebaa’kandaman i’iw gimashkimod. Miidash gaa-ni-zhi-maajaayan, gego wiin aabanaabi’ken. Baamaadash gigizheb giga-bi-inaab. Ayaangwaamizin; gizhiika’en oo’omaan ji-maajaayan; awiya giga-noondawaag i’iw ji-zaa’kwaanikwaa, oo’odash giga-igoog: ‘ ‘A’aw, gonowaa’k\!’ giga-igoog. Gego’dash aabanaabi’ken; ayaangwaamizin. Mii i’iw izhichigen i’iwisa ezhi-gi-gikino-amoonaan.”**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And when by the other he was helped with lifting on his pack, this was he then told: “When you have come nigh to the place where you (and the others) live, then select a large hollow space of ground, and there is where you should put down that pack of yours. And then you should continue on your way, and look not back behind you. Not till in the morning should you go and look. Exert yourself; make haste as you go on this path; for the sound of somebody will you hear yelling at you, and this you will be told: ’Hey, push him\!’ will you be told. So look not back; be careful. Do precisely as I have taught you.”*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">gaa-izhi-ombawana’igod (he was helped to lift the pack) · “Ani-besh-waabandaman iimaa endaayeg (“when you come near where you live) · onaabandan gizhichiwaano’kamigaag (choose a big hollow in the ground) · imaan ... bagajiwebaa’kandaman i’iw gimashkimod (there set down your pack) · gego wiin aabanaabi’ken (don’t look back) · baamaadash gigizheb giga-bi-inaab (not until morning go and look) · ayaangwaamizin; gizhiika’en (be careful; hurry) · awiya giga-noondawaag ... ‘gonowaa’k\!’ (you’ll hear someone: ‘push him\!’) · gego aabanaabi’ken (don’t look back) · Mii i’iw izhichigen ... ezhi-gi-gikino-amoonaan” (do just as I taught you”).</span>

<span class="l-orig">(13) Mīdạc kägäᵗ kā·i·cimādciba‘tōt. O·ō·widạc ugī·i·gōᵉ ‘iᵉi’ᵘ päminica·u·gut: “‘Āᵉa’ᵘ, kungwa·u··k\!” ugī·i·gōᵉ. Mīdạc kāwīn gayapī iwati nō‘piming kānibiminija·u·gusī, ugī·i·gōn. Mīdạc kägäᵗ kāwīn keyābi ugī·a·ni·a·mạnisutuwāsīn iwiti kāni‘kupīt. Mīdạc kägäᵗ kī·a·ni·a·ntawâbạndạnk i·i·māⁿ tcigiˢtcīwāna‘kạmigānig. Mīdạc kägäᵗ äciwâbạndạnk i·i·māⁿ ki‘tcīwāna‘kạmigānig, mīdạc i·i·māⁿ kā·i·jipạgạtciwäpa‘kāndạnk. Mīsa kägäᵗ kāwīn kī·ā·bạnābisī. Mīsa kāniijikīwät.</span>

<span class="l-fiero">**Miidash geget gaa-izhi-maajiiba’tood. Oo’owidash ogii-igoo’ i’iw beminizha’ogod: “‘A’aw, gonowaa’k\!” ogii-igoo’. Miidash gaawiin gaye-bii iwidi noopiming gaa-ni-biminizha’ogosii, ogii-igoon. Miidash geget gaawiin geyaabi ogii-ani-amanisotawaasiin iwidi gaa-ni-gobiid. Miidash geget gii-ani-andawaabandang imaan jigizhichiwaano’kamigaanig. Miidash geget e-zhi-waabandang imaan gizhichiwaano’kamigaanig, miidash imaan gaa-izhi-bagajiwebaa’kandang. Miisa geget gaawiin gii-aabanaabisii. Miisa gaa-ni-ji-giiwed.**</span>

<span class="l-eng">*And so truly off he started running. And this he was told by them who pursued him: “Hey, push him\!” he was told. And now, indeed, he heard them a short distance away; it seemed that now they would overtake him. So out upon the ice of a lake he came fleeing. And not-withstanding that, already was he coming close to the other side in his flight over the ice, yet exceedingly hard was he now being pressed by them who were pursuing him. And then presently was he arriving at the other side of the frozen lake; and when he was come at the other side of the ice, gone were they by whom he was pursued.*</span>

<span class="l-gloss">geget gaa-izhi-maajiiba’tood (truly off he ran) · ogii-igoo’ i’iw beminizha’ogod (he was told by his pursuers) · “‘A’aw, gonowaa’k\!” (“hey, push him\!”) · gaawiin ... noopiming gaa-ni-biminizha’ogosii (he would no longer be pursued at the forest) · gaawiin geyaabi ogii-ani-amanisotawaasiin (he no longer felt them) · gii-ani-andawaabandang imaan jigizhichiwaano’kamigaanig (he sought the big hollow ground) · gaa-izhi-bagajiwebaa’kandang (there he flung down his pack) · gaawiin gii-aabanaabisii (he did not look back) · Miisa gaa-ni-ji-giiwed (and so he went home).</span>
